The generating-function identity for elementary symmetric polynomials

For any variables $y_1,\dots,y_N$,

$$\prod_{j=1}^{N}(1+y_j)=\sum_{m=0}^{N}e_m(y_1,\dots,y_N).$$

Here $N=n-1$ and the variables are $\{r_\ell:\ell\neq i\}$, so the right side runs $e_0+\cdots+e_{n-1}$.

Why it's true — just expand the product. Each factor $(1+r_\ell)$ offers two choices: take the $1$, or take the $r_\ell$. Multiplying out, every term in the expansion corresponds to choosing a subset $S$ of the indices where you picked $r_\ell$ (and picked $1$ everywhere else):

$$\prod_{\ell\neq i}(1+r_\ell)=\sum_{S\subseteq\{\ell\neq i\}}\;\prod_{\ell\in S} r_\ell.$$

Now group those subsets by their size $m=|S|$:

$$\sum_{S\subseteq\{\ell\neq i\}}\prod_{\ell\in S} r_\ell =\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}\underbrace{\sum_{|S|=m}\prod_{\ell\in S}r_\ell}_{=\,e_m(r_{-i})} =\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}e_m(r_{-i}),$$

because “sum of all degree-$m$ squarefree monomials” is precisely the definition of the elementary symmetric polynomial $e_m$.

  • The empty subset gives $1=e_0$.
  • Singletons give $\sum_\ell r_\ell=e_1$.
  • The full subset gives $\prod_\ell r_\ell=e_{n-1}$.

Concrete check ($n-1=3$ variables $r_2,r_3,r_4$):

$$(1+r_2)(1+r_3)(1+r_4) =\underbrace{1}_{e_0} +\underbrace{(r_2+r_3+r_4)}_{e_1} +\underbrace{(r_2r_3+r_2r_4+r_3r_4)}_{e_2} +\underbrace{r_2r_3r_4}_{e_3}.$$
Equivalently: $\prod_{\ell}(1+r_\ell\,t)=\sum_{m} e_m\,t^{m}$ is the standard generating polynomial; setting $t=1$ gives the identity.
