The odds-ratio form: n equations are slices of one polynomial

The obstruction: each $W_i$ uses elementary symmetric polynomials $e_m(r_{-i})$ in a different set of variables $r_{-i}=\{r_\ell:\ell\neq i\}$. The fix: stop treating these as separate objects and read them all off a single global polynomial.

Everything is a deletion of one polynomial

Let

$$g(t)=\prod_{\ell=1}^{n}(1+r_\ell t)=\sum_{m=0}^{n}E_m\,t^m,\qquad E_m=e_m(r_1,\dots,r_n).$$

Dropping the variable $r_i$ just removes one factor:

$$h_i(t):=\prod_{\ell\neq i}(1+r_\ell t)=\frac{g(t)}{1+r_i t}=\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}e_m(r_{-i})\,t^m.$$

So “$e_m$ in the different set $r_{-i}$” $=$ the coefficients of $g(t)/(1+r_i t)$. The deletion formula

$$e_m(r_{-i})=\sum_{s=0}^{m}(-r_i)^s\,E_{m-s}$$

writes every one of your $n$ different-variable quantities in terms of the same global $E_1,\dots,E_n$ and the single scalar $r_i$. The variable-sets aren’t really different objects — they are $g$ seen through $n$ different single-factor quotients.

What $W_i$ becomes

Two linear functionals act on $h_i$: the value $\mathrm{ev}_1(h_i)=h_i(1)$, and the partial coefficient sum $L_k(h_i)=e_0+\cdots+e_{k-1}$ (note $L_k(p)=[t^{k-1}]\,p(t)/(1-t)$). Define the numerator and denominator

$$N_i:=L_k(h_i)=\sum_{m=0}^{k-1}e_m(r_{-i}),\qquad D_i:=h_i(1)=\prod_{\ell\neq i}(1+r_\ell)=\frac{g(1)}{1+r_i},$$

so that

$$W_i=\frac{N_i}{D_i}=\frac{L_k(h_i)}{h_i(1)}.$$

Now the structure is transparent.

  • Denominators alone determine everything. Since $D_i=g(1)/(1+r_i)$, $$\prod_i D_i=\frac{g(1)^n}{\prod_i(1+r_i)}=g(1)^{\,n-1} \;\Rightarrow\; g(1)=\Big(\textstyle\prod_i D_i\Big)^{1/(n-1)},\quad 1+r_i=\frac{g(1)}{D_i}.$$ So if you can recover the $D_i$, you recover all $r_i$. Injectivity $\iff$ the ratios $\{N_i/D_i\}$ pin down the $\{D_i\}$.
  • Why $k=1$ and $k=n-1$ are free. At $k=1$ the numerator is $L_1(h_i)=e_0=1$, so $W_i=1/D_i$ — denominators handed to you directly. At $k=n-1$, $$1-W_i=\frac{e_{n-1}(r_{-i})}{D_i}=\prod_{\ell\neq i}\frac{r_\ell}{1+r_\ell},$$ the dual statement. The hard middle $k$ is exactly where $N_i=L_k(h_i)$ is a nontrivial partial sum that entangles with $D_i$.

A lever for the general / aggregate attack

The deletions sum to something clean:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n}h_i(t)=g(t)\sum_i\frac{1}{1+r_i t}=n\,g(t)-t\,g'(t)=\sum_{m=0}^{n}(n-m)\,E_m\,t^m.$$

So the unnormalized numerators and denominators aggregate linearly into $g$:

$$\sum_i N_i=\sum_{m=0}^{k-1}(n-m)\,E_m,\qquad \sum_i D_i=n\,g(1)-g'(1).$$

The only nonlinearity in the whole problem is the per-$i$ division $W_i=N_i/D_i$. Clearing it, $W_i\,h_i(1)=L_k(h_i)$ with $g=(1+r_i t)\,h_i$, gives $n$ polynomial equations in $r_1,\dots,r_n$ whose coefficients are the observed $W_i$.

The sandwich / induction in $k$ is now visible as a filtration: $W^{(k)}$ and $W^{(k+1)}$ differ only by appending the layer $e_k(r_{-i})$ to each numerator — i.e. by extending the truncation $L_k\to L_{k+1}$ on the same $g$.

The odds ratio earns its keep not by making each $W_i$ simpler, but by revealing that all $n$ equations are coefficient functionals of one degree-$n$ polynomial and its single-factor deletions. The question “can we invert $W$?” becomes $$\text{does}\quad \Big\{\,\tfrac{L_k\!\big(g/(1+r_i t)\big)}{\mathrm{ev}_1\!\big(g/(1+r_i t)\big)}\,\Big\}_{i=1}^{n}\quad\text{determine } g \text{ and its labeling?}$$ — a single-polynomial recovery problem.
