The properness problem: from local to global injectivity of the win-probability map

4-WinProb · companion to jacobian-coupling-recursion.html · written for an economist (calculus, linear algebra, optimization, probability; no abstract algebra assumed) · every concept defined before use · 2026-07-18

The companion note jacobian-coupling-recursion.html settled the local question: the Jacobian of the win-probability map never vanishes on the open cube, so the map is a local diffeomorphism everywhere — locally invertible around every point. That is not the same as being globally one-to-one. A map can be locally invertible at every single point and still fold the domain over itself, sending two far-apart points to the same image. Closing that gap — upgrading "locally injective everywhere" to "globally injective" — is what this note is about. The bridge is a property called properness, and the bulk of the recent work is a long tower of reductions that turns properness into one sharp, still-open inequality. This note walks down that tower, one rung at a time, defining each object as it appears.

0. The setup, in one paragraph

Fix $n$ bidders competing for $k$ identical objects. Each bidder $\ell$ bids below a reference threshold with probability $x_\ell\in(0,1)$ and above with probability $1-x_\ell$, independently across bidders. A bidder secures one of the $k$ objects exactly when at most $k-1$ of their rivals bid above them (fewer than $k$ rivals beat them, so they land among the $k$ winners). Bidder $i$'s win probability is therefore

$$W_i(x)=\Pr\big[\#\{\ell\neq i:\ell\text{ above}\}\le k-1\big],$$

a function of the $n-1$ rivals only — it does not depend on $x_i$. The $n$ win probabilities assemble into the map $W=(W_1,\dots,W_n):(0,1)^n\to\mathbb R^n$ (input $n$-dimensional, output $n$-dimensional). Its Jacobian $M_{ij}=\partial W_i/\partial x_j=\Pr[\text{exactly }k-1\text{ of }[n]\setminus\{i,j\}\text{ above}]$ is the $n\times n$ symmetric matrix with zero diagonal (zero because $W_i$ ignores $x_i$). One probabilistic object underlies all of this and recurs throughout, so we name it once — and give it the reading that will carry the whole note:

Definition (the shadow, read as a win probability). A Poisson–binomial random variable is a sum $A=\sum_\ell B_\ell$ of independent Bernoulli coins that may have different success probabilities (unlike an ordinary binomial, where they are equal). Fix a field of $m$ opponents described by a vector $z\in(0,1)^m$: the entry $z_\ell$ is the probability that opponent $\ell$ bids below, so $1-z_\ell$ is the probability it bids above, independently. Let $A_{[m]}(z)=\#\{\ell\le m:\ell\text{ above}\}$ be the resulting above-count. The shadow is $$\Psi_k(z):=\Pr\big[\,A_{[m]}(z)\le k-1\,\big]\ \in\ \mathbb R,$$ a single number — a scalar, not a vector: the probability that at most $k-1$ of the $m$ opponents bid above. The point of the name is that this scalar is itself a win probability. Imagine one extra contestant — the shadow bidder — dropped into the pool against exactly these $m$ opponents, competing for the $k$ objects. The shadow bidder secures an object precisely when at most $k-1$ of the $m$ opponents bid above, so $\Psi_k(z)$ is the shadow bidder's win probability against the field $z$; and, exactly like a real bidder's, it depends only on the opponents, never on the shadow bidder's own bid.
The rule to remember. $\ \Psi_k$ applied to an $r$-vector $=$ the win probability of the shadow bidder against those $r$ opponents, in a $k$-object contest. Input dimension $r$; output one scalar.

This is precisely why the real win probabilities are "shadows" of the one function $\Psi_k$. Bidder $i$ faces the $n-1$ rivals $x_{-i}$ (all coordinates but $i$), so

$$W_i(x)=\Psi_k(x_{-i})$$

is nothing but the shadow bidder pitted against $i$'s rivals. The whole map $W$ is that single shadow function evaluated on each "leave-one-out" subfield — the $n$ shadows $\Psi_k$ casts, one per dropped coordinate. The word will recur: the fact that all $n$ of these leave-one-out win probabilities dropping forces the full-field shadow to drop is shadow monotonicity (§2), and the constraint region in that argument is the shadow set.

Two bookkeeping points that prevent later confusion. First, the field size $m$ is generic: $\Psi_k$ makes sense against a field of any size and is not tied to $n$ — the win map above invokes it at $m=n-1$, and §2 will invoke it at still other sizes. Second, the direction of monotonicity: raising $z_\ell$ makes opponent $\ell$ likelier to bid below, which lowers the above-count and hence raises the shadow bidder's win probability, so $\Psi_k$ is strictly increasing in every coordinate (a fact §2 leans on). Two derived objects will also appear: the gradient $p^{(k)}=\nabla\Psi_k$ (an $m$-vector of partials) and the single-level mass $P_k=\Pr[A=k]$ (a scalar, distinct from the cumulative $\Psi_k$).

The one fact we import from the companion note is the inertia of $M$. The inertia (or signature) of a symmetric matrix is the triple (number of positive, negative, zero eigenvalues). On the whole open cube,

$$\operatorname{In}\big(M(x)\big)=(1,\,n-1,\,0)\qquad\text{for all }x\in(0,1)^n:$$

exactly one positive eigenvalue, $n-1$ negative, none zero. In particular $\det M\neq0$ everywhere, which is precisely the local-invertibility statement. Keep the number "one positive eigenvalue" in mind — it returns, transformed, as the geometric heart of the whole attack (§4).

1. What properness buys, and the exact condition (P)

Here is the gap between local and global injectivity, stated plainly. Being a local diffeomorphism everywhere means the map has no critical points — it never crushes dimensions locally. But the image of the open cube could still be "wrapped" so that a point in the interior and a point drifting toward the boundary land on top of each other; or two interior sheets could cross. To rule this out we need a global, topological input.

Definition (proper map). A continuous map $f:X\to Y$ is proper if the preimage $f^{-1}(K)$ of every compact set $K$ is compact. For a map out of an open box, the intuition is: as you march toward the boundary of the box, the image must also march toward the boundary of the image — the map cannot "escape sideways," letting an interior point collide with a boundary point.

The relevant classical fact is a covering-space theorem: a proper local diffeomorphism onto a connected, simply-connected target is a diffeomorphism — in particular globally injective. Equivalently, in degree-theoretic language: for a local diffeomorphism the number of preimages of a point (its fiber), counted with the sign of the Jacobian, is a locally constant integer called the degree; properness makes it globally constant; and if it equals $1$ at even one point, it equals $1$ everywhere, which is injectivity. Because our Jacobian has constant sign ($\det M=(-1)^{n-1}P$ with $P>0$), the signed and unsigned fiber counts agree, so "degree $1$" really is "exactly one preimage."

So global injectivity needs two ingredients on top of the local result: (P) a properness statement, and (F) a fiber-anchoring statement fixing the degree at $1$. The reduction chain is

$$\textbf{(P)}:\quad W\big((0,1)^n\big)\ \cap\ \overline W\big(\partial[0,1]^n\big)\ =\ \varnothing.$$
In words: the image of the open cube is disjoint from the (closure of the) image of the boundary. No interior win-vector is ever produced by a degenerate configuration where some bidder is surely above or surely below.

This disjointness is exactly the "no escape to the boundary" content of properness for this map. Together with (F) — the fiber lemma, which is established for all $(k,n)$, and which supplies the single-preimage anchor at symmetric configurations — a standard degree/covering argument gives global injectivity. The extreme object counts are already fully closed by hand: at $k=1$, $W_i=\prod_{\ell\neq i}x_\ell$ and the product $\prod_iW_i=\big(\prod x_\ell\big)^{n-1}$ inverts explicitly; at $k=n-1$ the reflection $x\mapsto1-x$ reduces to $k=1$ (this reflection symmetry, $W^{(k)}(x)=1-W^{(n-k)}(1-x)$, is used constantly below). So (F) is in hand and the extreme cases are done; the entire remaining difficulty is the analytic statement (P). Everything after this section is the campaign to prove (P).

2. Reducing (P) to shadow monotonicity (SM)

(P) is proved by induction on the number of bidders $n$. The engine of the induction is a monotonicity property that is interesting in its own right.

Definition (Shadow monotonicity, (SM)). At parameters $(k,m)$ — a field of $m$ opponents, $k$ objects — take two fields $z,y\in(0,1)^m$. If $W(z)<W(y)$ holds coordinatewise (all $m$ entries drop: every leave-one-out win probability under $z$ is below its counterpart under $y$), then the full-field shadow drops too, $\Psi_k(z)<\Psi_k(y)$. Here $W(z),W(y)\in\mathbb R^m$ are vectors ($m$ entries each) while $\Psi_k(z),\Psi_k(y)\in\mathbb R$ are scalars.

In the shadow-bidder language this is a statement about defeating fields. Each coordinate $W_i(z)=\Psi_k(z_{-i})$ is the shadow bidder's win probability against the subfield that omits opponent $i$; the scalar $\Psi_k(z)$ is the win probability against the whole field of $m$. So (SM) reads: if switching the field from $y$ to $z$ lowers the shadow bidder's odds against every one of the $m$ "drop-one-opponent" subfields, it must also lower the odds against the full field — the $m$ leave-one-out win probabilities control the whole-field win probability. (As in §0, $m$ is generic; the reduction below applies (SM) at $m=n-1$.)

Why this closes the induction: the dangerous part of the boundary is the facet where one bidder is surely below, $x_j=1$. On that facet the $n$-bidder win-vector is built from $(n-1)$-bidder win-vectors, and (SM) at $n-1$ (available by the inductive hypothesis, via injectivity at $n-1$) forbids an interior point from matching a facet point. The reflection symmetry then hands over the opposite facet $x_j=0$, and the two facets are all of the boundary that matters. Schematically,

$$\text{inj}@(n-1)\ \Rightarrow\ (\mathrm{SM})@(n-1)\ \Rightarrow\ (\mathrm P)\text{'s facets}@n\ \xrightarrow{\ +\text{ reflection}\ }\ (\mathrm P)@n\ \Rightarrow\ \text{inj}@n.$$

So the whole problem now rests on proving (SM). And (SM) has a clean reformulation an economist will recognize on sight: it is a constrained-optimization statement. Fix the reference $y$ and form the shadow set

$$K(y)\ :=\ \{\,x\in[0,1]^m:\ W(x)\le W(y)\ \text{coordinatewise}\,\}.$$

Because $\Psi_k$ is strictly increasing in every coordinate and $W$ is coordinatewise increasing (all entries of $M$ are $\ge0$), $K(y)$ is a down-set (a "lower contour set"): if $x\in K(y)$ then so is anything below it. A short argument shows

$$(\mathrm{SM})\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \max_{x\in K(y)}\Psi_k(x)\ =\ \Psi_k(y).$$ The reference point $y$ must be the constrained maximizer of the shadow over its own shadow set. (SM) fails exactly if some other feasible point achieves a weakly higher shadow.

Now run the Lagrangian / KKT conditions for $\max\Psi_k$ subject to $W(x)\le W(y)$. At an interior maximizer $x^\ast\in[0,1]^m$, stationarity reads $\nabla\Psi_k=M^\top\lambda$ with a vector of multipliers $\lambda\in\mathbb R^m$, $\lambda\ge0$ (the shadow prices of the $m$ win-constraints), and $\lambda_i=0$ on any slack constraint. Because $M$ is symmetric this is $p=M\lambda$. The decisive input is an exact aggregation identity — the analogue, for this problem, of Euler's theorem for homogeneous functions (the identity an economist knows from constant-returns production, $\sum_j x_j\,\partial F/\partial x_j=F$):

Euler–$\Psi$ identity. $\displaystyle M(x)\,x=(m-k)\,\nabla\Psi_k$, hence $M^{-1}\nabla\Psi_k=\dfrac{x}{m-k}>0$ on the open cube.

(The proof is a one-line conditioning argument: contracting $M$ against $x$ counts, on the event "exactly $k-1$ above," the $m-k$ players who are below — a pure indicator computation.) Feed it into stationarity. Since $p=Mx^\ast/(m-k)$ and also $p=M\lambda$ with $M$ invertible,

$$\lambda=\frac{x^\ast}{m-k}\ >\ 0\quad\text{strictly.}$$

Every multiplier is strictly positive, so no constraint is slack: $W(x^\ast)=W(y)$. But injectivity in this dimension (the inductive hypothesis) then forces $x^\ast=y$. The interior maximizer is the reference. A parallel KKT computation, using a sign fact about $(M^{-1})_{jj}$ at the boundary, closes the codimension-one facets $x_j\in\{0,1\}$ as well. The upshot:

(SM) holds for every $k$, except possibly at maximizers that pin $\ge2$ coordinates at the extreme values $0$ or $1$. The two base cases $k=1$ and $k=m-1$ hold in every dimension by the explicit product formulas.

This is real progress: the maximizer, if it is to violate (SM), must sit on a face of the cube of codimension at least two — at least two players driven to certainty. That residual is the entire remaining problem, and it has a name.

3. GAP 3: the codimension-$\ge2$ residual

Fix a would-be violating maximizer that pins a block of coordinates — in the shadow-bidder picture, a configuration in which some opponents have been frozen to certainty. Freeze a block $Z$ of $|Z|$ opponents at $x=0$ (surely above), a block $O$ of $|O|$ opponents at $x=1$ (surely below), and leave a free block of $d=m-|Z|-|O|$ live opponents carrying $\zeta\in[0,1]^d$. The object count that matters for the free block drops to $\kappa=k-|Z|$: each surely-above frozen opponent is already one of the "beaters," using up one of the $k$ slots, so the shadow bidder now needs at most $\kappa-1$ of the $d$ live opponents above. On the free block, all the $\Psi,W,p,M$ objects are just the same objects at the smaller size $d$ and level $\kappa$. Freezing opponents turns the $m$-opponent shadow into a $d$-opponent shadow, dressed with a few extra inequality constraints (the "caps") inherited from the frozen blocks. With $q_r$ the Poisson–binomial probability that $r$ of the frozen opponents bid below, the reduced data are the objective $\Psi_\kappa^{(d)}(\zeta)$; a target value $T:=\Psi_k^{(m)}(y)=\sum_r q_r\,\Psi_{k-r}^{(d)}(y_\Phi)$ (the shadow bidder's original whole-field odds, re-expressed by conditioning on how the frozen opponents split); the free-block win-constraints $W_i^{(\kappa,d)}(\zeta)\le R_i$ with mixture right-hand sides $R_i=\sum_r q_r W_i^{(k-r,d)}(y_\Phi)$; and two cap constraints inherited from $Z$ and $O$. Here $y_\Phi\in[0,1]^d$ is the reference $y$ restricted to the free block.

GAP 3. On every such face, no feasible free-block point $\zeta$ beats the target: $\ \Psi_\kappa^{(d)}(\zeta)\le T$.

Two routes that look natural are both dead ends, and knowing why saves re-derivation. A convexity route fails because the relevant Hessian $M^{-1}$ has mixed signature (that lone positive eigenvalue again) — $\Psi\circ W^{-1}$ is not convex, and chord arguments are unavailable. A caps-only route — hoping the win-constraints are redundant once you keep the caps — is false: an explicit spread-out reference exhibits a caps-feasible $\zeta$ with $\Psi_\kappa(\zeta)=0.824\gg T=0.076$, which only the win-constraints exclude. So the win-block is load-bearing on every codim-$\ge2$ face. The problem genuinely couples the caps and the win-rows.

The face problem splits by which constraints are active at the maximizer (its KKT "signature"). A scout over many faces found three regimes: (i) only a cap active, maximizer all-equal; (ii) a cap together with a whole group of win-rows active (two distinct free-coordinate values) — this is the tight regime that carries the extremal configurations; and (iii) a mixed residue. Case (i) is closed (a self-contained argument built on a Poisson–binomial "pair-equalization" tool and a Wronskian-type identity — see the companion files). Within case (ii), the "type-1" references — those whose matched configuration is itself feasible — are also closed. What remains, and where the recent months of work live, is:

The live nut: case (ii), type-2 references (matched configuration row-infeasible). Here the clean cap-mass bound still holds but is not by itself enough; the proof must use the win-rows of $\zeta$. This is what the next two sections attack, and it is where the problem transforms into something an economist finds very familiar.

4. The excess potential: the whole problem is the curvature of one scalar

From here the shadow-bidder metaphor has done its job, and we let it go: the remaining sections are about the geometry of one scalar function on the $d$-dimensional free block, not about a contest. In its place we stay strict about dimensions. Work on the reduced problem — $d$ live opponents, with effective threshold $g:=d-\kappa$ — and let $\beta_x\in\{0,1,\dots,d\}$ be the number of them bidding above under a point $x\in[0,1]^d$. Define a single scalar function of $x$:

Definition (excess potential). $\displaystyle\Phi(x):=\mathbb E\big[(\beta_x-g)^+\big]=\sum_{w>g}(w-g)\,\rho_w(x)$, the expected amount by which the above-count overshoots the threshold $g$ (here $\rho_w=\Pr[\beta=w]$).

Two identities make $\Phi$ the organizing object of everything:

$$\nabla\Phi=W,\qquad \nabla^2\Phi=M.$$

Read those slowly, with dimensions attached: $\Phi:[0,1]^d\to\mathbb R$ is a single scalar, its gradient $W=\nabla\Phi$ is a $d$-vector, and its Hessian $M=\nabla^2\Phi$ is the $d\times d$ matrix. The win-probability vector $W$ is the gradient of the scalar potential $\Phi$; the Jacobian $M$ is its Hessian. In the language of economics: $W$ is an integrable (conservative) vector field — the problem has "potential-game" structure — and the object whose sign pattern controlled local injectivity in §0 is literally the curvature $\nabla^2\Phi$ of that potential. The entire GAP-3 inequality is a second-order geometric statement about one function.

Concretely, the type-2 leg reduces to a single tangent-domination inequality. Let $F_g(x):=\Pr[\beta_x\ge g]$ be the "cap function"; its level sets $\{F_g=\text{const}\}$ are the cap surfaces. The conjecture is:

Conjecture (X). If $\,F_g(u)=F_g(\zeta)\,$ (same cap level) then $$\Phi(u)\ \le\ \Phi(\zeta)+W(\zeta)\cdot(u-\zeta).$$

The right-hand side is the first-order Taylor expansion of $\Phi$ at $\zeta$ evaluated at $u$ — a supporting tangent. So (X) says: along any cap level surface, the potential $\Phi$ lies below its own tangent plane — it is concave in the directions tangent to the cap. Equivalently, the reference point is a minimizer of the "excess functional" $H_u(x)=\sum_i u_iW_i(x)-g\,T_+(x)$ on its own cap surface, because $\nabla H_u(x)=M(x)(u-x)$ vanishes at $x=u$ and $H_u(u)=\Phi(u)$. This is the same envelope/tangent-line reasoning behind supporting hyperplanes in consumer theory.

Why is such curvature control believable? Because of the "one positive eigenvalue" fact, now seen correctly. A symmetric matrix with exactly one positive eigenvalue and the rest negative has Lorentzian signature — the signature of Minkowski spacetime, one "time" direction and many "space" directions. Directions $h$ with $h^\top M h\le0$ are called spacelike. The key structural theorem is:

Lorentz signature of $M$ (Brändén–Huh). For every $x$ in the cube and every threshold $1\le g\le d-1$, $M(x)$ has at most one positive eigenvalue. This is the modern log-concavity theory of Lorentzian polynomials: the generating polynomial $\prod_k(v_ks+u_kt_k)$ is a product of positive linear forms, hence Lorentzian; taking derivatives preserves the class; and reading off a certain second-derivative block recovers $u_iu_l M_{il}$. Interlacing then bounds the positive eigenvalues of $M$ by one.

From this, the germ of (X) is a theorem: on any cap surface, the tangent directions $h$ (those with $\nabla F_g\cdot h=0$) satisfy $h^\top M h\le0$ — the cap surfaces are spacelike, and $\Phi$ is concave to second order along them. So (X) is true infinitesimally at every point; the entire open question is whether that local concavity integrates up to the global tangent-domination inequality across a whole cap surface. That is a Morse-theory-flavored global-from-local problem, and the next section is the machine that reduces it to something finite.

5. Pencil rigidity and the envelope route

The global step needs to control all the critical points of the excess functional $H_u$ on a cap surface, not just the reference. The surprising structural fact is that those critical points are rigid — they cannot be scattered around the surface; they lie on a single straight line.

Pencil rigidity. Consider the family $H_{a,\gamma}(x)=\sum_i a_iW_i(x)-\gamma\,T_+(x)$ (so $H_u=H_{u,g}$). Its gradient factors as $\nabla H_{a,\gamma}(x)=M(x)\big(a-\tfrac{\gamma}{g}x\big)$. At any interior critical point on a cap surface where $M$ is nonsingular, the vector $a-\tfrac{\gamma}{g}x$ must be proportional to $\mathbf 1-x$. Solving, $x$ lies on the pencil: the straight line through the fixed "apex" $\tfrac{g}{\gamma}a$ and the corner $\mathbf 1$. And $F_g$ is strictly monotone along that line, so the pencil meets each cap surface in at most one point.

So on each cap level, the excess functional has essentially one interior critical point — its pencil point. Two more exact facts make this into a genuine induction:

  • Strata recursion. Pinning one more free coordinate to $0$ or $1$ maps $H_{a,\gamma}$ to another member of the same family, one dimension down, with explicitly bookkept parameters. So the family is closed under the very pinning operation that generated the face in the first place — the reduction terminates.
  • Collapse. At the special reference $u=\mathbf 1$, $\sum_iW_i-gT_+=(d-g)F_g$, so $H_{\mathbf 1}$ is a function of the cap value alone. Consequently (X) holds with exact equality everywhere on the cap surface when $u=\mathbf 1$. This is not a coincidence: the adversarial numerical searches that push (X) to its tightest margins all pile up precisely on this manifold (shared "sure" coins). The collapse identity is the equality case, which is why the inequality has no slack to spare.

The reduction of (X) to a one-dimensional comparison uses the object economists know as the envelope theorem: the derivative of a value function equals the active Lagrange multiplier. Parametrize the pencil by its cap level $s$ and let $V(s)=H_{a,\gamma}(\text{pencil point at level }s)$ be the branch value. Then

$$\frac{dV}{ds}=c(s),\qquad c(s)=-(d-g)\,\frac{\gamma}{g}\,\frac{1-\tau}{\tau},$$

where $c$ is exactly the multiplier of the cap constraint along the pencil (and $\tau$ the pencil parameter). Because every branch — the parent problem and each of its "pin-children" one dimension down — shares the same anchor value $V(1)=\sum_i a_i-\gamma$ at the top of the range, the comparison of a parent branch against a child branch collapses to comparing their multipliers, integrated over the level range:

(PC), the pencil comparison. parent branch value $\le$ each pin-child branch value at the same cap level $\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \displaystyle\int_C^1\big(c-c_{\text{child}}\big)\,ds\ \ge\ 0.$ By induction on dimension, (PC) delivers the per-stratum inequalities, hence (X).

One warning that the numerics made sharp: the pointwise multiplier comparison $c\ge c_{\text{child}}$ is false (it dips as low as $-0.5$). Only the integrated comparison survives. So the remaining task is not to sign a function but to sign the area under a difference of two multiplier curves — and that is a single-crossing question.

6. The frontier: single-crossing (SC1) and the Descartes fingerprint

Change variables one last time, from the cap level $s$ to the multiplier parameter $\lambda$. Along the pencil the coordinates become tail probabilities $q_i(\lambda)=(\gamma-g\,a_i)/(\gamma+\lambda g)$, and the parent's cap level as a function of $\lambda$ is a Poisson–binomial tail $s_p(\lambda)=\Pr[\,\#\text{tails}\le d-g\,]$. Its "$1$-pin child" (the same problem with one coin frozen) has an entirely analogous $s_c(\lambda)$ with the same tail budget. The envelope integrand's sign turns out to be governed by which of the two cap-levels is larger at equal $\lambda$. The whole tower now rests on one statement — and it is, once again, a concept from monotone comparative statics:

(SC1), single-crossing. $\ \lambda\longmapsto s_p(\lambda)-s_c(\lambda)\ $ changes sign at most once on its range. Two Poisson–binomial cumulative distribution functions, indexed by the same parameter, cross at most once.

Single-crossing (à la Milgrom–Shannon) is exactly the hypothesis that makes an integrated comparison decidable from its endpoints: if $s_p-s_c$ crosses once, the sign of $\int_C^1(\cdots)$ is pinned down by its behavior at the ends of the range, where an explicit elementary-symmetric inequality fixes it. So (SC1) — plus the endpoint/cascade bookkeeping — would finish (X), hence the type-2 leg, hence GAP 3, hence (SM), hence (P), hence global injectivity. It is the last real inequality in the tower.

How do you prove two Poisson–binomial CDFs cross at most once? Clear denominators. Multiply through by the (positive) leading factors to turn the difference $s_p-s_c$ into a genuine polynomial in $\lambda$:

$$R(\lambda)\ :=\ P(\lambda)\,\big(\gamma'+\lambda(g-1)\big)^{d-1}\ -\ Q(\lambda)\,(\gamma+\lambda g)^{d},$$

where $P,Q$ are the two tail counts times their prefactors, both polynomials with nonnegative coefficients. The positive roots of $R$ are exactly the crossings of $s_p$ and $s_c$. So (SC1) becomes: $R$ has at most one positive root. And here enters a tool that needs no algebra beyond counting:

Descartes' rule of signs. The number of positive roots of a real polynomial is at most the number of sign changes in its sequence of coefficients (and has the same parity). So if we write $R$ in a basis where its coefficient sequence flips sign at most once, then $R$ has at most one positive root — which is exactly (SC1).

The discovery — the "Descartes fingerprint" — is that there is a distinguished basis in which this works. Recentre the variable at the smallest weight, $\mu=\lambda+\min_i a_i$ (the "exit-centered" basis, the unique shift making every atomic factor coefficient-positive). In that basis the exact coefficient sequence of $R$ shows a single sign flip — a block of negatives followed by a block of positives, $-\cdots-\,+\cdots+$ — and the number of sign changes equals the true number of positive roots exactly, instance after instance. (The naïve basis $\mu=\lambda+a_k$ fails, producing up to five sign changes — the choice of centre is doing real work.)

Verification status of the fingerprint. The single-flip pattern, and its exact agreement with the true root counts, has been checked exhaustively: numerically over exact rational arithmetic to $d\le8$ (3990 instances, zero anomalies); and symbolically — factoring each coefficient over the rationals with an independent numeric self-check gate — to $d\le6$, now extended to $d=7,8$ (83 further cases, self-check $0/9020$, the single-flip and root-count gates $0$ violations). The one subtlety: the position of the flip moves across the parameter region, so no individual coefficient is globally sign-definite; but the per-point single-flip shape holds everywhere. This is strong evidence, and a proof-shaped target — proving the sign pattern from the explicit coefficient formulas would prove (SC1) outright — but it is not yet a proof.

The final refinement upgrades "at most one sign change" to genuine single-crossing with the right orientation. At a tie $F_p=F_c$ (a root of $R$), the sign of $R'$ is fixed by a weighted comparison of the two "boundary" masses:

(M5), the live core target. At every tie, $\ \pi_{r+1}(\beta_1+\mu)\ >\ \kappa_{r+1}(\alpha_1+\mu)$, where $\pi,\kappa$ are the parent/child masses at the critical level and $\alpha_1,\beta_1$ the leading weights.

(M5) forces every root of $R$ to be a transversal up-crossing; up-crossings cannot accumulate, so there is at most one, and the overall orientation ($-\to+$) is fixed by the sign of the leading coefficient, which is provably positive. (M5) is minimal in a precise sense: the bare comparison $\pi_{r+1}>\kappa_{r+1}$, dropping the linear prefactors, fails at almost every crossing — the prefactor is load-bearing, so the inequality has no slack. Measured at the actual ties by exact rational bisection, (M5) holds in $2980/2980$ in-model instances (worst normalized margin $+2.2\times10^{-4}$, tightest near the degenerate merge corner $\gamma\approx g$). Turning that verified pattern into a proof — from the explicit formulas for $\pi_{r+1},\kappa_{r+1}$ on the tie variety — is the current front line.

7. Honest status

The properness statement (P) — the one analytic input that upgrades the everywhere-local injectivity of the win-probability map to global injectivity — has been reduced, rung by rung, to a single explicit inequality:
$$\text{inj}\ \Longleftarrow\ (\mathrm P)\ \Longleftarrow\ (\mathrm{SM})\ \Longleftarrow\ \text{GAP 3}\ \Longleftarrow\ \text{case (ii) type-2}\ \Longleftarrow\ (\mathrm X)\ \Longleftarrow\ (\mathrm{PC})\ \Longleftarrow\ (\mathrm{SC1})\ \Longleftarrow\ (\mathrm{M5}).$$
Everything to the left of (SC1) is proved or reduced: the degree/covering frame and the fiber lemma (F); the KKT reformulation of (SM) and its closure off codimension $\ge2$; case (i) of GAP 3 and the type-1 half of case (ii); the excess-potential structure $W=\nabla\Phi$, $M=\nabla^2\Phi$; the Lorentzian one-positive-eigenvalue theorem and the spacelike germ of (X); the pencil rigidity, strata recursion, collapse identity, and the envelope reduction of (X) to (PC), and of (PC) to the single-crossing (SC1). What is not yet a theorem is the right end: the sign-pattern proof of (SC1) via the Descartes fingerprint, its transversal refinement (M5), the cascade termination bookkeeping, the non-vanishing of $M$ for intermediate thresholds on the open cube, and the assembly of the pinned blocks of size $\ge2$. Every one of these has survived exhaustive exact-arithmetic falsification (nothing found; the fingerprint symbolic to $d=8$, (M5) at $2980/2980$ ties, (X) adversarially clean). So this is leverage of the strongest evidentiary kind — a specific, sharp, verified inequality with no known counterexample — but the last inequality is not closed, and until it is, global injectivity beyond $k\in\{1,n-1\}$ and the handful of small closed cases remains open.

8. The economist's cheat-sheet

The attack repeatedly borrows the shape of a familiar idea. Collected in one place:

$\Psi_k(z)$, the shadow↔ the win probability of a "shadow bidder" against a field of opponents $z$; recasts the whole reduction as a story about defeating fields. $\Psi_k$ on an $r$-vector = odds against $r$ opponents (a scalar); $W_i=\Psi_k(x_{-i})$ = the shadow bidder against $i$'s rivals.
Euler–$\Psi$ identity $Mx=(m-k)\nabla\Psi$↔ Euler's theorem for homogeneous functions; it is what pins the KKT multipliers positive.
$W=\nabla\Phi$, $M=\nabla^2\Phi$↔ integrability / potential-game structure; the win-vector is a conservative field, its Jacobian a Hessian.
(SM) as $\max_{K(y)}\Psi=\Psi(y)$↔ constrained optimization over a lower-contour set; the win-constraints carry shadow prices $\lambda$.
Conjecture (X)↔ a supporting-tangent / directional-concavity inequality on a level surface.
One positive eigenvalue of $M$↔ Lorentzian (Minkowski) signature; cap surfaces are "spacelike."
Envelope identity $dV/ds=c$↔ the envelope theorem; marginal value of a program equals its active multiplier.
(SC1) single-crossing↔ monotone comparative statics (Milgrom–Shannon); makes an integrated comparison decidable from endpoints.
Descartes' rule of signs↔ counting coefficient sign-changes to bound positive roots; no algebra beyond arithmetic.
