Related literature for the win-probability inversion

The papers referenced in the placement discussion (2026-06-27), grouped by the role each plays for the map $W\mapsto G$ (observed win probabilities → latent abilities). Titles link to UNC Libraries Articles+ (Summon) search. These are positioning references — what a referee will reach for — not a proof bibliography. The one question that decides the contribution, and therefore the journal: a referee will ask “isn't this already Berry–Gandhi–Haile, or Gale–Nikaido?” — answer it on page one.

1. Global univalence and invertibility — the theorem's lineage

Gale, D. & Nikaido, H. (1965). The Jacobian matrix and global univalence of mappings. Mathematische Annalen 159(2), 81–93.

The classical injectivity-via-Jacobian theorem: a P-matrix Jacobian on a rectangle implies global univalence. The benchmark you must distinguish from — and it does not apply here, because $M$ is a zero-diagonal Hessian with indefinite (Lorentzian) signature, not a P-matrix. “Injective despite an indefinite Jacobian” is the gap that justifies a new argument.

Berry, S., Gandhi, A. & Haile, P. (2013). Connected Substitutes and Invertibility of Demand. Econometrica 81(5), 2087–2111.

The modern invertibility-of-demand theorem; your closest living relative. The positive off-diagonal sign pattern of $M$ is exactly their “substitutes” structure. This is the “isn't this just BGH?” paper — meet it head-on. Likely escape: non-surjectivity plus the zero-diagonal / indefinite structure sit outside their sign conditions, so their theorem does not cover this map.

2. Identification by inversion — precedent that an inversion lemma is its own contribution

Berry, S. (1994). Estimating Discrete-Choice Models of Product Differentiation. RAND Journal of Economics 25(2), 242–262.

The original “shares → mean utilities” inversion. Precedent that an invertibility result is itself a foundational, citable object, and the template for framing $W\mapsto G$ as a demand-style inversion.

Hotz, V. J. & Miller, R. A. (1993). Conditional Choice Probabilities and the Estimation of Dynamic Models. Review of Economic Studies 60(3), 497–529.

The CCP inversion: choice probabilities → value differences. Rhetorical precedent for “value in itself” — a one-shot inversion lemma that seeded a vast empirical literature.

3. Structural auctions and order statistics — the auction hook, stated correctly

Athey, S. & Haile, P. (2002). Identification of Standard Auction Models. Econometrica 70(6), 2107–2140.

Identification of auctions from order statistics — the continuous cousin of “who finishes in the top $k$.” The legitimate auction-econometrics citation, and the bridge if you pursue the econometric-payload branch (Journal of Econometrics / Quantitative Economics).

4. Ranking and placement-probability models — direct structural cousins

Beggs, S., Cardell, S. & Hausman, J. (1981). Assessing the Potential Demand for Electric Cars. Journal of Econometrics 17(1), 1–19.

The rank-ordered (“exploded”) logit — maps qualities to full-ranking probabilities. Your $W_i=\Pr[\text{player }i\text{ in the top }k]$ is a coarsened placement-probability system; this is the discrete-choice lineage of that object.
