A complete proof of the Lorentzian signature — via the covariance matrix and log-concavity of $e_m$

Written so that someone comfortable with analysis and linear algebra (but not with matroids, strongly-Rayleigh measures, or "Lorentzian polynomials") can read every line and decide for themselves whether it is correct. The aim of this note is verification: to expose the whole argument, name every fact it leans on, and mark precisely the one place where an outside theorem is consumed (Fact 2 — now closed by citation, see §6). Its concise handoff is signature-via-log-concavity-handoff.md; this note supersedes the older per-$k$ congruence route (signature-reduction-results.html and its handoff unifying-statement-old.md).

One-paragraph summary. The hard core of the injectivity conjecture — the Jacobian signature, for every number of prizes $k$ and every number of players $n$, all at once — reduces to a single fact about one $n\times n$ matrix: that the matrix $G = D_q - \Sigma$ (a diagonal of probabilities minus a covariance matrix) is positive definite. Given $G\succ 0$, two short and completely rigorous steps then force the Jacobian to have inertia $(1,\,n-1,\,0)$ and determinant of sign $(-1)^{n-1}$, with no induction and no case analysis on $k$. And $G\succ 0$ is exactly the statement that the elementary symmetric polynomial $e_m$ is strictly log-concave on the positive orthant — which is a published theorem: Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa, J. Combin. Theory Ser. A 181 (2021), art. 105351, Theorem 1.1(i), applied to the uniform matroid $U_{m,n}$ (bib key Muraietal2021). With that citation every link is in place: the signature conjecture — $\det M\neq0$ with sign $(-1)^{n-1}$ on the whole cube — is proved, for all $1\le k\le n-1$ and all $n$. (The literature check that found this reference is recorded in §6; an exact computational certificate independently confirms $G\succ0$ for $n\le 6$.) One honest caveat, caught 2026-07-12: this is the local half of identifiability. A never-vanishing Jacobian makes $W$ a local diffeomorphism everywhere but does not by itself give global injectivity, even for polynomial maps of convex domains; the remaining local-to-global step (properness + one singleton fiber) is posed, with a proof plan, in globalization-gap.md.

0. The object and the claim

The contest model (full background in QUICKSTART.md; we only need the matrix). There are $n$ players and $k$ identical prizes, $1\le k\le n-1$. Player $i$ independently clears the in-the-money threshold ("is below the cut") with probability $x_i\in(0,1)$, and fails ("is above") with probability $1-x_i$. Player $i$ takes a prize exactly when at most $k-1$ of the other $n-1$ players are above. Writing $W_i(x)$ for player $i$'s win probability, the map $x\mapsto W(x)$ is the forward map; recovering the hidden $x$ from the observed $W$ ("identifiability") is the goal. The load-bearing ingredient is that the Jacobian $M = \partial W/\partial x$ never degenerates — this makes recovery locally unique everywhere; the further (easier-looking, still open) upgrade to global uniqueness is the subject of globalization-gap.md.

That Jacobian is the only object we study. It is the symmetric $n\times n$ matrix with

$$M_{ii} = 0,\qquad M_{ij} = \Pr[\text{exactly } k-1 \text{ of the } n-2 \text{ players other than } i,j \text{ are above}]\ \ (i\neq j).$$

Written out, with $S$ ranging over the $(k-1)$-subsets of the other players,

$$M_{ij} \;=\; \sum_{\substack{S\subseteq[n]\setminus\{i,j\}\\ |S|=k-1}}\ \Big(\prod_{\ell\in S}(1-x_\ell)\Big)\Big(\prod_{\ell\notin S\cup\{i,j\}}x_\ell\Big), \qquad [n]:=\{1,\dots,n\}.$$

Each entry is a strictly positive probability for $x$ in the open cube, and the diagonal is zero because the potential whose Hessian is $M$ is multilinear (so $\partial^2/\partial x_i^2$ kills it). The conjecture, posed in 2016 and the subject of this whole project, is a single sign statement:

$$\textbf{Conjecture.}\qquad \det M(x)\neq 0,\quad\text{with sign}\ (-1)^{n-1}, \qquad\text{for every } x\in(0,1)^n \text{ and every } 1\le k\le n-1.$$

A constant-sign, never-vanishing Jacobian gives local injectivity uniformly on the cube. An earlier version of this note claimed the upgrade to global injectivity was "a standard properness argument"; that was too glib. Convexity of the domain does not make the upgrade automatic — Pinchuk (1994) built a polynomial map of $\mathbb R^2$ with nowhere-vanishing Jacobian that is not injective, and even the symmetric-Lorentzian shape does not save it ($\nabla(e^x\cos y)$ has Hessian signature $(1,1)$ everywhere and is $2\pi$-periodic in $y$). The genuine upgrade path — properness of $W$ onto its image plus one singleton fiber, via the covering-map argument — is laid out as lemmas (P) and (F) in globalization-gap.md. Everything below is aimed at the determinant sign, which is the hard core; (P) and (F) are the remaining, more elementary, closing step.

1. The language we will use: inertia, congruence, two theorems of Sylvester

For a real symmetric matrix $A$, all eigenvalues are real. Its inertia is the triple

$$\operatorname{In}(A) = (n_+,\,n_-,\,n_0),$$

counting, with multiplicity, the positive, negative, and zero eigenvalues. We will prove the conjecture in the sharp form

$$\operatorname{In}(M) = (1,\,n-1,\,0),$$

"one positive eigenvalue, $n-1$ negative, none zero" — a signature called Lorentzian (the signature of spacetime). This is strictly stronger than the determinant claim: if $\operatorname{In}(M)=(1,n-1,0)$ then $M$ is nonsingular and $\det M = (\text{product of eigenvalues})$ has the sign of $(+)(-)^{n-1} = (-1)^{n-1}$, which is exactly the conjecture. Working with the full signature, rather than only the determinant, is what makes the argument clean.

Two classical facts, both due to Sylvester, do the heavy lifting. They sound similar but are different theorems; we use both, and it is worth keeping them straight.

Sylvester's law of inertia. If $C$ is any invertible matrix, then $C^\top A C$ has the same inertia as $A$. (The map $A\mapsto C^\top A C$ is called a congruence; it is how the matrix of a quadratic form changes under a linear change of the variable. Inertia is a congruence invariant — congruence can rescale and rotate eigenvalues but cannot change their signs.)

Sylvester's criterion. A real symmetric matrix is positive definite if and only if all of its leading principal minors (the determinants of the top-left $1\times1,\,2\times2,\dots, n\times n$ blocks) are strictly positive.

We will use the law of inertia three times (to replace $M$ by friendlier congruent matrices) and the criterion once (to certify $G\succ0$ by inspecting polynomials).

2. Rewriting $M$ as the average of a very simple quadratic form

This is Malyshev's 2016 idea (recorded in mse-2016-threads.md), made self-contained. Introduce a random subset of the players.

The measure $\mu$. Flip $n$ independent biased coins; coin $i$ lands "selected" with probability $x_i$. Let $\mathcal A\subseteq[n]$ be the selected set, and then condition on the event that exactly $m$ players were selected, where

$$m \;:=\; n-k+1 .$$

Concretely, $\mathcal A$ takes the value $A$ (for each $A$ with $|A|=m$) with probability

$$\Pr[\mathcal A = A] \;=\; \frac{p_A}{Z},\qquad p_A=\prod_{i\in A}x_i\prod_{i\notin A}(1-x_i),\qquad Z=\sum_{|A|=m}p_A .$$

Because $k$ runs over $1,\dots,n-1$, the conditioned size $m=n-k+1$ runs over $2,\dots,n$. Keep that range in mind: $m\ge 2$ always. It is the hinge of the whole proof.

To each subset $A$ attach the quadratic form (in a vector $v\in\mathbb R^n$)

$$Q_A(v) \;=\; \Big(\sum_{i\in A}v_i\Big)^2 - \sum_{i\in A}v_i^2 \;=\; \sum_{\substack{i,j\in A\\ i\neq j}} v_i v_j ,$$

and average it over $\mu$ (weighting by $p_A$, not yet normalized):

$$Q(v) \;=\; \sum_{|A|=m} p_A\, Q_A(v).$$

Let $M_Q$ be the symmetric matrix of this form, so $Q(v)=v^\top M_Q v$. We now read off its entries, step by step.

How to read a matrix off a quadratic form. If $M$ is any symmetric $n\times n$ matrix, expanding $v^\top Mv=\sum_{a,b}M_{ab}v_av_b$ and grouping the ordered pairs $(a,b)$ and $(b,a)$ (equal terms, since $M_{ab}=M_{ba}$) gives

$$v^\top M v \;=\; \sum_{a} M_{aa}\, v_a^2 \;+\; 2\sum_{a\lt b} M_{ab}\, v_a v_b .$$

So a quadratic form, viewed as a polynomial in $v$, determines its symmetric matrix by coefficient inspection:

Diagonal: $M_{aa}$ = the coefficient of $v_a^2$.

Off-diagonal: $M_{ab}$ = one half of the coefficient of the monomial $v_av_b$ ($a\neq b$).

(Equivalently, $M_{aa}=\tfrac12\,\partial^2 Q/\partial v_a^2$ and $M_{ab}=\tfrac12\,\partial^2 Q/\partial v_a\partial v_b$.)

Step 1 — the single form $Q_A$. Fix one subset $A$ and one pair $i\neq j$. In $Q_A(v)=\sum_{a\neq b\in A}v_av_b$ (ordered pairs), the monomial $v_iv_j$ appears exactly when both $i\in A$ and $j\in A$, and then exactly twice: once as the ordered pair $(a,b)=(i,j)$ and once as $(a,b)=(j,i)$. So

$$\text{coefficient of } v_iv_j \text{ in } Q_A \;=\; \begin{cases} 2, & i,j\in A,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise}, \end{cases} \qquad\text{and no } v_i^2 \text{ term ever appears (the sum forces } a\neq b\text{).}$$

Step 2 — the average $Q$. Coefficients are linear, so the coefficient of $v_iv_j$ in $Q=\sum_{|A|=m}p_A\,Q_A$ is the sum of the coefficients weighted by $p_A$:

$$\text{coefficient of } v_iv_j \text{ in } Q \;=\; \sum_{|A|=m} p_A\cdot\big(2\ \text{if } i,j\in A,\ \text{else } 0\big) \;=\; 2\!\!\sum_{\substack{|A|=m\\ A\ni i,j}}\!\! p_A .$$

Step 3 — halve it. By the read-off rule, $(M_Q)_{ij}$ is half of that coefficient, and the factor $2$ cancels the half. The diagonal entry is the coefficient of $v_i^2$, which is $0$ for every $Q_A$ (Step 1), hence $0$ in the average. Conclusion: $M_Q$ has zero diagonal, and its off-diagonal entry is the total weight of subsets containing both $i$ and $j$:

$$(M_Q)_{ij} \;=\; \sum_{\substack{|A|=m\\ A\ni i,j}} p_A \qquad (i\neq j),\qquad (M_Q)_{ii}=0.$$

The bridge to $M$ is an exact algebraic identity (Malyshev asserted it; here is the one-line bijection that proves it). Multiply the matrix entry $M_{ij}$ by $x_ix_j$:

$$x_i x_j M_{ij} = \sum_{\substack{S\subseteq[n]\setminus\{i,j\}\\ |S|=k-1}} x_i x_j \Big(\prod_{\ell\in S}(1-x_\ell)\Big)\Big(\prod_{\ell\notin S\cup\{i,j\}}x_\ell\Big).$$

Given the "above" set $S$ of size $k-1$, define the selected set $A = \{i,j\}\cup\big([n]\setminus(S\cup\{i,j\})\big)$ — that is, $i$, $j$, and everyone not in $S$. Then $A\ni i,j$, its size is $2+(n-2-(k-1))=n-k+1=m$, and its complement is exactly $S$. The product $x_ix_j\prod_{\ell\notin S\cup\{i,j\}}x_\ell\prod_{\ell\in S}(1-x_\ell)$ is precisely $p_A$. As $S$ ranges over $(k-1)$-subsets, $A$ ranges over the $m$-subsets containing $i$ and $j$ — a bijection. Hence

$$x_i x_j\, M_{ij} \;=\; \sum_{\substack{|A|=m\\ A\ni i,j}} p_A \;=\; (M_Q)_{ij} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad M = D_{1/x}\, M_Q\, D_{1/x},\quad D_{1/x}=\operatorname{diag}(1/x_i).$$

Verified symbolically (exact arithmetic over $\mathbb Q$, the polynomial identity $x_ix_jM_{ij}=(M_Q)_{ij}$ in the variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$) for $(k,n)=(2,4),(3,5),(2,5),(3,6),(4,6),(2,6)$ — and proved in general by the bijection above.

The matrix $D_{1/x}$ is diagonal with positive entries, hence invertible, so the relation $M = D_{1/x}M_Q D_{1/x}$ is a congruence. By Sylvester's law of inertia, $\operatorname{In}(M)=\operatorname{In}(M_Q)$. We have traded the win-probability matrix for the average of the dead-simple forms $Q_A$. Nothing has been lost or approximated.

3. The covariance matrix $G$

Now we compute $M_Q$ probabilistically. Work with the normalized measure (divide by $Z$); since $Z\gt 0$ this only rescales $M_Q$ by a positive constant and does not touch inertia. Write the indicator

$$\chi_i = \begin{cases}1 & i\in\mathcal A\\ 0 & i\notin\mathcal A\end{cases}, \qquad q_i=\Pr[i\in\mathcal A]=\mathbb E[\chi_i],\qquad q_{ij}=\Pr[i,j\in\mathcal A]=\mathbb E[\chi_i\chi_j].$$

The vector $q=(q_1,\dots,q_n)$ holds the marginal selection probabilities. The covariance matrix of the indicator vector is

$$\Sigma_{ij}=\operatorname{Cov}(\chi_i,\chi_j)=q_{ij}-q_iq_j\ (i\neq j),\qquad \Sigma_{ii}=\operatorname{Var}(\chi_i)=q_i-q_i^2=q_i(1-q_i).$$

Average $Q_{\mathcal A}$ under the normalized measure. Using $\chi_i\in\{0,1\}$ (so $\chi_i^2=\chi_i$, which is why $\sum_{i\in\mathcal A}v_i^2=\sum_i\chi_i v_i^2$), and the variance identity $\mathbb E[X^2]=\operatorname{Var}(X)+(\mathbb E X)^2$ applied to $X=\sum_i\chi_i v_i$:

$$\mathbb E_\mu\,Q_{\mathcal A}(v) = \mathbb E\Big[\big(\textstyle\sum_i\chi_i v_i\big)^2\Big] - \mathbb E\Big[\textstyle\sum_i\chi_i v_i^2\Big] = \underbrace{(q^\top v)^2}_{(\mathbb E X)^2} + \underbrace{v^\top \Sigma v}_{\operatorname{Var}X} - \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_i q_i v_i^2}_{\mathbb E[\sum\chi_i v_i^2]} .$$

Collecting the three pieces into one matrix and defining

$$G \;:=\; D_q - \Sigma,\qquad D_q=\operatorname{diag}(q_1,\dots,q_n),$$ $$\text{matrix of }\ \mathbb E_\mu Q_{\mathcal A}\ =\ q q^\top + \Sigma - D_q \ =\ q q^\top - G .$$

Entrywise $G$ is transparent: on the diagonal $G_{ii}=q_i-\Sigma_{ii}=q_i-q_i(1-q_i)=q_i^2$, and off the diagonal $G_{ij}=-\Sigma_{ij}=q_iq_j-q_{ij}$. (Selection of a fixed-size set is negatively associated: selecting $i$ makes $j$ less likely, so $q_{ij}\le q_iq_j$ and $G_{ij}\ge 0$. So $G$ has a positive diagonal and nonnegative off-diagonal entries — but that alone is far from positive definiteness; that is the work of §4.)

Combining §2 and §3 through the two positive-scalar/positive-diagonal congruences:

$$\operatorname{In}(M) \;=\; \operatorname{In}\!\big(q q^\top - G\big).$$

Verified numerically end-to-end: building the actual $M$ from its definition and $qq^\top-G$ from the measure, both have inertia $(1,n-1,0)$ and $\det M$ has sign $(-1)^{n-1}$, for $(k,n)=(2,4),(3,6),(4,7),(2,8),(5,9),(3,10)$.

So the entire conjecture is now a statement about the single matrix $qq^\top-G$: a rank-one piece $qq^\top$ minus the matrix $G$. The next section establishes three facts about $G$ and $q$; together they pin the inertia at $(1,n-1,0)$.

4. Three facts that finish it

Fact 1  —   $G\mathbf 1 = q$, and therefore $q^\top G^{-1} q = m$.

Let $\mathbf 1=(1,\dots,1)^\top$. The $i$-th entry of $\Sigma\mathbf 1$ is $\sum_j\Sigma_{ij}=\sum_j\operatorname{Cov}(\chi_i,\chi_j)=\operatorname{Cov}\big(\chi_i,\sum_j\chi_j\big) =\operatorname{Cov}(\chi_i,\,|\mathcal A|)$. But $|\mathcal A|=m$ is constant under $\mu$ (we conditioned on it!), and the covariance of anything with a constant is zero. Therefore $\Sigma\mathbf 1=0$, and

$$G\mathbf 1 = D_q\mathbf 1 - \Sigma\mathbf 1 = q - 0 = q.$$

This is a remarkable simplification. Malyshev's program (and the project's "thread B") needed a special vector — the normal to a hyperplane on which a form is negative definite, or the Perron eigenvector — and called it hard to pin down. Here it is, explicitly: $G$ sends the all-ones vector to $q$. If $G$ is invertible (which Fact 2 guarantees), then $G^{-1}q=\mathbf 1$, so

$$q^\top G^{-1} q \;=\; q^\top \mathbf 1 \;=\; \sum_i q_i \;=\; \mathbb E_\mu|\mathcal A| \;=\; m \;=\; n-k+1 .$$

The mechanism $G\mathbf 1=q$ was checked symbolically (exact, in the odds variables) for $(n,m)=(4,2),(5,3),(6,3),(7,4)$; the numeric value $q^\top G^{-1}q=m$ was confirmed exactly in every end-to-end trial of §3.

Fact 2  —   $G\succ 0$ (positive definite). This is log-concavity of $e_m$.

This is the one substantial fact, so we give both a conceptual identification and an elementary, checkable certificate.

The generating polynomial. Encode the measure in a polynomial in auxiliary variables $z=(z_1,\dots,z_n)$:

$$f(z) \;=\; \mathbb E_\mu\Big[\prod_{i\in\mathcal A} z_i\Big] \;=\; \sum_{|A|=m}\Pr[\mathcal A=A]\prod_{i\in A}z_i .$$

Substituting $\Pr[A]=p_A/Z$ and factoring $\prod_i(1-x_i)$ out of every term turns each $x_i$ into the odds $s_i := x_i/(1-x_i)\gt0$, and the sum becomes the elementary symmetric polynomial $e_m$ evaluated at the scaled variables:

$$f(z) \;=\; \frac{e_m(s_1 z_1,\dots,s_n z_n)}{e_m(s)},\qquad e_m(y) := \sum_{|A|=m}\ \prod_{i\in A} y_i .$$

(Recall $e_m(y)$ is the sum of all products of $m$ distinct variables; e.g. $e_2(y)=\sum_{i\lt j}y_iy_j$. Here $e_m(s)=Z/\prod_i(1-x_i)$, and the normalization makes $f(\mathbf 1)=1$.) Two elementary properties of $f$:

(i) $f$ is multiaffine — degree at most $1$ in each $z_i$ separately — because $e_m$ is a sum of squarefree monomials. In particular $\partial^2 f/\partial z_i^2 = 0$.

(ii) Differentiating $f$ at $z=\mathbf 1$ reproduces the moments: $f(\mathbf 1)=1$, $\partial_i f(\mathbf 1)=q_i$, and $\partial_i\partial_j f(\mathbf 1)=q_{ij}$ for $i\neq j$. (Each derivative just selects the subsets containing the differentiated indices.)

Now compute the Hessian of $\log f$ at $z=\mathbf 1$. Using $\partial_i\partial_j\log f = \dfrac{\partial_i\partial_j f}{f}-\dfrac{(\partial_i f)(\partial_j f)}{f^2}$ and evaluating at $\mathbf 1$ (where $f=1$):

$$\big(\nabla^2\log f\big|_{\mathbf 1}\big)_{ij}= \begin{cases} q_{ij}-q_iq_j=\Sigma_{ij}=-G_{ij}, & i\neq j,\\[2pt] \underbrace{\partial_i^2 f}_{=\,0\ \text{by (i)}} - (\partial_i f)^2 = -q_i^2 = -G_{ii}, & i=j. \end{cases}$$
$$\boxed{\ \nabla^2\log f\big|_{z=\mathbf 1} \;=\; -\,G\ } \qquad\text{so}\qquad G\succ 0\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \log f\ \text{is strictly concave at } \mathbf 1.$$

The identity $\nabla^2\log f|_{\mathbf 1}=-G$ was verified symbolically (coefficient-by-coefficient, exact) for $(n,m)=(4,2),(4,3),(5,2),(5,3)$.

Since $\log f(z)=\log e_m(s_1z_1,\dots,s_nz_n)-\log e_m(s)$ and $z\mapsto s\odot z$ is a linear map with positive diagonal, strict concavity of $\log f$ at $\mathbf 1$ is exactly strict concavity of $\log e_m$ at the positive point $s$. So:

$$G\succ 0\quad\text{for all }x\in(0,1)^n \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad e_m\ \text{is strictly log-concave on the positive orthant } \{y\gt 0\}.$$

Why this is a theorem, with the exact citation. The statement we need is, verbatim, the main result of Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa, "Strictness of the log-concavity of generating polynomials of matroids," J. Combin. Theory Ser. A 181 (2021), art. 105351 (bib key Muraietal2021; PDFs in References/):

Theorem 1.1(i) (Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa). Let $M$ be a simple matroid on $[n]$ of rank $r\ge2$. Then the Hessian matrix $H_{f_M}\big|_{x=a}$ has signature $(+,-,\dots,-)$ for every $a\in\mathbb R^n_{\gt0}$; in particular, the basis generating polynomial $f_M$ is strictly log-concave on $\mathbb R^n_{\gt0}$.

Their §3.1 defines strict log-concavity exactly in the pointwise-Hessian sense we need: $\log f$ strictly concave $\iff$ the Hessian of $\log f$ is negative definite at every point (equivalently, when $f(a)\gt0$: $H_f|_{a}$ has signature $(+,-,\dots,-)$). No gap between "strictly concave function" and "definite Hessian" is left open. Application to our case: $e_m$ is the basis generating polynomial of the uniform matroid $U_{m,n}$ of rank $m$, and $U_{m,n}$ is simple for $m\ge2$ (no loops — every element lies in a basis; no parallel elements — every circuit has size $m+1\ge3$). So Theorem 1.1(i) applies for the whole range $2\le m\le n$, giving $\nabla^2\log e_m\prec0$ at every $s\gt0$, i.e. $G\succ0$. Note that $m=1$ is excluded by their rank hypothesis — exactly where our statement degenerates ($\log e_1$ has a rank-one Hessian). Their proof is Hodge-theoretic (strong Lefschetz + Hodge–Riemann relations via the Brändén–Huh Lorentzian property), building on the graphic-matroid case of Nagaoka–Yazawa; the result is recent (2020/21), which explains why the 2016 discussions could not simply quote it.

Classical background, for orientation. (a) $e_m$ is real-stable / hyperbolic, and Gårding's theory gives $\log$ of a homogeneous hyperbolic polynomial concave on the hyperbolicity cone — but only semidefiniteness pointwise: in Gårding 1959 (References/58061.pdf) the equality case of Theorem 5 gives strictness of the function (no flat segments, since the lineality of $e_m$ is $0$ for $m\ge2$), while the pointwise statement — strict convexity of the level hypersurfaces — is literally "left to the reader" (end of his §4) and is weaker than Hessian-definiteness anyway. (b) The Lorentzian / completely-log-concave theory (Brändén–Huh, Anari–Oveis Gharan–Vinzant) gives log-concavity for all matroid basis generating polynomials. The strict pointwise version is precisely the Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa theorem above.

An elementary certificate you can check by hand. Independent of that theory, here is a finite, exact verification for any given $(n,m)$, using only Sylvester's criterion. Clear denominators: with $q_i = s_i\,e_{m-1}(s_{\hat\imath})/e_m(s)$ and $q_{ij}=s_is_j\,e_{m-2}(s_{\widehat{\imath\jmath}})/e_m(s)$ (here $s_{\hat\imath}$ means the odds with $s_i$ deleted, and $e_{m-1}(s_{\hat\imath})=\partial e_m/\partial s_i$), every entry of $G$ is a ratio with denominator $e_m(s)^2$. Define the integer-coefficient polynomial matrix $\widehat G := e_m(s)^2\,G$, with

$$\widehat G_{ii}=s_i^2\,e_{m-1}(s_{\hat\imath})^2,\qquad \widehat G_{ij}=s_is_j\Big(e_{m-1}(s_{\hat\imath})\,e_{m-1}(s_{\hat\jmath}) - e_{m-2}(s_{\widehat{\imath\jmath}})\,e_m(s)\Big).$$

Because $e_m(s)^2\gt0$, the leading principal minors of $G$ and of $\widehat G$ have the same signs. Now form each leading principal minor $\det\widehat G_{[1]},\dots,\det\widehat G_{[n]}$ — these are honest polynomials in $s_1,\dots,s_n$ — and inspect their coefficients. If each is a nonzero polynomial with no negative coefficient, then each is strictly positive at every $s\gt 0$ (a sum of nonnegative monomials with at least one of them positive), so by Sylvester's criterion $\widehat G\succ0$, hence $G\succ0$, for all $s\gt0$ — i.e. for all $x\in(0,1)^n$.

This certificate succeeds (all leading principal minors of $\widehat G$ have all-positive coefficients) for every case computed: $n=4,5,6$ across all valid $m$. Each such case is a complete, exact proof of $G\succ0$ for that $(n,m)$ — and hence of the full conjecture for that $(k,n)$ — needing nothing beyond polynomial arithmetic and Sylvester's criterion.

This is the same "all coefficients one sign" phenomenon the project saw in the older route, but on a vastly smaller and more structured object: $G$ is a covariance-type $n\times n$ matrix with degree-bounded entries, whereas the previous unifying statement asked it of a single determinant with hundreds of thousands of monomials. The uniform statement is now settled by the Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa citation above; the certificate remains valuable as an independent, self-contained corroboration, and extending it to all $(n,m)$ — by induction / a Schur-complement recursion on these minors — would upgrade the whole proof to an elementary one (§6).

Fact 3  —   the rank-one update: $(1,n-1,0)$ and sign $(-1)^{n-1}$.

Assume $G\succ 0$ (Fact 2) and $q^\top G^{-1}q=m$ (Fact 1). Because $G$ is positive definite it has a positive-definite square root $G^{1/2}$, and $G^{-1/2}$ is invertible. Conjugate $qq^\top - G$ by $G^{-1/2}$ — again a congruence, so inertia is preserved:

$$G^{-1/2}\big(qq^\top - G\big)G^{-1/2} = \big(G^{-1/2}q\big)\big(G^{-1/2}q\big)^\top - I = w\,w^\top - I,\qquad w:=G^{-1/2}q.$$

The length of $w$ is set by Fact 1: $\;\|w\|^2 = w^\top w = q^\top G^{-1} q = m.$ The matrix $w w^\top - I$ is now trivial to diagonalize. The rank-one matrix $ww^\top$ has eigenvalue $\|w\|^2=m$ along the direction $w$, and eigenvalue $0$ on the $(n-1)$-dimensional subspace $w^\perp$. Subtracting the identity shifts every eigenvalue down by $1$:

$$\text{eigenvalues of } ww^\top - I:\qquad \underbrace{m-1}_{\text{once, along } w},\qquad \underbrace{-1,\,\dots,\,-1}_{n-1\ \text{times, on } w^\perp}.$$

Here is where $m\ge 2$ earns its keep: $m-1\ge 1\gt 0$, so there is exactly one positive eigenvalue; the other $n-1$ are $-1\lt0$; none is zero. Therefore

$$\operatorname{In}(M)=\operatorname{In}(qq^\top-G)=\operatorname{In}(ww^\top-I)=(1,\,n-1,\,0),$$ $$\det M\ \text{has the sign of}\ (m-1)\cdot(-1)^{n-1}=(-1)^{n-1}.$$

(For the determinant sign directly: $\det(qq^\top-G)=\det(G)\det(ww^\top-I) =\det(G)\,(m-1)(-1)^{n-1}$, and $\det G\gt0$. The factor relating $\det M$ to $\det(qq^\top-G)$ is $\big(\prod_i x_i\big)^{-2}Z^{\,n}\gt0$, which does not affect the sign.)

That is the entire proof. No induction on $n$, no separate treatment of small $k$, no Perron–Frobenius input even needed — the single positive eigenvalue is produced by the rank-one bump, and its being exactly one is the arithmetic fact $m\ge2$.

5. The whole argument on one page

1. Target: $\operatorname{In}(M)=(1,n-1,0)$ (equivalently $\det M$ has sign $(-1)^{n-1}$). [§1]

2. $M=D_{1/x}M_Q D_{1/x}$ where $M_Q$ is the matrix of $\sum_{|A|=m}p_A Q_A$, $Q_A(v)=\sum_{i\neq j\in A}v_iv_j$. Positive-diagonal congruence $\Rightarrow$ $\operatorname{In}(M)=\operatorname{In}(M_Q)$. [§2, proved by a bijection]

3. Normalizing, $M_Q\propto qq^\top - G$ with $q$ = marginals, $\Sigma$ = covariance, $G=D_q-\Sigma$. So $\operatorname{In}(M)=\operatorname{In}(qq^\top-G)$. [§3, expand a variance]

4. $G\mathbf 1=q$ (because $|\mathcal A|=m$ is constant $\Rightarrow \Sigma\mathbf 1=0$), so $q^\top G^{-1}q=\sum q_i=m\ge2$. [§4, Fact 1]

5. $G\succ0$, because $-G=\nabla^2\log f|_{\mathbf 1}$ and $f$ is (a scaling of) the log-concave polynomial $e_m$. [§4, Fact 2 — the one classical input]

6. Conjugate by $G^{-1/2}$: $qq^\top-G\cong ww^\top-I$ with $\|w\|^2=m$; eigenvalues $m-1\ (\gt0)$ once and $-1$ $(n-1)$ times. Inertia $(1,n-1,0)$, sign $(-1)^{n-1}$. [§4, Fact 3]

Uniform in $k$ and $n$; the boundary $k=n$ ($m=1$) is excluded precisely because there the lone positive eigenvalue $m-1$ would be $0$.

Sanity check at the extremes. For $k=1$ we have $m=n$: the conditioned set is all of $[n]$ with probability one, so $q=\mathbf 1$, $\Sigma=0$, $G=I$, and $qq^\top-G=\mathbf 1\mathbf 1^\top - I = J-I$, whose eigenvalues are $n-1$ (once) and $-1$ ($n-1$ times). One can check the true Jacobian directly here ($M_{ij}=\prod_{\ell\neq i,j}x_\ell$ when $k=1$), and indeed $M=(\prod_\ell x_\ell)\,D_{1/x}(J-I)D_{1/x}$ — literally congruent to $J-I$ at every $x$. Specializing once more to the symmetric point $x_i\equiv x$ gives $M=x^{n-2}(J-I)$, which is Kearnes' 2016 symmetric-point formula $M=r(J-I)$ with $r=x^{n-2}$. The same rank-one mechanism that drives the general proof is already visible in this simplest case.

5′. Post-hoc simplification: $M$ is congruent to the Hessian of $e_m$ itself

Found while reading the Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa paper (2026-07-12): their central object is not merely an ingredient — it is our Jacobian, up to an explicit positive-diagonal congruence. The Hessian of $e_m$ has entries $\partial_i\partial_j e_m(s)=e_{m-2}(s_{\widehat{\imath\jmath}})$ for $i\neq j$ and zero diagonal (multiaffine). Rewriting the Step-2 bijection in the odds variables, $p_A=\prod_\ell(1-x_\ell)\prod_{i\in A}s_i$ gives $x_ix_jM_{ij}=\prod_\ell(1-x_\ell)\cdot s_is_j\,e_{m-2}(s_{\widehat{\imath\jmath}})$, and dividing by $x_ix_j$ (note $s_i/x_i=1/(1-x_i)$):

$$M \;=\; \prod_\ell(1-x_\ell)\cdot D\,H_{e_m}(s)\,D, \qquad D=\operatorname{diag}\!\Big(\frac{1}{1-x_i}\Big), \qquad \big(H_{e_m}\big)_{ij}=\partial_i\partial_j e_m .$$

Verified numerically for $(k,n)=(2,4),(3,5),(2,6),(4,7)$ at random interior points: max entrywise deviation $\sim10^{-16}$, and $H_{e_m}(s)$ has inertia $(1,n-1,0)$ in every trial.

Consequence: Theorem 1.1(i) of Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa, applied to $f_{U_{m,n}}=e_m$ at the point $a=s$, is verbatim the statement $\operatorname{In}(M)=(1,n-1,0)$ — the whole conjecture follows from the citation in one step, without Facts 1 and 3. Those facts are not wasted: the chain $M\cong qq^\top-G$ is the probabilistic unpacking of the standard identity $H_f\big|_{\mathbf 1}=qq^\top-G$ for the normalized $f$ (Hessian = log-Hessian plus rank-one gradient term), and it is what exhibits the lone positive eigenvalue explicitly as $m-1$, explains the $k=n$ degeneration, and identifies Malyshev's negative-definite subspace as $\{q\}^\perp$. For the paper: cite Theorem 1.1(i) for the signature in one line, keep the covariance route as the interpretation.

Status of each link. Read this as the audit trail.

Step 2 ($M\cong qq^\top-G$): proved (explicit bijection; also checked symbolically).

Step 3 (the variance expansion, definition of $G$): proved (one line of algebra).

Fact 1 ($G\mathbf 1=q$, hence $q^\top G^{-1}q=m$): proved (covariance with a constant is zero).

Fact 3 (rank-one update $\Rightarrow$ $(1,n-1,0)$): proved (elementary linear algebra, given Facts 1–2).

Fact 2 ($G\succ0$): proved, by citation — Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa 2021, Theorem 1.1(i) (strict log-concavity, in the pointwise-Hessian sense, of the basis generating polynomial of any simple matroid of rank $\ge2$), applied to $U_{m,n}$; independently confirmed for $n\le 6$, all $m$, by the exact all-positive-coefficient certificate.

The gap is closed (2026-07-11). The literature pass recommended by an earlier version of this note found the exact reference for face (ii) below: Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa, J. Combin. Theory Ser. A 181 (2021), art. 105351, Theorem 1.1(i) — stated and audited in §4, Fact 2. Applied to the uniform matroid $U_{m,n}$ (simple, of rank $m\ge2$, for the whole range $2\le m\le n$) it is verbatim the statement $G\succ0$ at every $s\gt0$. With this citation the signature conjecture ($\det M\neq0$, sign $(-1)^{n-1}$, local injectivity everywhere) is proved for all $1\le k\le n-1$ and all $n$; global injectivity additionally needs the local-to-global lemmas (P) and (F) of globalization-gap.md (caught 2026-07-12). For the record, the three equivalent faces of $G\succ0$ and their final status:

(i) Combinatorial / self-contained: every leading principal minor of $\widehat G=e_m^2 G$ has only nonnegative coefficients as a polynomial in the odds $s$. Proved case-by-case to $n=6$; still open in general, now as an optional project — a uniform induction or Schur-complement recursion (seeded by the splitting $e_m(s)=e_m(s_{\hat n})+s_n e_{m-1}(s_{\hat n})$) would make the entire proof elementary, needing nothing beyond polynomial arithmetic and Sylvester's criterion.

(ii) Analytic: $\log e_m$ is strictly concave on $\{y\gt0\}$ for $2\le m\le n$. Closed by citation (Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa Theorem 1.1(i), §4 Fact 2). Note that Gårding 1959 alone (References/58061.pdf) is not sufficient: his Theorem 5 with its equality case gives strict concavity of the function (the lineality of $e_m$ is $0$ for $m\ge2$), but the pointwise Hessian-definiteness — "the level hypersurfaces are strictly convex" — is left to the reader in his §4, and function-strictness is genuinely weaker than a kernel-free Hessian (compare $-t^4$ at $0$). The 2021 theorem supplies exactly the pointwise statement.

(iii) Probabilistic: for the size-$m$-conditioned product Bernoulli measure, $\Sigma\prec D_q$, i.e. $\operatorname{Var}_\mu(\sum_i v_i\chi_i)\lt\sum_i v_i^2 q_i$ for all $v\neq0$. Now a corollary of (ii) via $\nabla^2\log f|_{\mathbf 1}=-G$ — and a clean statement worth recording in the write-up, since it reads as a variance inequality for negatively-associated measures.

Next actions. (a) Close the local-to-global step — lemmas (P) properness and (F) singleton fiber — per the plan in globalization-gap.md; this is what stands between the proved signature statement and the global injectivity theorem. (b) Write the result up (TeX/Parreiras26c.tex), citing Muraietal2021 for Fact 2 (entry added to bibas.bib, all sibling projects synced) — structured per §5′: one-line citation for the signature, covariance route as interpretation, plus the globalization section. (c) Optionally pursue (i) for a fully self-contained, elementary proof.

Caution flag for the next conjecture (mixed inversion / principal minors). The strengthened target — every principal minor $M[S,S]$ of size $\ge2$ is nonzero, so any partially-observed contest is recoverable (QUICKSTART.md §2) — does not fall out of the same citation for free. Two reasons. First, by §5′ the minor $M[S,S]$ is congruent to the Hessian in $z_S$ of the positive specialization $e_m(s\odot z)\big|_{z_{S^c}=\mathbf 1}$ — no longer a basis generating polynomial of a matroid but a weighted independent-set-type polynomial (a positive combination $\sum_j e_{m-j}$-like object on the ground set $S$), outside the literal scope of Theorem 1.1(i). Second, Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa's own Theorem 1.1(ii), about exactly such independent-set generating polynomials, explicitly excludes uniform matroids — and for good cause: there is a genuine kernel, $(-\partial_0+\partial_1+\cdots+\partial_n)\overline P_{U_{r,n}}=0$ (end of their §2). That kernel involves the homogenizing $\partial_0$ direction, which our minors do not contain, so it is not a counterexample to the principal-minor conjecture — but it shows nondegeneracy genuinely fails one door over in the uniform independent-set world, so the strengthening needs its own argument, not a reflexive citation.

What this replaces. The earlier program (in signature-reduction-results.html and unifying-statement-old.md) reduced everything to "the polynomial $(-1)^{n-1}\det\widetilde N^{(k)}$ has all-positive coefficients," and proposed proving it by a Lindström–Gessel–Viennot network (blocked on darij grinberg's unproven common-sign lemma) or by sum-of-squares. That route is no longer needed: the present argument is uniform in $k$ (no per-$k$ matrix $\widetilde N^{(k)}$), and its positivity certificate lives on $G$ — an $n\times n$ covariance matrix — rather than on a giant determinant. Malyshev's R3 instinct (a single negative-definite subspace closing all $k$ at once) is vindicated: the subspace is $\{q\}^\perp$, $G$ is the form on it, and $G\succ0$ is the whole content.

7. Hidden-traps checklist

The places a proof like this can secretly fail, and where each stands.

T1. "Is $M$ really congruent to $qq^\top-G$, or only numerically close?" — Really congruent. The identity $x_ix_jM_{ij}=(M_Q)_{ij}$ is exact (bijection in §2, plus symbolic check). The two congruences use $D_{1/x}$ and $G^{-1/2}$, both invertible, so inertia transfers exactly.

T2. "Does the argument secretly assume the conclusion?" (e.g. invoking 'by continuity the signature is $(1,n-1)$', or using Perron to put in the positive eigenvalue) — No. The signature is never assumed. The lone positive eigenvalue is produced, in Fact 3, by the rank-one bump $ww^\top$ together with $\|w\|^2=m\ge2$. Perron–Frobenius is not used at all.

T3. "Is positive-definiteness of $G$ truly necessary, or would semidefinite do?" — Strictly necessary. Fact 3 needs $G^{-1/2}$ (invertibility), and Fact 1 needs $G^{-1}q$. If $G$ had a kernel the conjugation would be illegal and $q^\top G^{-1}q$ undefined. So the entire weight rests on strict $G\succ0$ — which is why the citation had to be checked to give pointwise Hessian-definiteness, not mere semidefiniteness or function-strictness (§4 Fact 2, §6(ii)): the Murai–Nagaoka–Yazawa theorem does.

T4. "Is $G\succ0$ maybe trivially true for any measure, making the result suspiciously cheap?" — No, and this is reassuring. For any measure on $\{0,1\}^n$ the same computation gives $-\nabla^2\log f|_{\mathbf 1}=D_q-\Sigma$, but this is indefinite when there is positive correlation (e.g. two coordinates forced equal). Positive definiteness uses the special negative-association / log-concavity of the size-conditioned product measure. The proof is not 'too good to be true' — it consumes a genuine structural input exactly once, at Fact 2.

T5. "Does 'all coefficients nonnegative' really give a strict minor $\gt0$?" — Yes. A polynomial with nonnegative coefficients that is not identically zero, evaluated at a strictly positive point, is a sum of nonnegative terms at least one of which is strictly positive. Each leading principal minor is then $\gt0$ for all $s\gt0$, which is the hypothesis of Sylvester's criterion. (We checked 'nonzero and no negative coefficient' for each minor.)

T6. "Are the boundary values of $k$ handled?" — Yes, and informatively. $m=n-k+1\in\{2, \dots,n\}$ for $k\in\{1,\dots,n-1\}$, so $m\ge2$ throughout and Fact 3 always gives one positive eigenvalue. The excluded $k=n$ gives $m=1$, where the positive eigenvalue $m-1$ would collapse to $0$ — the proof correctly predicts the one place the statement degenerates. The easy endpoints $k=1,\,k=n-1$ are not special-cased; they fall out of the same formula ($k=1$ reproduces Kearnes' $J-I$, §5).

T7. "Probability slice $x\in(0,1)$ vs. the orthant $s\gt0$?" — Equivalent, no loss. As $x_i$ ranges over $(0,1)$ the odds $s_i=x_i/(1-x_i)$ range over all of $(0,\infty)$, and the certificate is stated for all $s\gt0$. (This matches Speyer's 2016 homogeneity observation that the determinant's sign on the slice equals its sign on the whole positive orthant.)

T8. "Could the numerics be lying (floating-point artifacts)?" — The load-bearing checks are symbolic, not floating-point: the congruence identity (§2), $\nabla^2\log f=-G$ (Fact 2), and the positive-coefficient minors are exact computations over $\mathbb Q$. The floating-point runs (inertia, $q^\top G^{-1}q=m$) are only corroboration; an earlier round of false positives from extreme skew + a loose tolerance was diagnosed and is not present in the exact results.

T9. "Is the generating-polynomial formula $f=e_m(s\odot z)/e_m(s)$, with the right normalization, correct?" — Yes: $f(\mathbf 1)=1$ and $\partial_i f(\mathbf 1)=q_i$, $\partial_i\partial_j f(\mathbf 1)=q_{ij}$ as required; and the downstream identity $\nabla^2\log f|_{\mathbf 1}=-G$ was confirmed symbolically, which would fail if the formula or its normalization were wrong.

8. Reproduce it yourself

The checks above were run in Julia (Nemo for exact polynomial arithmetic, LinearAlgebra for numerics). The essential pieces, in compact form:

Build the measure and $G$ for given $(k,n)$ and a point $x$ (numeric):

m = n-k+1;  over all A ⊆ [n] with |A|=m:  p = ∏_{i∈A} x_i · ∏_{i∉A}(1-x_i)
Z = Σ p;  q_i = (Σ_{A∋i} p)/Z;  q_ij = (Σ_{A∋i,j} p)/Z
Σ = [q_ij - q_i q_j];  Σ_ii = q_i(1-q_i);  G = Diagonal(q) - Σ
# claims:  eigvals(G) .> 0;  qᵀ G⁻¹ q ≈ m;  In(q qᵀ - G) = (1, n-1, 0)

The exact certificate for $G\succ0$ (symbolic, in the odds $s_1,\dots,s_n$ over $\mathbb Q$):

e_r(vars) = Σ over r-subsets T of ∏_{i∈T} vars_i  (elementary symmetric)
Ĝ_ii = s_i² · e_{m-1}(s without i)²
Ĝ_ij = s_i s_j · ( e_{m-1}(s\i)·e_{m-1}(s\j) − e_{m-2}(s\{i,j})·e_m(s) )
# for j = 1..n:  d = det( Ĝ[1:j, 1:j] );  assert (d ≠ 0) and (no coefficient of d is < 0)
# all-positive ⇒ (Sylvester's criterion) Ĝ ≻ 0 ⇒ G ≻ 0 for all s > 0

Symbolic congruence to the true Jacobian (the §2 bridge): build $M_{ij}$ from its probability definition and check the polynomial identity $x_i x_j M_{ij} = \sum_{A\ni i,j,\,|A|=m} p_A$ over $\mathbb Q[x_1,\dots,x_n]$.
