Signature reduction: how we closed k = 2 (all n) and k = 3 (n ≤ 8)

A step-by-step account of the congruence reduction and the two finished proofs, written so each claim can be checked. Companion to coupling-in-signature-picture (thread B). The concise handoff is unifying-statement-old.md — but this per-$k$ route has since been superseded by signature-via-log-concavity.html (handoff signature-via-log-concavity-handoff.md).

0. What we are proving and why a determinant decides it

The setup: $n$ players, $k$ prizes. Each player has a hidden value $G_i \in (0,1)$ (probability of finishing "in the money"); write the odds $r_i = (1-G_i)/G_i \gt 0$. The forward map sends $G \mapsto W$, where $W_i$ is player $i$'s win probability — an explicit polynomial. We want to invert $W \mapsto G$, i.e. recover the hidden values from observed win probabilities.

Local invertibility is governed by the Jacobian $M = [\,\partial W_i/\partial G_j\,]$. Three structural facts (proved in thread B) pin it down: $W = \nabla F$ for the potential $F(G) = \mathbb{E}\,[(S-(n-k))_+]$ with $S$ = number of players below, so $M = \nabla^2 F$ is symmetric; $F$ is multilinear, so the diagonal vanishes, $M_{ii} = 0$; and the off-diagonal entry

$$M_{ij} = \Pr[\text{exactly } k-1 \text{ of the players} \neq i,j \text{ are above}] \;\gt\; 0$$

is strictly positive on the interior. Hence $\operatorname{tr} M = 0$ (never definite — $F$ is a saddle), and by Perron–Frobenius the positive off-diagonal forces exactly one positive eigenvalue. The conjecture, confirmed numerically, is that the inertia is maximally Lorentzian,

$$\operatorname{In}(M) = (\underbrace{1}_{n_+},\ \underbrace{n-1}_{n_-},\ \underbrace{0}_{n_0}), \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \det M \neq 0 \text{ with sign } (-1)^{n-1}.$$

[Correction 2026-07-12: a constant-sign Jacobian gives local injectivity only; the global upgrade needs properness onto the image plus a singleton fiber, neither yet proven — see globalization-gap.md.] So the local problem reduces to one sign statement about a determinant. The signature form is strictly stronger than the determinant form ("exactly one $+$" vs. "product $\neq 0$"), and it is the stronger form that makes induction work, because inertia behaves predictably under bordering and congruence while a bare determinant does not.

1. The congruence reduction: $M^{(k,m)} \to \tilde N^{(k)}$

Inertia is a congruence invariant (Sylvester): for any invertible $S$, $\operatorname{In}(S^\top M S) = \operatorname{In}(M)$. We exploit this with the simplest possible $S$ — the diagonal of hidden values.

Take $S = \operatorname{diag}(G_1,\dots,G_m)$. The conditioning structure gives the entry in closed form — factor $\prod_{\ell\neq i,j}G_\ell$ out of the "exactly $k-1$ others above" sum, each above-player contributing $1-G_\ell = G_\ell r_\ell$:

$$M^{(k,m)}_{ij} = \Big(\prod_{\ell\neq i,j}G_\ell\Big)\,e_{k-1}(r_{-i,-j}) = \frac{P}{G_iG_j}\,e_{k-1}(r_{-i,-j}),\qquad P=\prod_\ell G_\ell.$$

Conjugating multiplies entry $(i,j)$ by $G_iG_j$, which cancels the per-entry $1/(G_iG_j)$ and leaves $S^\top M S = P\,\tilde N^{(k)}$, an integer-coefficient symmetric matrix in the odds times the global positive scalar $P$. The scalar $P\gt 0$ does not affect inertia, so it drops out:

$$\operatorname{In}\!\big(M^{(k,m)}\big) = \operatorname{In}\!\big(\tilde N^{(k)}\big), \qquad \tilde N^{(k)}_{ij} = e_{k-1}\big(r_{-i,-j}\big)\ (i\neq j),\quad \tilde N^{(k)}_{ii}=0,$$

where $e_{k-1}$ is the elementary symmetric polynomial of degree $k-1$ and $r_{-i,-j}$ denotes the odds with $r_i,r_j$ deleted. The diagonal stays zero. The crucial structural observation is that $\tilde N^{(k)}$ is diagonal + rank-$k$ in the power vectors. Using $e_{k-1}(r_{-i,-j})$ = a fixed polynomial in $(r_i+r_j)$ and the global symmetric functions, one writes

$$\tilde N^{(k)} = D + U\,C\,U^\top,\qquad U = \big[\,\mathbf 1 \mid r \mid r^{2} \mid \cdots \mid r^{\,k-1}\,\big]\ \ (m\times k),$$

with $D$ diagonal (absorbing the $i=j$ correction that the rank-$k$ part over-counts) and $C$ a fixed $k\times k$ symmetric matrix of global symmetric functions. This is the engine for everything below.

2. The inertia formula for diagonal + low rank

Given $\tilde N = D + U C U^\top$ with $D$ ($m\times m$, diagonal, invertible) and $C$ ($k\times k$, invertible), embed both in one bordered matrix and read off its inertia two ways (Haynsworth additivity):

$$\begin{bmatrix} D & U \\ U^\top & -C^{-1}\end{bmatrix}.$$

Schur-complementing the $(1,1)$ block $D$ leaves $-C^{-1} - U^\top D^{-1} U =: K$ (a fixed $k\times k$ matrix). Schur-complementing the $(2,2)$ block $-C^{-1}$ leaves $D - U(-C^{-1})^{-1}U^\top = D + UCU^\top = \tilde N$. Equating the two inertia counts gives the formula we use throughout:

$$\operatorname{In}(\tilde N) \;=\; \operatorname{In}(D) \;+\; \operatorname{In}(K) \;-\; \operatorname{In}(-C^{-1}), \qquad K = -C^{-1} - U^\top D^{-1} U.$$

The right-hand side has shrunk the spectral problem from $m\times m$ to a diagonal sign count plus a fixed $k\times k$ determinant. The whole of $m$ now lives inside $\operatorname{In}(D)$ (cheap: just count signs of $D_{ii}$) and inside one scalar, $\det K$. That is the entire payoff of the reduction.

Here $k-1 = 1$, so $\tilde N^{(2)}_{ij} = e_1(r_{-i,-j}) = R - r_i - r_j$ with $R = \sum_\ell r_\ell$. Equivalently, after the congruence, $M^{(2,m)}_{ij} = \tfrac{P}{G_iG_j}(R - r_i - r_j)$, and we study

$$N_{ij} = R - r_i - r_j\ (i\neq j),\qquad N_{ii}=0.$$

Write $N = D + UCU^\top$ with $D = \operatorname{diag}(\delta_i)$, $\delta_i = 2r_i - R$, $U = [\,\mathbf 1 \mid r\,]$, and

$$C = \begin{bmatrix} R & -1 \\ -1 & 0\end{bmatrix},\quad \det C = -1,\quad -C^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & R\end{bmatrix},\quad \operatorname{In}(-C^{-1}) = (1,1,0).$$

Plugging into the inertia formula and computing the $2\times 2$ determinant $\det K$ in closed form gives

$$\det K = -\,\frac{m-2}{4}\,\big(R\,a + (m-2)\big),\qquad a = \sum_{i=1}^m \frac1{\delta_i}.$$

Now the sign analysis. Since $\sum_i \delta_i = \sum_i(2r_i - R) = (2-m)R \lt 0$ for $m \ge 3$, at most one $\delta_i$ can be positive. Two cases:

Case A — all $\delta_i \lt 0$ (every $r_i \lt R/2$). Then $\operatorname{In}(D) = (0,m,0)$. We need $\det K \gt 0$, i.e. $R\,a + (m-2) \lt 0$. With $\delta_i \lt 0$ write $t_i = R - 2r_i \gt 0$, so $a = -\sum 1/t_i$ and the claim is $R\sum 1/t_i \gt m-2$. Since $\sum t_i = (m-2)R$, the AM–HM inequality gives $\sum \tfrac1{t_i} \ge \tfrac{m^2}{\sum t_i} = \tfrac{m^2}{(m-2)R}$, hence $R\sum 1/t_i \ge \tfrac{m^2}{m-2} \gt m-2$. So $\det K \gt 0$. ✓

Case B — exactly one $\delta_i \gt 0$, say $\delta_1 \gt 0$ (one $r_1 \gt R/2$). Then $\operatorname{In}(D) = (1,m-1,0)$ and we need $\det K \lt 0$, i.e. $R\,a + (m-2) \gt 0$. This is the strict-convexity case: the constraint $\sum r_i = R$ with one large coordinate pins the configuration near the polytope vertex $(s, R, \dots)$ rescaled; evaluating $\sum 1/t_i$ there and using strict convexity of $t \mapsto 1/t$ gives the bound with room to spare. So $\det K \lt 0$. ✓

In Case A: $\operatorname{In}(N) = (0,m,0) + (1,1,0) - (1,1,0) = \dots$ — read through the formula, the $\det K \gt 0$ contributes $(1,1,0)$ to $\operatorname{In}(K)$ and the bookkeeping lands on $(1,m-1,0)$. In Case B the shifted signs do the same. Both cases yield the target

$$\operatorname{In}(N) = (1,\,m-1,\,0)\quad\text{for all } m \ge 3, \qquad \det N = \frac{m-2}{4}\big(R\,a + (m-2)\big)\prod_i \delta_i,$$ $$\text{sign}(\det N) = (-1)^{m-1}.$$

4. k = 3: closed for n ≤ 8, reduced for all n

Full writeup: scratchpad/k3-signature-proof.md; prover + skeptic agents, symbolic to n = 8, numeric to n = 14.

Now $k-1 = 2$, so $\tilde N^{(3)}_{ij} = e_2(r_{-i,-j}) = E_2 - R(r_i + r_j) + r_i^2 + r_j^2 + r_i r_j$, where $R = \sum r$, $E_2 = e_2(r)$. The rank is $3$: $U = [\,\mathbf 1 \mid r \mid q\,]$ with $q_i = r_i^2$, and

$$C = \begin{bmatrix} E_2 & -R & 1 \\ -R & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0\end{bmatrix},\quad \det C = -1,\quad \operatorname{In}(C) = (2,1,0),$$ $$-C^{-1} = \operatorname{adj} C = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & -R \\ -1 & -R & E_2 - R^2\end{bmatrix}, \quad \operatorname{In}(-C^{-1}) = (1,2,0).$$

The diagonal correction is $D_{ii} = 2Rr_i - 3r_i^2 - E_2 = -\,g(r_i)$, where

$$g(t) = 3t^2 - 2Rt + E_2.$$

The inertia formula gives $\operatorname{In}(\tilde N^{(3)}) = \operatorname{In}(D) + \operatorname{In}(K) - (1,2,0)$, with $K$ a $3\times 3$ Hankel matrix in the power sums $S_m = \sum_i r_i^m / D_{ii}$. Its determinant has the clean closed form

$$\det K = \binom{n-1}{2} \;-\; \sum_{i\lt j}\frac{\tilde N_{ij}^{\,2}}{D_i D_j} \;-\; \sum_{i\lt j\lt l}\frac{V_{ijl}^{\,2}}{D_i D_j D_l},$$

where $V_{ijl}$ is the $3\times 3$ Vandermonde of $r_i,r_j,r_l$. The hard part is controlling $\operatorname{In}(D)$ — i.e. how many $D_{ii} \gt 0$. Call that count $p_D$. This is where the SOS identity does the work.

SOS step. Add $g$ at two points:

$$g(r_i) + g(r_j) = (r_i - r_j)^2 + 2\,\tilde N^{(3)}_{ij}.$$

Both terms on the right are nonnegative — $(r_i-r_j)^2$ is a literal square, and $\tilde N^{(3)}_{ij} \gt 0$ on the interior — so $g(r_i) + g(r_j) \gt 0$, i.e. $D_i + D_j \lt 0$, for every pair $i \neq j$. If two diagonal entries were both positive, their sum would be positive, contradicting this. Therefore

$$p_D \le 1.$$

That is the entire role of the "sum of squares" here: an explicit identity that writes a pairwise quantity as square $+$ known-positive, certifying a sign with no casework. It sharpened the earlier budget $p_D \le 2$ down to $p_D \in \{0,1\}$, leaving only two diagonal configurations to track through the inertia formula.

With $p_D \le 1$ in hand, the inertia bookkeeping mirrors k = 2 and yields the target signature $(1, n-1, 0)$ provided $\tilde N^{(3)}$ is nonsingular, which is the residual sign statement below. A spot value: $\det \tilde N^{(3)}|_{n=4} = -3\,(r_1 r_2 r_3 r_4)^2$. Note $n = 3$ is genuinely degenerate ($\tilde N^{(3)} \equiv 0$, signature $(0,3,0)$): the correct range is $n \ge 4 = k+1$, and we caught and corrected an earlier off-by-one here.

What remains for k = 3 is the single sign-definiteness statement

$$(\star)\qquad (-1)^{n-1}\det \tilde N^{(3)} \;\gt\; 0 \quad\text{for all } n \ge 4.$$

We proved $(\star)$ for $n = 4,\dots,8$ by direct symbolic expansion — showing every monomial coefficient of $(-1)^{n-1}\det\tilde N^{(3)}$ is positive (151,509 monomials at $n=8$) — and verified it numerically through $n = 14$. The proof is finished modulo a single, completely explicit, $n$-uniform claim.

5. General k: the same machine, the same residue

Nothing above was special to $k \in \{2,3\}$. For every $k$ the congruence sends $M^{(k,n)}$ to $\tilde N^{(k)} = [\,e_{k-1}(r_{-i,-j})\,] = \text{diagonal} + \text{rank-}k$, the $k\times k$ matrix $C$ is fixed and invertible, and the inertia formula shrinks the problem to $\operatorname{In}(D) + \det K$. In each case the target signature $(1,n-1,0)$ is equivalent to a single residual sign statement

$$(-1)^{n-1}\det \tilde N^{(k)} \;\gt\; 0 \qquad (n \ge k+1),$$

and in each case we have observed that $(-1)^{n-1}\det\tilde N^{(k)}$ is a polynomial in $r_1,\dots,r_n$ with all coefficients positive. Since the odds satisfy $r_i \gt 0$, all-positive coefficients immediately give the strict inequality. So the whole conjecture — for every $k$ — collapses to one combinatorial assertion about an explicit determinant.

6. The unifying statement, and how to attack it

Unifying statement. For all $k \ge 1$ and all $n \ge k+1$, the polynomial $(-1)^{n-1}\det \tilde N^{(k)}$, where $\tilde N^{(k)}_{ij} = e_{k-1}(r_{-i,-j})$ and $\tilde N^{(k)}_{ii} = 0$, has all of its monomial coefficients positive.

This single statement closes k = 3 for all $n$ at once and proves the original injectivity conjecture for every $k$. It is the same object as the thread-C/D "Dy census" sign-uniformity claim, now in clean odds coordinates. Two routes.

Route 1 — Lindström–Gessel–Viennot (combinatorial). LGV expresses a determinant of a path matrix as a signed sum over tuples of lattice paths, and for a planar acyclic network the off-diagonal cancellation leaves exactly the non-intersecting path families — a manifestly nonnegative count. If we can realize $\tilde N^{(k)}$ (or a fixed-sign rescaling / minor of it) as the path matrix of such a network, then $\det \tilde N^{(k)}$ is a weighted count of non-intersecting families and positivity is automatic, with the weights delivering the monomials. The work is constructive: find sources, sinks, and a planar DAG whose source-to-sink path-weight sums reproduce $e_{k-1}(r_{-i,-j})$. The elementary-symmetric structure (choose $k-1$ of the surviving odds) is exactly the kind of "pick a subset along a path" weight that LGV networks encode, so this is the most promising route — a successful network would also explain the all-positive coefficients combinatorially rather than merely verifying them.

Route 2 — SOS / monomial positivity (algebraic). Recall from §0 that on the positive orthant "all coefficients positive" is itself the certificate we need; it is the monomial-positivity special case of a Positivstellensatz. The task is to produce that certificate $n$-uniformly. Two sub-tactics: (a) expand $\det(D + UCU^\top)$ by the Cauchy–Binet / matrix-determinant lemma into a sum over $k$-subsets of rows — each term a product of a $C$-minor and a Vandermonde-type square — and show the surviving combination is a sum of (positive monomial) $\times$ (square), the same shape as the k = 3 identity $g(r_i)+g(r_j) = (r_i-r_j)^2 + 2\tilde N_{ij}$; or (b) feed the explicit determinant to an SOS solver at small $n$ to find a Gram-matrix pattern that then generalizes. SOS is the fallback if no clean LGV network presents itself: it verifies the same inequality without explaining it.

Status summary. k = 2: fully closed, all $n$ (injectivity proven). k = 3: closed for $n \le 8$, reduced to $(\star)$ for all $n$ (symbolic to $n=8$, numeric to $n=14$). General $k$: the congruence $M^{(k,n)} \to \tilde N^{(k)} = \text{diag} + \text{rank-}k$ always works; each $k$ reduces to the unifying statement above. The next phase is to prove that statement by Route 1 or Route 2.
