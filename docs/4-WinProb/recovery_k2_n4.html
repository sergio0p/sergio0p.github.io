Inverting $W$ for $k=2,\ n=4$ — why the recovery isn’t circular

The worry: “recover $D_i$” sounds circular, because $D_i$ is itself built from the unknown $r$. The resolution: for a concrete $(k,n)$ the equations close on two scalar unknowns, and a finite solve returns a unique admissible point. Here is the whole thing for $k=2,\,n=4$.

Setup

Work in $a_\ell:=1+r_\ell$ (so $r_\ell>0 \iff a_\ell>1$), with the two global scalars

$$A:=\sum_{\ell=1}^{4}a_\ell,\qquad G:=\prod_{\ell=1}^{4}a_\ell .$$

For $k=2$ the numerator is $N_i=e_0+e_1(r_{-i})=1+\sum_{\ell\neq i}r_\ell$ and $D_i=\prod_{\ell\neq i}(1+r_\ell)=G/a_i$, so

$$W_i=\frac{N_i}{D_i}=\frac{a_i\,(A-2-a_i)}{G}.$$

(The factor $a_i$ cancels against the $a_i$ inside $G$, so $W_i$ really depends only on the others — e.g. $W_1=\dfrac{-2+a_2+a_3+a_4}{a_2a_3a_4}$. The form above is the convenient one for inversion.)

The inversion closes on two scalars

Read $W_i=\dfrac{a_i(A-2-a_i)}{G}$ as a quadratic in $a_i$:

$$\boxed{\,a_i^{\,2}-(A-2)\,a_i+W_i\,G=0\,}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad a_i=\frac{(A-2)\pm\sqrt{(A-2)^2-4\,W_iG}}{2}.$$

Every $a_i$ is a root of the same quadratic family — only the constant term $W_iG$ changes with $i$. The apparent self-reference collapses, because once the two numbers $(A,G)$ are fixed, each $a_i$ is an explicit function of the data $W_i$. The system is closed by the two definitions themselves:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{4}a_i(A,G)=A,\qquad \prod_{i=1}^{4}a_i(A,G)=G.$$

So inversion is two scalar equations in two scalar unknowns $(A,G)$ (plus a choice of $\pm$ branch per coordinate) — not a circular chase through $r$.

A worked point

Take the true odds ratios $r=\big(\tfrac12,\,1,\,\tfrac32,\,2\big)$, i.e. $a=\big(\tfrac32,\,2,\,\tfrac52,\,3\big)$. Then $A=9$, $G=\tfrac{45}{2}$, and

$$W=\Big(\tfrac{11}{30},\ \tfrac{4}{9},\ \tfrac12,\ \tfrac{8}{15}\Big)\approx(0.367,\ 0.444,\ 0.500,\ 0.533).$$

Now forget $r$ and invert. With $A=9,\ G=\tfrac{45}{2}$ the discriminant is $(A-2)^2-4W_iG=49-90\,W_i$, and each $a_i=\dfrac{7-\sqrt{49-90W_i}}{2}$ (smaller branch):

Uniqueness on the domain

Solving the full polynomial system (without assuming $A,G$) returns many real roots — corner points where some $a_i\in\{0,1,2\}$, plus higher algebraic branches — but exactly one with all $a_i>1$:

$$a=\big(\tfrac32,\,2,\,\tfrac52,\,3\big),\qquad r=\big(\tfrac12,\,1,\,\tfrac32,\,2\big).$$
The $\pm$ per coordinate gives up to $2^4$ branch combinations, and the $(A,G)$ closure has several roots; most land at corners ($a_i\le 1$) or off the positive orthant. Injectivity is exactly the statement that only one branch+root lands inside $(1,\infty)^4$. This is where the domain $B(0,1)_{++}$ does real work — it selects the admissible branch.
Why this isn’t circular, in one line. $D_i$ is not recovered “from $r$”; it is recovered from the data $W$ by solving the two-scalar system in $(A,G)$. For general $(k,n)$ the same move works: each $a_i$ (or $1+r_i$) is a root of a fixed low-degree polynomial whose coefficients are the global symmetric functions $E_m$, and the equations $\sum a_i=A$, $\prod a_i=G$ (and their higher analogues for larger $k$) close the system. The research question is whether the admissible branch is always unique on $(1,\infty)^n$.
