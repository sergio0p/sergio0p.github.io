Analytic construction of the binary elimination-contest equilibrium

Offline working roadmap · 4-player, 2-round all-pay pairwise elimination, IPV, full revelation · binary types $v_H=3,\ v_L=1$, equal prior · written 2026-06-17.
Needs internet on first open (KaTeX loads from CDN). All numbers are reproducible from Python/ — see §9.

The one-paragraph takeaway

We feared a functional fixed point: the overlap ODE's coefficients are the Round-2 continuation values $\tilde W$, which are built from the Round-1 strategy — which is what the ODE solves for. It dissolves exactly, for a structural reason. The tournament is pairwise single elimination: you reach the final by beating your single Round-1 rival, and there you meet the winner of the other pair — whose bid is the $\max$ of two independent population draws. So the CDF of your belief about that opponent (their perceived-High probability) is exactly $F(\mu)=G(\mu)^2$, and it is independent of your own bid. Substituting $F=G^2$, the continuation values become explicit functions of the Round-1 CDF $G$ alone, and the overlap collapses to a self-contained ODE in $G$ — no $F$ to guess, no iteration. What remains is elementary: the overlap is two quadratures and a single boundary scalar (the atom mass $G_0$) closes it. No grid, no tail. (See the correction box below: the support is two regions — Low atom + overlap — and the construction is now solved, $G_0=0.2221$.)

Correction (2026-06-22): $b_{lo}=0$, no belief jump, no high-only tail

Two structural claims further down are superseded; the construction is simpler than written, and it is now solved.

  • No gap, no jump: $b_{lo}=0$, $\mu_0=0$. The overlap is flush against the atom (all-pay no-gap: a bid at $b_{lo}>0$ could slide down into an empty $(0,b_{lo})$ at the same win probability $G_0$ and the same continuation, but lower cost — a strict gain, so no $b_{lo}>0$ survives). Low's break-even $b_{lo}=G_0\tilde W_L(\mu_0)=0$ with $G_0>0$ then forces $\tilde W_L(\mu_0)=0$, i.e. $\mu_0=0$. The belief starts at $0$ continuously; it does not jump to a positive $\mu_0$.
  • No high-only tail. A high-only tail needs belief $\equiv1$ on it, hence the overlap reaching $\mu=1$ at its top. But integrating the §7 ODE from $\mu_0=0$, the CDF $G(\mu)$ reaches $1$ before $\mu$ does (numerically verified: $G_0\!=\!0.05\to G\!=\!1$ at $\mu\!\approx\!0.996$; $G_0\!=\!0.3\to$ at $\mu\!\approx\!0.92$), so the belief never reaches $1$ within $G\le1$. Since $\tilde W_H$ decreases in $\mu$, High has no incentive to separate upward anyway. Both types pool all the way to $b_{top}$, where $G=1$ at a belief $\mu_{\max}<1$.

The support is therefore two regions: a pure-Low atom at $0$, then a co-mixing overlap $[0,b_{top}]$ with belief rising $0\to\mu_{\max}$. It closes with a single unknown $G_0$ (the atom mass), pinned by High's normalization $\int_{G_0}^{1}\mu(G)\,dG=\tfrac12$:

$G_0=0.2221,\quad a_L=0.4443,\quad \mu_{\max}=0.9525,\quad b_{top}=0.1746,\quad U_H=2G_0=0.4443,\quad U_L=0.$

Both indifference levels check at both ends: $U_H=2G_0$ at the atom edge equals $\tilde W_H(\mu_{\max})-b_{top}$ at the top (both $0.4443$); $U_L=0$ at the atom equals $\tilde W_L(\mu_{\max})-b_{top}$ at the top (both $0$). 1. Setup & notation

  • Tournament: pairwise single elimination — four players, two Round-1 matches of two. Each match winner advances; the two Round-1 losers are eliminated (no consolation match). The two winners meet in the Round-2 final, so your finalist opponent is the winner of the other pair (§4).
  • Index convention: row 0 = High ($v_H$), row 1 = Low ($v_L$); $v$-grid descending.
  • $\sigma_k(b)$ = Round-1 mixed strategy of type $k$. Population (single-rival) bid CDF $G(b)=\sum_k p_k \!\int_0^b \sigma_k$, density $g(b)=G'(b)$.
  • Belief from a bid: $\mu(b)=\Pr(\text{High}\mid \text{bid}=b)$. Equal prior $p_H=p_L=\tfrac12$ gives the split $$f_H(b)=\frac{g(b)\,\mu(b)}{p_H}=2\,g\mu,\qquad f_L(b)=2\,g\,(1-\mu),$$ where $f_k$ is type $k$'s own bid density.
  • $\tilde W(v,\mu)$ = Round-2 continuation value of advancing as type $v$ when the finalist opponent holds belief $\mu=\Pr(\text{High})$ about you. The bid enters Round 2 only through $\mu$: $V(v,b)=\tilde W(v,\mu(b))$.
  • Round-1 indifference (the project's established condition): an active type $v$ has $$U(v,b)=G(b)\,\tilde W\!\big(v,\mu(b)\big)-b=\text{const}\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \frac{d}{db}\big[G\,\tilde W\big]=1\ \Longleftrightarrow\ g\,\tilde W + G\,\tilde W_\mu\,\mu' = 1. \tag{I}$$

3. The heart: (†) reduces to two quadratures

On the overlap, write (I) for both types. Two linear equations in the two unknowns $g$ and $G\mu'$; Cramer with $D=\tilde W_L\tilde W_H'-\tilde W_H\tilde W_L'$ gives the (†) laws:

$$g=\Phi(\mu)=\frac{\tilde W_H'-\tilde W_L'}{D}\quad(\dagger.\text{a}), \qquad G\,\mu'=\Psi(\mu)=\frac{\tilde W_L-\tilde W_H}{D}\quad(\dagger.\text{b}).$$

Now change the independent variable from $b$ to $\mu$ (monotone on the overlap, since $\mu'=\Psi/G>0$ — proved in Why $\mu(b)$ increases below). Dividing $(\dagger.\text a)$ by $(\dagger.\text b)$ cancels $D$ and the system separates:

$$\boxed{\ \frac{d\ln G}{d\mu}=\kappa(\mu)\equiv\frac{\tilde W_H'-\tilde W_L'}{\tilde W_L-\tilde W_H} \ \Longrightarrow\ G(\mu)=G_0\,\exp\!\Big(\textstyle\int_{\mu_0}^{\mu}\kappa\Big)\ }$$ $$\boxed{\ \frac{db}{d\mu}=\frac{G(\mu)}{\Psi(\mu)} \ \Longrightarrow\ b(\mu)=b_0+\int_{\mu_0}^{\mu}\frac{G(s)}{\Psi(s)}\,ds\ }$$

That is the whole overlap: one quadrature for $G(\mu)$, a second for $b(\mu)$, then read off the density $g=\Phi(\mu)$ and the split $f_H=2g\mu,\ f_L=2g(1-\mu)$. Invert $b(\mu)$ to get $\mu(b)$ on any bid range you like.

(Superseded — there is no tail.) As $\mu\to1$, $\Phi(\mu)\to 1/\tilde W_H(1)$, the density the uniform tail would have. This was read as "the overlap lands on the tail height for free," but the overlap never reaches $\mu=1$ (the CDF is spent at $\mu_{\max}<1$), so this limit is never attained and no tail attaches — see the correction box. The overlap simply ends at $b_{top}$ with $G=1$, $\mu=\mu_{\max}$.

Why $\mu(b)$ increases on the overlap — and why it matters

This monotonicity is not an assumption; it is forced by the two indifference conditions, and it is exactly what makes the change of variable above (and the $F=G^2$ of §4) legitimate. Bayes restatement. For a general prior $p_H\in(0,1)$, $$\mu(b)=\frac{p_H\,f_H(b)}{p_H\,f_H(b)+p_L\,f_L(b)},\qquad \frac{f_H}{f_L}=\frac{p_L}{p_H}\,\frac{\mu}{1-\mu},$$ so $\mu(b)$ increasing $\iff$ the likelihood ratio $f_H/f_L$ increases $\iff$ the two types' bid distributions satisfy MLRP (higher bids carry relatively more High-type mass). The prior is only a constant multiplier $p_L/p_H$ on that ratio: it shifts the level of $\mu$, never whether it rises. (The equal-prior case $p_H=\tfrac12$ collapses this to $\mu=f_H/(f_H+f_L)$ with the split $f_H=2g\mu,\ f_L=2g(1-\mu)$.) The real question is why MLRP holds when both types mix over the same interval.

The one-line argument: $G\cdot\Delta\tilde W$ is constant

On the overlap both types are indifferent, so for the equilibrium payoff constants $U_H,U_L$, $$G(b)\,\tilde W_H(\mu(b))-b=U_H,\qquad G(b)\,\tilde W_L(\mu(b))-b=U_L.$$ Subtract — the $-b$ cancels: $$\boxed{\ G(b)\,\big[\tilde W_H(\mu(b))-\tilde W_L(\mu(b))\big]=U_H-U_L=\text{const}>0.\ }\tag{*}$$ As $b$ rises, $G(b)$ rises (a CDF, with positive density on the overlap), so the value gap $\Delta\tilde W(\mu)\equiv\tilde W_H-\tilde W_L$ must fall. And the gap is decreasing in $\mu$ (closed forms, §7): $$\Delta\tilde W'(\mu)=\tilde W_H'-\tilde W_L'=\underbrace{-2(1-G^2)}_{<0}-\underbrace{\tfrac23 G^2}_{>0} =\tfrac23\big(2G^2-3\big)<0\quad\text{on }G\in(0,1).$$ A decreasing function that must decrease $\Rightarrow$ $\mu$ must increase. Hence $\mu(b)$ rises with $b$.

Equivalently, in differential form: subtracting the two marginal conditions $g\tilde W_v+G\tilde W_v'\mu'=1$ gives $\mu'=-\,g\,\Delta\tilde W\big/\big(G\,(\tilde W_H'-\tilde W_L')\big)>0$.

The economics behind $\Delta\tilde W'<0$. The two types value being perceived strong in opposite directions:

  • High: $\tilde W_H'=-2(1-G^2)<0$ — being thought strong hurts. Standard all-pay logic: the perceived favorite has its rent competed away while the underdog extracts surplus, so High would rather be underestimated.
  • Low: $\tilde W_L'=\tfrac23 G^2>0$ — being thought strong helps. A Low type that looks strong induces the opponent to concede (take the atom); bluffing pays.

So as $\mu$ climbs, High's value drops and Low's rises — they converge and the gap closes. That single-crossing in valuations is what simultaneously sustains the co-mixing and orients the belief upward.

None of this uses the equal prior. Relation $(*)$ is just the difference of the two indifference conditions — no prior appears. And the sign $\Delta\tilde W'<0$ is prior-robust: the prior enters the continuation values only through the equilibrium law $G$ (hence $F=G^2$), never through the functional forms $\tilde W_H'=-2(1-F)\le 0$ and $\tilde W_L'=\tfrac23 F\ge 0$. Since $F$ is a probability, $F\in[0,1]$ for any prior, so those two signs are locked and $\Delta\tilde W'=-2+\tfrac43 F\le-\tfrac23<0$ throughout. A different $p_H$ moves the levels — $\mu_0$, the atom masses, the split $f_H=g\mu/p_H,\ f_L=g(1-\mu)/p_L$, the §6 normalizations — but cannot flip the single-crossing, so $\mu(b)$ stays monotone. (What an extreme prior can change is the regime itself — whether the co-mixing (Low-atom + overlap) structure survives — but within that regime the belief is monotone regardless of $p_H$.)

The equilibrium is non-separating, not "non-monotone." Both types pool over the overlap and there is a concession atom, so the strategy is not a clean separating pure strategy. The only sense in which bidding is "non-monotone" is at the level of realizations: the supports overlap, so a High type can draw a lower bid than a Low type. At the levels that carry economic content the equilibrium is monotone — the posterior $\mu(b)$ is (weakly) increasing ($\mu_0=0$ at the atom, strictly rising on the overlap to $\mu_{\max}<1$ at the top), and the type-conditional bid distributions are FOSD-ordered (High FOSD Low). Pooling supports with different proportions (MLRP) is exactly what lets a non-separating equilibrium carry a monotone belief and FOSD-ordered bids. We therefore reserve "non-monotone" for the bid realizations and call the equilibrium non-separating.

Numerical check of $(*)$. From Table 1, $\Delta\tilde W$ runs $1.901$ at $\mu=0.05$ down to $0.659-0.186=0.473$ at $\mu=0.95$ — a factor $1.901/0.473\approx4.02$. So $(*)$ predicts $G$ rises by the same factor across the overlap; Table 2 has $G(\mu)/G_0=4.023$ at $\mu=0.95$. They agree to rounding — the data really does hold $G\cdot\Delta\tilde W$ constant.

Why $F=G^2$ needs this. $F(\mu)=\Pr(\nu\le\mu)=\Pr(\mu(W)\le\mu)=\Pr\big(W\le b(\mu)\big)=G(b(\mu))^2$ holds only because $\mu(\cdot)$ is invertible — the event $\{\mu(W)\le\mu\}$ collapses to the interval $\{W\le b(\mu)\}$. Without monotonicity that step, and the change of variable above, would both fail.

4. Why there is no fixed point: the belief-about-the-opponent CDF is exactly $G^2$

The continuation value is an average of the Round-2 payoff over the opponent you might face, $$\tilde W(v,\mu)=\mathbb E_{\nu}\big[\pi_2\big(v;\ \mu,\ \nu\big)\big],$$ where $\mu$ is the belief your bid induces (others' belief about you) and $\nu$ is the belief your finalist opponent's bid induces. The fear was that the law of $\nu$ is an endogenous functional of the whole Round-1 strategy $\sigma$ — so $\tilde W$ depends on $\sigma$, whose own ODE (§3) carries $\tilde W$ in its coefficients. The pairwise structure removes the circularity outright.

The finalist opponent is the winner of one independent pair

Four players, two Round-1 matches of two. You advance by beating your single rival — probability $G(b)$, the first power of $G$ in (I). In the final you meet the winner of the other match. That match does not involve you, so its winner's bid $W=\max(b',b'')$ over two i.i.d. population draws is independent of your own bid, with

$$\Pr(W\le t)=G(t)^2.$$

The belief map $\mu(\cdot)$ is the same strictly-increasing function for everyone, so the belief about the opponent, $\nu=\mu(W)$, satisfies, in the belief variable,

$$\boxed{\ F(\mu)\equiv\Pr(\nu\le\mu)=\Pr\big(W\le b(\mu)\big)=G(\mu)^2\ }$$

writing $G(\mu)$ for the population CDF at the bid that induces belief $\mu$. So $F$ is not a free unknown — it is literally the square of the very $G$ the overlap ODE produces.

Two consequences make this the whole game:

  • The average is against a fixed law. Because $W$ is independent of your bid, $\tilde W(v,\mu)$ integrates $\pi_2$ against a law that does not shift when you move your own bid — your bid enters $\tilde W$ only through $\mu$, exactly the structure (I)/(†) assume. (In a single-pool "top-2-of-4" contest the opponent would be "best of the other three given you survived," coupled to your own bid, and $F\ne G^2$; the pairwise bracket is what buys the clean separation.)
  • The loop fuses into one ODE. Substituting $F=G^2$ into the §7 continuation-value formulas turns $\tilde W_H',\tilde W_L'$ into explicit functions of $G$. The Round-1$\leftrightarrow$Round-2 "fixed point" is then just a differential equation in $G$ — solved once, not iterated.

Why substituting $F=G^2$ is legitimate, not circular. Deriving (†) holds $\tilde W(\cdot)$ fixed and asks a player to be indifferent across the beliefs their own bid can reach; $\tilde W'(\mu)$ there is the response of your continuation to your own belief, with the opponents' law frozen (best response to others' strategies). The identity $F=G^2$ is a separate equilibrium-symmetry step: in a symmetric equilibrium the frozen opponent law equals $G^2$ of the same $G$. You never differentiate through your own deviation into $F$. The two steps are logically distinct; the ODE merely bundles them.

Numerical corroboration. The exact statement also shows up in the data. $\tilde W_H(\mu)$ computed from three well-converged profiles (grid M200 gap 0.04, M100 0.035, low-temp 0.02) agree to <1% across all $\mu$ (a deliberately bad gap-0.14 profile drifts only at high $\mu$); $\tilde W_L(\mu)$ agrees to $\sim\!0.001$–$0.003$ absolute. And $F=G^2$ holds on the overlap to max deviation $0.045$ — within the sim's own non-convergence. "$\tilde W$ barely moves" was the symptom; $F=G^2$ is the cause. Economic reading: $\tilde W_H$ falls in $\mu$ (being under-estimated helps High); $\tilde W_L$ rises from $0$ (bluffing pays for Low only once the opponent fears you).

5. The overlap construction, step by step

  1. Obtain $\tilde W_H(\mu),\tilde W_L(\mu)$ in closed form from Round 2 (§7): $\tilde W_H=2-2\mu+2Y$, $\tilde W_L=\tfrac23 Y$ with $Y=\int_0^\mu G^2$ — or the Table-1 data as a stopgap. (Because these depend on $G$, the literal recipe below is the §7 ODE; with empirical $\tilde W$ as a frozen input it is a one-pass approximation.)
  2. Form $\tilde W_H',\tilde W_L'$, then $D$, $\Phi=(\tilde W_H'-\tilde W_L')/D$, $\Psi=(\tilde W_L-\tilde W_H)/D$, $\kappa=\Phi/\Psi$.
  3. Pick the overlap-start belief $\mu_0$ and level $G_0=G(\mu_0)$ (= the atom mass below; see §6). Integrate $K(\mu)=\int_{\mu_0}^\mu\kappa$ ⇒ $G(\mu)=G_0e^{K}$.
  4. Integrate $b(\mu)=b_0+\int_{\mu_0}^\mu G/\Psi$ from $\mu_0=0$ until the CDF is spent, $G=1$, at $\mu=\mu_{\max}<1$. The top $b_{top}=b(\mu_{\max})$ is an output.
  5. Density $g(b)=\Phi(\mu(b))$ on the whole overlap $[0,b_{top}]$; there is no tail (correction box).

6. Gluing — the scalars and the equations that close them

Corrected (2026-06-22). With $b_{lo}=0$ and no tail (correction box), this section's scalar count collapses: the only free scalar is the atom mass $G_0$, pinned by one normalization. The solved values are $G_0=0.2221,\ a_L=0.4443,\ \mu_{\max}=0.9525,\ b_{top}=0.1746,\ U_H=2G_0=0.4443,\ U_L=0$.

Free scalars (small set): the Low atom mass $a_L$ (with $a_H=0$, so $G_0=p_La_L$), the overlap-start belief $\mu_0$ and bid $b_0=b_{lo}$, and the top $b_{top}$. The closing equations:

  • $b_{lo}=0\Rightarrow\mu_0=0$ (not a free scalar). All-pay no-gap forces the overlap flush against the atom, $b_{lo}=0$. Low's break-even $G_0\,\tilde W_L(\mu_0)-b_{lo}=U_L=0$ then reads $G_0\,\tilde W_L(\mu_0)=0$, so $\tilde W_L(\mu_0)=0$, i.e. $\mu_0=0$. The belief starts at $0$ continuously — there is no jump and no bluff-value offset. (The pure-Low atom fixes $U_L=\tfrac12 G_0\,\tilde W_L(0)=0$ as before.)
  • High shuns the atom (a constraint, not an equation). A High type conceding would earn only $\tfrac12 G_0\,\tilde W_H(0)=G_0$ (max belief, tiny win probability), which must not beat its equilibrium payoff: $U_H>G_0$. Verified on M200 ($U_H=0.369>G_0=0.122$, §9) — this is why $a_H=0$ is consistent, not imposed.
  • Both types top out together at $b_{top}$ (no $b_{split}$, no tail). The overlap runs until the CDF is exhausted, $G=1$, at belief $\mu_{\max}=0.9525$ and bid $b_{top}=0.1746$. High's support is $[0,b_{top}]$, Low's is $\{0\}\cup[0,b_{top}]$ (atom plus overlap). No bid is revealed-High.
  • No upward deviation (replaces the old density-continuity-at-$b_{split}$ point). A type bidding above $b_{top}$ already wins with probability $1$ (since $G(b_{top})=1$), so it only adds cost; and any off-path belief there gives High $\tilde W_H\le\tilde W_H(\mu_{\max})$ (decreasing), so even a "revealed-High" reading does not help. Both types strictly prefer $b_{top}$ to anything above. No tail is needed to deter it.
  • Normalization — one independent equation ($a_H=0$). High carries no atom, so its whole unit of mass sits on the overlap: $$\int_{b_{lo}}^{b_{top}}\!\! f_H\,db=2\!\int_{G_0}^{1}\!\mu(G)\,dG=1\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \int_{G_0}^{1}\!\mu(G)\,dG=\tfrac12,$$ which pins $G_0$. Low's normalization $a_L+\int_{b_{lo}}^{b_{top}} f_L\,db=1$ is then automatic: it reduces to $a_L=2G_0$ (using $\int_{G_0}^1(1-\mu)\,dG=\tfrac12-G_0$ and $a_L=2G_0$). So two normalizations collapse to one.
  • Indifference levels are outputs, not extra unknowns: $U_H=2G_0=0.4443$ (evaluate $G\tilde W_H-b$ at the atom edge $\mu=0,b=0$) and $U_L=0$ — both verified to agree with the values obtained by integrating to the top (§correction box).

Count: one free scalar $G_0$, one equation $\int_{G_0}^1\mu\,dG=\tfrac12$. Solved: $G_0=0.2221$. Everything else (the closed-form §3/§7 overlap and the §7 first integral) is explicit; no Mathematica grind and no solver call remain.

7. SOLVED — closed-form $\tilde W$ in $G$, and the overlap ODE

Worked out and verified against the solver (posteriors_to_payoffs) to $\sim10^{-16}$ on 2026-06-17; see Python/_verify_closed_form.py for the checks.

The binary Round-2 payoff to a finalist of value $v$ whose opponent believes it is High with prob $\mu$, and who believes its own opponent is High with prob $\nu$, is

$$\pi_2(\text{High};\mu,\nu)=2\big(1-\min(\mu,\nu)\big),\qquad \pi_2(\text{Low};\mu,\nu)=\tfrac23\,(\mu-\nu)^+,$$

with a concession atom of mass $\tfrac23|\mu-\nu|$ held by the weaker-perceived finalist. (These are the $v_H=3,v_L=1$ numbers; structurally $\pi_2(\text{High};\mu,\nu)=(v_H-v_L)\,\big(1-\min(\mu,\nu)\big)$.)

Average over the belief about the finalist opponent, $\nu$, and apply the envelope theorem. §4 supplied the law of $\nu$ in closed form, $F(\mu)=G(\mu)^2$, so we substitute it on the spot and never carry $F$ as an unknown:

$$\boxed{\ \tilde W_H'(\mu)=-2\big(1-G(\mu)^2\big),\qquad \tilde W_L'(\mu)=\tfrac23\,G(\mu)^2\ }$$

Integrating from $\mu=0$ (where $\tilde W_H=2$, $\tilde W_L=0$), with $Y(\mu)=\int_0^\mu G(s)^2\,ds$,

$$\tilde W_H(\mu)=2-2\mu+2\,Y(\mu),\qquad \tilde W_L(\mu)=\tfrac23\,Y(\mu).$$

Every quantity on the right is the Round-1 CDF $G$ — there is no opponent distribution left to supply. Parameter-free cross-check (holds at every $\mu$ in the data, to $10^{-3}$): $\ \tilde W_H'-3\,\tilde W_L'=-2\big(1-G^2\big)-2G^2=-2=-(v_H-v_L)$.

The (†) coefficients in $G$ — the fixed point closes

Feed these into the §3 reduction. Both pieces of $\kappa=(\tilde W_H'-\tilde W_L')/(\tilde W_L-\tilde W_H)$ carry a common factor $\tfrac23$:

$$\tilde W_H'-\tilde W_L'=\tfrac23\big(2G^2-3\big),\qquad \tilde W_L-\tilde W_H=\tfrac23\big(3(\mu-1)-2Y\big),$$

so the $\tfrac23$ cancels and the two §3 quadratures become a closed system in $(G,Y)$:

$$\boxed{\ \frac{dG}{d\mu}=\frac{G\,(2G^2-3)}{3(\mu-1)-2Y},\qquad \frac{dY}{d\mu}=G^2,\qquad \frac{db}{d\mu}=\frac{2G\big(G^2(\mu-1)-Y\big)}{3(\mu-1)-2Y}\ }$$

A self-contained 2-D ODE in $(G,Y)$ plus the bid quadrature — integrate with NDSolve from the boundary; no solver call, no iteration, no $F$. The density is $g(b)=\Phi=(2G^2-3)\big/\big(2(G^2(\mu-1)-Y)\big)$. On the overlap $G\in(0,1)$ makes both boxed numerators and denominators negative, so $G$ and $b$ both increase in $\mu$, as required.

7b. Arbitrary $v_H,v_L$ — the construction is value-covariant

Re-derived for general binary values on 2026-06-22; verified numerically across $v_H/v_L\in\{3/1,2/1,4/1,3/2,5/1,10/1\}$ and against the scale-invariance $(6,2)=2\times(3,1)$. The $3/1$ numbers of §7–§8 are the special case. Write $\Delta\equiv v_H-v_L$.

The Round-2 payoffs keep their shape; only the constants move. Solving the asymmetric binary all-pay final (your reputation $\mu$, opponent's $\nu$) gives both High payoffs equal to $\Delta(1-\min(\mu,\nu))$ and the stronger-perceived Low a bluff rent $K(\mu-\nu)^+$ with $K=v_L\Delta/v_H$:

$$\pi_2(\text{High};\mu,\nu)=\Delta\big(1-\min(\mu,\nu)\big),\qquad \pi_2(\text{Low};\mu,\nu)=\frac{v_L\Delta}{v_H}\,(\mu-\nu)^+,$$

with a concession atom of mass $\tfrac{\Delta}{v_H}|\mu-\nu|$ held by the weaker-perceived finalist. ($v_H=3,v_L=1$: $\Delta=2$, $K=\tfrac23$, atom $\tfrac23|\mu-\nu|$ — the §7 numbers.) The symmetric case $\mu=\nu=q$ gives $\pi_2(\text{High})=\Delta(1-q)$, $\pi_2(\text{Low})=0$, as a plain all-pay with two-point values must.

Averaging over $\nu\sim F=G^2$ and applying the envelope theorem (exactly as in §7), with $Y(\mu)=\int_0^\mu G^2$:

$$\boxed{\ \tilde W_H'(\mu)=-\Delta\big(1-G^2\big),\quad \tilde W_L'(\mu)=K\,G^2,\qquad \tilde W_H=\Delta(1-\mu+Y),\quad \tilde W_L=K\,Y\ }$$

Parameter-free cross-check: $\tilde W_H'-\tfrac{\Delta}{K}\tilde W_L'=-\Delta$ (at $3/1$, $\tfrac{\Delta}{K}=3$, recovering $\tilde W_H'-3\tilde W_L'=-2$).

The overlap ODE, first integral, and closed form (general $v$)

The §3 reduction now reads, with the common factor cancelling as before,

$$\frac{dG}{d\mu}=\frac{G\,(\Delta G^2-v_H)}{v_H(\mu-1)-\Delta Y},\qquad \frac{dY}{d\mu}=G^2.$$

The §3 relation $G(\tilde W_H-\tilde W_L)=U_H-U_L$ is the first integral. With $\mu_0=0$, $U_L=0$, $U_H=\Delta G_0$ (all from the unchanged $b_{lo}=0$ argument), it gives $G\big[(1-\mu)+\tfrac{\Delta}{v_H}Y\big]=G_0$. Substituting collapses the denominator to $-v_H G_0/G$ and the ODE separates:

$$\frac{dG}{d\mu}=\frac{G^2\,(v_H-\Delta G^2)}{v_H\,G_0} \ \Longrightarrow\ \mu(G)=1-\frac{G_0}{G}+G_0\,s\big[\operatorname{arctanh}(sG)-\operatorname{arctanh}(sG_0)\big], \quad s=\sqrt{\tfrac{\Delta}{v_H}}.$$

10. Cautions & open questions

  • The bottom spike. $\Phi$ at $\mu_0$ is set by $\tilde W_L'(\mu_0^+)$ — get $\tilde W_L$ near zero analytically; do not trust the grid there.
  • Which type holds the atom? (RESOLVED — pure Low.) The concession atom is held only by Low: $a_H=0$, $\mu(0)=0$. Reason: a revealed-Low finalist earns $\tilde W_L(0)=0$, so conceding costs Low nothing in continuation and fixes $U_L=0$; a High type would never accept the atom's tiny win probability when its bids earn $U_H>G_0$. Confirmed on M200 ($a_H=0.0002$, $\mu(0)=0.0009$). The old "High hides low ⇒ High carries the atom" guess belonged to the scrapped non-monotone-belief story; the current construction has a monotone belief, so the lowest bid (the atom) carries the lowest belief, i.e. Low.
  • The two powers of $G$ (resolved). The pairwise structure pins both: you advance by beating your single rival, so the advancement factor in (I) is $G(b)$ (first power); the finalist opponent is the winner of the other pair, so its belief CDF is $F=G^2$ (§4). The first power lives in (I), the square lives in $\tilde W$ — distinct roles, no ambiguity. (A single-pool top-2-of-4 contest would break both; the model is pairwise.)
  • Self-consistency. After solving, recompute $\tilde W$ from the analytic $\sigma$ and confirm it matches Table 1 (§4 predicts it will). If it drifts, iterate once.
  • Generality. Re-run §7 for $(v_L,v_H)=(1,2)$ to confirm the structure is not special to $3/1$ (there the feasibility cap $v_L+d$ starts to bind).
