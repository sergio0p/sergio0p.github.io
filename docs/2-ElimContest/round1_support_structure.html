Round-1 Support Structure

Why IPV gives simple supports — the monotone posterior, the mirror symmetry, and the signaling obstruction

Pedagogical companion to round1_support_structure_handoff.md. 4-player, 2-round all-pay elimination contest; types $v\in\{L,M,H\}=\{1,2,3\}$, uniform prior.

Round 1 looks like an ordinary all-pay contest, but it hides a twist: the prize you win for advancing depends on how you bid, because your bid is a signal of your type. This note explains what that twist does to the bid supports — why the private-values (IPV) structure makes them simple single intervals (unlike Turocy's multi-interval supports), where the analysis gets stuck, and how that obstruction is finally resolved (§6): the continuation is $\sigma$-invariant in three types, and the equilibrium curve is closed-form.

scope of this note

The core notation — $G,\Pi_2,W,p,q,p',U_v,\Psi$ (§1–§2) — is defined for a general number of types $K$. Every panel below carries a scope marker, general K / K=2 / K=3, recording which properties the argument inside actually uses; unmarked prose inherits the scope stated at its section or subsection heading. §4 and §6 work entirely inside the $K{=}3$ model ($v_L,v_M,v_H=1,2,3$, uniform prior), except where a panel is marked otherwise.

resolved K=3

The open gate below — does $\sigma$-invariance survive with three types? — is now closed. In the coordinate $t=G(b)$ the opponent-reputation law is universally $\Psi(t)=t^2$, strategy-free, exactly as $\Psi=G^2$ in binary. The fixed point dissolves and the co-mixing curve solves in closed form (an $\operatorname{arctanh}$, §6), internally verified by $t\cdot D_{HL}\equiv\text{const}$ (with $D_{HL}$ the High-vs-Low continuation gap, defined in §6c).

1. The object: a signaling all-pay auction

general K

Fix a player of type $v$ among $K$ types. Within a group, two players bid in an all-pay auction; the higher bid advances to the final. Write

  • $H_v(b)$ — the CDF of type $v$'s Round-1 bid, with density $h_v(b)$;
  • the chance of winning Round 1 is the rival's bid CDF, $G(b)=\sum_u f_u\,H_u(b)$, where $f_u$ is the prior probability of type $u$; the profile of densities $\sigma=(h_v)_{v=1}^K$ is the Round-1 strategy we solve for;
  • the bid is a signal: winning with bid $b$ induces a posterior over your own type, $$p_v(b)=\frac{f_v\,h_v(b)}{\sum_u f_u\,h_u(b)}\qquad\text{\small(post)}$$ a point in the type simplex $\Delta^{K-1}$ ($K{-}1$ free coordinates, since $\sum_v p_v=1$); K=3 with the uniform prior used from §2 on, $f_v\equiv\tfrac13$ cancels in (post) and the simplex is $\Delta^2$;
  • the continuation value of reaching the final as type $v$ with reputation $p$ (= the posterior belief vector $p=(p_v)_{v=1}^K$ your winning bid induced) is $$W(v,p)=\int \Pi_2\!\big(v;\,p,\,q\big)\,d\Psi(q),\qquad\text{\small(W)}$$ where $q$ is the opponent finalist's reputation, $\Psi$ its distribution, and $\Pi_2$ the Round-2 payoff; K=3 $\Pi_2$ is piecewise-affine over a 10-cell complex (derived in docs/tropical_cell_structure.html / memory three-type-r2-tropical).

The interim Round-1 payoff of bidding $b$ as type $v$ is

$$U_v(b)\;=\;\underbrace{G(b)}_{\text{win prob}}\cdot\underbrace{W\big(v,\,p(b)\big)}_{\text{prize}}\;-\;\underbrace{b}_{\text{all-pay cost}}.\qquad\text{\small(U)}$$
the twist general K

The prize $W(v,p(b))$ depends on your own bid through the belief $p(b)$. This is not a Siegel contest, where the prize is a fixed number. It is a signaling all-pay auction — the genus of Hörner & Sahuguet, Costly Signalling in Auctions (2007). Siegel's clean support theory does not apply off the shelf; the question is whether IPV restores enough structure to recover his conclusions anyway.

2. The indifference conditions

general K

Where a type mixes, every bid in its support must give the same payoff: $U_v(b)\equiv\text{const}$. Differentiating in $b$ splits the marginal incentive into two channels:

$$\underbrace{G'(b)\,W\big(v,p(b)\big)}_{\text{win-probability channel}}\;+\;\underbrace{G(b)\,\nabla_p W\big(v,p(b)\big)\cdot p'(b)}_{\text{signaling channel}}\;=\;1.\qquad\text{\small($\star_v$)}$$

Subtracting one type's condition from another cancels the $1$ and leaves the co-mixing (single-crossing) relations $(\star_{vv'})$ — the difference of $(\star_v)$ and $(\star_{v'})$, one for each active pair:

$$G'(b)\,\big[W(v,p)-W(v',p)\big]\;+\;G(b)\,\big[\nabla_p W(v,p)-\nabla_p W(v',p)\big]\cdot p'(b)\;=\;0.\qquad\text{\small($\star_{vv'}$)}$$

K=2 there is a single pair, and the single-crossing fact is $\nabla_p\big[W(H,p)-W(L,p)\big]<0$ (roadmap §7). K=3 the active pairs are $(\star_{HL})$ and $(\star_{ML})$; the entire §6 solve is these two instances integrated. Everything below is about understanding the two ingredients in $(\star_v)$: the signaling channel (governed by the monotone posterior) and the fact that $W$ is the same unknown as the strategy (the obstruction).

3. The monotone posterior — what IPV buys

main focus K=3, DL run

The deep-learning Round-1 run confirms the posterior is monotone: as the bid rises, $p_H(b)$ climbs $0.05\to0.65$ and $p_L(b)$ falls $0.59\to0.10$ (the middle share $p_M(b)$ pinned by $p_L{+}p_M{+}p_H{=}1$). High bids signal high types. This single fact drives three reductions.

3a. The signaling channel has a fixed sign

general K, specialized to K=2 and K=3 below

Fix a bid $b$ and look at the second term of $(\star_v)$, the signaling channel $$\nabla_p W\big(v,p(b)\big)\cdot p'(b).$$ We want conditions under which this has a sign that does not change as $b$ sweeps the support. Two ingredients enter: the velocity $p'(b)$ of the belief path, and the gradient $\nabla_pW(v,\cdot)$ of the prize. Monotonicity of the posterior constrains the first; the Round-2 economics constrains the second. The argument below is stated for a general number of types $K$, then specialized to $K=2$ (where it degenerates to something already familiar) and to $K=3$ (where it has real content).

The state space, and why its dimension is the whole story

With $K$ types the reputation $p=(p_v)_{v=1}^K$ lives in the simplex $\Delta^{K-1}$, so $\sum_v p_v=1$ and the velocity satisfies $\sum_v p_v'=0$. The tangent space is therefore of dimension $K-1$:

typesreputationtangent spaceunit directions available
$K=2$$p_H$, with $p_L=1-p_H$ eliminated$\dim=1$ — a lineexactly two: $\pm1$
$K=3$$(p_H,p_L)$, with $p_M=1-p_H-p_L$ eliminated$\dim=2$ — a planea whole circle

This dimension count is what separates the two cases below.

what monotonicity actually gives general K

The monotone posterior of §3 says the High share rises and the Low share falls with the bid: $$p_H'(b)>0,\qquad p_L'(b)<0\qquad\text{throughout the co-mixing region.}\tag{M}$$ This is a statement about the signs of the components, i.e. about which orthant the velocity occupies. Equivalently: there is a fixed convex cone $\mathcal{C}$ in the tangent space with $$p'(b)\in\mathcal{C}\quad\text{for every }b.$$ K=3 $\mathcal{C}=(\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{R}_-)\times(\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{R}_+)$, an open quadrant. K=2 only the first inequality survives and $\mathcal{C}=\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{R}_-$, an open half-line. Note what (M) does not say: nothing about the ratio of the components, hence nothing about where inside $\mathcal{C}$ the velocity points.

The principle: two cones in duality

general K

Write the channel as an inner product in the tangent space and recall the definition of the polar (dual) cone, $$\mathcal{C}^{\circ}\;=\;\{z:\ \langle z,x\rangle\le 0\ \ \text{for all }x\in\mathcal{C}\}.$$

the correct principle — any $K$ $$\left.\begin{aligned}p'(b)&\in\mathcal{C}\\ \nabla_pW(v,p(b))&\in\operatorname{int}\mathcal{C}^{\circ}\end{aligned}\right\}\ \text{for all }b \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \nabla_pW(v,p(b))\cdot p'(b)<0\ \text{ for all }b,$$

and symmetrically $\nabla_pW\in\operatorname{int}(-\mathcal{C}^{\circ})$ gives a strictly positive channel — immediate from the definition of the polar cone. The hypotheses are membership in the two orthants only: no constancy of the direction $p'/|p'|$, no magnitude bound on either vector. Smoothness of $W$ (§3b) is what makes $\nabla_pW$ exist so that it can be placed in a cone at all.

Specialization I: $K=2$ — the degenerate case

The tangent space is a line, so $p'(b)$ and $\nabla_pW(v,p)$ are scalars and the channel is a product of two numbers, $$\nabla_pW\big(v,p(b)\big)\cdot p'(b),\qquad p'(b)>0 .$$ Its sign is simply the sign of $\nabla_pW(v,p)$, and the cone condition $\nabla_pW\in\operatorname{int}\mathcal{C}^\circ=\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{R}_+$ is the statement $\nabla_pW(v,p)<0$. The solved binary case supplies both signs explicitly (roadmap §7, with $\Psi=G^2$ and $\Delta=v_H-v_L$):

$$\nabla_pW(H,p)=-\Delta\big(1-\Psi(p_H)\big)\;\le\;0,\qquad\qquad \nabla_pW(L,p)=\frac{v_L\Delta}{v_H}\,\Psi(p_H)\;\ge\;0 .$$

(The first is the identity quoted in §3b, at $(v_H,v_L)=(3,1)$.) High negative, Low positive — the fixed-sign channel, obtained with no cone machinery because in one dimension there is nothing for the machinery to do. Both types are extreme when $K=2$, so the argument covers the whole type space and the binary story is complete.

Specialization II: $K=3$ — where the content is

Now the tangent space is a plane; write $\nabla_pW(v,p)=\big(\partial W/\partial p_H,\ \partial W/\partial p_L\big)$ in the coordinates $(p_H,p_L)$. The cone $\mathcal{C}$ is the quadrant $(\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{R}_-)\times(\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{R}_+)$, and its polar is $$\mathcal{C}^{\circ}=\mathbb{R}_{-}\times\mathbb{R}_{+}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{\partial W}{\partial p_H}\le0,\ \ \frac{\partial W}{\partial p_L}\ge0 .$$ The gradient is available in closed form. The derivation is six short steps, and worth following in full: each step cashes in a result established earlier in this note.

  1. From the opponent's reputation to the opponent's position. Along the equilibrium the opponent finalist's reputation is a function of their bid quantile, $q=P(s)$ with $s\in[0,1]$, and by §6a their position carries density $2s\,ds$. So $(W)$ reads $$W(v,p)=\int_0^1 \Pi_2\big(v;\,p,\,P(s)\big)\,2s\,ds .$$ Here $p$ is free: $\Pi_2(v;p,q)$ is defined for any pair of reputations and $\Psi$ is a fixed measure over the opponent's $q$, so $W(v,\cdot)$ is a genuine function on the simplex and $\nabla_p$ has something to act on.
  2. "Which cell" becomes "which side of $s=t$". The integrand changes formula as $s$ sweeps, but only once. The split has exactly two pieces — not three or more — because the equilibrium holds both finalists' reputations in the interior of belief space, away from the extreme beliefs that the other Round-2 cells require, so the pair $\big(p,P(s)\big)$ only ever falls in cell 1 or cell 2 (the DL run confirms this; had the curve entered a third cell, the split below would carry a third piece). There is thus a single boundary to cross; by §3 reputation is monotone in position, so own reputation ahead of the opponent's is the same event as own position ahead of theirs. Evaluating at $p=P(t)$, that boundary is $s=t$ — opponent behind ($s<t$, own ahead, cell 1) versus opponent ahead ($s>t$, own behind, cell 2): $$W\big(v,P(t)\big)=\int_0^{t}\Pi_2^{\text{ahead}}\big(v;p,P(s)\big)\,2s\,ds\;+\;\int_{t}^{1}\Pi_2^{\text{behind}}\big(v;p,P(s)\big)\,2s\,ds .$$
  3. Differentiate — Leibniz gives an interior part and a boundary part. Moving $p$ moves the integrands and moves the split point, since a different own reputation ties a different opponent; write that point $s^\ast(p)$. Then $$\nabla_pW=\int_0^{s^\ast}\!\!\nabla_p\Pi_2^{\text{ahead}}\,2s\,ds\;+\int_{s^\ast}^{1}\!\!\nabla_p\Pi_2^{\text{behind}}\,2s\,ds\;+\;\Big[\Pi_2^{\text{ahead}}-\Pi_2^{\text{behind}}\Big]_{s=s^\ast}\!\!2s^\ast\,\nabla_p s^\ast .$$
  4. The boundary term vanishes. The bracket is zero, because the two affine forms agree on the diagonal — so nothing need be known about $\nabla_ps^\ast$, which is what makes the computation possible at all. Setting $q=p$ in the §6b forms:
    type $v$$\Pi_2^{\text{ahead}}$ at $q=p$$\Pi_2^{\text{behind}}$ at $q=p$
    High$2-\tfrac{p_H}{3}-p_M-\tfrac{5p_H}{3}=2-p_M-2p_H$$2-p_M-\tfrac{5p_H}{3}-\tfrac{p_H}{3}=2-p_M-2p_H$
    Mid$1+\tfrac{p_H}{3}-p_M-\tfrac{4p_H}{3}=1-p_M-p_H$$1-p_M-\tfrac{2p_H}{3}-\tfrac{p_H}{3}=1-p_M-p_H$
    Low$\tfrac12(p_M-p_M)+\tfrac23(p_H-p_H)=0$$0$
    Equal in every row. $\Pi_2$ has a kink at the diagonal, not a jump — the slope changes, the value does not — so integrating across it costs nothing. This is §3b in its most concrete form, and it is the step the whole computation rests on.
  5. The two cell gradients are constants, so they leave the integrals. On each cell $\Pi_2$ is affine in the own reputation (§6b), so $\nabla_p\Pi_2^{\text{ahead}}(v;\cdot)$ depends on neither $p$ nor $q$, hence not on $s$. For High, in the own coordinates $(p_M,p_H)$, it is $\big(0,-\tfrac13\big)$, against $\big(-1,-\tfrac53\big)$ when behind. (This is also what licenses the "$\cdot$" in $\Pi_2^{\text{ahead}}(v;\cdot)$: the suppressed arguments cannot matter.)
  6. Evaluate the two weights. $\int_0^{t}2s\,ds=t^2=\Psi(t)$ and $\int_{t}^{1}2s\,ds=1-t^2$.
$$\nabla_p W(v,p)\;=\;t^2\,\nabla_p \Pi_2^{\text{ahead}}(v;\cdot)\;+\;(1-t^2)\,\nabla_p \Pi_2^{\text{behind}}(v;\cdot).\tag{$\nabla$}$$

In words: the marginal value of reputation is a probability-weighted average of the two cells' marginal values — with probability $\Psi(t)=t^2$ the opponent is behind you and cell 1's slope applies, with probability $1-t^2$ they are ahead and cell 2's applies. The cell coefficients are constants, so this is a convex combination of two fixed vectors with weight $t^2$, and no unknown integrals survive: the opponent's curve enters through the weight only, never through the slopes. (Accordingly the moments $A_2,A_3,H_3$ of §6c, which are built from that curve, are invisible to $\nabla_p$.)

general K Steps 3–6 never used the type count. With cells $j=1,\dots,J$ visited and every cell boundary a threshold in position, the same argument gives $\nabla_pW(v,p)=\sum_j\Psi(\text{cell }j)\,\nabla_p\Pi_2^{(j)}(v;\cdot)$ — a $\Psi$-weighted average of the cells' gradients. $(\nabla)$ is that formula at $J=2$.

Reading the coefficients off the §6b table in the shares $(m,h)=(p_M,p_H)$ and converting to $(p_H,p_L)$ via $\partial/\partial p_H=\partial/\partial h-\partial/\partial m$ and $\partial/\partial p_L=-\partial/\partial m$ (each at the other coordinate fixed):

type $v$$\partial W/\partial p_H$$\partial W/\partial p_L$cone membership on $t\in(0,1)$

Worked example, High type. From §6b, $\Pi_2^{\text{ahead}}(H;\cdot)=2-\tfrac h3-mp-\tfrac{5hp}{3}$ has own-gradient $(\partial/\partial m,\partial/\partial h)=(0,-\tfrac13)$, and $\Pi_2^{\text{behind}}(H;\cdot)=2-m-\tfrac{5h}{3}-\tfrac{hp}{3}$ has own-gradient $(-1,-\tfrac53)$. By $(\nabla)$, $\partial W/\partial m=-(1-t^2)$ and $\partial W/\partial h=-\tfrac{t^2}{3}-\tfrac53(1-t^2)=-\tfrac{5-4t^2}{3}$. Converting, $\partial W/\partial p_H=\partial W/\partial h-\partial W/\partial m=-\tfrac{5-4t^2}{3}+(1-t^2)=\tfrac{t^2-2}{3}$ and $\partial W/\partial p_L=-\partial W/\partial m=1-t^2$. Consistency check: subtracting the Low row reproduces the §6c coefficients of $D_{HL}$ exactly — $\partial D_{HL}/\partial m=-1+\tfrac{t^2}{2}$ and $\partial D_{HL}/\partial h=-\tfrac53+\tfrac{2t^2}{3}$, matching the terms $-m+\tfrac m2t^2$ and $-\tfrac{5h}{3}+\tfrac{2h}{3}t^2$. The same check passes for $D_{ML}$.

  • High — strictly negative, uniformly. $\partial W/\partial p_H=\tfrac{t^2-2}{3}\in[-\tfrac23,-\tfrac13]$ never vanishes, and $\partial W/\partial p_L=1-t^2\ge0$. Both terms of the inner product are $\le0$ with the first strict. The effects reinforce: bidding higher makes you look more High (which hurts) and less Low (which also hurts, since looking Low is what a High type wants). Compare $K=2$, where $\nabla_pW(H,p)=-\Delta(1-\Psi)$: same sign, and only this first column is visible there.
  • Low — strictly positive. $\partial W/\partial p_H=\tfrac{t^2}{6}>0$ and $\partial W/\partial p_L=-\tfrac{t^2}{2}<0$, the exact mirror pattern; both terms positive, again reinforcing. Compare $K=2$, where $\nabla_pW(L,p)=\tfrac{v_L\Delta}{v_H}\Psi\ge0$.
  • Mid — genuinely ambiguous, with no binary analogue. $\partial W/\partial p_H=\tfrac13>0$ but $\partial W/\partial p_L=1-t^2\ge0$ as well, so the gradient lies in neither $\mathcal{C}^\circ$ nor $-\mathcal{C}^\circ$. The channel is $\tfrac13p_H'+(1-t^2)p_L'$ — a positive term plus a negative one, whose sign turns on the ratio $|p_L'|/p_H'$ and on $t$. No sign-chasing rescues this; one needs the magnitudes, i.e. the solved curve of §6. This is the first structural feature with no counterpart at $K=2$, where every type is extreme.
summary of the logic K=2 vs K=3

Monotonicity (M) confines the velocity to a fixed cone $\mathcal{C}$. The Round-2 economics of §3c confines the gradient to the polar cone $\mathcal{C}^\circ$ (High) or to $-\mathcal{C}^\circ$ (Low). Duality of the two cones fixes the sign of their pairing at every bid, with no reference to a direction and no magnitudes. At $K=2$ the cones are half-lines and the whole thing collapses to $\nabla_pW(H,p)<0$, $\nabla_pW(L,p)>0$; at $K=3$ it delivers the two extreme types and stops.

What this does and does not deliver. A constant-sign channel for the extreme types is the single-crossing input an interval-support argument wants, and it is consistent with the finding (§6e) that each support is one connected interval $[0,\beta_v]$. It is not by itself a proof that the supports are intervals: for $K=3$ that would additionally require a verdict for Mid, which the cone argument does not supply. For $K=2$, where both types are extreme, the corresponding gap does not arise — another reason the binary case reads as easier than it is. Treat "supports are single intervals" as strongly indicated by the fixed-sign channel at the extremes together with the solved curve, not as established by §3a alone.

3b. The prize is smooth, even though $\Pi_2$ is kinked

The Round-2 payoff $\Pi_2$ is full of kinks (the $\min(p_H,q_H)$, the $(p_H-q_H)^+$). One might fear those kinks make the indifference ODE ill-posed. They do not — because the prize is an average:

key general K $$W(v,p)=\int \Pi_2(v;p,q)\,d\Psi(q)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad W(v,\cdot)\ \text{is}\ C^1,\quad \nabla_p W=\Psi\big|_{\text{kink locus}}.$$

Integrating a kinked function against an atomless $\Psi$ smooths the kinks away: the kink locus (the set of opponent reputations $q$ where $\Pi_2$ bends — e.g. the diagonal $p=q$) has $\Psi$-measure zero, so it contributes nothing to the average; the kink cancels and only its slope survives, which is the content of $\nabla_p W=\Psi|_{\text{kink locus}}$ (an envelope / Leibniz computation). This matches the $K=2$ identity $\nabla_pW(H,p)=-2\big(1-\Psi(p_H)\big)$ with $\Psi=G^2$. The kinks of $\Pi_2$ never reach the bidder. So $(\star_v)$ is a genuine, well-posed first-order ODE.

3c. The sign is type-dependent — the engine of "hide and bluff"

general K mechanism (extreme types); illustrated at K=3

Here the signaling channel earns its name. Being perceived as High hurts the High type ($\partial W/\partial p_H<0$ at $v=H$): a strong player would rather be underestimated, so the finalist across the table under-prepares. Therefore:

TypeWin-prob channel as $b\uparrow$Signaling channel as $b\uparrow$Behavior
Highprize-weight rises ($G'\!>0$)prize falls (being seen High hurts)channels conflict → hides (bids low)
Lowprize-weight risesprize rises (being seen High helps)channels align → bluffs (bids high)

This is the same mechanism that makes binary bidding non-monotone in realizations, now spread across three types.

why supports are single intervals (the Siegel point) general K argument

Monotone $p(b)$ + atomless $\Psi$ make the $(\star_v)$ coefficients monotone and single-crossing in $v$ — precisely the hypothesis an interval-support argument needs. So each type's support is one connected interval $[0,\beta_v]$ in bid space, with a common floor $b_{lo}=0$. K=3, DL nested ceilings $\beta_H>\beta_M>\beta_L$ (approximate: $0.69>0.62>0.54$; atoms at $0$ grow as type falls: Low $0.49$, Mid $0.30$, High $\approx0$). Symbol caution: §6 works in the quantile coordinate $t=G(b)$ and writes $\beta_L=0.9746$ for the same Low-exit event — the $t$-coordinate of this bid ceiling, not a different number. Bid-space $\beta_L\approx0.54$ and $t$-space $\beta_L=0.9746$ are one exit point in two coordinates.

Contrast with Turocy. Turocy's rich multi-interval / gapped supports need a non-monotone or non-single-crossing prize (correlation / common value). IPV with atomless $\Psi$ rules that out. IPV is exactly what collapses the support to a single interval.

4. The mirror symmetry

K=3 — uses the specific 10-cell Round-2 complex and its cell forms

needed for the solve

The two groups A and B are interchangeable. Let $\tau$ be the involution that swaps the two finalist roles (own $\leftrightarrow$ opponent, A $\leftrightarrow$ B). On Round-2 regime words it flips every $\mathtt{A}\leftrightarrow\mathtt{B}$.

Under $\tau$, the two Round-2 cells the equilibrium actually visits swap into each other:

cell 1  [B,A,B,A,A]   $\xrightarrow{\ \tau\ }$   cell 2  [A,B,A,B,B]

The diagonal $p=q$ (own reputation equals opponent reputation) is the fixed locus of $\tau$. In a symmetric equilibrium, both finalists draw their reputation from the same distribution $\Psi$, so the whole configuration is invariant under $\tau$. Three consequences:

  • the equilibrium payoff is invariant under own $\leftrightarrow$ opponent;
  • cells 1 and 2 carry the same payoff form, related by the swap:
    cell$\Pi_2(H;p,q)$  (own aggregate reputation $A2{=}p_M{+}p_H$, $A3{=}p_H$; opponent $B2{=}q_M{+}q_H$, $B3{=}q_H$. In the §6b shares $(m,h)/(mp,hp)$: $A2{=}m{+}h$, $A3{=}h$, $B2{=}mp{+}hp$, $B3{=}hp$ — so this row equals the §6b cell-1/cell-2 High entry.)
    cell 1$2 - B2 - \tfrac13 A3 - \tfrac23 B3$
    cell 2$2 - A2 - \tfrac23 A3 - \tfrac13 B3$
  • the DL run visits only these two cells (~41% each) plus the diagonal wall (~14%); the lopsided corner cells 9,10 are never visited — reputations never get extreme.

Why it matters for solving. We can solve on one side of the diagonal and reflect — gluing cell 1 and cell 2 across $p=q$, exactly as the binary case glued its two cells across $p=q$. This collapses the Round-1 state from "full 2-D reputation per finalist over 10 cells" to a 2-piece, mirror-symmetric payoff in one own-vs-opponent variable. It is the single biggest simplification available — and it is legitimate only because the equilibrium is symmetric and stays off the corners.

5. The obstruction

main focus general K

The trouble is not the kinks (smoothed away, §3b) and not monotonicity (clean, §3a). It is that the prize and the belief are the same unknown.

Look again at what the prize is built from:

$$p_v(b)=\frac{f_v\,h_v(b)}{\sum_u f_u\,h_u(b)}\quad\text{and}\quad \Psi=\big(\text{pushforward of }G\text{ through }p(\cdot)\big).$$

Both the posterior $p$ and the opponent-reputation law $\Psi$ are made out of the very densities $h_v$ that the indifference ODE $(\star_v)$ is trying to solve for — and the co-mixing relations $(\star_{vv'})$ inherit the same circularity. So $(\star_v)$, and with it $(\star_{vv'})$, is a fixed-point ODE, not a quadrature: you cannot integrate $W$ as if it were an exogenous prize, because $W$ is defined through the unknown strategy. This circularity is the structural signature of a signaling equilibrium: the interim payoff $G(b)\,W(v,p(b))-b$ looks like an ordinary all-pay objective, but the prize $W$ silently depends on the very strategy being chosen.

How the binary case escaped

K=2

In the binary case the continuation turned out to be $\sigma$-invariant: $\Psi=G^2$ pinned $W$ independently of the strategy. The fixed point dissolved, leaving the §7 separable ODE with a first integral and a single scalar

$$G_0=0.2221\quad\text{pinned by}\quad \int_{G_0}^{1}p_H\,dG=\tfrac12,\qquad U_H=2G_0,\quad U_L=0.$$

The entire solvability of the binary model rested on that one fact.

the gate — now resolved (§6) K=3

Does $\sigma$-invariance survive with three types? The 2-cell reduction (§4) makes this checkable: with only cells 1,2 active, $W(v,p)$ is a 2-piece affine functional of $p$, and $\nabla_p W$ enters $(\star_v)$ only through $\Psi$ evaluated at the two cell boundaries. So the whole question reduces to: does the cell-1/cell-2 affine $W$, pushed through the belief map, depend on $\sigma$ only via a scalar (as $G^2$ did)?

Answer: yes — $\sigma$-invariant. In $t=G(b)$, $\Psi(t)=t^2$ universally, so the supports close in the binary form: a few scalars pinned by moment conditions like $\int p\,d\Psi=\text{prior}$, with $[0,\beta_v]$ in closed form. The full curve is in §6.

6. Resolution — $\sigma$-invariance holds, and the curve is closed-form

K=3 — $(v_L,v_M,v_H)=(1,2,3)$, uniform prior — except where marked otherwise

The concrete next step — set up $(\star_v)$ in the state $t=G(b)$, substitute the cell-1/cell-2 forms and $\Psi$, and test for a first integral — was carried out in Wolfram. The verdict is affirmative.

6a. State, reputation curve, and the universal $\Psi$

Work in $t=G(b)\in[0,1]$ (own win-prob / bid quantile); the population position is $t\sim\text{Uniform}[0,1]$, with the atom spread on $[0,t_0]$, $t_0=G(0)$. Let $m(t),h(t)$ be the Mid/High reputation shares at position $t$ (so $\text{Low}=1-m-h$). The opponent-finalist law is

the $\sigma$-invariance general K — independent of type count $$\Psi(t)=t^2\quad\text{universally — strategy-free, exactly as }\Psi=G^2\text{ in binary.}\qquad\text{\small($\Psi$)}$$

Why $t^2$: a finalist is a Round-1 winner, so the opponent finalist's position is the population position $s\sim\text{Uniform}[0,1]$ size-biased by its win probability $G=s$ (one rival, 2-player group) — density $2s$, hence CDF $\Psi(t)=\int_0^t 2s\,ds=t^2$. The derivation uses only the 2-player group-vs-group bracket structure — not $\sigma$, not the type count $K$ — so $W$ is pinned by the curve alone and the Round-1↔Round-2 fixed point dissolves for any $K$.

6c. Continuation differences and the first integral

Each difference $D_{vL}$ below is $W(v,p)-W(L,p)$ with the continuation $W=\int\Pi_2\,d\Psi$ (eq. (W)) split at the own position $t$: opponent behind ($s<t$, cell 1) vs. opponent ahead ($s>t$, cell 2), integrated against $d\Psi=2s\,ds$. The opponent-behind moments are $A_2(t)=\int_0^t 2s\,m\,ds$, $A_3(t)=\int_0^t 2s\,h\,ds$ (both including the atom mass at $t_0$) and $H_3=A_3(1)=\mathbb{E}_{\text{finalist}}[h]$ (integral terms verified to $\sim10^{-15}$):

$$\begin{aligned} W(H)-W(L)&=:D_{HL}=2-m-\tfrac{5h}{3}+\big(\tfrac{m}{2}+\tfrac{2h}{3}\big)t^2-\tfrac{A_2}{2}-\tfrac{2A_3}{3}-\tfrac{H_3}{3},\\[2pt] W(M)-W(L)&=:D_{ML}=1-m-\tfrac{2h}{3}+\big(\tfrac{m}{2}+\tfrac{h}{3}\big)t^2-\tfrac{A_2}{2}-\tfrac{A_3}{3}-\tfrac{H_3}{3}. \end{aligned}$$

Co-mixing of both active types means $t\,D_{HL}=k_1$ and $t\,D_{ML}=k_2$ are constant ($k_1=U_H-U_L$, $k_2=U_M-U_L$) — these are the first integrals of $(\star_{HL})$ and $(\star_{ML})$. Differentiating both in $t$ makes the integral terms $A_2,A_3,H_3$ cancel; subtracting the two eliminates $m$ (this is the $m$-free difference $D_{HM}$ of §6f, which also gives the floor relation $k_1-k_2=t_0$), leaving a clean ODE in $h$ alone:

the closed-form curve $$k_1-k_2=t_0\quad(\text{floor relation}),\qquad h'(t)=\frac{3\,t_0}{t^2\,(3-t^2)},$$ $$\boxed{\,h(t)=1-\frac{t_0}{t}+\frac{t_0}{\sqrt3}\Big(\operatorname{arctanh}\tfrac{t}{\sqrt3}-\operatorname{arctanh}\tfrac{t_0}{\sqrt3}\Big),\quad h(t_0)=0.\,}$$

The $\sqrt3=\sqrt{v_H}$ turns the first integral into an $\operatorname{arctanh}$ — the genuinely new feature versus binary's plain rational; the high valuation enters transcendentally. $m(t)$ follows from a second elementary quadrature (the $C^1$ condition).

6f. The $[\beta_L,1]$ sliver — closed exactly

On $(\beta_L,1]$ Low has exited, so only High and Mid co-mix and the population constraint becomes $m=1-h$ (Low $=0$). The decisive simplification is that the H–M difference

$m$-free difference $$D_{HM}=D_{HL}-D_{ML}=1-h+\tfrac{h}{3}t^2-\tfrac{A_3}{3}$$

has its $m$ and $A_2$ terms cancel — it depends on $h$ alone. So the H–M co-mixing relation $(\star_{HM})=(\star_{HL})-(\star_{ML})$, in integrated form $t\,D_{HM}=k_1-k_2=t_0$, is an ODE in $h$ only, identical in both regions. Hence $h(t)$ is one global $\operatorname{arctanh}$ curve, $C^1$ across $\beta_L$; $t_0$ and $H_3$ (which see only $h$) are exact and unchanged. Only $m(t)$ switches — to $1-h$ — on the sliver.
