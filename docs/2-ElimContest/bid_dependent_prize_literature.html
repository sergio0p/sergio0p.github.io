All-pay auctions where the value of winning depends on the bid

Reading list for the elimination-contest project · grouped by the economic channel that makes the prize bid-dependent · compiled 2026-06-22. Where the value of advancing/winning is a function of the player's own type and bid, as in our Round-1 payoff $\tilde W(v,\mu(b))$.

The three channels. "Value of winning depends on the bid" is not one feature but three distinct mechanisms. Categories 1 and 2 give own-bid dependence (the feature in our model); category 3 gives rival-bid dependence. Our model is unusual in combining 2 (own bid signals type across rounds) with 3 (rival's bid carries interdependent-value information inside Round 2).

0. The starting reference (not actually this feature)

  • Siegel, R. (2009). "All-Pay Contests." Econometrica 77(1), 71–92. all-pay
    Complete information. The general function of the action is the cost $c_i(\cdot)$, not the prize: each player has a fixed prize valuation $v_i^{w}$. So the value of winning is not bid-dependent here — listed to mark the boundary of what Siegel's framework does and does not cover.
  • Siegel, R. (2010). "Asymmetric Contests with Conditional Investments." American Economic Review 100(5), 2230–2260.
    Relaxes when the bid is paid (conditional on winning/losing), still complete info, still a fixed prize.

1. Mechanical / technological — the prize is literally a function of the (winning) bid

Closest literal match: reward to the winner is an increasing function of effort/bid, with private types.

  • Kaplan, T., Luski, I., Sela, A. & Wettstein, D. (2002). "All-Pay Auctions with Variable Rewards." Journal of Industrial Economics 50(4), 417–430. incomplete info all-pay verify cite
    Private types; the winner's reward increases in the winning bid. The most literal "value of winning = function of type and bid" paper. Exact year/journal to confirm before citing.
  • Chung, T.-Y. (1996). "Maximizing-Output Contests." (rent-seeking with effort-dependent prize.) verify cite
    And the broader rent-seeking-with-endogenous-rent strand — mostly complete information, included as background for "prize grows with aggregate effort."

2. Signaling — the bid is observed and changes a later-stage payoff

The channel our model uses: the Round-1 bid signals type, moving the belief $\mu(b)$ a future opponent holds, hence the continuation value of advancing.

  • Goeree, J. K. (2003). "Bidding for the Future: Signaling in Auctions with an Aftermarket." Journal of Economic Theory 108(2), 345–364. incomplete info winner-pay
    The winning bid signals the winner's type to an aftermarket, so the value of winning is endogenous to the bid. Conceptually the tightest analogue to our $\tilde W(v,\mu(b))$ — first-price rather than all-pay.
  • Haile, P. A. (2003). "Auctions with Private Uncertainty and Resale Opportunities." Journal of Economic Theory 108(1), 72–110. incomplete info winner-pay
    Value of winning depends on bids via the resale outcome.
  • Garratt, R. & Tröger, T. (2006). "Speculation in Standard Auctions with Resale." Econometrica 74(3), 753–769. incomplete info winner-pay
  • Hafalir, I. & Krishna, V. (2008). "Asymmetric Auctions with Resale." American Economic Review 98(1), 87–112. incomplete info winner-pay
    Resale makes the prize endogenous to the bids; canonical asymmetric treatment.
  • Zhang, J. & Wang, R. (2009). "The Role of Information Revelation in Elimination Contests." The Economic Journal 119(536), 613–641. incomplete info all-pay
    Our direct lineage: full revelation across rounds makes the value of advancing bid-dependent. (No symmetric separating equilibrium under full revelation — the result our project builds on.)
  • Moldovanu, B. & Sela, A. (2006). "Contest Architecture." Journal of Economic Theory 126(1), 70–96. incomplete info all-pay
    Multi-stage / split contests with private values — but typically without the inter-round bid signaling that drives the non-monotone bidding in our non-separating equilibrium.
  • Fu, Q. & Lu, J. (2012). "The Optimal Multi-Stage Contest." Economic Theory 51(2), 351–382. incomplete info all-pay verify cite

3. Interdependent / common values — value depends on signals correlated with the rival's bid

Here the value of winning depends on the rival's type, surfacing through the rival's bid in equilibrium — not the player's own bid. This is the Round-2 structure we cross-check against.

  • Krishna, V. & Morgan, J. (1997). "An Analysis of the War of Attrition and the All-Pay Auction." Journal of Economic Theory 72(2), 343–362. incomplete info all-pay
    Affiliated / interdependent values in the all-pay auction and war of attrition.
  • Rentschler, L. & Turocy, T. L. (2016). "Two-Bidder All-Pay Auctions with Interdependent Valuations, Including the Highly Competitive Case." Journal of Economic Theory 163, 435–466. incomplete info all-pay
    Our References/Turocy2015.pdf. The Round-2 game of our model is an instance; we solve it closed-form and use Turocy only as a cross-check.

Where our novelty plausibly sits. Categories 2 (own bid signals type across rounds) and 3 (rival's bid carries interdependent-value information inside Round 2) are combined in an all-pay elimination contest with full revelation. No single paper above appears to do both — worth a targeted search before claiming priority. Companion: docs/analytic_solution_roadmap.html.
