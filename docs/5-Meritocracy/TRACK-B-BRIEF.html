1.1 Point-by-point (brief items a–e)

(c) The inequality chain (lines 225–229). Correct, including the top type \(v_L = \bar v_L\): the chain

$$\frac{\partial U_L}{\partial b} \;<\; \frac{h}{h-1} \cdot \frac{1-\lambda}{\bar v_L} \cdot v_L - 1 \;\le\; \frac{h}{h-1}(1-\lambda) - 1 \;<\; 0$$

is strict because Definition 1's \(\sup\) inequality is strict. Edge cases queried by the brief:

1.3 Replacement proof (new material — integrate, don't differentiate)

Proposed restatement. Let \(k = 1\), \(F_H\) continuous, \(V_H\) \(\lambda\)-intimidate \(V_L\), and \(h\lambda \ge 1\). Then [H plays \(b_H(v) = \int_{\underline v_H}^{v} s\, dF_H^{h-1}(s)\); every L bids 0 regardless of type] is an equilibrium.

Proof. H side: as in 1.1(d)(i)–(ii). L side: \(b_H\) is continuous, strictly increasing, \(b_H(\underline v_H) = 0\), so any deviation \(b \in (0, \bar b_H]\) equals \(b_H(z)\) for some \(z\) with \(F_H(z) > 0\). For any \(v_L \le \bar v_L\), writing \(F = F_H\),

$$\begin{aligned} U_L(b_H(z) \mid v_L) &= F(z)^h v_L - \int_{\underline v_H}^{z} s\, dF^{h-1}(s) \\[2pt] &< F(z)^h v_L - \frac{\bar v_L}{1-\lambda} \int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F(s)\, dF^{h-1}(s) \\[2pt] &= F(z)^h v_L - \frac{\bar v_L}{1-\lambda} \cdot \frac{h-1}{h}\, F(z)^h \\[2pt] &= F(z)^h \left[\, v_L - \frac{(h-1)\,\bar v_L}{(1-\lambda)\,h} \right] \;\le\; F(z)^h\, \bar v_L \left[\, 1 - \frac{h-1}{(1-\lambda)\,h} \right] \;\le\; 0, \end{aligned}$$

using: (first inequality) intimidation pointwise inside the Stieltjes integral — \(s > F(s) \cdot \bar v_L/(1-\lambda)\) for all \(s > 0\), strict on the full \(F^{h-1}\)-mass of \([\underline v_H, z]\); (second equality) \(\int F\, dF^{h-1} = (h-1)\int F^{h-1}\, dF = \frac{h-1}{h} F^h\) for continuous \(F\); (last) \(h\lambda \ge 1 \iff \frac{h-1}{(1-\lambda)h} \ge 1\). Deviations \(b > \bar b_H\) are dominated by \(\bar b_H\); \(b = 0\) yields exactly \(0 > U_L(b)\) for every \(b > 0\) (no atom of \(G_H\) at 0, so the \(b = 0\) win probability is 0). ∎

Properties: no density, no differentiability, no "almost everywhere", all deviation points covered, strict even for the top type at \(h\lambda = 1\). Three lines longer than the current proof and strictly weaker hypotheses. Sharpness: both hypotheses bind. With \(F_H\) uniform on \([0, V]\), \(U_L(b_H(z) \mid v_L) = z^h V^{1-h}\!\left(\frac{v_L}{V} - \frac{h-1}{h}\right)\) exactly; as \(\lambda \downarrow 1/h\) and \(\bar v_L \uparrow (1-\lambda)V\) the top L type's payoff \(\uparrow 0\) — at the boundary she is indifferent across all bids (this is the \(\beta = 1\) boundary of the implicit-bias example: \(U_L = (v_L/5 - 1)\,b\)).

3.2 Existence of monotone equilibrium — does Athey apply to the all-pay auction?

Yes, in substance — with checkable caveats. The logic: given rivals using (weakly) increasing strategies, an all-pay bidder's interim payoff \(v \cdot W(b) - b\) has strictly increasing differences in \((b, v)\) (\(W(\cdot)\) increasing), so best replies are monotone and Athey's single crossing condition holds. Athey (2001, Econometrica 69(4), 861–889) then gives existence of a monotone pure-strategy equilibrium; my recollection [CHECK] is that her applications explicitly include all-pay auctions alongside first-price. Caveats to verify when reading:

  1. Tie handling. Athey's auction existence results work through finite bid grids + limits; ties matter at atoms. In our setting Lemma A confines atoms to \(b = 0\), where the payoff discontinuity is real (an atom of rivals at 0 makes \(W\) jump at 0). The standard resolution — deviating to \(0^+\) — should be checked against her exact tie-breaking assumptions.
  2. \(k\) prizes. Athey's theorems are for one-dimensional actions and cover our \(k\)-prize winner-take-top-\(k\) payoff in principle (it is still a monotone-in-rank allocation), but the cleanest citations for general monotone existence beyond the single-prize case are McAdams (2003, Econometrica, multi-unit) and especially Reny (2011, Econometrica 79(2), "On the Existence of Monotone Pure-Strategy Equilibria in Bayesian Games"), which generalizes Athey and is the modern workhorse cite. [HIGH]
  3. For symmetric all-pay/war-of-attrition benchmarks: Krishna & Morgan (1997, JET). [HIGH]

Why this matters for uniqueness-within-a-class: if \(\mathcal{C}\) = monotone-strategy equilibria, Athey/Reny guarantee \(\mathcal{C} \ne \varnothing\), so "unique within \(\mathcal{C}\)" is a statement about a nonempty, canonical class — the strongest defensible claim short of full uniqueness.

3.3 What is known about uniqueness in asymmetric IPV all-pay auctions

  • \(n = 2\): solved. Amann & Leininger (1996, GEB 14, 1–18): existence and uniqueness for two asymmetric bidders, via reduction to a single ODE. [HIGH] Lizzeri & Persico (2000, GEB) prove uniqueness for a class of two-bidder auctions with a reserve — [CHECK] whether the class includes the all-pay payoff. Lu & Parreiras (GEB, ~2017, "Monotone equilibrium of two-bidder all-pay auctions Redux") — Sergio's own; postdates the AER submission; may contain reusable machinery. [CHECK] — pull the actual paper.
  • \(n \ge 3\) asymmetric: open in general. Parreiras & Rubinchik (2010, GEB 68) document the pathologies (non-monotone expected payoffs, atoms at zero, drop-out) showing \(n \ge 3\) is qualitatively different. [HIGH] — own paper. The companion WP Li/Fei–Parreiras (~/Dropbox/Research/TeX/LiParreiras15/) reportedly proves within-group symmetry and has uniqueness machinery — status unknown, folder not yet read; top retrieval priority.
  • Symmetric benchmark: the no-L continuation game. The symmetric equilibrium is unique among symmetric equilibria (standard); uniqueness among ALL equilibria is what Theorem 1's line-275 claim needs. Chawla & Hartline (2013, EC, "Auctions with unique equilibria") prove the symmetric equilibrium is the unique equilibrium for rank-based/all-pay formats with iid bidders — [CHECK] exact scope: \(n\) bidders, \(k\) prizes, atom treatment — if it covers \(k\) prizes, it closes the H-side gap outright.
  • Technique to mine — the first-price uniqueness program: Lebrun (1999 IER; 2006 GEB), Maskin & Riley (2003, GEB, "Uniqueness of equilibrium in sealed high-bid auctions"). The method — characterize equilibria as solutions of the inverse-bid ODE system and pin the solution by a boundary condition at the bottom of the bid range — is precisely what the Online Appendix's ODE sketch (lines 380–385) is missing: our singularity sits at \(b = 0\) where atoms can live, the exact analogue of their low-end boundary analysis. [HIGH]

3.4 Lemma C — no full reverse equilibrium (maestro's sketch, completed)

Claim: under Assumption 1 (\(\bar v_H > \bar v_L\)), no equilibrium has all H individuals inactive. Argument, now with the needed case split:

  • \(\ell \ge k\): if all H bid 0 and the L's play any equilibrium among themselves, the L top bid satisfies \(\bar b_L \le \bar v_L\) (no type bids above own value in equilibrium). An H of type near \(\bar v_H\) deviating to \(\bar b_L + \varepsilon\) wins a prize with probability 1 and earns \(\bar v_H - \bar b_L - \varepsilon \ge \bar v_H - \bar v_L - \varepsilon > 0\), versus at most 0 from bidding 0 (with \(\ell \ge k\) actives, all \(k\) prizes go to positive bids). Contradiction.
  • \(\ell < k\): prizes outnumber the active L's, so even a zero bid wins with positive probability; but then every L strictly prefers 0 (winning is free), so all \(h + \ell\) players pool at 0 and each wins w.p. \(k/(h+\ell) < 1\); any high type deviating to \(\varepsilon > 0\) wins surely — contradiction again.

So R1's literal "women enter, men stay out" scenario dies unconditionally (no intimidation needed). What remains live is partial multiplicity, which is exactly what the class-uniqueness theorem (§3.5) addresses inside the intimidation region and what examples should map outside it (dossier 1.1(b)–(c)).

3.5 Proposed target statement and what stands between us and it

Target Theorem (uniqueness within a class). Let \(h > k\) and let \(V_H\) \((k/h)\)-intimidate \(V_L\). Then within the class \(\mathcal{C}\) of monotone-strategy equilibria, the equilibrium outcome is unique: every L type bids 0, and the H side plays the symmetric \(k\)-prize equilibrium of the \(h\)-player game. (Athey/Reny: \(\mathcal{C} \ne \varnothing\).)

Status of the required pieces: Lemma A (atoms only at 0) — standard, unwritten. Symmetric-side uniqueness — Chawla–Hartline if its scope covers \(k\) prizes, else an in-house ODE argument.
