Stieltjes integration by parts in the drop-out proof

The proof of Proposition 1 replaces the integral $\int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F_H(s)\,dF_H^{h-1}(s)$ by the closed form $\tfrac{h-1}{h}F_H(z)^h$, citing only "continuity of $F_H$ and the Stieltjes integration by parts." Here is that one clause, fully unpacked.

1. Reading the integral

A Stieltjes integral $\int_a^z g(s)\,dG(s)$ is the limit of the sums

$$\sum_i g(s_i)\,\bigl[\,G(s_{i+1})-G(s_i)\,\bigr],$$

so you weight $g$ by the increments of $G$ instead of by $ds$. If $G$ happens to have a density, $G'=\gamma$, then $dG=\gamma\,ds$ and the whole thing collapses to the ordinary integral $\int_a^z g(s)\,\gamma(s)\,ds$. The Stieltjes form is exactly what lets the proof write $dF_H^{h-1}$ directly, without ever assuming $F_H$ has a density.

In context, $G=F_H^{h-1}$ is the distribution of the highest of the $h-1$ rival H-valuations, so $dF_H^{h-1}(s)$ is the chance that this top rival lands near $s$.

2. The by-parts rule

For two functions $u,v$ of bounded variation with no common point of discontinuity,

$$\int_a^z u\,dv \;+\; \int_a^z v\,du \;=\; u(z)\,v(z) - u(a)\,v(a).$$

This is the exact analogue of the familiar $\int u\,dv = uv-\int v\,du$; the boundary term $u(z)v(z)-u(a)v(a)$ plays the role of $[uv]_a^z$. The only fine print — "no common discontinuity" — is satisfied for free here, because $F_H$ is continuous. That single fact is the entire regularity the step uses.

3. The computation

We want

$$A \;=\; \int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F_H(s)\,dF_H^{h-1}(s).$$

Put $u=F_H$ and $v=F_H^{h-1}$ in the by-parts rule. Then $uv=F_H\cdot F_H^{h-1}=F_H^{h}$, and since $F_H(\underline v_H)=0$ the lower boundary term vanishes, leaving

$$\underbrace{\int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F_H\,dF_H^{h-1}}_{A} \;+\; \underbrace{\int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F_H^{h-1}\,dF_H}_{B} \;=\; F_H(z)^h. \qquad (1)$$

So the two integrals add to $F_H(z)^h$. One more relation pins down $A$ alone. Evaluate $B$ by the substitution $u=F_H(s)$ — legitimate precisely because $F_H$ is continuous, so as $s$ runs from $\underline v_H$ to $z$, $u$ runs continuously from $0$ to $F_H(z)$:

$$B=\int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F_H^{h-1}\,dF_H =\int_0^{F_H(z)} u^{h-1}\,du =\frac{1}{h}\,F_H(z)^h. \qquad (2)$$

Substituting $(2)$ into $(1)$ gives $A = F_H(z)^h - \tfrac1h F_H(z)^h$, that is,

$$\int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F_H(s)\,dF_H^{h-1}(s)\;=\;\frac{h-1}{h}\,F_H(z)^h.$$

This is the substitution made in the L-deviation line of the proof: the awkward $\dfrac{\overline v_L}{1-\lambda}\displaystyle\int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F_H\,dF_H^{h-1}$ becomes the clean $\dfrac{\overline v_L}{1-\lambda}\dfrac{h-1}{h}F_H(z)^h$.

4. Why continuity — not a density — is enough

Track where regularity actually entered. The by-parts rule needed $u$ and $v$ to share no jump: true because $F_H$ is continuous. The evaluation of $B$ needed the substitution $u=F_H(s)$ to carry the interval continuously onto $[0,F_H(z)]$: again just continuity, i.e. atomlessness. At no point was $F_H$ differentiated — what got differentiated was $u^{h-1}$, a plain polynomial. So no density, and hence no absolute continuity, is required; an atomless $F_H$ suffices.

Indeed the whole identity is one substitution end to end:

$$A=\int_{\underline v_H}^{z} F_H\,d\!\left(F_H^{h-1}\right) =\int_0^{F_H(z)} u\,d\!\left(u^{h-1}\right) =(h-1)\int_0^{F_H(z)} u^{h-1}\,du =\frac{h-1}{h}\,F_H(z)^h.$$

An atom would break both moves at once: a jump in $F_H$ at some $v^\*$ is a common discontinuity of $u$ and $v$ (boundary term picks up the jump), and the substitution $u=F_H(s)$ skips the interval $[F_H(v^{\*-}),F_H(v^{\*})]$ — the closed form $\tfrac{h-1}{h}F_H^h$ then fails. This is the same atomless requirement discussed for $F_H,F_L$ generally.
