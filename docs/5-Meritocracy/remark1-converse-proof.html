Remark 1 — the converse: drop-out implies intimidation

Remark 1 asserts the necessity direction of Proposition 1: when $F_H(v)/v$ is non-increasing, any equilibrium in which the L individuals drop out forces the intimidation ranking. No proof was ever written for it. Here is one, reconstructed from the model. The threshold it produces is tight, and it lands at exactly $\lambda=1/h$ — the $k=1$ case of the $k/h$-intimidation used in the Drop-Out Theorem.

Throughout, $h$ is the number of class-H individuals, $F_H$ the (atomless) H valuation distribution on $[\underline v_H,\overline v_H]$, and $\overline v_L$ the top L valuation. The non-increasing hypothesis forces $\underline v_H=0$: if $\underline v_H>0$ then $F_H(v)/v$ equals $0$ below $\underline v_H$ and is positive just above it, so the ratio rises — it cannot be non-increasing. So we take $F_H(0)=0$.

1. What a drop-out equilibrium forces on L

Fix an equilibrium in which every L bids $0$. Conditional on the L's being inactive, the $h$ H individuals compete for the single prize exactly as in a contest among themselves, so they play the symmetric all-pay equilibrium

$$b_H(v)=\int_{\underline v_H}^{v}s\,dF_H^{h-1}(s),$$

strictly increasing from $b_H(0)=0$. On the equilibrium path an L wins only when every H also bids $0$, an event of probability zero because $F_H$ is atomless; so an L's equilibrium payoff is $0$.

Now let an L of valuation $v_L$ deviate to a positive bid $b_H(z)$. She beats every other L (all at $0$) for sure, and beats a given H iff that H's valuation is below $z$, i.e. with probability $F_H(z)$. Against the $h$ independent H's her win probability is $F_H(z)^h$, and her cost is $b_H(z)$, so

$$U_L\!\left(b_H(z)\mid v_L\right)=F_H(z)^h\,v_L-\int_{\underline v_H}^{z}s\,dF_H^{h-1}(s).$$

Equilibrium requires this to be $\le 0$ for every $z$ and every $v_L\le\overline v_L$. The binding type is the largest, $v_L=\overline v_L$, which gives the necessary condition

$$\int_{\underline v_H}^{z}s\,dF_H^{h-1}(s)\ \ge\ \overline v_L\,F_H(z)^h \qquad\text{for all }z. \qquad(\ast)$$

This is the same no-deviation inequality that appears in the proof of Proposition 1 — only now it is a fact we are handed by the equilibrium, to be mined, rather than a conclusion to be reached.

2. Rewriting the left side by parts

Apply Stieltjes integration by parts with $u=s$, $v=F_H^{h-1}$. The lower boundary term vanishes because $F_H(0)=0$:

$$\int_{\underline v_H}^{z}s\,dF_H^{h-1}(s) =\Big[\,s\,F_H^{h-1}(s)\,\Big]_{0}^{z}-\int_{0}^{z}F_H^{h-1}(s)\,ds =z\,F_H(z)^{h-1}-\int_{0}^{z}F_H^{h-1}(s)\,ds.$$

3. The monotone ratio collapses it to a pointwise bound

Write the hypothesis as $F_H(s)=c(s)\,s$, where $c(s)=F_H(s)/s$ is non-increasing. For $s\le z$ we then have $c(s)\ge c(z)$, so

$$\int_{0}^{z}F_H^{h-1}(s)\,ds=\int_{0}^{z}c(s)^{h-1}s^{h-1}\,ds \ \ge\ c(z)^{h-1}\int_{0}^{z}s^{h-1}\,ds=c(z)^{h-1}\,\frac{z^{h}}{h}.$$

Feeding this lower bound on the subtracted integral into the by-parts identity gives an upper bound on the whole left side of $(\ast)$:

$$\int_{\underline v_H}^{z}s\,dF_H^{h-1}(s) \ \le\ z\,c(z)^{h-1}z^{h-1}-c(z)^{h-1}\frac{z^{h}}{h} =c(z)^{h-1}z^{h}\,\frac{h-1}{h}.$$

Combine with $(\ast)$, whose right side is $\overline v_L\,F_H(z)^h =\overline v_L\,c(z)^h z^h$:

$$\overline v_L\,c(z)^{h}z^{h}\ \le\ c(z)^{h-1}z^{h}\,\frac{h-1}{h}.$$

Divide by $c(z)^{h-1}z^{h}>0$ (any interior $z$). The result holds at every such $z$:

$$\frac{F_H(z)}{z}=c(z)\ \le\ \frac{h-1}{h\,\overline v_L}\qquad\text{for all }z.$$

4. Reading off intimidation

Because $F_H(v)/v$ is non-increasing, its supremum sits at the left end, $\sup_{v>0}F_H(v)/v=\lim_{v\searrow 0}F_H(v)/v$. The pointwise bound of Section 3 holds at every $z$, so it bounds that supremum — and, in passing, shows the limit is finite without that being assumed:

$$\sup_{v>0}\frac{F_H(v)}{v}\ \le\ \frac{h-1}{h\,\overline v_L} =\frac{1-\lambda}{\overline v_L}\ \ \text{ at }\ \lambda=\tfrac1h.$$

That is exactly the intimidation ranking of Definition 1 at $\lambda=1/h$, i.e. $V_H$ $(1/h)$-intimidates $V_L$. So a drop-out equilibrium forces intimidation, and at the very $\lambda$ where Proposition 1's threshold $h\lambda\ge 1$ first binds — the single-prize instance of the $k/h$-intimidation in the Drop-Out Theorem.

Strict vs. weak. Sufficiency (Proposition 1) assumes the strict intimidation inequality $\sup F_H/v<(1-\lambda)/\overline v_L$; necessity yields the weak one, $\le$. The two meet exactly at $\lambda=1/h$. This is the same boundary subtlety already flagged in TODO — the converse closes to an "iff" up to that endpoint, not through it.

Why non-increasing alone is enough

Monotonicity does two jobs at once: it powers the bound in Section 3 (it is the step $c(s)\ge c(z)$), and it places the supremum at $v\to 0$ so a bound near the origin controls the whole ranking. That is the only hypothesis the Remark needs. A finiteness condition $\lim_{v\searrow 0}F_H(v)/v<+\infty$ — which an earlier draft carried — is redundant: Section 3 already shows that in any drop-out equilibrium $F_H(z)/z\le (h-1)/(h\overline v_L)$ at every $z$, so the ratio is automatically bounded. Contrapositively —

if $F_H(v)/v$ is non-increasing with $\lim_{v\searrow 0}F_H(v)/v=+\infty$, then no drop-out equilibrium exists.

So adding finiteness restricts nothing and proves nothing extra: where it fails, the antecedent — existence of a drop-out equilibrium — already fails, and the implication holds vacuously. The Remark is therefore stated with the single hypothesis that $F_H(v)/v$ be non-increasing.

Tightness check. Take the constant-ratio family $F_H(v)=\frac{h-1}{h\overline v_L}\,v$ on $[0,\,\frac{h\overline v_L}{h-1}]$, which meets the bound with equality. Then $(\ast)$ reads $\frac{h-1}{h}c^{\,h-1}z^h=\overline v_L\,c^{\,h}z^h$ with $c=\frac{h-1}{h\overline v_L}$, i.e. $\tfrac{h-1}{h}=\overline v_L c$ — an identity, holding with equality at every $z$. The threshold is exact: a ratio any larger violates $(\ast)$ and kills the drop-out equilibrium; any smaller leaves it slack.
