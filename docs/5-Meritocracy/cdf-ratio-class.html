Cumulative distribution functions with non-increasing $F(x)/x$

A standalone survey of the class of (continuous, compactly supported) CDFs $F$ for which the ratio $F(x)/x$ is non-increasing — what it is, whether it is empty, its examples, equivalent definitions, properties, where it sits among classical classes, the finite-limit subclass $\lim_{x\downarrow 0}F(x)/x<\infty$, and applications. Results are stated with proof sketches; pointers to the literature are collected at the end.

0. The object, and a normalization

Throughout, $F$ is the CDF of a non-negative random variable $X$, continuous (atomless) and with compact support $[a,b]\subseteq[0,\infty)$, so $F(a)=0$, $F(b)=1$. The class of interest is

$$\mathcal{S}\;=\;\Bigl\{\,F:\ \text{continuous CDF, compact support}\subseteq[0,\infty),\ \ x\mapsto \tfrac{F(x)}{x}\ \text{non-increasing on }(0,\infty)\,\Bigr\}.$$

Scale invariance ⇒ normalize $b=1$. If $X\in\mathcal{S}$ and $c\gt 0$, then $cX$ has CDF $F(x/c)$ and $\dfrac{F(x/c)}{x}=\dfrac1c\cdot\dfrac{F(x/c)}{x/c}$, a positive constant times a non-increasing function of $x$ — still non-increasing. So $\mathcal{S}$ is closed under positive scaling and we may rescale the support to $[0,1]$ without loss. (It is not translation-invariant; see §2.)

One observation used repeatedly: because $F(x)/x$ is non-increasing on $(0,\infty)$, its supremum is its left limit,

$$m\;:=\;\sup_{x\gt 0}\frac{F(x)}{x}\;=\;\lim_{x\downarrow 0}\frac{F(x)}{x}\ \in\ \Bigl[\tfrac1b,\,+\infty\Bigr].$$

The finiteness of this single number $m$ splits the class into two regimes and defines the subclass studied in §6.

1. Is the class empty? — No; it is large

The class is non-empty and infinite-dimensional. A representative gallery (all on $[0,1]$):

$F(x)$ on $[0,1]$$F(x)/x$in $\mathcal S$?$m=\lim_{x\downarrow0}F/x$note
$x$ (uniform)$1$ (constant)yes$1$boundary case; unique constant ratio
$x^{\alpha},\ \alpha\in(0,1]$$x^{\alpha-1}$yes$+\infty$ if $\alpha\lt1$; $1$ if $\alpha=1$constant-elasticity family

$x+3x(\tfrac12-x)^2$ on $[0,\tfrac12]$, then $x$$1+3(\tfrac12-x)^2$, then $1$yes$\tfrac74$not concave — see below

Three structural sources of members.

  • The uniform $F(x)=x/b$ is the unique member with constant ratio $F/x\equiv 1/b$. It is the "boundary" of $\mathcal{S}$ (weakly, not strictly, non-increasing) and, as §5 shows, the extremal least-concentrated member.
  • Every concave CDF with $F(0)=0$ belongs to $\mathcal{S}$: along a concave graph through the origin, chord slopes $F(x)/x$ fall as $x$ grows. Concavity $\iff$ non-increasing density, so this already supplies an infinite family (e.g. all $x^{\alpha}$, $\alpha\le 1$; all $1-(1-x)^{k}$).
  • Genuinely non-concave members exist, so $\mathcal{S}$ is strictly larger than the concave CDFs. Take, on $[0,1]$, $$F(x)=\begin{cases} x+c\,x\bigl(\tfrac12-x\bigr)^2,&0\le x\le\tfrac12,\\[2pt] x,&\tfrac12\le x\le1,\end{cases}\qquad 0\lt c\le 12.$$ Then $F(x)/x = 1+c(\tfrac12-x)^2$ on $[0,\tfrac12]$ (falling from $1+\tfrac c4$ to $1$) and $\equiv1$ afterward — non-increasing, so $F\in\mathcal{S}$. But its density $f(x)=1+c(\tfrac14-2x+3x^2)$ on $[0,\tfrac12]$ has $f'(x)=2c(3x-1)$: it falls then rises (minimum at $x=\tfrac13$), so $F$ is not concave. (Validity: the density's minimum is $1-\tfrac{c}{12}\ge0$ exactly when $c\le12$.) This single example certifies the strict inclusion of §4.

2. The left endpoint is forced to $0$ (continuity vs. $a\gt0$)

Proposition (left endpoint). If $F\in\mathcal{S}$ is a proper continuous CDF with support $[a,b]$, then $a=0$. Moreover

$$F(x)\ \ge\ \frac{x}{b}\qquad\text{for all }x\in[0,b].$$

Sketch. Non-increasing ratio gives, for every $0\lt x\le b$, $\dfrac{F(x)}{x}\ge\dfrac{F(b)}{b}=\dfrac1b$, hence $F(x)\ge x/b\gt0$ for all $x\gt0$. So $F$ is already positive arbitrarily close to $0$: the left endpoint of the support is $0$. If instead $a\gt0$, then $F\equiv0$ on $(0,a)$ forces $F(x)/x=0\lt\frac1b$ there, contradicting the ratio being non-increasing down to its supremum. $\square$

Why a strictly positive lower support bound needs an atom (or a restricted domain). The obstruction is exactly continuity at $a$. For a continuous $F$ with support $[a,b]$ one has $F(a)=0$, so the ratio starts at $F(a)/a=0$ and must rise — impossible for a non-increasing function. There are precisely two ways to live with $a\gt0$:

  • Allow an atom at the left endpoint. A mass $p\gt0$ at $a$ gives $F(a)/a=p/a\gt0$; the ratio can then be non-increasing on $[a,b]$ (it is seeded positive at $a$ and decays). This leaves the continuous class but is the honest way to obtain a genuinely positive lower bound. The quantity $\sup_{x\gt0}F(x)/x$ remains well defined and is automatically finite ($\le 1/a$).
  • Impose the ratio condition only on a subinterval $[a,b]$ bounded away from $0$. Then $a\gt0$ is unconstrained; what one loses is the clean global statement that $\sup F/x$ is attained at the origin.

So: for a continuous CDF, "non-increasing $F(x)/x$ on the whole support" and "left endpoint $\gt0$" are mutually exclusive — the support must begin at $0$.

Two immediate corollaries of $F\ge x/b$ (valid for the whole class, finite $m$ or not):

  • Domination by the uniform. $F\ge F_{U[0,b]}$ pointwise, i.e. $X\preceq_{\mathrm{st}}U[0,b]$ (uniform first-order stochastically dominates every member). Members pile mass toward $0$.
  • Mean bound. $\mathbb{E}[X]=\int_0^b(1-F)\le\int_0^b(1-\tfrac xb)\,dx=\tfrac b2$, and more generally $\mathbb{E}[X^k]\le b^k/(k+1)$. The uniform maximizes every such moment within $\mathcal S$.

3. Equivalent definitions

For absolutely continuous $F$ on $[0,b]$ with $F(0)=0$ and density $f$, the following are equivalent (and (C1)–(C3) need no density):

conditionreading
(C1)$F(x)/x$ non-increasing on $(0,b]$definition
(C2)$F(\alpha x)\ge \alpha F(x)$ for all $\alpha\in[0,1]$super-homogeneity ("star-shaped from below")
(C3)$F(x)\ge \tfrac{x}{y}F(y)$ for $0\lt x\le y$graph lies above every chord from the origin
(C4)$\eta_F(x):=\dfrac{x f(x)}{F(x)}\le 1$ a.e.CDF-elasticity $\le 1$
(C5)$\tilde r(x):=\dfrac{f(x)}{F(x)}\le \dfrac1x$reversed hazard rate $\le 1/x$
(C6)$\dfrac1x\displaystyle\int_0^x f(t)\,dt$ non-increasingrunning average density non-increasing

Sketch of the links. (C1)$\Leftrightarrow$(C2)$\Leftrightarrow$(C3) is the algebra of $F(\alpha x)/(\alpha x)\ge F(x)/x$. Differentiating, $\frac{d}{dx}\frac{F}{x}=\frac{xf-F}{x^2}\le0\iff xf\le F$, which is (C4); dividing by $F$ gives (C5), since $\tilde r=\frac{d}{dx}\log F$. And $F(x)=\int_0^x f$ makes (C6) a restatement of (C1). The log–log form is memorable: (C1) $\iff \log F(x)-\log x$ non-increasing $\iff$ the elasticity $\dfrac{d\log F}{d\log x}\le1$. $\square$

One sufficient (not necessary) condition: $F$ concave on $[0,b]$ (equivalently $f$ non-increasing) $\Rightarrow$ $F\in\mathcal S$. The non-concave example of §1 shows the converse fails.

Geometry. (C2) says the hypograph $\{(x,y):0\le y\le F(x)\}$ is star-shaped with respect to the origin: every segment from $(0,0)$ to a graph point $(x,F(x))$ stays under the graph. This is the precise sense in which $\mathcal S$ is a "star-shapedness" class — see §4.

4. Where the class sits among classical classes

The Bruckner–Ostrow hierarchy, mirrored. Bruckner & Ostrow (1962) study, on $[0,b]$, the chain of strict inclusions $$\underbrace{K}_{\text{convex}}\ \subsetneq\ \underbrace{S}_{\substack{\text{star-shaped:}\\ \phi(x)/x\ \uparrow}}\ \subsetneq\ \underbrace{\bar S}_{\text{superadditive}}.$$ Our class is the reflection of this picture — replace "non-decreasing ratio / convex / superadditive" by "non-increasing ratio / concave / subadditive":

$$\bigl\{\text{concave CDF},\,F(0)=0\bigr\}\ \subsetneq\ \mathcal{S}\ \subsetneq\ \bigl\{\text{subadditive CDF}\bigr\}.$$

Sketch. Left inclusion: concave through origin $\Rightarrow$ chord slopes fall $\Rightarrow$ (C1); strictness is the §1 non-concave example. Right inclusion: for $x,y\gt0$ put $s=x+y$; by (C3), $F(x)\ge\frac{x}{s}F(s)$ and $F(y)\ge\frac{y}{s}F(s)$, so $$F(x)+F(y)\ \ge\ \frac{x+y}{s}\,F(s)\ =\ F(x+y),$$ i.e. $F$ is subadditive. (For $s\gt b$ use $F(x)+F(y)\ge\frac{x+y}{b}\gt1=F(s)$ from §2.) Strictness: a subadditive CDF with a non-monotone $F/x$ lies in $\bar S\setminus\mathcal S$. $\square$

Reliability analogue (IFRA). A life distribution is IFRA (increasing failure rate average) iff its cumulative hazard $\Lambda(x)=-\log\bar F(x)$ is star-shaped, i.e. $\Lambda(x)/x$ is non-decreasing. Our condition is the very same star-shaped template, but applied to $F$ itself (not to $\Lambda$) and with the opposite monotonicity. So $\mathcal S$ is to the CDF what IFRA is to the cumulative hazard: a structurally parallel, logically independent "star-shapedness" class. In the reversed-hazard language of (C5), $\mathcal S$ is the set of distributions whose reversed hazard never exceeds the benchmark $1/x$ of the uniform.

The concave core is Khinchin's class. A CDF $F$ is concave on $[0,\infty)$ $\iff$ its density is non-increasing $\iff$ $X$ is unimodal with mode $0$ $\iff$ (Khinchin) $X\stackrel{d}{=}U\cdot Y$ with $U\sim\mathrm{Unif}[0,1]$ independent of $Y\ge0$ — a scale mixture of uniforms. These are exactly the concave members of $\mathcal S$; the class $\mathcal S$ relaxes "density non-increasing" to "average density non-increasing" (C6), admitting the non-concave members of §1.

$\mathcal S$ is the star order relative to the uniform. Recall the classical star order: $X\le_{*}Y$ iff $F_Y^{-1}\!\bigl(F_X(x)\bigr)/x$ is non-decreasing. Taking the benchmark $Y\sim U[0,1]$ (so $F_Y^{-1}(p)=p$) gives

$$F\in\mathcal S\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad U[0,1]\ \le_{*}\ X,$$

i.e. $\mathcal S$ is exactly the set of laws lying above the uniform in the star order, with the uniform as the star-minimal element. (Check: $U\le_* X \iff F_X^{-1}(x)/x\uparrow \iff F_X(x)/x\downarrow$, which is (C1).) Since the star order implies the Lorenz order, every member is at least as Lorenz-spread as the uniform — the inequality reading of $F\ge x/b$ in §5.

Relationship to log-concave distributions

"Log-concave" is three conditions, not one. For a law on $[0,b]$ one can ask log-concavity of the density, of the CDF, or of the survival function:

object"log-concave" meansstandard name
density $f$$\log f$ concavestrongest of the three (Bagnoli–Bergstrom)
CDF $F$$\log F$ concaveDRHR: reversed hazard $f/F$ non-increasing
survival $\bar F$$\log\bar F$ concaveIFR: hazard $f/\bar F$ non-decreasing

By Bagnoli–Bergstrom, log-concave density $\Rightarrow$ both log-concave CDF and log-concave survival; the converses fail. The notion that touches $\mathcal S$ is the middle one, log-concave CDF = DRHR, since both are conditions on $F$ itself.

Same variable, different demand. Both conditions constrain $\log F$ through the reversed hazard rate $\tilde r:=(\log F)'=f/F$:

$$F\in\mathcal S\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \tilde r(x)\le \tfrac1x\quad(\text{a \emph{level} bound: reversed hazard}\le\text{the uniform's}),$$ $$F\ \text{log-concave CDF}\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \tilde r\ \text{non-increasing}\quad(\text{a \emph{monotonicity}: DRHR}).$$

In the log–log picture of §3: $\mathcal S$ caps the elasticity, $\dfrac{d\log F}{d\log x}\le1$; log-concavity of $F$ instead makes $\log F$ concave in $x$. One bounds the slope of $\log F$; the other forces that slope to trend down. They are independent demands.

Neither class contains the other.

  • Log-concave but not in $\mathcal S$. $F(x)=x^2$ on $[0,1]$ has density $2x$ with $\log(2x)$ concave — so $F$ is log-concave at the density level, a fortiori a log-concave CDF. Its reversed hazard $\tilde r=2/x$ is decreasing (DRHR holds), but it sits above the benchmark: $2/x\gt1/x$. Equivalently $F/x=x$ increases. So $F\notin\mathcal S$.
  • In $\mathcal S$ but not log-concave. Take the density $f=1.5$ on $[0,\tfrac12]$, then $f$ rising linearly from $0.2$ to $0.8$ on $[\tfrac12,1]$ (so $F(\tfrac12)=0.75$, $F(1)=1$). One checks $F(x)/x$ is non-increasing and $\tilde r\le 1/x$ throughout, so $F\in\mathcal S$; yet $\tilde r=f/F$ rises on $[\tfrac12,1]$ (from $\approx0.28$ to $0.8$ — verified), so $\log F$ is not concave and $F$ is not a log-concave CDF. The structural reason such members exist: $\mathcal S$ is convex (closed under mixtures, §5), whereas the log-concave classes are not mixture-closed.

The overlap is the concave core. A concave CDF with support from $0$ lies in both classes: concave $\Rightarrow$ star-shaped ($\in\mathcal S$, §4) and concave $\Rightarrow$ log-concave (the log of a concave positive function is concave). Sharper, a non-increasing log-concave density forces $F$ concave, hence into $\mathcal S$. So the Khinchin scale-mixtures of the uniform (§4) sit squarely in $\mathcal S\cap\{\text{log-concave}\}$, and the two classes diverge exactly when the reversed hazard either exceeds $1/x$ while staying monotone ($x^2$) or stays under $1/x$ while wiggling (the mixture-type example).

5. Properties

  • Trapped above the diagonal. $F(x)\ge x/b$ on $[0,b]$ (§2); equality everywhere iff $F$ is uniform. So the uniform is the pointwise-smallest CDF in $\mathcal S$ — the least concentrated member — and the unique one attaining the minimal $m=1/b$.
  • Supremum at the origin. $m=\sup_x F/x=\lim_{x\downarrow0}F/x$, with $m\ge1/b$ and $m=1/b\iff$ uniform.
  • Convexity of the class. $\mathcal{S}$ (fixed support) is convex: a mixture $\lambda F_1+(1-\lambda)F_2$ has ratio $\lambda\frac{F_1}{x}+(1-\lambda)\frac{F_2}{x}$, a sum of non-increasing functions. Mixtures of members are members.

The power-$\le 1$ closure is worth isolating: the elasticity of $F^\alpha$ is exactly $\alpha$ times that of $F$, so raising a member to a power in $(0,1]$ can only reduce the elasticity below the threshold $1$ — the class is closed downward under the "make-the-tail-thinner-at-0" operation, but taking a maximum (power $\gt1$) drives elasticity up through $1$ and exits.

6. The subclass $\lim_{x\downarrow0}F(x)/x\lt\infty$

Because the ratio is monotone, its left limit equals its supremum, so the defining condition of the subclass is the finiteness of $m$:

$$\mathcal{S}_0\;=\;\Bigl\{\,F\in\mathcal S:\ m=\lim_{x\downarrow0}\tfrac{F(x)}{x}\lt\infty\,\Bigr\} \;=\;\Bigl\{\,F\in\mathcal S:\ \sup_{x\gt0}\tfrac{F(x)}{x}\lt\infty\,\Bigr\}.$$

Equivalent descriptions of $\mathcal S_0$.

  • Linear bound at the origin: $\exists\,m\lt\infty$ with $F(x)\le m x$ for all $x$ (the least such $m$ is the limit). $F$ is Lipschitz at $0$.
  • Right-differentiable at $0$: since $F(0)=0$, finiteness of $m$ is exactly $F'(0^+)=m\lt\infty$. If the density has a right limit, $m=f(0^+)$.
  • Bounded reversed-hazard benchmark: $x\,\tilde r(x)\le 1$ and the elasticity stays bounded as $x\downarrow0$ (no infinite spike of mass at the origin).

Sandwich. Every $F\in\mathcal S_0$ on $[0,b]$ is trapped between two lines through the origin:

$$\frac{x}{b}\ \le\ F(x)\ \le\ m\,x,\qquad x\in[0,b],\qquad m\ge \tfrac1b.$$

The lower bound is §2; the upper bound is the definition of $m$ together with monotonicity. Geometrically $\mathcal S_0$ members leave the origin with a finite slope wedged in $[1/b,m]$.

What lives in $\mathcal S_0$ vs. its complement.

  • In $\mathcal S_0$: the uniform ($m=1/b$), the concave $2x-x^2$ ($m=2$), the non-concave §1 example ($m=1+\tfrac c4$), and any member with bounded density at $0$.
  • In $\mathcal S\setminus\mathcal S_0$ ($m=+\infty$): the "infinite-spike" members, whose density blows up at $0$ while the law stays atomless. The prototypes are $F(x)=x^{\alpha}$, $\alpha\in(0,1)$: density $\alpha x^{\alpha-1}\to\infty$, ratio $x^{\alpha-1}\to\infty$. These are precisely the laws regularly varying at the origin with index $\alpha\lt1$ (more generally $F(x)=x^{\alpha}\ell(x)$ with $\ell$ slowly varying at $0$, $\alpha\in(0,1)$). They are "almost an atom at $0$" — all the action is an unbounded reversed hazard near the origin — without ever being one.

Thus $\mathcal S_0$ is exactly the "Lipschitz-at-the-origin" part of $\mathcal S$, equivalently the part on which the functional $\sup_x F(x)/x$ — a natural measure of how much mass a law concentrates near $0$ relative to linear — is finite.

7. Applications

  • Auction & contest theory. Three distinct strands meet here.
    • The order itself. The condition (C1) is the star order against the uniform (§4): a member is star-larger than $U[0,1]$. The star order — and its companions the convex-transform and dispersive orders — are standard comparison devices for value/cost distributions (Shaked–Shanthikumar 2007 §4.B; Marshall–Olkin–Arnold 2011).
    • The reverse-hazard reading. Condition (C5), $f/F\le1/x$, is a reverse-hazard-rate comparison of a member against the uniform benchmark. The reverse-hazard-rate order is the workhorse assumption for ranking bidders in asymmetric all-pay/first-price auctions and contests — see Kirkegaard's program (Econometrica 2012; GEB 2012, 2014; EER 2013).
    • The supremum statistic. The functional $\sup_{x\gt0}F(x)/x$ — finite exactly on $\mathcal S_0$ — quantifies near-zero concentration relative to the uniform, and (C1) is what makes it endpoint-attained, $=\lim_{x\downarrow0}F/x$. This precise sup-functional, used as an intimidation/stochastic-order criterion, is the device of Parreiras' contest model rather than a standard textbook order; the survey above isolates the real-analytic class on which it is well-behaved.
  • Mechanism design / pricing. The elasticity $\eta_F=xf/F$ and the reversed hazard rate $\tilde r=f/F$ are standard regularity inputs; "$\eta_F\le1$" (an inelastic CDF) and "$\tilde r\le1/x$" are exactly conditions (C4)–(C5). The economics literature (Veres-Ferrer & Pavía; Belzunce–Candel–Ruiz) develops the reversed-hazard-as-elasticity reading directly.
  • Reliability / ageing. $\mathcal S$ is the CDF-side analogue of IFRA (§4): a positive "star-shapedness" condition, here on $F$ via its reversed hazard rather than on the cumulative hazard.
  • Extreme behaviour at the left endpoint. The complement $\mathcal S\setminus\mathcal S_0$ is governed by regular variation at $0$ (index $\lt1$), which controls the laws of minima and the lowest order statistics near $0$.
  • Simulation. The concave core is Khinchin's scale-mixture class, giving the direct generator $X=U\cdot Y$ ($U$ uniform); useful whenever a member happens to be concave.
  • Inequality / distributional comparison. $F\ge x/b$ is a Lorenz-type domination: every member places at least as much mass below each level as the uniform — "more at the bottom, at every scale."

8. One-line summary of the structure

$\mathcal S$ = continuous compactly-supported CDFs whose hypograph is star-shaped about the origin $\iff$ $F(x)/x\downarrow$ $\iff$ CDF-elasticity $\le1$ $\iff$ reversed hazard $\le1/x$. It necessarily begins at $0$, dominates the uniform from above ($F\ge x/b$, mean $\le b/2$), sits strictly between the concave CDFs (Khinchin scale-mixtures) and the subadditive CDFs, is convex and closed under scaling, mixtures and powers $\le1$ (but not maxima, sums, or shifts), and is split by the single number $m=\lim_{x\downarrow0}F/x=\sup_x F/x$ into the Lipschitz-at-$0$ subclass $\mathcal S_0$ ($m\lt\infty$, sandwiched $x/b\le F\le mx$) and the regularly-varying-at-$0$ remainder ($m=\infty$, e.g. $x^{\alpha}$, $\alpha\lt1$).
