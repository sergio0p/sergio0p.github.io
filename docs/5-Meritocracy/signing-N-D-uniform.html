Signing $N$ and $D$ near the top is uniform â€” no case split needed

The worry: the annihilator identity $\gH N=\gL D$ is division-free, but to run the one-shot contradiction we still need $N>0$ and $D<0$ near $L$'s top. At a common top the entries of $A$ collapse neatly as $\GH,\GL\to1$; the fear is that in the drop-out regime, where $\GL\to1$ but $\GH<1$, the entries no longer simplify and the signs get messy â€” so we would be forced into a two-case proof.

The fear is not founded. Everything relevant lives on the top edge $\GL=1$, and there the four entries factor through a single common positive weight. Write $\lambda=k/h$ and $G:=\GH$.

The four entries on $\GL=1$

With $W_H=\Pr\{X_H+Y_L\le k-1\}$, $X_H\sim\mathrm{Bin}(h-1,1-\GH)$, $Y_L\sim\mathrm{Bin}(\ell,1-\GL)$ (and $W_L$ the analogue with $h$ strong and $\ell-1$ weak rivals), the partials $a_{HH}=\partial_{\GH}W_H$, $a_{HL}=\partial_{\GL}W_H$, $a_{LH}=\partial_{\GH}W_L$, $a_{LL}=\partial_{\GL}W_L$, evaluated at $\GL=1$, are

$$ a_{HH}=(h-1)\tbinom{h-2}{k-1}\,\Phi,\quad a_{HL}=\ell\tbinom{h-1}{k-1}\,\Phi\,G,\quad a_{LH}=h\tbinom{h-1}{k-1}\,\Phi\,G,\quad a_{LL}=(\ell-1)\tbinom{h}{k-1}\,\Phi\,G^{2}, $$

where the common weight is

$$\Phi:=(1-\GH)^{\,k-1}\,\GH^{\,h-1-k}\;>\;0\qquad\text{on }\GH\in(0,1).$$

This one factor $\Phi$ carries the entire degeneracy: it is exactly what vanishes as $\GH\to1$ for $k\ge2$ (hence $\det A\to0$ and the densities blow up at a common top). But $\Phi$ multiplies all four entries equally, so it cancels out of the ratio identity. Signing $N$ and $D$ never sees the degeneracy.

Both signs collapse to one inequality

Divide through by $\Phi>0$. Using $\tbinom{h-1}{k-1}=\tfrac{h-1}{h-k}\tbinom{h-2}{k-1}$ and $\tbinom{h}{k-1}=\tfrac{h}{h-k+1}\tbinom{h-1}{k-1}$,

$$ N=a_{HH}\pH-a_{LH}\pL=\Phi\,(h-1)\tbinom{h-2}{k-1}\Big[\pH-\tfrac{h}{h-k}\,G\,\pL\Big], $$ $$ D=a_{LL}\pL-a_{HL}\pH=\Phi\,\tbinom{h-1}{k-1}\,G\Big[(\ell-1)\tfrac{h}{h-k+1}\,G\,\pL-\ell\,\pH\Big]. $$

So $N>0\iff \pH>\tfrac{h}{h-k}\,G\,\pL$ and $D<0\iff \pH>\tfrac{(\ell-1)h}{\ell(h-k+1)}\,G\,\pL$. Both are lower bounds on $\pH/\pL$ of the same shape $\pH>\kappa\,G\,\pL$, and

$$ \kappa_N=\frac{h}{h-k}\;>\;\frac{(\ell-1)h}{\ell(h-k+1)}=\kappa_D, $$

because $\tfrac1{h-k}>\tfrac1{h-k+1}$ and $\tfrac{\ell-1}{\ell}<1$. The $N$ bound is the binding one: once it holds, $D<0$ is automatic. Both sign facts reduce to a single condition.

$$ N>0\ \text{and}\ D<0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \pH \;>\; \frac{h}{h-k}\,\GH\,\pL \;=\; \gamma\,\GH\,\pL, \qquad \gamma=\frac{1}{1-\lambda},\ \ \lambda=\frac{k}{h}. $$

Why this kills the two-case worry

The single threshold $\gamma\,\GH$ is monotone in $\GH$ and largest at $\GH=1$. So the common top $\GH=1$ is the hardest point, where the condition is the pure intimidation inequality $\pH>\gamma\,\pL$ â€” i.e. $\overline v_H>\gamma\,\overline v_L$ (at $(k,h)=(2,3)$: $\overline v_H>3\,\overline v_L$). In the drop-out regime $\GH<1$ the threshold is strictly smaller, so the same intimidation condition delivers the signs a fortiori. The full range $\GH\in(\,\cdot\,,1]$ is covered by one inequality, with the common top as its boundary case â€” not a separate branch. Signs at $\GL=1$ are strict, so they persist on a left-neighbourhood by continuity, which is all the $\gH N=\gL D$ contradiction needs.

The constant $\gamma=1/(1-\lambda)$ is exactly the Track B conjecture, now derived (not guessed) and shown to be the $N$-binding threshold for every $k,h,\ell$.

Verification

The two base cases reproduce the known $\det A$ constants: $\det A=-18\,(1-G)^2G^2$ at $(k,h,\ell)=(2,3,2)$ and $-60\,(1-G)^2G^4$ at $(2,4,3)$, matching $B=-18,-60$. A finite-difference check of the four partials against the closed forms above, and of the sign-collapse, passes on $(2,3,2),(2,4,3),(3,5,4),(2,5,3),(3,6,2),(4,7,5)$ â€” including triples outside the base set.

Scope: the clean factorization is taken at the top edge $\GL=1$; for $b$ just below $L$'s top there are $O(1-\GL)$ corrections, but the strict leading signs carry over by continuity. The result concerns signing only â€” it does not by itself establish that the equilibrium reaches either configuration.
