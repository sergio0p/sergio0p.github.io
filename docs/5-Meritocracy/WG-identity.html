With one prize the win probabilities are "everyone else below me," $W_H=G_H^{\,h-1}G_L^{\,\ell}$ and $W_L=G_H^{\,h}G_L^{\,\ell-1}$, and multiplying each by its own group CDF gives the same symmetric product:

$$W_HG_H=G_H^{\,h}G_L^{\,\ell}=W_LG_L\qquad(k=1).$$

For $k>1$ this exact symmetry breaks, but the difference factors cleanly.

1. The identity

$$\boxed{\;W_L\,G_L\;-\;W_H\,G_H\;=\;(G_L-G_H)\,\widehat W^{\,(k-1)}_{\,h-1,\,\ell-1}\;}$$

where $\widehat W^{\,(k-1)}_{\,h-1,\,\ell-1}$ is the win probability in the once-smaller contest â€” $k-1$ prizes against $h-1$ H-rivals and $\ell-1$ L-rivals. It is a one-step recursion in $(k,h,\ell)$: the gap between the two $W\!\cdot\!G$ products at level $(k,h,\ell)$ equals the CDF gap times a win probability one level down.

2. Derivation (telescoping)

Write $\Pi=G_H^{\,h}G_L^{\,\ell}$ and the "odds of being above" $S_i=(1-G_i)/G_i$. With $k$ prizes a bidder wins iff at most $k-1$ rivals are above, so expanding "exactly $a$ H-rivals and $b$ L-rivals above" relative to the all-below term:

$$W_HG_H=\Pi\!\!\sum_{a+b\le k-1}\!\!\binom{h-1}{a}\binom{\ell}{b}S_H^aS_L^b,\qquad W_LG_L=\Pi\!\!\sum_{a+b\le k-1}\!\!\binom{h}{a}\binom{\ell-1}{b}S_H^aS_L^b.$$

Subtract and apply Pascal's rule $\binom{h}{a}=\binom{h-1}{a}+\binom{h-1}{a-1}$, $\binom{\ell}{b}=\binom{\ell-1}{b}+\binom{\ell-1}{b-1}$ to the coefficient:

$$\binom{h}{a}\binom{\ell-1}{b}-\binom{h-1}{a}\binom{\ell}{b} =\binom{h-1}{a-1}\binom{\ell-1}{b}-\binom{h-1}{a}\binom{\ell-1}{b-1}.$$

Reindex the two pieces ($a\!\mapsto\!a\!-\!1$ in the first, $b\!\mapsto\!b\!-\!1$ in the second); both collapse onto the simplex $a+b\le k-2$ and pull out a common factor:

$$W_LG_L-W_HG_H=\Pi\,(S_H-S_L)\!\!\sum_{a+b\le k-2}\!\!\binom{h-1}{a}\binom{\ell-1}{b}S_H^aS_L^b .$$

Two simplifications finish it. First $S_H-S_L=\dfrac{1-G_H}{G_H}-\dfrac{1-G_L}{G_L} =\dfrac{G_L-G_H}{G_HG_L}$. Second, the remaining sum is exactly the once-smaller win probability: $\;G_H^{\,h-1}G_L^{\,\ell-1}\sum_{a+b\le k-2}\binom{h-1}{a}\binom{\ell-1}{b}S_H^aS_L^b =\widehat W^{\,(k-1)}_{\,h-1,\,\ell-1}$. Hence

$$W_LG_L-W_HG_H=\Pi\cdot\frac{G_L-G_H}{G_HG_L}\cdot\frac{\widehat W^{\,(k-1)}_{\,h-1,\,\ell-1}}{G_H^{\,h-1}G_L^{\,\ell-1}} =(G_L-G_H)\,\widehat W^{\,(k-1)}_{\,h-1,\,\ell-1}. \qquad\blacksquare$$

3. Reading it

  • $k=1$. $\widehat W^{(0)}=0$ (zero prizes â‡’ never win), so the RHS vanishes and you recover the symmetric case $W_HG_H=W_LG_L=G_H^{\,h}G_L^{\,\ell}$.
  • $k=2$. $\widehat W^{(1)}_{h-1,\ell-1}=G_H^{\,h-1}G_L^{\,\ell-1}$ (everyone below), so $$W_LG_L-W_HG_H=(G_L-G_H)\,G_H^{\,h-1}G_L^{\,\ell-1}.$$
  • General $k$. The difference is driven by the group-CDF gap $G_L-G_H$, scaled by the smaller contest's win probability â€” a strictly positive weight on $(0,\bar b)$. So the sign of $W_LG_L-W_HG_H$ is the sign of $G_L-G_H$ throughout the support.

5. Participation in $G$ and $W$, not $g$

The drop-out (participation) result is usually closed by showing a bid density goes negative, $g_L<0$. One would rather argue in the integrated objects $G,W$. The point is that density-positivity is not an incidental technicality one swaps out â€” it is the economic primitive wearing a derivative:

$$g_i\ge0\iff G_i\text{ nondecreasing}\iff \varphi_i=F_i^{-1}(G_i)\text{ increasing}\iff \text{higher types bid higher (sorting).}$$

So "participation in $G$" is the honest statement "$G_L$ would have to turn down near $\bar b$, and a CDF cannot decrease" â€” same content as $g_L<0$, said about $G_L$. To make the FOC act on $G$ rather than $g$, integrate it; the natural integrated form is the envelope / bid identity

$$b\;=\;\varphi_i(b)\,W_i(b)\;-\;\int_{\underline b_i}^{b}W_i(\beta)\,\varphi_i'(\beta)\,d\beta\;-\;U_i(\underline v_i).$$

Here the only derivative is the type sorting rate $\varphi_i'$, and the only requirement is $\varphi_i'\ge0$ = sorting = $G_i$ monotone. This is as close to "$G,W$ only" as one can get: every derivative is pushed onto the well-behaved monotone $\varphi_i$, with $W_i,G_i$ as the integrated actors. A fully derivative-free contradiction is presumably impossible â€” optimality is inherently first-order â€” but the bid density $g_i$ need never be named.

The reframing of drop-out. Combine the two faces. At a common top both $W_iG_i\to\Pi_k\to1$, so the corrections $(1-G_i)W_i^{(k-1)}\to0$; the whole question is which group's tail correction dies faster. Participation of $L$ up to $\bar b$ requires its tail $1-G_L$ to survive alongside $1-G_H$; intimidation ought to be exactly the condition that makes $1-G_L$ extinguish first â€” $L$ drops out at some $\bar b_L<\bar b_H$. That is "participation" as a tail-extinction statement in $G$ and $W$, with no appeal to the sign of a density.
