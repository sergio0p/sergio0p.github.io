Stieltjes integration by parts — reference and tricks

A working reference for Riemann–Stieltjes integration by parts and the manoeuvres it unlocks when the integrator is a CDF. For the specific $\int F_H\,dF_H^{h-1}$ step in the drop-out proof, see the companion stieltjes-by-parts.html; this file is the general toolbox.

1. The integral, in one line

For an integrand $g$ and an integrator $\alpha$ of bounded variation,

$$\int_a^b g\,d\alpha \;=\; \lim_{\|P\|\to 0}\sum_i g(\xi_i)\,\bigl[\alpha(x_i)-\alpha(x_{i-1})\bigr],$$

so $g$ is weighted by the increments of $\alpha$, not by $ds$. Three regimes in one notation: if $\alpha$ has density $\alpha'=a$, then $d\alpha=a\,ds$ and it is an ordinary integral $\int g\,a\,ds$; if $\alpha$ jumps by $\Delta\alpha(c)= \alpha(c^{+})-\alpha(c^{-})$ at $c$, that point contributes $g(c)\,\Delta\alpha(c)$; a mixed $\alpha$ simply adds the two. Writing $dF$ instead of $f\,ds$ is what lets a proof handle densities, atoms, and mixtures with one symbol.

Endpoint convention matters. For a right-continuous CDF use the half-open interval: $\int_{(a,b]} dF = F(b)-F(a)$. The notation $\int_a^b$ is ambiguous about whether a jump sitting on an endpoint is counted — when atoms are in play, write $(a,b]$ or $[a,b]$ explicitly.

2. Integration by parts — the continuous case

If $u$ and $v$ are of bounded variation and share no point of discontinuity (in particular, if either is continuous),

$$\int_a^b u\,dv \;+\; \int_a^b v\,du \;=\; u(b)v(b)-u(a)v(a)\;=\;[uv]_a^b.$$

Symmetric in $u,v$ — the two integrals split the boundary term $[uv]_a^b$ between them. Reading it as $\int u\,dv = [uv]_a^b-\int v\,du$ recovers the schoolbook rule. Almost every trick below is this identity with a clever choice of which factor is $u$ and which is $dv$.

3. Integration by parts — atom-safe

When $u$ and $v$ may jump at the same point, the symmetric formula needs a correction. For right-continuous BV functions on $(a,b]$, the clean asymmetric form evaluates one factor from the left ($u(t^{-})$):

$$u(b)v(b)-u(a)v(a)\;=\;\int_{(a,b]} u(t^{-})\,dv(t)\;+\;\int_{(a,b]} v(t)\,du(t).$$

The fully symmetric version carries the jump-product sum explicitly, with $\Delta u(t)=u(t)-u(t^{-})$:

$$u(b)v(b)-u(a)v(a)\;=\;\int_{(a,b]} u(t^{-})\,dv\;+\;\int_{(a,b]} v(t^{-})\,du\;+\!\!\sum_{aThe lone $\sum\Delta u\,\Delta v$ is precisely what the continuous-case rule silently assumes away. This is the "stochastic integration by parts" used for mixed distributions and discrete-continuous hybrids.

4. Trick — the survival/tail formula

The single most used consequence. For $X\ge 0$ with $\bar F=1-F$,

$$\mathbb E[X]=\int_0^\infty x\,dF(x)=\int_0^\infty \bar F(x)\,dx.$$

By parts on $[0,b]$: $\int_0^b x\,dF=[x\,F]_0^b-\int_0^b F\,dx = bF(b)-\int_0^b F\,dx = \int_0^b\bar F\,dx - b\bar F(b)$. The boundary term $b\bar F(b)\to 0$ exactly when the mean is finite — that vanishing is the whole content of the identity, and the place sloppy proofs go wrong. On the real line: $\mathbb E[X]=\int_0^\infty\bar F\,dx-\int_{-\infty}^0 F\,dx$.

Layer-cake generalization: for $\phi$ with $\phi(0)=0$ and $\phi$ absolutely continuous, $\mathbb E[\phi(X)]=\int_0^\infty \phi'(x)\,\bar F(x)\,dx$ for $X\ge0$ — replace "area under $\bar F$" by "$\phi'$-weighted area."

5. Trick — powers of a CDF (order statistics)

If $X_1,\dots,X_n$ are iid with CDF $F$, the maximum $M_n=\max_i X_i$ has CDF $F^n$, and

$$d\bigl(F^n\bigr)=n\,F^{n-1}\,dF,\qquad \mathbb E[M_n]=\int s\,dF^{n}(s)=\int_0^\infty\bigl(1-F^{n}\bigr)\,dx.$$

The differential rule $dF^{n}=nF^{n-1}dF$ is the chain rule inside the Stieltjes integral, valid whenever $F$ is continuous; it is what turns $\int s\,dF_H^{h-1}$ (expected top of $h-1$ rivals) into something integrable in closed form. By parts, $\int_a^b s\,dF^{n}=bF^{n}(b)-aF^{n}(a)-\int_a^b F^{n}\,ds$ — the move behind the Proposition 1 boundary manipulation.

6. Trick — the probability integral transform

Substituting $u=F(x)$ (legitimate when $F$ is continuous, so the interval maps continuously onto a $u$-interval) turns any CDF-integral into a plain integral over $[0,1]$:

$$\int g(x)\,dF(x)=\int_0^1 g\bigl(F^{-1}(u)\bigr)\,du,\qquad U=F(X)\sim\mathrm U[0,1].$$

This is why $\int F^{h-1}\,dF=\int_0^1 u^{h-1}\,du=\tfrac1h$ collapses instantly: the integrand became a monomial. Whenever the integrand is a function of $F$ itself, transform first and integrate a polynomial.

7. Trick — hazard and reverse-hazard differentials

Two logarithmic-derivative identities convert rate conditions into integrals and back. With forward hazard $r=f/\bar F$ and reverse hazard $\tilde r=f/F$,

$$dF=F\,\tilde r\,dx,\qquad d\!\ln F=\tilde r\,dx,\qquad \bar F(x)=\exp\!\Bigl(-\!\int_0^x r(t)\,dt\Bigr).$$

The reverse-hazard form is the bridge to the star-order footnote: since $\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln\dfrac{F(x)}{x}=\tilde r(x)-\dfrac1x$, the ratio $F(x)/x$ is non-increasing exactly when $\tilde r\le 1/x$. Monotone-ratio hypotheses and hazard-rate hypotheses are the same statement read through this differential.

8. Trick — partial expectations and truncation

By parts on a finite window gives the conditional-tail building blocks directly:

$$\int_a^b s\,dF(s)=[s\,F]_a^b-\int_a^b F\,ds,\qquad \mathbb E\!\left[X\mathbf 1_{\{X>a\}}\right]=a\,\bar F(a)+\int_a^\infty\bar F\,dx.$$

The second is the survival trick truncated at $a$; dividing by $\bar F(a)$ yields $\mathbb E[X\mid X>a]$ without touching a density. The same skeleton gives the expected-shortfall and incomplete-moment formulas used throughout auction and insurance calculations.

9. Trick — Hoeffding's covariance identity

A two-dimensional integration by parts expresses covariance through the gap between the joint CDF and the product of the marginals:

$$\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\!\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \bigl[F_{XY}(x,y)-F_X(x)F_Y(y)\bigr]\,dx\,dy.$$

Useful because it reduces a correlation question to a sign question about $F_{XY}-F_XF_Y$ (the basis of association orders), with no densities required.

10. Pitfalls

— Shared atoms. The continuous-case rule of §2 drops the $\sum\Delta u\,\Delta v$ term; if $u$ and $v$ jump together, use §3 or the answer is wrong by that sum.

— Endpoint jumps. $\int_{(a,b]}$ and $\int_{[a,b]}$ differ by $g(a)\,\Delta F(a)$. Fix the convention before evaluating boundary terms.

— Vanishing boundary term. The tail formula $\mathbb E[X]=\int\bar F$ needs $b\bar F(b)\to0$; this is a hypothesis (finite mean), not automatic.

— Transform validity. The substitution $u=F(x)$ of §6 needs $F$ continuous; an atom makes $u=F(x)$ skip an interval and breaks the closed form.
