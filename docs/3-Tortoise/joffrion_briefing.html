The Tortoise paper — where it stands, and where it's going

An onboarding briefing for Justin · drafted June 2026 · Docs/joffrion_briefing.html

Short version: the paper you last saw (the 2014/15 GEB submission, JoffrionParreiras14.tex) and the current draft (JoffrionParreiras26.tex) share a skeleton but tell a different story. The multiplicity result is now constructive, the multiplicity is now explained (not just exhibited), and the comparative statics is now quantified. That shift is what puts JET on the table — and supersedes the May/June editorial plan that defaulted to Economic Theory.

1 · How we're selling it now

The pitch rests on three hooks, in order of how much referees will remember them.

① Conceptual — why multiplicity, structurally

The static all-pay auction has a unique equilibrium payoff because it is strategically zero-sum in the sense of Moulin–Vial (1978): by their Theorem 3 no correlation device can lift both players above it; Pavlov (2025) sharpens this to "the Nash equilibrium is the unique correlated equilibrium," and Hwang–Rey-Bellet (2020) give the orthogonal identical-interest / zero-sum / nonstrategic decomposition. Our penultimate-round game breaks that property: once the magnitude of the lead a player carries into the last round enters the rival's payoff, the continuation payoff is kinked in the lead, the game is no longer a separable (strategically-zero-sum) contest, and the uniqueness logic collapses. Multiplicity is the consequence, not an accident. This framing is entirely new relative to 2014/15 and is the conceptual headline.

② Methodological — one engine builds the whole family

The "comb march": a single forward recursion in one scalar shooting parameter $q_1$ that constructs every finite-support equilibrium $\sigma(m,n)$ one effort level at a time, for general polynomial costs. The same recursion builds each equilibrium, ranks them (rent dissipation rises with support size), and locates the thresholds at which an equilibrium ceases to exist. The equilibria are not merely shown to exist — they're computed by a transparent procedure, with the competitive limit emerging as $m\to\infty$. This is "the comb-march unifying the family," and it's the single biggest difference from the old paper. A technique that travels is what separates JET from a strong field placement.

③ Economic — a disciplined rent-dissipation spectrum

Equilibria are classified by rent dissipation: collusive at one pole (one player builds a lead, the other slacks and earns nothing — and strikingly the leader need not be the favorite), the unique competitive equilibrium at the other (continuous randomization, full dissipation, both earn zero), and a ranked family in between. Comparative statics then disciplines the set — "anything goes" is false. Lowering the effort elasticity kills the low-dissipation end; raising valuation asymmetry or an initial lead kills the high-dissipation end. The boldest economic sentence — full rent dissipation at the competitive pole, with the family marching toward it — is backed by payoff convergence $u_1^*\sim C/m^2\to 0$, which we have.

Venue
GEB is closed (rejected, YGAME-D-14-00309). The May/June strategy doc defaulted to Economic Theory with the monotonicity as a conjecture and judged JET "probably not enough alone." That read predates the constructive engine. With the recursion as a methodological contribution and the monotonicity reduced to a single lemma (verified exactly through $(5,5)$), JET is now a credible target — that's the current ambition, and it's the reason to fold the new machinery into the body.

4 · The engine, in one screen

So you have a concrete feel for what "the comb march" is — symmetric quadratic case ($v_1=v_2=1,\ \Delta=0,\ c(e)=e^2$), then how it generalizes.

Supports interleave

P2's atoms and P1's atoms alternate along a comb, $$y_1

Atoms are ratios, masses are gaps

With gap coordinates $d_j=y_{j+1}-x_j,\ \delta_j=x_j-y_j$, $$q_{j+1}=(1{+}Q_j)\,d_j(d_j{+}2),\quad p_j=(1{+}P_{j-1})\,\delta_j(\delta_j{+}2),$$ so mass positivity $\iff$ interleaving — existence is read straight off the comb.

One dial closes it

The whole sweep is driven by $q_1$ (P2's mass at $0$). One normalization fixes the leftover top mass; the other is a single scalar residual $F(q_1)=0$, solved by bisection. The apex payoff is closed-form: $$u_1^*=\frac{q_1^2}{1+q_1}\quad(\text{quadratic}),$$ $$u_1^*=q_1\!\left[1-(1+\rho)^{1-\theta}\right],\ \ \rho=q_1^{1/(\theta-1)}\ \ (\text{cost-general}).$$

Monotonicity, reduced

Since $u_1^*$ rises in $q_1$, the chain decline is exactly "$q_1^*$ falls along the chain," and the new atom's mass equals $-F_{\text{prev}}(q_1)$. So the entire main theorem collapses to one lemma: $F'(q_1)>0$ (single-crossing).

What it unifies: the same march runs for quadratic symmetric (closed form per step), quadratic asymmetric — the whole $(v_1,v_2,\Delta)$ asymmetry collapses into one shift $f=w_2-w_1-\Delta$ entering only the boundary atom and P2's translated cost — and polynomial $\theta\ge3$ (each step becomes a 1-D degree-$(\theta{-}1)$ root solve). That single procedure spanning the whole table is the "unifying" claim.
