Dead-zone obstruction: equilibrium structure in $\Gamma(\Delta, v, v)$ when $\Delta > 0$

Two-round dynamic contest of Joffrion–Parreiras with equal valuations $v_1 = v_2 = v$ and entering lead $\Delta > 0$ for player 1. We derive a unilateral constraint on player 2's strategy — valid for any player-1 strategy — and work out its implications for the structure of equilibria (continuous, discrete, or mixed). Source: Docs/joffrion_part_I_v12.md (Lemma 1, Prop. 2, Prop. 5) and Docs/proof_ideas.md.

Setup

Each player chooses round-1 effort $e_i \geq 0$ at cost $c(e_i)$, where $c$ is strictly increasing, convex, with $c(0)=0$. Set $w = c^{-1}(v)$. The round-2 (last round) is a static contest with leads; by Lemma 1, player 2's round-2 continuation value as a function of the round-2 lead $\Delta_2 = \Delta + e_1 - e_2$ is

$$ U^T_2(\Delta_2 \mid v, v) \;=\; \begin{cases} 0 & \Delta_2 \geq 0,\\ v - c(w + \Delta_2) & -w \leq \Delta_2 \leq 0,\\ v & \Delta_2 \leq -w. \end{cases} $$

Under symmetric values, player 2 gets zero round-2 payoff whenever player 1 still leads ($\Delta_2 \geq 0$).

Player 2's round-1 expected payoff at effort $e_2$, against any player-1 strategy $G_1$ supported on $[0,\infty)$, is

$$ U_2(e_2) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{e_1 \sim G_1}\bigl[U^T_2(\Delta + e_1 - e_2 \mid v,v)\bigr] \;-\; c(e_2). $$

This is not a Siegel-style payoff: $e_1$ enters through the smooth continuation $U^T_2$ via the lead $\Delta_2$, not through a 0/1 win indicator.

The dead-zone lemma

Lemma. For any $\Delta > 0$, $v > 0$, cost $c$, and any $G_1$ on $[0,\infty)$: $$ U_2(e_2) \;=\; -\,c(e_2) \qquad \text{for all } e_2 \in [0,\, \Delta]. $$ In particular $U_2(0) = 0$ and $U_2(e_2) < 0$ for $e_2 \in (0, \Delta]$.
Proof. For $e_2 \in [0,\Delta]$ and any $e_1 \geq 0$: $\;\Delta + e_1 - e_2 \geq \Delta - e_2 \geq 0$, so $U^T_2(\Delta + e_1 - e_2) = 0$ pointwise. Hence $\mathbb{E}_{G_1}[U^T_2(\cdots)] = 0$ and $U_2(e_2) = -c(e_2)$. ∎
Corollary. Every $e_2 \in (0,\Delta]$ is strictly dominated by $e_2 = 0$, for any $G_1$. Therefore in any Nash equilibrium: $G_2$ assigns zero mass to $(0,\Delta)$ — neither continuous density nor atoms — and player 2's equilibrium payoff satisfies $u_2^* \geq U_2(0) = 0$.

Structure of equilibrium strategies

P2's forced gap

The corollary implies $\mathrm{supp}(G_2) \subseteq \{0\} \cup ({\Delta}, \infty)$. In fact the gap is slightly wider: $U_2(\Delta) = \mathbb{E}_{G_1}[U^T_2(e_1)] - c(\Delta)$. Since $e_1 \geq 0$ gives $U^T_2(e_1) = 0$, we get $U_2(\Delta) = -c(\Delta) < 0$, so $\Delta$ itself is not in P2's support either. Defining $a^* = \inf(\mathrm{supp}(G_2) \setminus \{0\})$, we have $a^* > \Delta$.

The location of $a^*$ is determined by the indifference condition at the lower boundary of P2's active region:

$$ \mathbb{E}_{e_1 \sim G_1}\bigl[U^T_2(e_1 - a^* + \Delta)\bigr] \;=\; u_2^* + c(a^*). $$

The left side is positive only when P1 has mass below $a^* - \Delta$ (i.e. the overtake event). For $a^*$ near $\Delta$ this probability is small and the equation cannot balance $c(a^*) > 0$; $a^*$ must therefore be bounded away from $\Delta$ by an amount that depends on $G_1$'s mass near zero.

Two cases: $u_2^* = 0$ vs. $u_2^* > 0$

$u_2^* = 0$. Action $e_2 = 0$ is a best response (though P2 need not play it). P2's support is a subset of $\{0\} \cup [a^*, b]$ with $a^* > \Delta$. The atom mass $p_0 = G_2(\{0\}) \geq 0$ — "concede with probability $p_0$" — is a free parameter to be pinned by P1's indifference.

$u_2^* > 0$. Action $e_2 = 0$ yields payoff $0 < u_2^*$, so P2 never plays 0. Her support lies entirely in $[a^*, b]$ with $a^* > \Delta$. For the indifference condition to hold at $a^*$ at a positive level, P1 must place sufficient mass below $a^* - \Delta$. In equilibrium this couples P1's strategy to the location of $a^*$.

Candidate P2 strategy structures

  1. Discrete (atoms only). $G_2 = \sum_l \tilde p_l \,\delta_{y_l}$ with $y_1 = 0$ and $y_l \geq a^* > \Delta$ for $l \geq 2$. This is the $\sigma(m,n)$ family — the paper's main equilibrium object. The interleaving constraint $x_j + \Delta < y_{j+1}$ is the discrete restatement of the gap.
  2. Mixed: atom at 0 plus continuous density on $[a^*, b]$. $G_2 = p_0\,\delta_0 + (1-p_0)\,H_2$ with $H_2$ absolutely continuous on $[a^*, b]$. Indifference of P2 on $[a^*, b]$ gives an ODE for the density $h_2$; P1's strategy $G_1$ must simultaneously satisfy P1's indifference. Natural candidate for a "continuous limit" of the $\sigma(m,n)$ chain.
  3. Continuous on $[a^*, b]$ with $u_2^* > 0$ (no atom at 0). P2 never concedes. The existence of such an equilibrium — whether some $G_1$, $G_2$ pair with these properties satisfies mutual indifference — is the open question in the $(v_1 = v_2, \Delta > 0)$ case; the dead-zone argument does not rule it out.

What the dead zone immediately rules out

Any strategy $G_2$ that is continuous with $G_2(0) = 0$ places positive density immediately above $0$, hence mass in $(0, \varepsilon)$ for all $\varepsilon > 0$. Some of that mass falls in $(0, \min(\varepsilon, \Delta))$, which is dominated. Contradiction.

This dispenses in particular with the symmetric competitive equilibrium $G_1 = G_2 = G$ with $G(0) = 0$ (Proposition 2 of the paper). Its failure under $\Delta > 0$ is unsurprising — the environment is asymmetric, so identical strategies are not the natural equilibrium object — but the dead-zone gives the clean mechanism.

Illustration

Fix $c(x) = x^2$, $v = 1$, $\Delta = 0.1$. Taking as a specific $G_1$ the $\Delta = 0$ competitive CDF $G_0(x) = e^x - 1$ on $[0, \ln 2]$, the integral $U_2(e_2)$ evaluates in closed form: $$ U_2(e_2) \;=\; \begin{cases} -\,e_2^2 & 0 \leq e_2 \leq \Delta, \\[3pt] \Delta(\Delta - 2\,e_2) & \Delta \leq e_2 \leq \Delta + \ln 2. \end{cases} $$ (Second piece: $\int_0^{a}[1-(1-u)^2]e^{a-u}\,du = a^2$ with $a = e_2 - \Delta$.) $U_2$ is negative throughout except at $e_2 = 0$, so $G_0$ cannot be P1's equilibrium strategy when $\Delta > 0$ — a concrete illustration of the dead-zone obstruction.

$U_2(e_2)$ computed against $G_1 = G_0$ (the $\Delta=0$ competitive CDF) with $\Delta = 0.1$. The shaded red band is the dead zone $(0,\Delta)$ where $U_2 = -e_2^2$ regardless of $G_1$. Beyond $\Delta$ the payoff continues to be negative under $G_0$, confirming $G_0$ is not an equilibrium strategy for P1 when $\Delta > 0$.
Schematic anatomy of a candidate P2 strategy. Atom at $e_2 = 0$ (mass $p_0$, "concede"); forced gap $(0, a^*)$ with $a^* > \Delta$ (no mass from dead-zone corollary); continuous density on $[a^*, b]$. In the discrete $\sigma(m,n)$ family the continuous part is replaced by atoms at $y_2 < \cdots < y_n$.

Remarks

Asymmetric valuations ($v_1 \neq v_2$). Proposition 5 of the paper handles the asymmetric-values case via an ODE argument. P2's indifference on her continuous support gives a linear ODE for $G_1$; the explicit solution is $$ G_1(x) \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{v_1}+\Delta}{\sqrt{v_2}-\underline{e}_1}\;\exp\!\Bigl(\tfrac{x-\underline{e}_1}{\sqrt{v_2}}\Bigr)-1. $$ The top-of-support condition $G_i(\overline{e}_i) = 1$ combined with the support-difference constraint (Lemma 3: $\overline{e}_2 - \overline{e}_1 = \underline{e}_2 - \underline{e}_1 = \sqrt{v_1}+\Delta-\sqrt{v_2}$) yields a transcendental equation with no valid solution. The dead zone plays the same role with $w_1 - w_2 = \sqrt{v_1} - \sqrt{v_2}$ in place of $\Delta$.

The open case: asymmetric continuous, $v_1 = v_2$, $\Delta > 0$. Neither the dead-zone lemma nor Prop. 5 closes candidate structure (3) above — a continuous $G_2$ on $[a^*, b]$ with $a^* > \Delta$ and $u_2^* > 0$. Transporting the Prop. 5 ODE machinery to the degenerate $v_1 = v_2$ boundary, or constructing an explicit obstruction, is the open problem in this regime.

Takeaway. The dead-zone lemma is a unilateral constraint on P2: for any $G_1$, any effort in $(0, \Delta]$ is dominated by $e_2 = 0$. This forces a gap $(0, a^*)$ with $a^* > \Delta$ in P2's strategy, and either an atom at 0 or a positive equilibrium payoff. The real content is the structural constraint on all candidates — discrete, continuous, or mixed — that survive: forced gap $(0, a^*)$ with $a^* > \Delta$, and either an atom at 0 or a positive equilibrium payoff for P2.
