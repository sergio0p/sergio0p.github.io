The MC(0) dial

Marginal cost at the origin, $c'(0)$, as a new existence floor in the σ(m,n) family — and what it really does as the cost is made affine. Joffrion & Parreiras (Tortoise). 2026‑06‑24 (revised, wider‑range rerun).

Summary. Give the cost a linear part so the marginal cost of the very first unit of effort is strictly positive, $c'(0)=\mathrm{MC}(0)>0$ (pure powers have $c'(0)=0$). This single number is a new dial, independent of the exponent $\theta$, the lead $\Delta$, and the value gap $v_1/v_2$. It imposes an exact floor on player 2's boundary mass, $q_1 > c'(0)/c'(w)$. Correction to the earlier draft: this floor does not truncate the family at finite friction. For every finite friction level, all rungs $\sigma(m,n)$ exist; their boundary mass $q_1^*$ approaches the floor from above as $(m,n)$ grows — the margin $q_1^*-\text{floor}$ shrinks toward zero but never crosses. The friction degenerates the family ($q_1^*\!\to$ floor, payoffs $u_1^*\!\to 0$ extremely fast); it collapses to $\sigma(1,1)$ only in the strict limit where the cost becomes affine, $c'(0)/c'(w)\to1$. The earlier "finite death table / $N^\star(\mu)$ staircase" was a numerical resolution artifact.
What changed and why. The previous version of this note claimed the high $(m,n)$ rungs die at finite $\mu$ (a death table: $(2,2)$ at $\mu\approx8$, etc.). A rerun over a wider parameter range with adaptive grid refinement shows those were points where the admissible $q_1$‑window fell below the dial step dstep, not where the rung ceased to exist: refine the grid and every "dead" rung comes back alive, with $q_1^*$ sitting just above the floor. The floor mechanism is real and exact; the finite truncation was not.

All experiments symmetric — $v_1=v_2=1$, $\Delta=0$ — run with the general‑convex comb march Julia/converge/convex_march.jl.

1. The setup and the question

The project's discrete equilibrium family $\sigma(m,n)$ — player 1 mixing over $m$ effort atoms, player 2 over $n$, on an interleaved "comb" — is built and ranked under pure power costs $c(e)=e^\theta$, whose marginal cost at zero vanishes ($c'(e)=\theta e^{\theta-1}\to0$). Every standard knob keeps that property:

  • θ the exponent (curvature) — Prop 4 existence threshold $\theta^\star=\log2/\log(4/3)\approx2.409$;
  • Δ the lead — death at $\Delta^\star(2,2)\approx0.211$ (support‑merge);
  • v₁/v₂ the value gap — death at $v_1^\star(2,2)\approx1.417$ (apex collapse).

The new question: what if the marginal cost at the origin is strictly positive? We turn that knob two ways, both still strictly convex with $c(0)=0$ (so all the comb machinery applies unchanged):

$$\textbf{(A)}\quad c(e)=e^2+\mu e:\quad \text{fix curvature }=1,\ \text{raise the toll }c'(0)=\mu.$$ $$\textbf{(B)}\quad c(e)=\tfrac{1}{k}e^2+e:\quad \text{fix the toll }c'(0)=1,\ \text{shrink curvature }\tfrac1k\ \text{so } c\to\text{linear as }k\to\infty.$$

Family (B) is the clean way to watch "cost $\to$ linear" while holding $c'(0)$ pinned. Both drive the ratio $c'(0)/c'(w)\to1$ in their affine limit.

2. The mechanism: an exact floor on the boundary mass

The comb march builds player 1's smallest atom $x_1$ first, from the first‑node first‑order condition (symmetric case $w_1=w_2=w$, $f=0$): $$q_1\, c'(w-x_1) \;=\; c'(x_1),$$ where $q_1$ is player 2's mass on the boundary atom at $0$ and $w=c^{-1}(v)$ is the reach. The residual $g(x)=c'(x)-q_1\,c'(w-x)$ is strictly increasing, with $g(w^-)=c'(w)-q_1c'(0)>0$ always. So an interior root $x_1$ exists exactly when $g(0^+)<0$:

$$g(0^+) = c'(0) - q_1\,c'(w) < 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \boxed{\,q_1 \;>\; \dfrac{c'(0)}{c'(w)}\;=\;\dfrac{\mathrm{MC}(0)}{\mathrm{MC}(w)}\,.}$$

For pure powers $c'(0)=0$, the floor is $0$ and the condition is vacuous — the known case. For $c'(0)>0$ the floor is strictly positive. This is a conditional on $q_1$. Whether a given rung clears it depends on the rung's equilibrium value of $q_1$, which §4 settles. Closed forms at $v=1$:

$$\textbf{(A)}\ \ q_1^{\text{floor}}(\mu)=\frac{\mu}{\sqrt{\mu^2+4}} \qquad\qquad \textbf{(B)}\ \ q_1^{\text{floor}}(k)=\frac{1}{c'(w)},\ \ c'(w)=\tfrac{2w}{k}+1,\ \ w=\tfrac{-k+\sqrt{k^2+4k}}{2}.$$

3. What the solver actually shows: approach, not truncation

The boundary mass $q_1^*$ is pinned by the equilibrium, and along the chain it decreases with rung size. The earlier draft read that decrease as "$q_1^*$ falls below the floor at some finite $(m,n)$, killing the rung." It does not. For every finite friction level, $q_1^*(m,m)$ decreases monotonically toward the floor from above, and the gap $q_1^*-\text{floor}$ shrinks to near zero without crossing:

Two readings of the same numbers. The $q_1^*$ column: a sequence packing into an ever‑thinner layer just above the floor — approaching it asymptotically, never reaching it. The $u_1^*$ column: this is where the "vanishing" really lives. Player 1's equilibrium payoff collapses many orders of magnitude as the cost gets more linear ($\sigma(5,5)$ at $k=8$ pays $u_1\approx1.7\times10^{-7}$), while the equilibrium itself formally persists. The friction does not delete rungs; it makes the high ones economically negligible.

Across the wide $k$‑range, square rungs survive as high as the grid can resolve: to $m=30$ for $k\le16$, and to $m=22$ / $m=16$ for $k=32$ / $k=64$ — and those last two are capped only because the $q_1$‑window drops below dstep$=10^{-6}$, not because a rung dies.

4. The "deaths" were a grid artifact — shown by revival

The decisive test: take a rung the old (coarse) runs reported dead, and refine the dial grid. If it were genuine nonexistence the rung would stay dead; instead it revives, with $q_1^*$ microscopically above the floor.

So the analytic floor is exact, but the old $\mu^\star(m,m)$ death values and the $N^\star(\mu)$ staircase marked where the admissible window fell below the grid step — not where existence ended. The honest statement is a limit, not a threshold: as $c'(0)/c'(w)\to1$ (i.e. $\mu\to\infty$ in (A), $k\to\infty$ in (B); the cost becomes affine), the gap for every $m\ge2$ rung is squeezed to zero and only $\sigma(1,1)$ ($q_1\equiv1$) can survive.

5. Survivors are genuine Nash; the right oracle

The comb march's post‑hoc global best‑response check certifies the revived rungs: across $(2,2),(3,3),(5,5)$ at $k=1$ and $(2,2),(4,4),(5,5)$ at $k=8$, both best‑response gains are $\sim10^{-17}$ — exact Nash.

A solver caution. The Newton outer solver in Julia/solver/all_sizes.jl gives false negatives on these costs: it seeds the outer Broyden loop from the pure‑quadratic GNE atoms, and as the cost gets linear that seed is far enough off that the loop collapses the support onto a lower rung (reporting nonexistence). The comb march with adequate dstep, plus its BR check, is the reliable existence oracle here — not the stale‑seeded Newton sweep.

6. It is MC(0), not the quadratic

The derivation used only $c'(0)$ and the monotonicity of $c'$, so the floor $q_1>c'(0)/c'(w)$ holds for any strictly convex cost. The cubic‑plus‑linear $c(e)=e^3+\mu e$ (floor $\mu/(3w^2+\mu)$) confirms it: at $\mu=3$ the floor is $0.9055$, and — correcting the earlier draft — all of $(3,3)$ through $(8,8)$ exist, with $q_1^*$ approaching $0.9055$ from above, exactly the same asymptotic squeeze. The relevant parameter is the marginal cost at the origin; the linear term is just the cleanest way to switch it on.

7. Contrast with the exponent dial θ

It is tempting to file MC(0) as "θ's mirror image — θ kills the small rungs, MC(0) kills the large ones." That symmetry is false, and the correction is exactly why. The threshold $\theta^\star$ is a genuine finite death: below it the small rungs $\sigma(1,1),\sigma(1,2)$ still solve their local system but lose Nash to a real upward deviation beyond the top atom — nonexistence at a finite parameter value. MC(0) is different in kind: an asymptotic squeeze with no finite death. It removes no particular rung at finite friction; it presses the whole family against the $q_1$‑floor, and only the affine limit extinguishes the $m\ge2$ rungs. Two independent dials acting at opposite ends of the chain — but one is a threshold, the other a limit.

8. Economic interpretation

Read the first‑node condition $q_1\,c'(w-x_1)=c'(x_1)$ as economics.

An entry toll versus a pivot probability

The right side is an entry toll. With $c'(0)>0$ the marginal cost of a tiny first effort is positive: effort near zero is no longer almost‑free — there is a non‑vanishing toll on participating at all in the low‑effort range. (Pure powers have $c'(0)=0$: the first unit is free, so anyone can dabble at the bottom for nothing.)

The left side is a pivot probability. A small $e_1$ is pivotal essentially only when the rival sits at the bottom, $e_2=0$ — an event of probability $q_1$. So $q_1\,c'(w)$ is the expected marginal return to a tiny effort, and a player participates at the bottom only if that return clears the toll.

In words: a player puts probability on tiny efforts only when the opponent stays at the bottom often enough that those tiny efforts are pivotal often enough to pay the entry toll. A positive $c'(0)$ makes small efforts a gamble that must pay.

Where the mass actually is

It is tempting to picture high‑$(m,n)$ equilibria as clustered near zero. The opposite is true: the bottom is where they are thinnest. The boundary atom shrinks toward the floor, the support climbs toward the competitive ceiling $\ln 2\approx0.693$, the interior atoms carry roughly equal mass ($\approx 1/m$ each) with spacing tightening upward — so local density rises toward the top. For the quadratic symmetric case the competitive equilibrium is

$$G(x)=e^{x}-1,\qquad g(x)=e^{x}\quad\text{on }[0,\ln 2],$$ a density that is strictly increasing — $g(0)=1$ at the bottom, $g(\ln 2)=2$ at the top. Effort near zero is the least populated region, not the most.

The match is sharp; for σ(6,6) the empirical interval density (mass / gap) versus $e^x$ at each interval midpoint:

The friction bites on the extensive margin — in the limit

The toll $c'(0)$ sits exactly where the equilibrium is thinnest — the lower tail, where the boundary atom is being pushed toward the floor. So it does not kill high $(m,n)$ by displacing probability; there is hardly any down there to displace. Instead it makes the thin lower tail worth less and less: the pivot return at the bottom is driven toward the toll, the rung's payoff $u_1^*$ collapses toward zero, and only in the affine limit does the willingness to maintain that tail disappear entirely. A positive $c'(0)$ kills cheap jockeying at the bottom — but as a limit, by starving it, not as a finite cull.

Corollary — an obstruction to competitive convergence (sharpened). The $\mu=0$ family converges to the competitive equilibrium with $q_1\to0$ (no atom at the bottom). A positive $c'(0)$ pins $q_1 \ge \text{floor} > 0$ for every rung. So the degenerate limit of the discrete family is floor‑pinned, never competitive: MC(0) is an obstruction to the $\sigma(m,n)\to$ competitive convergence that the frictionless family exhibits — the family approaches a positive‑$q_1$ wall instead of the zero‑$q_1$ competitive density.

9. Asymmetric values $v_1\neq v_2$ — a third dial, and the all‑pay limit

Repeating the $e^2/k+e$ linear‑limit sweep off symmetry ($v_1=1.2,\ v_2=1,\ \Delta=0$) gives a qualitatively different — and cleaner — picture. First, the value gap truncates the ladder by itself, with $k$ held fixed at 1:

These are genuine non‑closures, not grid artifacts: for (2,2) at $v_1=1.2$ the comb's termination function never crosses zero (nFcross=0, nroots=0) at every dstep down to 1e‑7 — qualitatively unlike the MC(0) "window<dstep" deaths of §4, which revive on refinement. The value asymmetry simply forbids the comb from closing. So the value gap is a third dial: a genuine finite truncator (like the exponent $\theta$ of §7, unlike MC(0)).

Then $k$ removes the last non‑trivial rung. Of the two survivors at $v_1=1.2$, $\sigma(1,2)$ lives only to $k\approx 2$ (it dies genuinely — nFcross=0 — between $k=1.5$ and $k=2$, not a window artifact), while $\sigma(1,1)$ survives for all $k$. So for any large $k$, $\sigma(1,1)$ is the unique equilibrium (already alone past $k\approx2$).

The surviving payoff is the all‑pay rent. $\sigma(1,1)$'s payoff as cost $\to$ linear:

The linear‑cost limit collapses the whole dynamic contest to the complete‑information all‑pay auction: the high‑value player wins, keeps exactly the value gap $v_1-v_2$, and the low‑value player is fully dissipated to 0.

This sharpens the symmetric result rather than contradicting it. Symmetric is the knife‑edge $v_1=v_2$ of the same rule $u_1\to v_1-v_2$: there the limit rent is 0 (full dissipation) and — because the gap is zero — the ladder is not truncated at finite $k$ (all rungs persist to the resolution ceiling, each separately degenerating). Off symmetry the gap does two things at once: it genuinely truncates the ladder to $\{(1,1),(1,2)\}$ even at $k=1$, and it leaves a positive limit rent $v_1-v_2$ for $\sigma(1,1)$. (This also settles §10's prior open item on the asymmetric floor: with $v_1>v_2$ the binding obstruction off symmetry is not the $q_1$‑floor but comb non‑closure.)

10. Caveats and open ends

  • Resolution, not death. Near the floor the admissible $q_1$‑window has near‑zero measure, so any fixed dial grid eventually reports nonexistence. That is a grid limit, not a result — always refine dstep before claiming a rung is gone. (The old default 4e‑3, even 3e‑4, badly under‑resolves.)
  • The asymptote is numerical, not proven. "$q_1^*(m,m)\to\text{floor}^+$, never crossing at finite friction" rests on monotone‑positive gaps out to the resolution ceiling, not a theorem; it reduces to the open closed form for $q_1^*(m,m)$. A genuine finite truncation at very large $(m,n)$ cannot be excluded by numerics alone.
  • Asymmetry (resolved, see §9). The off‑symmetry first‑node FOC is $q_1 c'(w_2-\Delta-x_1)=c'(x_1)$, with floor $q_1>c'(0)/c'(w_2-\Delta)$. But for $v_1>v_2$ the binding obstruction turns out not to be this floor at all — it is the comb's failure to close (the value gap is a genuine finite truncator). The linear limit then yields the all‑pay auction rent $u_1\to v_1-v_2$, $u_2\to 0$.
  • Paper angle. The defensible result is a friction that degenerates the equilibrium family — collapsing payoffs, $q_1$ pinned to a positive floor — and obstructs competitive convergence, with full collapse to $\sigma(1,1)$ only in the affine limit. Not a finite top‑down truncation. It pairs with, but is structurally unlike, the $\theta^\star$ threshold.

11. Reproduce

// in the Julia MCP workspace (include into Main; refer via Main if needed)
Base.include(Main, "Julia/converge/convex_march.jl")
qk(k)  = PolyCost([0.0, 1.0, 1.0/k])      // (B) c(e) = e²/k + e   (→ linear as k→∞)
qmu(m) = PolyCost([0.0, m, 1.0])          // (A) c(e) = e² + μe

// ADAPTIVE dstep — refine until the (thin) window resolves, else you get FALSE deaths:
function alive(cost, m, n)
    for ds in (5e-4, 1e-4, 2e-5, 5e-6, 1e-6)
        r = solve_rung(cost=cost, v1=1, v2=1, Δ=0, m=m, n=n, dstep=ds, check=true)
        r.exists && return (true, r.q1, r.u1, r.br)   // r.br.g1, r.br.g2 ~ 1e-17 ⇒ Nash
    end
    return (false,)
end
