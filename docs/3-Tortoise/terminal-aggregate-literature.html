Repeated Play with a Terminal Aggregate — Literature Map

Session 2026-07-03, strategy/editorial mode. Citations are from model memory and NOT yet verified against the library — click a reference to run the UNC Articles+ search before citing. Companion to Docs/examples_first_interleaving_plan.md and Docs/paper_decisions.md.

Summary

The class — players play a finite sequence of identical stage games; total payoff = sum of stage payoffs plus a terminal payoff $g(x_T)$ evaluated at a summary statistic $x_T$ of past play (for us: the accumulated lead $\Delta$) — has no canonical named theory of its own. It sits at the intersection of four literatures: (i) discrete-time difference games in Bolza form supply the formal skeleton; (ii) Benoit–Krishna supplies the multiplicity logic (endgame correspondence as carrot and stick); (iii) Blackwell approachability is the zero-sum ancestor; (iv) dynamic contribution games and cumulative-effort tournaments are the economic cousins. In every smooth-terminal member of the class the equilibrium is (essentially) unique; the all-pay terminal function is kinked, and the kink turns the continuation function into a continuation correspondence. That contrast is the paper's abstract address.

1. The class, formally

Rounds $t = 1, \dots, T$. Each round the two contestants play the same stage action set with stage payoff $-c(e_{it})$, independent of the state. The state aggregates linearly, $\Delta_{t+1} = \Delta_t + e_{1t} - e_{2t}$, and enters payoffs only at the deadline, through the terminal continuation $g_i(\Delta_T)$. In control language this is a finite-horizon problem in Bolza form — running (Lagrange) cost plus terminal (Mayer) payoff — with a one-dimensional state that is a summary statistic of the action history. The pre-period $t \le T-2$ is a repeated game in the precise sense that stage payoffs and transitions are identical and translation-invariant; it is not a repeated game in the strict sense, because actions move the state that the terminal function prices.

Two structural facts frame everything below. First, the stage game alone (pure effort costs, no prize) has a unique and trivial Nash equilibrium — zero effort — so a strict repeated-game reading would force strategic death by backward induction; all pre-period richness is financed by the terminal term. Second, whether that financing produces uniqueness or multiplicity is decided entirely by the shape of $g$: smooth $g$ yields a continuation function and essentially unique feedback equilibria; the all-pay auction's kinked $g$ yields a continuation correspondence.

2. The formal skeleton — difference games in Bolza form

The dynamic-games/control tradition treats exactly this payoff shape: additive stage costs plus a terminal salvage value $g(x_T)$. The discrete-time ("multi-stage") half of Başar–Olsder is the relevant one — finite-horizon Markov games with a state variable, in engineering notation. The tradition's questions, however, are computational (saddle points, feedback laws, linear-quadratic Riccati solutions), not economic: with smooth concave terminal payoffs, backward induction delivers feedback equilibria essentially uniquely, and multiplicity is degenerate. The economist-friendly entry point is Dockner–Jørgensen–Long–Sorger. Cite this family for the taxonomy, one sentence's worth; the value of the citation is contrast, not machinery. The economics-native alternative phrasing: a finite-horizon stochastic game with deterministic transitions and a one-dimensional state (Maskin–Tirole for Markov-perfect language), with the paper's carrot-and-stick constructions deliberately non-Markov.

3. The multiplicity logic — Benoit–Krishna

Benoit–Krishna show that finitely repeated games escape backward-induction uniqueness precisely when the endgame offers multiple continuation payoffs to use as carrots and sticks. The dynamic contest transplants that mechanism: the $T$-shortened endgame (our two-round characterization) supplies a continuum of continuation equilibria indexed by the inherited lead $\Delta$ and by selection, and $\Delta$-dependent selection is the enforcement instrument. This is the honest sentence for the introduction: the penultimate-round equilibrium set is the building block, and its multiplicity proliferates backward in the manner of Benoit–Krishna — without promising a $T$-round characterization. It also accommodates the $T=3$ deterrence fact (a deterring effort above $w_2/3$) — the endgame correspondence disciplines, but does not mechanically replicate, pre-period play. Dutta's folk theorem for stochastic games is the state-variable analogue.

4. The zero-sum ancestor — Blackwell approachability

Blackwell's approachability problem is literally: repeated play with vector outcomes, where what matters is a function of the average (a summary statistic) of past play. Zero-sum and asymptotic, so an intellectual ancestor rather than a tool — but it is the one place where terminal evaluation of an aggregate of repeated play is the definition of the problem.

5. Economic cousins — two families

Dynamic contribution games. Per-period costly contributions; payoff from the cumulative stock; terminal/threshold payoff kinked at completion — which is exactly what generates their multiplicity and gradualism. Structurally the contest with a cooperative rather than adversarial terminal function: Admati–Perry, Marx–Matthews, Compte–Jehiel, Battaglini–Nunnari–Palfrey, Georgiadis.

Cumulative-effort tournaments. Identical rounds, convex per-round costs, prize awarded at the deadline on cumulative performance: Yildirim (complete information, Tullock success function), Aoyagi, Ederer, Gershkov–Perry, Meyer. The noise or contest-success function smooths the terminal payoff — and they get uniqueness. Hirata is the two-stage all-pay boundary case: cost of the total bid makes the intertemporal split payoff-irrelevant, so multiplicity is payoff-equivalent and the unique-payoff prediction survives.

Framing sentence for the paper: the model is a finitely repeated effort game with a terminal payoff evaluated at an aggregate of past play (Bolza form); the smooth-terminal members of this class — noisy tournaments, concave salvage values — deliver uniqueness; the contribution is the characterization of the terminal correspondence (the two-round equilibrium set) that any Benoit–Krishna-style analysis of the longer game must take as input.

Caveat: the exact pairing "repeated + terminal aggregate" may have an abstract treatment not recalled here; the Petrosyan (St. Petersburg) school studies multistage games with terminal payoffs, mostly in cooperative/zero-sum settings unlikely to help at ET/TE. 6. Rosetta stone — reading the control and CS dialects

The barrier with the differential/difference-games literature is terminology, not substance: the concepts are mostly familiar ones under lab-coat names. Restricted to what touches this paper:

feedback (closed-loop, memoryless) equilibrium $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ Markov perfect equilibrium

open-loop equilibrium $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ Nash in precommitted time paths — no subgame perfection off the path

closed-loop with memory $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ history-dependent SPE (the non-Markov carrot-and-stick constructions live here)

value function, HJB / Isaacs equation $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ continuation value, Bellman equation (Isaacs = the zero-sum case)

salvage / scrap / terminal value $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ terminal continuation payoff — our $g(\Delta_T)$

Bolza / Lagrange / Mayer form $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ flow payoff + terminal payoff / flow only / terminal only

costate (adjoint) variable, maximum principle $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ shadow price, Euler / first-order conditions

controls, state trajectory $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ actions, path of the state variable

game of kind vs. game of degree $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ who-wins (qualitative) vs. payoff-valued; the all-pay auction is a game of degree with a kink inherited from the game of kind underneath

The distinction that does real work: much of that literature reports open-loop equilibria because they are computable, and economists rightly discount those as non-credible. Its multiplicity discussions are usually open-loop vs. feedback giving different answers — not a feedback correspondence being fat, which is this paper's phenomenon. The vocabulary hides how little applies as much as what does.

Same story in the CS / algorithmic-game-theory dialect:

truthful / strategyproof $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ dominant-strategy incentive compatible

coarse correlated equilibrium $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ the Hannan set — what no-regret learning converges to; Blackwell approachability is equivalent to no-regret, reconnecting to §4

price of anarchy $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ worst-equilibrium welfare ratio

smoothness (Roughgarden) $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ a sufficient condition bounding the price of anarchy

online learning / bandits $\;\longleftrightarrow\;$ adaptive play / experimentation and learning

The CS contest thread (Colonel Blotto variants, sequential auctions with price-of-anarchy bounds) occasionally brushes near dynamic all-pay games, but asks welfare-bound questions rather than characterization.
