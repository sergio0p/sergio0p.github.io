σ(m,n) FOC Solver — Block Structure & Schur Reduction

Companion notes to Docs/numeric_solver_notes.md. Covers the σ(2,2) case worked out for both quadratic and general convex cost, then the (n,n) and (m,n) generalizations.

1. The Schur complement (refresher)

For a block matrix

$$ M \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{bmatrix}, \qquad A \text{ invertible}, $$

the Schur complement of $A$ in $M$ is

$$ M/A \;:=\; D - C\,A^{-1}\,B. $$

Block-elimination of the top variable $x$ in $\begin{bmatrix}A & B \\ C & D\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}b_x\\b_y\end{bmatrix}$ gives

$$ (D - C A^{-1} B)\,y \;=\; b_y - C A^{-1} b_x, \qquad x = A^{-1}(b_x - B\,y). $$

The payoff: the original $(m+n)$-dim system collapses to an $n$-dim system in $y$ alone — especially cheap when $A$ is diagonal so $A^{-1}$ is free.

2. σ(2,2), quadratic cost $c(e)=e^2$

Unknowns: $(x_1, x_2, y_2)$ with $y_1 = 0$. The FOC system is

  • P1 at $x_1$: $(1+q_1)\,x_1 \;=\; q_1(w-\Delta)$
  • P1 at $x_2$: $2\,x_2 - q_2\,y_2 \;=\; (w-\Delta)\quad$ [since $Q_2 = q_1+q_2 = 1$]
  • P2 at $y_2$: $-p_2\,x_2 + (1+p_2)\,y_2 \;=\; p_2(w+\Delta)$

Matrix form

$$ M \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} 1+q_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & -q_2 \\ 0 & -p_2 & 1+p_2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad b \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} q_1(w-\Delta) \\ w-\Delta \\ p_2(w+\Delta) \end{bmatrix}. $$

Partitioning rows/cols as $(x_1,x_2)\ |\ (y_2)$:

$$ A \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} 1+q_1 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad B \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ q_2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad C \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} 0 & p_2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad D \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} 1+p_2 \end{bmatrix}. $$

So $A = \mathrm{diag}(1+q_1,\ 1+Q_2) = \mathrm{diag}(1+q_1,\ 2)$ — the diagonal block for the $x$-unknowns.

Schur reduction

$A^{-1} = \mathrm{diag}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{1+q_1},\ \tfrac{1}{2}\right)$, so $C A^{-1} = [\,0,\ p_2/2\,]$ and

$$ C A^{-1} B \;=\; \tfrac{p_2 q_2}{2}, \qquad D - C A^{-1} B \;=\; 1 + p_2 - \tfrac{p_2 q_2}{2} \;=\; \tfrac{2(1+p_2) - p_2 q_2}{2}. $$

RHS: $C A^{-1} b_x = p_2(w-\Delta)/2$ and $b_y + C A^{-1} b_x = p_2(3w+\Delta)/2$. (Sign convention: writing $M=\begin{bmatrix}A&-B\\-C&D\end{bmatrix}$ flips the sign of the off-diagonal terms.)

$$ \boxed{\;\; y_2 \;=\; \frac{p_2\,(3w + \Delta)}{2(1+p_2) - p_2 q_2} \;\;} $$

Back-substitution

  • $x_1 = \dfrac{q_1(w-\Delta)}{1+q_1}$ — standalone (no dependency on $y_2$).
  • $x_2 = \dfrac{(w-\Delta) + q_2\,y_2}{2}$.

Three unknowns, two cheap divisions and one $1\times 1$ solve.

3. σ(2,2), general convex cost

Drop the quadratic assumption. The FOC system becomes nonlinear:

  • $c'(x_1) \;=\; q_1\, c'(w-\Delta-x_1)$
  • $c'(x_2) \;=\; q_1\, c'(w-\Delta-x_2) + q_2\, c'(w-\Delta-x_2+y_2)$
  • $c'(y_2) \;=\; p_2\, c'(w+\Delta+x_2-y_2)$

Use Newton's method on $F(v) = 0$, $v = (x_1, x_2, y_2)$. Define the gaps

$$ s_{k,\ell} \;=\; w - \Delta - x_k + y_\ell, \qquad t_{k,\ell} \;=\; w + \Delta + x_k - y_\ell, $$

with $y_1 = 0$. The Jacobian $J = \partial F / \partial v$ has the same block structure as the quadratic case, just with $c''$-weighted entries:

$$ J \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & A_{22} & -B_{22} \\ 0 & -C_{22} & D_{22} \end{bmatrix} $$
  • $A_{11} = c''(x_1) + q_1\, c''(s_{1,1})$
  • $A_{22} = c''(x_2) + q_1\, c''(s_{2,1}) + q_2\, c''(s_{2,2})$
  • $B_{22} = q_2\, c''(s_{2,2})$
  • $C_{22} = p_2\, c''(t_{2,2})$
  • $D_{22} = c''(y_2) + p_2\, c''(t_{2,2})$

Newton step

Solve $J\,\Delta v = -F$ via the same Schur recipe:

  1. $\Delta x_1 = -F_1/A_{11}$ — standalone.
  2. Eliminate $\Delta x_2 = (-F_2 + B_{22}\,\Delta y_2)/A_{22}$, then $1\times1$ solve: $$ \left(D_{22} - \tfrac{C_{22} B_{22}}{A_{22}}\right) \Delta y_2 \;=\; -F_3 + \tfrac{C_{22}}{A_{22}}\,F_2. $$
  3. Back-substitute $\Delta x_2$.

Quadratic check. With $c''(\cdot) \equiv 2$, entries reduce to $A_{11}=2(1+q_1)$, $A_{22}=2(1+q_1+q_2)=4$, $B_{22}=2q_2$, etc. The factor 2 scales out and a single Newton step lands exactly on the closed-form solution above — "linear is Newton-in-one-step."

Convergence. Strict convexity $c''>0$ makes $J$ positive-definite on the feasible region (diagonals dominate when $q_\ell, p_k \le 1$), so Newton from any reasonable seed (e.g., the quadratic-$c$ answer at the same $(p,q)$) converges quadratically. Continuation along $c_t = (1-t)\,c_{\text{quad}} + t\,c_{\text{target}}$ is the safety net for nasty $c$.

4. General σ(m,n): dimensions of the blocks

Unknowns are $(x_1,\dots,x_m,\ y_2,\dots,y_n)$, dimension $m + (n-1) = m+n-1$.

  • $A$: $\;m \times m\;$ — diagonal. Entry $k$: $c''(x_k) + \sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\, c''(s_{k,\ell})$. Quadratic case: $A = \mathrm{diag}(1+Q_1,\dots,1+Q_m)$.
  • $D$: $\;(n-1)\times(n-1)\;$ — diagonal. Entry $\ell$: $c''(y_\ell) + \sum_{k<\ell} p_k\, c''(t_{k,\ell})$. Quadratic case: $D = \mathrm{diag}(1+P_2,\dots,1+P_n)$, $P_\ell=\sum_{k<\ell}p_k$.
  • $B$: $\;m \times (n-1)\;$ — lower-triangular ($B_{k,\ell}\ne 0$ iff $\ell \le k$). Entry $q_\ell\, c''(s_{k,\ell})$.
  • $C$: $\;(n-1)\times m\;$ — lower-triangular ($C_{\ell,k}\ne 0$ iff $k < \ell$). Entry $p_k\, c''(t_{k,\ell})$.

For the symmetric case $m=n$: $A$ is $n\times n$, $D$ is $(n-1)\times(n-1)$, and after Schur-elimination of $x$ you solve an $(n-1)$-dim system for $y$.

Cost per linear / Newton solve

$A^{-1}$ free (diagonal). With $B$ and $C$ both lower-triangular, $C A^{-1} B$ is strictly lower-triangular, so the Schur complement $S = D - CA^{-1}B$ is $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ lower-triangular (diagonal $1+P_\ell$): solve by forward substitution in $O(n^2)$, no LU needed. Back-substitute $x$ in $O(m)$ since $A$ is diagonal.

Total: $O((m+n)^2)$ per linear (or per Newton) solve. The hard work is the outer loop on $(p,q)$ via the indifference conditions — which are quadratic in $(x,y)$ for quadratic $c$, non-polynomial for general $c$.
