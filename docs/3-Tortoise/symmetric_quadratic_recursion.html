Symmetric quadratic $\sigma(m,n)$: an explicit forward recursion

Solving the discrete equilibrium family without a Gröbner basis · Tortoise project · 2026-06-18
Summary: In the fully symmetric quadratic case ($v_1=v_2=1$, $\Delta=0$, $c(e)=e^2$), the mixed equilibrium $\sigma(m,n)$ is not an irreducible algebraic tangle — it is a one-dimensional shooting problem. Change variables from atoms to the rival's cumulative moments; the first-order conditions become a ratio, and the indifference conditions — which look quadratic — collapse to explicit, closed-form mass updates because a telescoping kills the constant term of each quadratic. The resulting system is triangular: a single unknown $q_1$ feeds a forward sweep ($O(m)$ arithmetic) that produces every atom and mass, closed by one normalization. This reproduces the entire chain $(1,1),(1,2),(2,2),\dots$ — including the cases that were still pending on the cluster — to machine precision, in the browser.

1. Setup and notation

We work in the fully symmetric quadratic specialization throughout:

  • values $v_1=v_2=1$, lead $\Delta=0$, cost $c(e)=e^2$, so the reach is $w=c^{-1}(1)=1$;
  • Player 1 mixes over atoms $x_1<\dots<x_m$ with masses $p_1,\dots,p_m$;
  • Player 2 mixes over atoms $y_1<\dots<y_n$ with masses $q_1,\dots,q_n$;
  • WLOG labeling $u_2^*=0$, which forces $y_1=0$;
  • the supports interleave: $y_1<x_1<y_2<x_2<\cdots$ (this is the $f=0$ comb).

The round-2 continuation against a single rival atom is Lemma 1's kinked payoff. Against P2's atom $y_\ell$, Player 1's round-2 lead is $\Delta_2 = x-y_\ell$, and on the interior branch ($0<\Delta_2<w=1$) the continuation is $1-c(w-\Delta_2)=1-(1-(x-y_\ell))^2$. Writing $g_\ell := 1+y_\ell$ for P2's reach point, this is $1-(g_\ell-x)^2$.

The truncation rule (from the interleaving) selects which rival atoms are on the interior branch: at P1's atom $x_k$ the active rival atoms are exactly $\ell\le k$; at P2's atom $y_\ell$ the active rival atoms are exactly $k<\ell$. (The asymmetry — $\le$ for P1, $<$ for P2 — is the ties-to-the-leader convention; it is why $x_1>0$ but $y_1=0$.)

2. The payoff as a function, and the change of variables

Fix P2's strategy. For a P1 effort $x$ lying in the cell where the active rival atoms are $\ell\le k$ (i.e. $y_k<x<y_{k+1}$), P1's payoff is the single smooth function

$$V_1(x)\;=\;\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\big[1-(g_\ell-x)^2\big]\;-\;x^2 \;=\;Q_k-\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell (g_\ell-x)^2-x^2 .$$

This is continuous across cell boundaries: a new rival atom $\ell=k{+}1$ enters at $x=y_{k+1}$, where its contribution $1-(g_{k+1}-y_{k+1})^2 = 1-1 = 0$. So $V_1$ is one continuous, piecewise-quadratic curve whose local maxima are P1's atoms, all at the common height $u_1$.

Change of variables. Replace the atoms/masses by the truncated cumulative moments of the rival's distribution. For the P1 side these are the moments of P2's law (reach points $g_\ell=1+y_\ell$); by the mirror symmetry of the symmetric case the P2 side uses P1's moments with reach points $h_j=1+x_j$. Each has an equivalent summation form and incremental form:

$$\begin{aligned} Q_k&=\textstyle\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell &&=Q_{k-1}+q_k, &\qquad P_k&=\textstyle\sum_{j\le k} p_j &&=P_{k-1}+p_k,\\[2pt] S_k&=\textstyle\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\,g_\ell &&=S_{k-1}+q_k g_k, &\qquad T_k&=\textstyle\sum_{j\le k} p_j\,h_j &&=T_{k-1}+p_k h_k,\\[2pt] M_k&=\textstyle\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\,g_\ell^{2} &&=M_{k-1}+q_k g_k^{2}, &\qquad N_k&=\textstyle\sum_{j\le k} p_j\,h_j^{2} &&=N_{k-1}+p_k h_k^{2}, \end{aligned}$$

(zeroth / first / second moment; all sequences start at $Q_0=S_0=M_0=P_0=T_0=N_0=0$). These six sequences are derived bookkeeping, not new unknowns. Each is a running total of masses already in the profile; substituting the definitions back would erase them entirely. We carry them only so that every condition below is an $O(1)$ formula instead of a growing sum — the genuine unknowns remain the atoms, the masses, and $u_1$.

3. First-order conditions are linear

Differentiating $V_1$ in the cell $\ell\le k$,

$$V_1'(x)=2\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell(g_\ell-x)-2x = 2\big[S_k-(1+Q_k)\,x\big],$$

so the interior maximizer is simply a ratio of moments:

$$\boxed{\,x_k=\dfrac{S_k}{1+Q_k}\,}\qquad\text{(P1 FOC)},\qquad\qquad \boxed{\,y_\ell=\dfrac{T_{\ell-1}}{1+P_{\ell-1}}\,}\qquad\text{(P2 FOC).}$$

This is the sense in which "the best responses are linear": each atom is a moment-weighted average of the rival's reach points. The nonlinearity lives entirely in the indifference conditions — which is what we tackle next.

4. The value identity

Substitute the FOC $x_k=S_k/(1+Q_k)$ back into $V_1$. Expanding the quadratic and using $S_k=(1+Q_k)x_k$,

$$\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell(g_\ell-x_k)^2 = M_k-2x_kS_k+Q_kx_k^2 = M_k-(2+Q_k)x_k^2 ,$$

so that

$$u_1=V_1(x_k)=Q_k-\big[M_k-(2+Q_k)x_k^2\big]-x_k^2 = Q_k-M_k+(1+Q_k)x_k^2 .$$

Value identity. For every $k$,

$$u_1 \;=\; Q_k - M_k + \frac{S_k^{2}}{1+Q_k}. $$

A single algebraic identity replaces P1's $m$ indifference equations: all $m$ right-hand sides equal the one number $u_1$.

5. Why $u_1^\ast=q_1^2/(1+q_1)$ is universal

Evaluate the value identity at $k=1$. Because $y_1=0$ we have $g_1=1$, hence $Q_1=M_1=q_1$ and $S_1=q_1$:

$$u_1 = \underbrace{(q_1-q_1)}_{=\,0} + \frac{q_1^{2}}{1+q_1} = \frac{q_1^{2}}{1+q_1}.$$

The closed form that "holds for all $(m,n)$" is nothing but the $k=1$ slice of the identity: the $u_2^*=0$ labeling pins $y_1=0$, which makes the moment difference $Q_1-M_1$ vanish for every member of the family. There is no separate miracle to prove.

6. The vanishing-constant step: masses in closed form

Indifference across P1's support means $V_1(x_{k+1})=V_1(x_k)=u_1$. Write $Z=1+Q_k$ and recall $g_{k+1}=1+y_{k+1}$. The value identity (§4), evaluated at level $k{+}1$, reads

$$u_1 \;=\; Q_{k+1}-M_{k+1}+\frac{S_{k+1}^{2}}{1+Q_{k+1}},$$

which we treat as an equation for the single new unknown $q_{k+1}$ (everything at level $k$ is known, and $y_{k+1}$ is supplied by the P2 side). The new atom enters only through the increments $Q_{k+1}=Q_k+q_{k+1}$, $S_{k+1}=S_k+q_{k+1}g_{k+1}$, and $M_{k+1}=M_k+q_{k+1}g_{k+1}^{2}$. Clearing the denominator (multiply this identity through by $1+Q_{k+1}=Z+q_{k+1}$) and substituting these increments,

$$(Z+q_{k+1})\big[(Q_k-M_k-u_1)+q_{k+1}(1-g_{k+1}^{2})\big] +S_k^{2}+2S_k g_{k+1}\,q_{k+1}+g_{k+1}^{2}\,q_{k+1}^{2}=0.$$

The new atom is still buried in the level-$(k{+}1)$ moments; isolating it means collecting powers of $q_{k+1}$. The $q_{k+1}^{2}$ has two sources that sum to coefficient $1$ — the $g_{k+1}^{2}q_{k+1}^{2}$ inside $S_{k+1}^{2}$ and the $q_{k+1}\!\cdot\!q_{k+1}(1-g_{k+1}^{2})$ cross term — leaving a quadratic in $q_{k+1}$:

$$q_{k+1}^{2}\;+\;B\,q_{k+1}\;+\;\underbrace{\big[\,Z\,(Q_k-M_k-u_1)+S_k^{2}\,\big]}_{\textstyle C}\;=\;0 \qquad\text{with}\qquad B = Q_k+Z-M_k-g_{k+1}^{2}Z+2S_k g_{k+1}-u_1.$$

The constant term is identically zero. The level-$k$ identity already gives $u_1=Q_k-M_k+S_k^2/Z$, i.e. $Q_k-M_k-u_1=-S_k^{2}/Z$. Substituting,

$$C = Z\!\left(-\frac{S_k^{2}}{Z}\right)+S_k^{2}=0.$$

So the quadratic factors as $q_{k+1}(q_{k+1}+B)=0$. The spurious root $q_{k+1}=0$ is "don't add an atom"; the genuine root is $q_{k+1}=-B$.

Simplifying $-B$ with $u_1-(Q_k-M_k)=S_k^2/Z$ and $x_k=S_k/Z$, then factoring out $Z$ and substituting its value $Z=1+Q_k$ (with $g_{k+1}=1+y_{k+1}$):

$$-B=\frac{S_k^{2}}{Z}-2S_k g_{k+1}+g_{k+1}^{2}Z-Z =\frac{(S_k-g_{k+1}Z)^2}{Z}-Z =Z\,(x_k-g_{k+1})^2-Z =Z\big[(x_k-g_{k+1})^2-1\big] =(1+Q_k)\big[(1+y_{k+1}-x_k)^2-1\big].$$

Explicit mass updates.

$$q_{k+1}=(1+Q_k)\big[(1+y_{k+1}-x_k)^2-1\big], \qquad\quad p_{j}=(1+P_{j-1})\big[(1+x_j-y_j)^2-1\big].$$

The P2 update is the mirror image: identical algebra with $u_2=0$ (the binding IR plays the role the level-$k$ identity played above, so its constant term vanishes for the same reason).

Gap interpretation

With interleaving gaps $d_j=y_{j+1}-x_j>0$ and $\delta_j=x_j-y_j>0$, the updates read

$$q_{j+1}=(1+Q_j)\,d_j(d_j+2),\qquad p_j=(1+P_{j-1})\,\delta_j(\delta_j+2).$$

New mass $=$ (cumulative rival mass $+1$) $\times$ gap $\times$ (gap $+2$). In particular mass positivity is equivalent to interleaving — the two structural facts are one fact.

Scope. The explicit closed-form mass updates rely on $V_1$ being piecewise quadratic, which is special to $c(e)=e^2$. For a general power cost $c(e)=e^\theta$ the value identity still holds, but the per-step update becomes a 1-D degree-$(\theta-1)$ solve rather than a formula. So the shooting reduction is cost-general; the closed-form masses are quadratic-only.

7. The complete system, assembled

We have introduced the pieces one at a time. Before turning them into an algorithm, here is the entire $\sigma(m,n)$ system collected in one place. Nothing below is new — it is exactly §§3–6 put side by side.

Unknowns. P1 atoms $x_1<\dots<x_m$ and masses $p_1,\dots,p_m$; P2 atoms $y_1<\dots<y_n$ and masses $q_1,\dots,q_n$; the common P1 value $u_1$. Two labels are fixed once and for all: $y_1=0$ and $u_2=0$.

(A) Moment bookkeeping — six running totals defined from the masses ($g_\ell=1+y_\ell$, $h_j=1+x_j$); not independent unknowns:

$$Q_k=Q_{k-1}+q_k,\quad S_k=S_{k-1}+q_k g_k,\quad M_k=M_{k-1}+q_k g_k^{2},$$ $$P_k=P_{k-1}+p_k,\quad T_k=T_{k-1}+p_k h_k,\quad N_k=N_{k-1}+p_k h_k^{2}\qquad(\text{all }{}_0=0).$$

(B) Stationarity — locates each player's atoms from the rival's current moments:

$$x_k=\frac{S_k}{1+Q_k}\ \ (k=1,\dots,m),\qquad y_\ell=\frac{T_{\ell-1}}{1+P_{\ell-1}}\ \ (\ell=2,\dots,n),\qquad y_1=0.$$

(C) Indifference — each player's own indifference pins the rival's masses. P1's indifference is the value identity $u_1=Q_k-M_k+S_k^2/(1+Q_k)$ for all $k$; read across consecutive levels (§6) it solves explicitly, and P2's binding IR $u_2=0$ gives the mirror:

$$q_{k+1}=(1+Q_k)\big[(1+y_{k+1}-x_k)^2-1\big],\qquad p_{j}=(1+P_{j-1})\big[(1+x_j-y_j)^2-1\big],\qquad u_1=\frac{q_1^{2}}{1+q_1}.$$

(D) Closure — the two normalizations:

$$\sum_{k} p_k = 1,\qquad \sum_{\ell} q_\ell = 1.$$

How the groups interlock. Group (B) gives each player's atoms in terms of the rival's masses; group (C) gives each player's masses in terms of the rival's atoms. The two cross over and feed each other. Group (A) is pure bookkeeping that keeps (B)–(C) cheap. The only inputs not produced by (A)–(C) are the single number $q_1$ and the two normalizations (D) — and, as the next section shows, one normalization merely fixes the one "leftover" top mass while the other is the lone scalar equation that $q_1$ must satisfy. So the system has one free parameter and one closing equation.

8. Triangularization: how one unknown $q_1$ pins the rest

Counting unknowns the naive way is alarming: we now carry $x$'s, $y$'s, $p$'s, $q$'s and six moment sequences. How can adding $Q,S,M,P,T,N$ make a system easier? And if $q_1$ "pins everything," why does $Q_k$ visibly depend on $q_k$ as well as $Q_{k-1}$ — isn't that circular? Both worries have the same answer.

The moments are not unknowns. The six sequences in group (A) are neither equations to be solved nor degrees of freedom: each is an explicit running total of masses already in the profile. Eliminating them (back-substituting the definitions) would return the original atoms-and-masses system unchanged. The change of variables alters the form of the equations, not their number. The genuine unknown list is the same one we started with — the atoms, the masses, and $u_1$.

No circularity — just an order of computation. "$Q_k$ depends on $q_k$ and $Q_{k-1}$" is true, but it is an update performed after $q_k$ is already known, not a constraint that needs $q_k$ and $Q_k$ at once. Each unknown is produced by exactly one equation, and that equation refers only to unknowns produced earlier. The dependency graph is acyclic with a single source, $q_1$:

q₁  (free shooting parameter)
  ↳ x₁ = q₁/(1+q₁),  u₁ = q₁²/(1+q₁)  init, g₁=1
  ↳ p₁ = (1+P₀)[(1+x₁−y₁)²−1]  P2 indiff → P1 mass
  ↳ y₂ = T₁/(1+P₁)  P2 FOC → P2 atom
  ↳ q₂ = (1+Q₁)[(1+y₂−x₁)²−1]  P1 indiff → P2 mass
  ↳ x₂ = S₂/(1+Q₂)  P1 FOC → P1 atom
  ↳ p₂ → y₃ → q₃ → x₃ → ⋯  (repeat)

When $q_{k+1}$ is formed it uses $Q_k$ — the total through level $k$, already in hand — never $Q_{k+1}$. Only afterwards do we fold it in: $Q_{k+1}=Q_k+q_{k+1}$. So $Q_{k+1}$ depends on $q_{k+1}$, but $q_{k+1}$ depends on $Q_k$, not on $Q_{k+1}$. The update $Q_k\to Q_{k+1}$ sits downstream of computing $q_{k+1}$, not upstream — there is no cycle.

This is precisely a triangular (forward-substitution) system. Order the unknowns down the cascade — $q_1,\,x_1,\,u_1,\,p_1,\,y_2,\,q_2,\,x_2,\,p_2,\,y_3,\dots$ — and each is an explicit function of those above it. A triangular system whose top is a one-parameter row is solved by sweeping downward from any chosen value of that parameter; here the parameter is $q_1$.

The degree-of-freedom count.

  • One free parameter: $q_1$.
  • Determined by the forward sweep: every atom, the value $u_1$, and every mass except one "leftover" top mass — all explicit functions of $q_1$.
  • One normalization spends itself fixing that leftover top mass: $\sum p=1$ fixes $p_m$ on an $(m,m)$ rung, $\sum q=1$ fixes $q_{m+1}$ on an $(m,m{+}1)$ rung.
  • The other normalization is the lone closing equation $F(q_1)=0$, solved by 1-D shooting.

Net: one unknown, one equation — square — and solved by a monotone bisection rather than an $m^2$-degree elimination.

9. The algorithm: forward recursion + one shot

Everything downstream of $q_1$ is now explicit and alternating. Treat $q_1$ as a shooting parameter:

0. $x_1=\dfrac{q_1}{1+q_1}$,   $u_1=\dfrac{q_1^2}{1+q_1}$,   $y_1=0$.

1. For $j=1,2,\dots$ alternate the four explicit maps: $$p_j=(1+P_{j-1})\big[(1+x_j-y_j)^2-1\big] \;\longrightarrow\; y_{j+1}=\frac{T_j}{1+P_j} \;\longrightarrow\; q_{j+1}=(1+Q_j)\big[(1+y_{j+1}-x_j)^2-1\big] \;\longrightarrow\; x_{j+1}=\frac{S_{j+1}}{1+Q_{j+1}}.$$

2. Close with one scalar equation; adjust $q_1$ until it holds (1-D root find).

The closure is just a normalization, and which normalization depends on the rung — see the next section. There is no elimination, no resultant, no Gröbner basis: each evaluation is $O(m)$ arithmetic, and the outer solve is a monotone 1-D bisection.

10. The two rungs: $(m,m)$ and $(m,m{+}1)$

The chain alternates between equal rungs $\sigma(m,m)$ and step-up rungs $\sigma(m,m{+}1)$. The recursion is identical; only the bookkeeping of the very top atom differs.

feature$\sigma(m,m)$$\sigma(m,m{+}1)$
top atom (largest in support)$x_m$ (P1)$y_{m+1}$ (P2)
masses fixed by indifference$q_2,\dots,q_m$;  $p_1,\dots,p_{m-1}$$q_2,\dots,q_m$;  $p_1,\dots,p_{m}$
"leftover" mass (in no indiff. eq.)$p_m$$q_{m+1}$
leftover fixed by$\sum p=1$$\sum q=1$
shooting target on $q_1$$\sum q = 1$$\sum p = 1$

The logic: the top atom's mass never appears in any rival indifference condition (there is no rival atom above it to be indifferent at), so it is a free leftover pinned by its own player's normalization. The other player's normalization is the one genuine scalar equation that determines $q_1$. For the step-up rung the recursion simply runs the $p$-updates one rung further ($p_1,\dots,p_m$, now all from indifference), the extra atom $y_{m+1}=T_m/(1+P_m)$ sits above $x_m$, and the shooting target swaps to $\sum p=1$.

12. Bonus: monotonicity $\Rightarrow$ uniqueness (open)

Define the shooting residual $F(q_1)=\sum_\ell q_\ell - 1$ (for an equal rung; $\sum_k p_k-1$ for a step-up rung). Numerically, $F$ is strictly monotone with a single crossing for every $(m,n)$ tested — so the shooting root, hence the symmetric-quadratic equilibrium, is unique on each rung. Because $F$ is an explicit composition of the rational maps above, this is a genuine real-analysis statement, not a numerical artifact, and it would deliver constructive existence + uniqueness for the symmetric quadratic chain — exactly the regime where the global Theorem 3 (det-$J$/Sard route) is still open. Proving $F'<0$ (equivalently single-crossing) is tracked as a TODO.

13. Relation to the Gröbner pipeline

The elimination polynomial $w(m,m)$ has degree $m^2$ and $w(m,m{+}1)$ degree $m(m{+}1)$; computing them on the cluster (msolve F4+FGLM) is what made the larger rungs expensive. The recursion sidesteps the elimination entirely: it produces the numerical equilibrium directly, and gives an independent cross-check of every root the Gröbner runs reported. It is also how the calculator above evaluates $(6,6)$ and $(6,7)$ — the rungs that were still listed as "remaining" — without a basis computation.
