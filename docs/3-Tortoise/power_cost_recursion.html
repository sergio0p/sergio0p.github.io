Power-cost $\sigma(m,n)$: the degree-$(\theta-1)$ solve

What stays and what breaks when $c(e)=e^2$ becomes $c(e)=e^\theta$ · Tortoise project · 2026-06-18
Summary: The companion page symmetric_quadratic_recursion.html solves the symmetric $\sigma(m,n)$ family by a triangular forward sweep with closed-form mass updates. Those closed forms are special to $c(e)=e^2$. For a general power cost $c(e)=e^\theta$ ($\theta\ge2$ integer) the very same architecture survives — change of variables to the rival's cumulative moments, value identity, triangular cascade, 1-D shooting on $q_1$ — with one change: locating each atom is no longer a ratio but a polynomial root of degree $\theta-1$. The first-order condition $\sum q_\ell(g_\ell-x)^{\theta-1}=x^{\theta-1}$ is linear at $\theta=2$ (hence the ratio $x_k=S_k/(1+Q_k)$) and genuinely degree $\theta-1$ for $\theta\ge3$. The indifference equations stay linear in each new mass, so masses remain explicit once the atom is known — which is exactly why the shooting reduction is cost-general while the closed-form masses are quadratic-only.

There is a deeper lesson the quadratic case hides. For $\theta\ge3$ the per-step polynomial has several roots in general — but only one lies in the interleaving window, because strict convexity makes the round-1 payoff strictly concave on each interior branch, so the bracket from the previous atom to the reach isolates a unique stationary point (confirmed numerically across $\theta$ up to 8, mixed polynomials, and asymmetry). The subtlety the quadratic hides is therefore not a per-step choice among candidate roots; it is that a solution of the first-order + indifference system is necessary but not sufficient globally: it can admit a profitable upward deviation across branches (abandonment or the free-win shelf). That is precisely Example 5 of the paper (cubic $(2,2)$ solves the local system yet is not an equilibrium), and the threshold is $\theta^\star=\log 2/\log(4/3)\approx2.409$ (Proposition 4): at $\theta^\star$ the small rungs $\sigma(1,1)$ and $\sigma(1,2)$ die, while larger rungs survive (cubic $\sigma(3,3)$ — the paper's Example 3 — is reproduced here exactly).

Payoff structure (§8). The shooting scalar pins the payoff outright via the exact identity $u_1^*=q_1^2/(1+q_1)$ (cost-general: $u_1^*=q_1[1-(1+q_1^{1/(\theta-1)})^{1-\theta}]$, increasing in $q_1$), so the cross-rung payoff decline reduces to monotonicity of a single seed — established by an interlacing argument modulo one open lemma (the same seed-monotonicity that would also give per-rung uniqueness).

1. Setup — only the cost exponent changes

We stay in the fully symmetric specialization of the companion page and only swap the cost:

  • values $v_1=v_2=1$, lead $\Delta=0$, cost $c(e)=e^\theta$, so the reach is still $w=c^{-1}(1)=1$;
  • Player 1 mixes over $x_1<\dots<x_m$ (masses $p_j$); Player 2 over $y_1<\dots<y_n$ (masses $q_\ell$);
  • WLOG labeling $u_2^*=0$, forcing $y_1=0$; supports interleave $y_1<x_1<y_2<x_2<\cdots$ (the $f=0$ comb);
  • reach points $g_\ell:=1+y_\ell$ (for P1, against $y_\ell$) and $h_j:=1+x_j$ (for P2, against $x_j$).

Round-2 against a single rival atom $y_\ell$ gives P1 the interior continuation $1-c(w-\Delta_2)=1-(g_\ell-x)^\theta$, where $\Delta_2=x-y_\ell$. The truncation rule is unchanged (it is geometric, not cost-specific): at P1's atom $x_k$ the active rival atoms are $\ell\le k$; at P2's atom $y_\ell$ they are $k<\ell$.

2. The payoff curve is now piecewise degree-$\theta$

Fix P2's strategy. For a P1 effort $x$ in the cell where the active rival atoms are $\ell\le k$,

$$V_1(x)\;=\;\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\big[1-(g_\ell-x)^\theta\big]-x^\theta \;=\;Q_k-\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell (g_\ell-x)^\theta-x^\theta .$$

At $\theta=2$ this is the downward parabola the companion page exploits. For $\theta\ge3$ it is a smooth degree-$\theta$ curve on each cell. Continuity across cells is identical to the quadratic case: a new rival atom enters at $x=y_{k+1}$, where its contribution $1-(g_{k+1}-y_{k+1})^\theta=1-1^\theta=0$ and the level $Q$ jumps by exactly the same $q_{k+1}$. So $V_1$ is one continuous, piecewise-degree-$\theta$ curve whose local maxima are P1's atoms, all at the common height $u_1$. The cost shape has changed the arcs; it has not changed the comb.

3. The first-order condition — where degree $\theta-1$ is born

Differentiate $V_1$ inside the cell $\ell\le k$:

$$V_1'(x)=\theta\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell(g_\ell-x)^{\theta-1}-\theta\,x^{\theta-1},$$

so the interior maximizer solves

Stationarity (general $\theta$).

$$\boxed{\;\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\,(g_\ell-x_k)^{\theta-1}\;=\;x_k^{\theta-1}\;}$$

A single polynomial equation of degree $\theta-1$ in the atom $x_k$. Its leading coefficient is $(-1)^{\theta-1}Q_k-1$, which is nonzero (since $0<Q_k<1$) — so the degree is exactly $\theta-1$, never less.

Why $\theta=2$ is special. At $\theta=2$ the exponent $\theta-1=1$, the equation is linear, and it inverts to the ratio of moments

$$x_k=\frac{\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell g_\ell}{1+\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell}=\frac{S_k}{1+Q_k}.$$

This single fact — "stationarity is linear" — is the source of every closed form on the companion page. The moment $x_k=S_k/(1+Q_k)$ is what then collapses the value identity to $u_1=Q_k-M_k+S_k^2/(1+Q_k)$ and, through the vanishing-constant step, to the explicit mass updates. Remove the linearity and the cascade of closed forms unwinds from the top.

What $\theta\ge3$ looks like. The exponent $\theta-1\ge2$, and the atom is the relevant root of a genuine degree-$(\theta-1)$ polynomial. For the very first atom ($k=1$, $g_1=1$, $Q_1=q_1$) it reads, transparently,

$$q_1\,(1-x_1)^{\theta-1}=x_1^{\theta-1}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad x_1=\frac{q_1^{1/(\theta-1)}}{1+q_1^{1/(\theta-1)}}.$$

(The single-active-atom case still has a closed root — a $(\theta-1)$-th root, not a rational expression. With two or more active atoms there is no such formula and the root is found numerically.) Already here one sees the calculator's job: a degree-$(\theta-1)$ polynomial can have several positive roots, but only one falls in the interleaving window — and strict convexity (the round-1 payoff is strictly concave on each interior branch) makes that in-window root the local maximizer. So the per-step task is a single 1-D root-find on the correct bracket, not a comparison among candidates; the within-window uniqueness is hidden at $\theta=2$ only because there the equation is linear. The genuine multiplicity is global — the cross-branch upward deviation of §7 below — and is settled by a post-hoc best-response check, not by per-step root enumeration.

4. The value identity holds; masses stay linear

Substituting the FOC back, the common P1 value is

$$u_1=V_1(x_k)=Q_k-\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell(g_\ell-x_k)^\theta-x_k^\theta\qquad(\forall k).$$

For $\theta=2$ the binomial $(g_\ell-x_k)^2=g_\ell^2-2g_\ell x_k+x_k^2$ folds into just three moment sequences $(Q,S,M)$, giving the tidy $u_1=Q_k-M_k+S_k^2/(1+Q_k)$. For $\theta\ge3$ the expansion of $(g_\ell-x_k)^\theta$ needs $\theta+1$ moments and does not telescope — there is no two-moment value identity. But the equation is still affine in each individual mass. Isolate the newest mass $q_k$ (everything else known): both the indifference $V_1(x_k)=u_1$ and the FOC are linear in $q_k$, so

$$q_k=\frac{u_1-\Big[Q_{k-1}-\sum_{\ell<k}q_\ell(g_\ell-x_k)^\theta\Big]+x_k^\theta}{\,1-(g_k-x_k)^\theta\,}.$$

So why are the closed-form masses "quadratic-only"? Not because the mass equation becomes nonlinear — it never does. It is because the mass formula feeds on the atom $x_k$, and at $\theta=2$ the atom is the closed-form ratio $S_k/(1+Q_k)$, so the mass inherits a closed form; at $\theta\ge3$ the atom is a degree-$(\theta-1)$ root with no formula, so the mass — though still an explicit linear function of that atom — inherits the root-find. The non-closed object is the atom, and the mass rides along.

5. The per-step solve: one atom, one mass, coupled

There is a subtlety the linearity above glosses. At a P1 step the newest mass $q_k$ and the newest atom $x_k$ are introduced together: $q_k$ appears in the FOC that locates $x_k$, and $x_k$ appears in the indifference that pins $q_k$. So each step is a small $2\times2$ system. Two clean ways to dispatch it:

  • Slave the mass, root-find the atom. Use the (linear) indifference to write $q_k=q_k(x_k)$ as above, substitute into the FOC, and solve the resulting single equation in $x_k$ by 1-D bisection. This is the practical method used in the calculator.
  • Single-rival steps collapse. When only one rival atom is active (the first atom on each side, and every step that introduces a brand-new rival), eliminating the mass between FOC and indifference cancels cleanly. For P2's atom $y_2$ against P1's lone $x_1$ ($h_1=1+x_1$) one gets the closed form $$y_2=h_1-h_1^{-1/(\theta-1)},\qquad p_1=h_1\,y_2^{\theta-1},$$ the exact $\theta$-generalization of the quadratic $y_2=[(1+x_1)^2-1]/(1+x_1)$.

Degree bookkeeping, stated honestly: the atom-location FOC at fixed masses is degree $\theta-1$ (this is the scope-box's count, and it is literal for the first atom and every single-rival step). When the newest mass must be co-determined, slaving it linearly and clearing denominators raises the per-step equation in $x_k$ to degree $\le 2\theta-2$ (the top $x_k^{2\theta-1}$ term cancels identically). Either way it is one monotone 1-D root-find per atom — never an elimination, never a Gröbner basis.

6. The cascade is still triangular — still one shot on $q_1$

None of the dependency structure changes. Each unknown is still produced by one equation referring only to unknowns produced earlier, with a single source $q_1$:

q₁  (free shooting parameter)
  ↳ x₁ : solve  q₁(1−x₁)θ−1 = x₁θ−1  degree-(θ−1) root
  ↳ u₁ = q₁[1−(1−x₁)θ] − x₁θ  value at the atom
  ↳ (y₂, p₁) : P2 FOC + IR  single-rival → closed form
  ↳ (x₂, q₂) : P1 FOC + indiff  2×2 → slave mass, root-find atom
  ↳ (y₃, p₂) → (x₃, q₃) → ⋯  (repeat)

and the run is closed exactly as before: the top atom's mass is a free leftover fixed by its own normalization, while the other normalization is the lone scalar residual $F(q_1)=0$ solved by 1-D shooting. The architecture is identical to the quadratic page; only the "$x_k=\,$ratio" boxes became "$x_k=\,$root."

7. What the quadratic case hides: necessary $\ne$ sufficient

At $\theta=2$ the payoff is a concave parabola on each cell and the comb construction is automatically a global best response — solving the local system is the equilibrium. For $\theta\ge3$ that free lunch ends, in three linked ways.

7a. $\sigma(1,2)$ becomes infeasible

With $p_1=1$ the single-rival collapse gives the closed form $x_1=2^{(\theta-1)/\theta}-1$, $y_2=2^{-1/\theta}$, and the P1 FOC then forces

$$q_1=\Big(\tfrac{x_1}{1-x_1}\Big)^{\theta-1}.$$

This is a legitimate probability ($q_1\le1$) iff $x_1\le\tfrac12$, i.e. iff $\theta\le\theta^\star:=\dfrac{\log2}{\log(4/3)}\approx2.409$. At $\theta=2$, $q_1=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}\approx0.707$ (the published value); at $\theta=3$, $q_1\approx2.03>1$ — no equilibrium.

7b. $\sigma(1,1)$ loses Nash — at the same threshold

The one-atom-each profile is $x_1=\tfrac12$, $y_1=0$, with $u_1=1-2^{1-\theta}$. Player 2, sitting at $0$ with payoff $0$, can deviate to effort $y$; the best upward deviation is $y=\tfrac34$, worth $1-2(\tfrac34)^\theta$. This is $\le0$ — no deviation — iff $(\tfrac34)^\theta\ge\tfrac12$, i.e. iff $\theta\le\theta^\star=\log2/\log(4/3)\approx2.409$. This is exactly Proposition 4's pure-strategy existence threshold, recovered as a global deviation in the recursion. (Independent confirmation that the printed threshold should read $\log2/\log(4/3)\approx2.409$, not its reciprocal.)

7c. $\sigma(2,2)$ at $\theta=3$: solves the system, still not an equilibrium

This is the paper's Example 5. The cubic $(2,2)$ first-order + indifference system has a unique solution (the cascade finds it, interleaved, with masses summing to 1), yet it is not a Nash equilibrium: player 2 has a strictly profitable deviation to an effort above the support. The recursion reproduces the failure quantitatively — the best upward deviation gains $\approx0.0125$. The paper's appendix states the obligation directly: solving the square system rules out deviations below the top atoms, but "we still must prove that no deviations above $x_m$ or $y_n$ are profitable," and checks it separately (numerically).

The local-sufficiency that does survive is between adjacent rival atoms, where the payoff is strictly concave (single-peaked) for any convex $c$. What fails for $\theta>\theta^\star$ is control of deviations beyond the top atom — and that is exactly where small supports are too thin to plug the gap. Larger supports do plug it: the cubic $\sigma(3,3)$ is a genuine equilibrium (the paper's Example 3), and the forward sweep here reproduces it to four decimals — $q_1=0.2723$, P2 at $\{0,0.4799,0.6636\}$, P1 at $\{0.3428,0.5817,0.7335\}$. 8. Payoff structure: the seed is the payoff

The shooting scalar $q_1$ does more than close the system — it pins the equilibrium payoff outright. That turns the cross-rung ranking question (does $u_1^*$ fall as the supports grow?) into a statement about a single number, and the recursion supplies an interlacing argument for it. One lemma remains open (flagged below); the rest is rigorous or structural.

8a. The seed–payoff identity

The first atom sees only $y_1=0$, so $g_1=1$, $Q_1=q_1$, and its FOC is $q_1(1-x_1)^{\theta-1}=x_1^{\theta-1}$, i.e. $x_1/(1-x_1)=\rho$ with $\rho:=q_1^{1/(\theta-1)}$, giving $x_1=\rho/(1+\rho)$. Substituting into the value at that atom, $u_1=q_1[1-(1-x_1)^\theta]-x_1^\theta$, everything collapses:

$$\boxed{\,u_1^*=q_1\big[\,1-(1+\rho)^{\,1-\theta}\,\big],\quad \rho=q_1^{1/(\theta-1)}\,} \qquad\xrightarrow{\ \theta=2\ }\qquad u_1^*=\frac{q_1^{\,2}}{1+q_1}.$$

Both factors rise with $q_1$ ($q_1\uparrow$, and $(1+\rho)^{1-\theta}\downarrow$ since the exponent is negative), so $u_1^*$ is strictly increasing in the seed for every $\theta\ge2$. This is exact and cost-general — confirmed to machine precision at $\theta=2$ and $\theta=3$ (table below, and §9b). The consequence is the whole point: ranking the payoffs is the same as ranking the seeds.

8b. Why the seed must drop — an interlacing argument

Three structural facts about the cascade (all hold for any $\theta$, in the validity region):

  1. Stop-independence. The cascade is one infinite, seed-determined sequence $q_1(q_1),q_2(q_1),q_3(q_1),\dots$; rung $(m,m)$ merely truncates it. (Verified: the early $q_\ell$ are identical across $(3,3),(4,4),(5,5)$ at a common seed.)
  2. The closing condition is a partial sum. Rung $(m,m)$ closes when $G_m(q_1):=\sum_{\ell\le m}q_\ell(q_1)-1=0$ (the leftover fixes $p_m$). Since every mass is positive, $$G_{m+1}(q_1)=G_m(q_1)+q_{m+1}(q_1)>G_m(q_1)\quad\text{pointwise.}$$
  3. The next residual is positive at the current root. At $q_1^*(m,m)$, $G_m=0$, so $G_{m+1}\big(q_1^*(m,m)\big)=q_{m+1}>0$.

Now suppose $G_m$ is increasing in the seed. A function that is increasing in $q_1$ and already strictly positive at $q_1^*(m,m)$ must have crossed zero earlier:

$$q_1^*(m{+}1,m{+}1)\;<\;q_1^*(m,m)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad u_1^*(m{+}1,m{+}1)the entire diagonal by induction — in one stroke, not rung by rung. This is a cleaner lever than the $\alpha$-homotopy/envelope route: it tracks one scalar root, not a per-pair Jacobian sign.

8c. The open crux

Everything in §8b is rigorous except one input: that $G_m(q_1)=\sum_{\ell\le m}q_\ell(q_1)$ is strictly increasing in the seed ("more seed $\Rightarrow$ more cumulative mass"). This is unproven for every $\theta$, including $\theta=2$. It is exactly the single-crossing/monotonicity property already needed for per-rung uniqueness of the shooting root — so a proof would settle uniqueness and cross-rung payoff decline at once (two results, one lemma). Numerically it holds decisively: $\theta=2$ through $(6,6)$, and $\theta=3$ throughout the existence window.

Two power-cost riders. (i) Existence window. For $\theta>\theta^\star\approx2.409$ the small rungs vanish (§7), so the ranking is meaningful only where consecutive rungs both exist (the $\theta=3$ chain starts at $(3,3)$). (ii) Off-diagonal. The $(m,m{+}1)$ rungs close on the other partial sum $B_m=\sum_{k\le m}p_k$, giving a parallel interlacing in the $p$-family; stitching the two families into one fully monotone chain needs one extra cross-comparison (root of $A_m{=}1$ vs root of $B_m{=}1$).

The table below shows the identity and the interlacing together for the symmetric quadratic diagonal: the recursion's $u_1^*$ equals $q_1^2/(1+q_1)$ to all printed digits, the seed $q_1^*$ falls monotonically, and the next residual $G_{m+1}(q_1^*)$ is positive at each root — the engine of the drop.

10. Relation to the rest of the project

The companion symmetric_quadratic_recursion.html is the $\theta=2$ slice of this page: there the FOC is linear, so atoms and masses are closed forms and the global check is free. The general fully-asymmetric algorithm ($v_1\neq v_2$, $\Delta\neq0$) is in Docs/general_algorithm_v1v2_delta.md; its §4 "Scope" box is the statement this page expands. The continuous power-cost equilibrium (Proposition 2 / §7 of QUICKSTART.md) is a different object: for $c(e)=e^\theta/\theta$ the competitive density solves a constant-coefficient ODE whose characteristic polynomial is a truncated exponential (Schur 1929: not solvable in radicals for $\theta\ge6$). Whether the discrete $\sigma(m,n)$ family converges to that density as supports grow is open; this page is about the discrete family and the exact point where its small rungs stop being equilibria.
