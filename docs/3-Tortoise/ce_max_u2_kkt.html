Maximizing $u_2$ over correlated equilibria

A symbolic characterization of the $u_2$-maximizing correlated equilibrium of the round-1 reduced game, for general valuations $v_1,v_2$ and lead $\Delta$, quadratic cost $c(e)=e^2$. The whole family collapses to two numbers $r=\sqrt{v_2/v_1}$ and $\delta=\Delta/\sqrt{v_1}$; every object below is closed-form in $(r,\delta)$. The symmetric case $v_1=v_2$ is the single line $r=1$. Numbers in §7 are illustrative examples and a solver cross-check.

The result in one line. Write $r=\sqrt{v_2/v_1}$ (relative reach) and $\delta=\Delta/\sqrt{v_1}$ (effective lead). On an explicit window of $(r,\delta)$ the $u_2$-maximizing correlated equilibrium is a three-atom device whose entire description is closed-form. Its key parameter — player 2's attack effort — is $$\boxed{\ \beta^\star=\frac{K+\sqrt{-2K}}{\,r-\delta\,},\qquad K=(r+2)(r-1)-\delta(r+1)\ }$$ (real units $\beta^\star_{\!*}=\sqrt{v_1}\,\beta^\star$), and the attained payoff is $\max_{\mathrm{CE}}u_2=v_1\,p_1\,(r^2-2\beta^{\star2})>0$. Whenever this value is positive it is achieved by no Nash equilibrium — correlation is strictly essential. At $r=1$ this reduces to the symmetric $\beta^\star=2\sqrt\Delta/(1+\sqrt\Delta)$.

1. The game and the two-parameter reduction

Players choose efforts $e_1,e_2\ge0$; the round-2 lead is $\Delta_2=\Delta+e_1-e_2$. With reaches $w_1=\sqrt{v_1},\ w_2=\sqrt{v_2}$ (so $c(w_i)=v_i$) and quadratic cost, the Lemma-1 continuations are piecewise quadratic, each player's interior branch governed by the rival's reach:

$$U_1(z)=\begin{cases}0 & z\le w_2-w_1\\ v_1-(w_2-z)^2 & w_2-w_1

Dead zone. $U_2(z)=0$ for all $z\ge w_2-w_1$: to earn anything in round 2, player 2 must overspend the effective lead, $e_2>\Delta+e_1-(w_2-w_1)$.

Two free parameters. The rescaling $e_i\mapsto w_1e_i$, $\Delta\mapsto w_1\Delta$, payoff $\mapsto v_1\cdot(\text{payoff})$ is an exact symmetry. It sends $(w_1,w_2,\Delta)$ to $(1,\ r,\ \delta)$ with

$$r=\frac{w_2}{w_1}=\sqrt{\frac{v_2}{v_1}},\qquad \delta=\frac{\Delta}{w_1}=\frac{\Delta}{\sqrt{v_1}}, \qquad(\text{normalized } v_1=1,\ v_2=r^2).$$

So the entire family depends only on $(r,\delta)$. We work in normalized units and restore by $\beta^\star_{\!*}=w_1\beta^\star$, efforts $\times w_1$, payoffs $\times v_1$. The best replies are $\mathrm{BR}_1(e_2)=\tfrac{r-\delta+e_2}{2}$ and $\mathrm{BR}_2(e_1)=\tfrac{1+\delta+e_1}{2}$. $r<1$ is the regime of interest (player 1 the stronger valuation, $v_1>v_2$); $r=1$ is symmetric.

2. The window of interest

The construction is the maximizer on an explicit open window of $(r,\delta)$, bounded by two structural events (derived in §§5–6):

$$\delta_{\min}(r)=2r+1-2\sqrt2\quad(a_{\mathrm{lo}}=0),\qquad \delta_{\max}(r)=\frac{\tfrac12 r^3+c_1\,(r^2+r-2)}{\tfrac12 r^2+c_1\,(r+1)},\quad c_1=1+(1-\sqrt2)\,r \quad(\beta^\star=\tfrac{r}{\sqrt2}).$$ $$\text{window}\;=\;\big(\max(0,\delta_{\min}(r)),\ \delta_{\max}(r)\big).$$
  • Lower edge $\delta_{\min}=2r+1-2\sqrt2$. Here the hedge atom collapses ($a_{\mathrm{lo}}\!\downarrow\!0$) and the maximum is attained without correlation. For $r<\tfrac{2\sqrt2-1}{2}\approx0.914$ this edge is negative, so for all physical $\delta>0$ the hedge atom is present and the binding lower limit is simply $\delta>0$.
  • Upper edge $\delta_{\max}(r)$. Here player 2's best attack stops paying, $r^2-2\beta^{\star2}\!\downarrow\!0$ ($\beta^\star\!\uparrow\!r/\sqrt2$); player 2 concedes and $\max_{\mathrm{CE}}u_2=0$ (dead-CE).

As player 2 weakens ($r\downarrow$) the window slides toward smaller leads and narrows, closing at $r_{\mathrm{crit}}\approx0.84$, i.e. $v_1\approx1.42\,v_2$ (where $\delta_{\max}=0$); beyond that $\max_{\mathrm{CE}}u_2=0$ for every $\Delta$. The symmetric endpoints are recovered at $r=1$: $\delta_{\min}=3-2\sqrt2\approx0.1716$, $\delta_{\max}=\tfrac{9+4\sqrt2}{49}\approx0.2991$.

Proposition (correlation is essential). On the window the unique Nash payoff for player 2 is $0$ — the only equilibrium on the $u_2$-axis is the pure profile $\big(\tfrac{w_2-\Delta}{2},0\big)$. The CE that maximizes $u_2$ delivers a strictly positive payoff $v_1\,p_1(r^2-2\beta^{\star2})>0$. Hence the max-$u_2$ CE, whenever $u_2>0$, is never a Nash equilibrium.

3. The maximizing device

The optimum is supported on the product grid $\{a_{\mathrm{lo}},a_{\mathrm{hi}}\}\times\{0,\beta^\star\}$ with three atoms:

cell$(e_1,e_2)$weightreading
$C_1$ (attack)$(a_{\mathrm{lo}},\,\beta^\star)$$p_1$ P1 hedges low, P2 attacks — the only cell paying P2
$C_0$ (hedge)$(a_{\mathrm{lo}},\,0)$$p_0$ P1 hedges low, P2 stays
$C_H$ (commit)$(a_{\mathrm{hi}},\,0)$$p_H$ P1 commits high, P2 stays

The signals are correlated: P1's low recommendation $a_{\mathrm{lo}}$ is paired with both of P2's actions, so on seeing $a_{\mathrm{lo}}$ player 1 is unsure whether P2 attacks; and on seeing "stay" player 2 is unsure whether P1 hedged or committed. The two conditional beliefs are

$$\lambda=\Pr(e_2{=}0\mid a_{\mathrm{lo}})=\frac{p_0}{p_1+p_0},\qquad \rho=\Pr(e_1{=}a_{\mathrm{lo}}\mid e_2{=}0)=\frac{p_0}{p_0+p_H}.$$

4. The smooth skeleton (first-order conditions)

Three of the four obedience constraints are interior and fix the atoms in closed form:

$$a_{\mathrm{hi}}=\mathrm{BR}_1(0)=\frac{r-\delta}{2},\qquad a_{\mathrm{lo}}=2\beta^\star-1-\delta\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \beta^\star=\mathrm{BR}_2(a_{\mathrm{lo}})=\frac{1+\delta+a_{\mathrm{lo}}}{2},$$ $$\lambda=\frac{a_{\mathrm{lo}}}{(r-\delta)-a_{\mathrm{lo}}},\qquad\qquad u_2(\text{attack cell})=r^2-2\beta^{\star2}\quad(\text{in }v_1\text{ units}).$$

$a_{\mathrm{hi}}$ is P1's unconstrained best reply to a quiet P2; $a_{\mathrm{lo}}$ is her best reply when hedging (she believes P2 attacks w.p. $1-\lambda$, against which she is in the dead zone); and $\beta^\star$ is P2's best attack against $a_{\mathrm{lo}}$. The attack-FOC substitution gives the clean identity $u_2(\text{attack})=r^2-2\beta^{\star2}$ used throughout. This leaves $\beta^\star$ (equivalently $a_{\mathrm{lo}},\lambda$) and $\rho$ undetermined — pinned by the two global incentive constraints the first-order approach misses (the all-pay payoff is non-concave across the dead zone).

5. The two global constraints, solved

5.1 Player 1 must not counter-attack → $\beta^\star$

Seeing $a_{\mathrm{lo}}$, player 1's profitable global deviation is to jump up and beat the attacking P2, to $e_1^{c}=\tfrac12\big[(r-\delta)+(1-\lambda)\beta^\star\big]$. Obedience binds at indifference $V_1^{\mathrm{mix}}(a_{\mathrm{lo}})=V_1^{\mathrm{mix}}(e_1^{c})$. Clearing denominators turns this into a cubic in $\beta$ (for every $r$). It always carries the spurious root $\beta=\tfrac{2+\delta+r}{4}$ — the degenerate point $a_{\mathrm{lo}}=a_{\mathrm{hi}}$. Factoring it out leaves a quadratic,

$$(r-\delta)\,\beta^2-2K\,\beta+(r+1)K=0,\qquad K=(r+2)(r-1)-\delta(r+1).$$

The discriminant collapses for a structural reason — the identity $K-(r-\delta)(r+1)\equiv-2$ holds for all $(r,\delta)$ — so $b^2-4ac=4K\!\cdot\!2=(-)(\,\cdots\,)$ reduces to $-2K$ and

$$\boxed{\ \beta^\star=\frac{K+\sqrt{-2K}}{\,r-\delta\,}\ }\qquad \text{real}\iff K<0\ \big(\text{automatic for }r\le1\big).$$

At $r=1$: $K=-2\delta$, the quadratic is $(1-\delta)\beta^2+4\delta\beta-4\delta=0$, and $\beta^\star=\tfrac{2\sqrt\delta-2\delta}{1-\delta}=\tfrac{2\sqrt\delta}{1+\sqrt\delta}$ — the symmetric formula. The atoms follow: $a_{\mathrm{lo}}=2\beta^\star-1-\delta$, and the lower edge $a_{\mathrm{lo}}=0$ gives $\delta_{\min}=2r+1-2\sqrt2$ (the cubic in $\delta$ factors as $(\delta-r)\big[\delta^2-(4r+2)\delta+(4r^2+4r-7)\big]$, whose relevant root has discriminant exactly $32$). The upper edge $r^2-2\beta^{\star2}=0\Leftrightarrow\beta^\star=r/\sqrt2$ gives $\delta_{\max}(r)$ of §2.

5.2 Player 2 must not attack when told to stay → $\rho$

Seeing "stay", player 2 (believing P1 hedged w.p. $\rho$) could deviate two ways: a partial attack beating only the weak atom $a_{\mathrm{lo}}$, or a full attack beating both. Each yields a cap; the binding one is the smaller.

$$\rho^\star=\min\!\big(\rho_{\text{part}},\,\rho_{\text{full}}\big),\qquad \rho_{\text{part}}=\frac{4\beta^{\star2}}{r^2}-1,$$ $$\rho_{\text{full}}=\frac{\sqrt{\,g_{\mathrm{lo}}^2+g_{\mathrm{hi}}^2-2r^2\,}-2\beta^\star}{a_{\mathrm{hi}}-a_{\mathrm{lo}}}, \qquad g_{\mathrm{lo}}=2\beta^\star,\quad g_{\mathrm{hi}}=\frac{2+\delta+r}{2}=1+\delta+a_{\mathrm{hi}}.$$

The partial cap binds for smaller leads, the full-attack root for larger leads. At $r=1$ these recover $\rho_{\text{part}}=4\beta^{\star2}-1$ and $\rho_{\text{full}}=\big[\sqrt{8\beta^{\star2}+4\beta^\star g+g^2-2}-2\beta^\star\big]/g$, $g=a_{\mathrm{hi}}-a_{\mathrm{lo}}$.

6. The optimal weights and value

Both global constraints bind: the value $\mathbb E[u_2]=p_1(r^2-2\beta^{\star2})$ is decreasing in $\beta$ (so $\beta$ is pushed to its floor, §5.1) and increasing in $\rho$ (so $\rho$ is pushed to its cap, §5.2). The beliefs then fix the weights:

$$p_1=\frac{(1-\lambda)\,\rho^\star}{\lambda+(1-\lambda)\rho^\star},\qquad p_0=\frac{\lambda}{1-\lambda}\,p_1,\qquad p_H=\frac{1-\rho^\star}{\rho^\star}\,p_0,$$ $$\boxed{\ \max_{\mathrm{CE}}u_2=v_1\,p_1\,(r^2-2\beta^{\star2})\ }$$ $$\mathbb E[u_1]=v_1\Big(-p_1a_{\mathrm{lo}}^2+p_0\big[1-(r{+}1{-}2\beta^\star)^2-a_{\mathrm{lo}}^2\big]+p_H\big[1-2a_{\mathrm{hi}}^2\big]\Big).$$

Everything on the right is a closed-form function of $(r,\delta)$, with overall scale $v_1$. This is the complete characterization for general $(v_1,v_2,\Delta)$.

7. The shape of the whole CE payoff set

A corollary of the analysis concerns the entire set of CE payoffs $\mathcal U=\{(u_1(\mu),u_2(\mu)):\mu\text{ a CE}\}$, not just its $u_2$-maximal point — and explains a qualitative change in its shape as the asymmetry grows.

Lemma (convexity). $\mathcal U$ is convex. The correlated equilibria are the distributions satisfying the linear obedience inequalities — a convex set — and the payoff map $\mu\mapsto(u_1,u_2)$ is linear, so its image is convex. On a fixed effort grid $\mathcal U$ is exactly a convex polygon; in the continuum it is convex with a possibly curved boundary.

Triangle vs. curved frontier. Public randomization between any two CE is a CE, so the whole chord joining two payoff points lies in $\mathcal U$; the only question is whether anything sits northeast of it. If every efficient CE is just a mixture of the two single-player-optimal anchors, the Pareto frontier collapses onto that chord and $\mathcal U$ is the triangle $(0,0)$–$(u_1^{\max},0)$–$(0,u_2^{\max})$. That is the degenerate, knife-edge case. Generically, intermediate welfare weights are served by genuinely different correlation devices that beat those mixtures on both margins, sweeping out a continuum of distinct extreme CE — a strictly convex, curved frontier.

Why the P1-dominant regime curves. In the balanced regime the origin is itself a CE: each player can be held to zero — P2 concedes, and enough threatened attacks pin P1 — so the lower-left corner sits exactly at $(0,0)$ and the two axes are edges. Push $(v_1,\Delta)$ into the P1-dominant regime and P1 can no longer be held to zero: with P2 conceding, P1's best reply $a_{\mathrm{hi}}$ earns strictly positive $u_1$, and a high-valuation P1 is too expensive for P2 to suppress. Hence $\min_{\mathrm{CE}}u_1>0$, the origin drops out of $\mathcal U$, and the corner that used to sit at $(0,0)$ lifts into a curve — the min-$u_1$ frontier: for each level of $u_2$, the cheapest-for-P2 way to hold P1 down, varying continuously with how much $u_2$ P2 insists on.

This dovetails with §6: the max-$u_2$ vertex is $(\mathbb E[u_1],\,u_2^{\max})$ with $\mathbb E[u_1]>0$ (e.g. $0.106$ at $v_1{=}v_2{=}1,\ \Delta{=}0.28$) — already off the $u_2$-axis. $\mathcal U$ has detached from both the origin and the $u_1{=}0$ axis, and the left boundary joining that vertex down to $(\min u_1,0)$ is a genuine arc, not an edge.

Left: balanced regime — $\mathcal U$ is the triangle $(0,0)$–$(u_1^{\max},0)$–$(0,u_2^{\max})$. Right: P1-dominant regime — the origin leaves $\mathcal U$ ($\min u_1>0$), the max-$u_2$ vertex is off the $u_2$-axis, and the lower-left corner becomes the curved min-$u_1$ frontier.

So curvature here is expected, not error — provided it is convex. A genuine smooth frontier shows up, at finite grid size $N$, as a many-faceted convex polygon that sharpens and converges as $N$ grows (the grid LP is an outer approximation, contracting toward the continuum). The only signature of numerical trouble is a non-convex feature: a boundary point dominated to the northeast by a chord of two others, or a boundary that will not stabilize under refinement.

Cross-check against the grid LP solver (independent of the construction). The solver maximizes $u_2$ directly over the CE polytope on an $N\times N$ effort grid; its value overstates the continuum (obedience is enforced only against grid deviations) and descends toward the closed form as $N$ grows. For $v_1{=}v_2{=}1$: at $\Delta{=}0.28$, closed form $0.0343$, grid $0.048\!\to\!0.043\!\to\!0.039$ (still falling); at $\Delta{=}0.22$, closed form $0.162$, grid $0.184\!\to\!0.184\!\to\!0.177\!\to\!0.174$ at $N{=}80,120,160,200$.
Open: tightness. The three-atom device is a certified correlated equilibrium, so $\max_{\mathrm{CE}}u_2$ above is a rigorous lower bound for every $(v_1,v_2)$. It is the exact maximizer on the symmetric line. For $v_1>v_2$ (the focus here) the obvious slack-extracting deviation — holding player 1 down to her reservation while loading $u_2$ — is weak, because a high-valuation P1 is hard to hold to $0$; so the device is expected tight throughout the window, but this has not yet been LP-confirmed. The opposite asymmetry is different: when $v_2>v_1$ ($r>1$) the device is not optimal (e.g. $v_2{=}1.1,v_1{=}1$: the LP reaches $0.391$ with $u_1$ pushed to $0$, versus the device's $0.256$), and for $v_2$ large enough $K\ge0$ so $\beta^\star$ ceases to be real — the maximizing support genuinely gains structure off the $r\le1$ side and is not described here.

9. Summary

For general quadratic contests, parametrized by $r=\sqrt{v_2/v_1}$ and $\delta=\Delta/\sqrt{v_1}$, the $u_2$-maximizing correlated equilibrium on the window $\big(\max(0,2r{+}1{-}2\sqrt2),\,\delta_{\max}(r)\big)$ is the three-atom device of §3, with $\beta^\star=\big(K+\sqrt{-2K}\big)/(r-\delta)$, $K=(r+2)(r-1)-\delta(r+1)$, the atoms and beliefs of §§4–5, and value $\max_{\mathrm{CE}}u_2=v_1\,p_1(r^2-2\beta^{\star2})>0$. The symmetric case is the single point $r=1$. The value is positive throughout the window and is attained by no Nash equilibrium: correlation is what lets player 2 extract a positive round-1 payoff against a committed leader, and the window of contests where it can do so closes once player 1's valuation exceeds player 2's by roughly $42\%$.
