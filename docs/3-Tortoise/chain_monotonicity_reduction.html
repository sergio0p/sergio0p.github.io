Chain monotonicity via the $q_1$-dial: a one-lemma reduction

Tortoise (Joffrion & Parreiras), symmetric quadratic case $v_1=v_2=1$, $\Delta=0$, $c(e)=e^2$. Builds on the explicit forward recursion of Docs/symmetric_quadratic_recursion.md (and its HTML twin). Goal: reduce the main monotonicity result — $u_1^\ast(m,n)$ strictly decreasing along the chain — to a single scalar sign lemma. Status of Part I (§1–§6): the reduction (§2–§4) is an exact identity; the surviving lemma (§5–§6) — "every comb gap widens in the dial" — was open when this file was first written (2026-06-19) but is now proved for the quadratic case (two independent ways; see the banner at §5). Part II (§7 onward), added 2026-07-07, carries the whole reduction to general convex cost, states the crux there, proves it for cubic cost ($\theta=3$), and lays out exactly how the remaining cases are being attacked. Part II is written for a human referee: every symbol is defined on first use, and every step of the argument in rg2_positivity_attack.md is spelled out. It takes the numerical verifications in that file as ground truth and does not re-derive them.

Summary: The recursion is driven by the single dial $q_1$ (P2's boundary mass at $y_1=0$); everything downstream is explicit. The chain's main result reduces, via the closed form $u_1^\ast=(q_1^\ast)^2/(1+q_1^\ast)$, to: $q_1^\ast$ is strictly decreasing along the chain. Dialing the new atom's mass instead of $q_1$ exposes an exact identity — the new atom's mass, as a function of $q_1$ along the next rung's recursion, equals minus the previous rung's residual, $q_{n+1}(q_1)=-F_{\mathrm{prev}}(q_1)$. This identity makes "born at zero mass" a tautology and collapses the entire monotonicity proof to one universal statement: each rung's shooting residual $F(q_1)$ is strictly increasing (single-crossing). That is the same scalar that is the Schur complement of the full equilibrium Jacobian, so this one lemma also delivers Theorem 1 (regularity) and Theorem 3 (uniqueness) in the symmetric-quadratic regime.

1. Setup: the dial and the residual

Notation follows Docs/symmetric_quadratic_recursion.md: P1 mixes over atoms $x_1<\cdots<x_m$ with masses $p_1,\ldots,p_m$; P2 over $y_1=0<\cdots<y_n$ with masses $q_1,\ldots,q_n$; supports interleave. The recursion march(q_1, m, n) is triangular with single source $q_1$:

$$q_1 \to x_1, u_1 \to p_1 \to y_2 \to q_2 \to x_2 \to p_2 \to y_3 \to \cdots$$

every entry an explicit rational map of $q_1$ (quadratic cost). It enforces all FOCs and all indifference conditions by construction, leaving one scalar slack — the closing normalization. Per rung:

$\sigma(m,m)$: every $q_\ell$ ($\ell=1,\ldots,m$) is recursion-determined; the leftover is P1's top mass $p_m$, fixed by $\sum p=1$. The residual is $F_{m,m}(q_1) = \sum_{\ell=1}^m q_\ell(q_1) - 1$.

$\sigma(m,m+1)$: every $p_k$ ($k=1,\ldots,m$) is recursion-determined; the leftover is P2's top mass $q_{m+1}$, fixed by $\sum q=1$. The residual is $F_{m,m+1}(q_1) = \sum_{k=1}^m p_k(q_1) - 1$.

The equilibrium is the root $q_1^\ast$ of $F$. By $u_1^\ast=(q_1^\ast)^2/(1+q_1^\ast)$ (strictly increasing in $q_1^\ast$), the main result

$$u_1^\ast \text{ strictly decreasing along the chain} \iff q_1^\ast \text{ strictly decreasing along the chain}.$$

The chain alternates step types: $(m,m)\to(m,m+1)$ adds a P2 atom (an "n-step"); $(m,m+1)\to(m+1,m+1)$ adds a P1 atom (an "m-step").

2. The key identity: new atom mass $=-(\text{previous residual})$

Take an n-step $(n,n)\to(n,n+1)$ (P2 gains the top atom $y_{n+1}$). The interior sweep is driven only by $q_1$ and is identical for both rungs up to the point of closure, so at the same $q_1$ the two recursions share $q_1,\ldots,q_n$ bit-for-bit (verified: $\max|\Delta|=0$ across the shared block; see symmetric_quadratic_recursion.md §10 and the warm-start experiments). In the $(n,n+1)$ recursion the new mass $q_{n+1}$ is the leftover:

$$q_{n+1}(q_1) = 1 - \sum_{\ell=1}^{n} q_\ell(q_1) = 1 - \big(F_{n,n}(q_1) + 1\big) = -\,F_{n,n}(q_1).$$

By the mirror argument an m-step $(m,m+1)\to(m+1,m+1)$ shares $p_1,\ldots,p_m$ bit-for-bit, and the new P1 mass is

$$p_{m+1}(q_1) = 1 - \sum_{k=1}^{m} p_k(q_1) = -\,F_{m,m+1}(q_1).$$

So in both cases, with $R$ the previous rung and $\widetilde R$ its successor (tilde = successor rung; a prime is never used for succession in this document):

$$\boxed{\ (\text{new atom's mass along the } \tilde R \text{ recursion})(q_1) \;=\; -\,F_R(q_1).\ }$$

The new atom belongs to whichever player gains it; its mass-as-a-function-of-$q_1$ is literally minus the previous rung's shooting residual. (Symbolically verified over $\mathbb{Q}(q_1)$ for the first several rungs — §6.)

3. The reduction to a single sign lemma

Three consequences, each immediate from the boxed identity.

(i) Born at zero is a tautology. At $q_1=q_1^\ast(R)$ we have $F_R=0$, so the new atom's mass is exactly 0. The "new atom is born with zero mass" degeneracy seen in the warm-start experiments is not a coincidence requiring proof — it is the identity evaluated at the previous root.

(ii) The new atom carries positive mass at the successor equilibrium. $\sigma(\widetilde R)$ is a genuine equilibrium with a real atom, so its new mass is positive: with $q_1=q_1^\ast(\widetilde R)$,

$$-\,F_R(q_1^\ast(\tilde R)) \;=\; (\text{new mass at } \tilde R) \;>\; 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad F_R(q_1^\ast(\tilde R)) < 0.$$

(iii) Root ordering. We have $F_R(q_1^\ast(R))=0$ and $F_R(q_1^\ast(\widetilde R))<0$. If $F_R$ is strictly increasing, then

$$F_R(q_1^\ast(\tilde R)) < 0 = F_R(q_1^\ast(R)) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad q_1^\ast(\tilde R) < q_1^\ast(R).$$

That is the chain step. Note it is the previous rung's residual F_R whose monotonicity is used — so to order every consecutive pair we need F strictly increasing for every rung R. Conditional on existence of the family with positive atom masses (input (ii)), the entire main result is therefore equivalent to:

Lemma (single-crossing). For every rung, the shooting residual $F(q_1)$ is strictly increasing on the relevant interval. $\big(F'(q_1) > 0.\big)$

4. What the reduction consolidates

The single lemma $F'(q_1)>0$ is the Schur complement of the full equilibrium Jacobian. Solve all but one of the equilibrium equations triangularly (the march does exactly this); the determinant of the full system then factors as $\det(\partial H/\partial z)=(\prod\text{ explicit pivots})\cdot F'(q_1)$, with the pivots manifestly nonzero in the interior cone. So the scalar $F'(q_1)$ alone governs the whole Jacobian — which turns the per-rung reduction into two global results. These hold for every rung $\sigma(m,n)$ of the chain, not merely the $\sigma(2,2)$ base case where they were first isolated:

Theorem 1 (Regularity). At every rung $\sigma(m,n)$, the equilibrium $z^\ast$ is a regular zero of the equilibrium map $H$: $\det(\partial H/\partial z)(z^\ast)\ne0$. Equivalently — since the pivots never vanish in the interior cone — $F'(q_1^\ast)\ne0$ at the equilibrium seed.

Theorem 3 (Uniqueness). Each rung $\sigma(m,n)$ carries at most one equilibrium. Because $F$ is strictly increasing ($F'>0$), its shooting residual has at most one zero $q_1^\ast$, so the triangular march $q_1\mapsto(x,y,q,p,u_1)$ has at most one admissible seed; with existence supplied separately, the rung's equilibrium is unique.

So one scalar inequality yields three things at once: chain monotonicity of $u_1^\ast$ (§3), regularity (Theorem 1), and uniqueness (Theorem 3). The same $F'>0$ this document reduces to — established for the symmetric-quadratic chain at all $n$, and now for the cubic ($\theta=3$) cost — is what carries all three.

5. The surviving lemma and a route to it

Target: $F'(q_1) > 0$ for every rung, on $(0, q_1^\ast]$ (or all of (0,1)).

A sufficient condition that is cleaner than $F'$ directly. Since $F_{n,n} = \sum_\ell q_\ell - 1$ and $F_{n,n+1} = \sum_k p_k - 1$, it suffices that every marginal mass is individually increasing in $q_1$:

$$\frac{dq_\ell}{dq_1} > 0 \ (\forall \ell), \qquad \frac{dp_k}{dq_1} > 0 \ (\forall k).$$

Then $F'=\sum(\text{positive})>0$ term by term, for both rung types at once.

Gap-coordinate tangent recursion. In interleaving gaps $d_j=y_{j+1}-x_j>0$, $\delta_j=x_j-y_j>0$ the mass laws are

$$q_{j+1} = (1+Q_j)\,d_j(d_j+2), \qquad p_j = (1+P_{j-1})\,\delta_j(\delta_j+2),$$

each strictly increasing in its own gap (gap > 0). Differentiating the four explicit maps w.r.t. $q_1$ gives a linear forward recursion in the tangents $(x_k',y_\ell',p_j',q_k',Q_k',\ldots)$, triangular in the same order, driven by $q_1'=1$. The whole question becomes a sign-propagation induction:

Induction target. Along the sweep, every gap derivative is positive: $d_j'>0$ and $\delta_j'>0$. Then $1+Q_j$ and $1+P_{j-1}$ have positive derivatives (sums of positive mass derivatives), and the gap laws give $q_{j+1}'>0$, $p_j'>0$.

The crux to stress-test. The induction must close on the gaps. The danger: $q_{j+1}'$ mixes the (positive) derivative of $1+Q_j$ with the derivative of the gap; whether the gap map is monotone in the right direction — i.e. whether feeding a larger $q_1$ widens every subsequent comb gap — is the real content. The base step is clean ($q_1=q_1$, $x_1=q_1/(1+q_1)$ increasing, first gap increasing); the inductive step is the open work.

2026-06-19 — $F'(q_1)>0$ confirmed exactly; lemma reduced to one nub ("gaps widen")

Ran the recursion symbolically over the exact rational-function field $\mathbb{Q}(t)$, $t=q_1$ (Nemo/AbstractAlgebra), for both families through $(5,5)$. Everything below is exact arithmetic, not floating point.

Identity (§2) verified exactly. The shared-block fact q[1:n] (resp. p[1:m]) is bit-for-bit identical across the adjacent rungs holds as an identity in $\mathbb{Q}(t)$; hence $\text{leftover}=1-\sum=-F_{\mathrm{prev}}$ exactly, for every rung tested.

The residual factors cleanly. Write F = N(t)/D(t). The denominators are manifestly positive on t>0:

$$D_{n,n} \propto \prod_{k=1}^{n-1}(2k\,t+1)^2, \qquad D_{m,m+1} \propto \prod_{k=1}^{m}\big((2k-1)t+1\big)^2.$$

So $\operatorname{sign}F=\operatorname{sign}N$. Universal facts: $F(0)=-1$ (every rung) and $N$ has positive leading coefficient with $\deg N = \deg D + 1$, so $F\to+\infty$. For $(n,n)$, lead $N=((2n-1)!!)^2$, lead $D=((2n-2)!!)^2$.

Every numerator $N$ has exactly one sign change in its coefficient sequence — a block of positive high-degree coefficients followed by a block of negatives ending in $-1$. By Descartes that is exactly one positive root. With $F(0)<0$, $F(\infty)>0$ this forces the sign pattern $-/+$: $F<0$ left of the root, $F>0$ right. That is precisely the §3(iii) hypothesis, and it is weaker than global monotonicity — single upward crossing is all the chain argument needs.

But the stronger claim is also true: $F'(t)>0$ for all $t>0$. Writing $F'=G/D^2$ with $G=N'D-ND'$, in every rung $G$ has all-nonnegative coefficients (#negative coeffs = 0), and moreover factors as

$$G \;=\; (\text{positive const})\times\Big(\textstyle\prod (\text{linear } kt{+}1)\Big)\times P(t),$$

with $P$ a polynomial having strictly positive coefficients. For $(n,n)$: the linear block is $\prod_{k=2}^{2n-1}(kt+1)$ and $P$ has degree $2n-2$, leading coeff $(2n-1)!$, constant term $(2n-1)$. So $F'>0$ termwise on $t>0$ — $F$ is globally strictly increasing, single crossing a fortiori.

Why $F'>0$ — the structural engine (exact through $(5,5)$, both families). As $q_1$ increases:

every atom moves right: $x_k'>0$, $y_\ell'>0$ (only $y_1\equiv0$ is fixed);

every comb gap widens: $\delta_j' = (x_j-y_j)'>0$ and $d_j' = (y_{j+1}-x_j)'>0$;

hence every recursion-determined mass increases: $q_{j+1}=(1{+}Q_j)\,d_j(d_j{+}2)$ and $p_j=(1{+}P_{j-1})\,\delta_j(\delta_j{+}2)$ are products of positive, increasing factors, so $q_\ell'>0,\ p_k'>0$;

the residual is the sum of exactly the recursion-determined masses minus 1, so $F'=\sum(\cdot)'>0$;

the one leftover atom $=1-\sum(\cdot)$ therefore decreases. This is the same fact as "$-F_{\mathrm{prev}}$ grows from 0 as $q_1$ drops below the previous root" — the chain step, seen from the mass side.

A provable sub-lemma (atom monotonicity by a phantom-zero barycenter). Each atom is a weighted average with a point pinned at 0:

$$y_{j+1}=\frac{\sum_{k\le j} p_k\,(1+x_k)}{1+\sum_{k\le j} p_k},\qquad x_{k}=\frac{\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\,(1+y_\ell)}{1+\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell},$$

(the "1" in the denominator is unit weight on a phantom point of value 0). Differentiate $y_{j+1}$: its numerator over $(1{+}\sum p)$ equals $\sum_k p_k'\,[(1+x_k)-y_{j+1}] + \sum_k p_k\,x_k'$. Since atoms lie in $[0,1)$ (reach $w=1$), $1+x_k>1>y_{j+1}$, so the first sum is $\ge0$ when $p_k'\ge0$, and the second is $\ge0$ when $x_k'\ge0$. Both the moving points rising and the weights on those above-average points rising push the average up. So $y_{j+1}'>0$ follows from $\{p_k',x_k'\ge0\}_{k\le j}$; mirror argument gives $x_k'>0$ from $\{q_\ell',y_\ell'\ge0\}$. Atom-monotonicity thus propagates given mass-monotonicity.

The single remaining nub. Mass-monotonicity needs the gaps to widen, $d_j'=y_{j+1}'-x_j'>0$ (and $\delta_j'>0$) — a difference of two positive atom-derivatives, which the barycenter lemma does not by itself sign. Two difference-identities the induction can lean on:

$$A_{j-1}\,(x_j-x_{j-1}) = q_j\,(1-\delta_j),\qquad B_{j-1}\,(y_{j+1}-y_j) = p_j\,(1-d_j),$$

with $A_k=1+Q_k$, $B_k=1+P_k$ (these give comb ordering / $\delta_j,d_j\in(0,1)$). Closing $d_j'>0,\ \delta_j'>0$ by induction along the sweep is the one open step; everything else ($\text{atoms}\uparrow\Longrightarrow\text{masses}\uparrow\Longrightarrow F'>0\Longrightarrow\text{single crossing}\Longrightarrow\text{chain monotone}$) then follows.

Net status. §5's lemma is no longer conjectural for any tested rung: $F'>0$ holds exactly through $(5,5)$ on both families, with an explicit positive-coefficient factorization. The general-$n$ proof is reduced to the lone statement "every comb gap is increasing in $q_1$," with atom-monotonicity already handled by the phantom-zero barycenter lemma. The $G$-coefficient-nonnegativity ("$G_n$ has no negative coefficient") is an equivalent, fully concrete polynomial target for an alternative proof.

A telescoping identity for the gaps, and why the crux is irreducible. Rearranging the mass laws gives, exactly,

$$(1+d_j)^2 = \frac{A_{j+1}}{A_j},\qquad (1+\delta_j)^2 = \frac{B_j}{B_{j-1}},\qquad A_k=1+\textstyle\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell,\ \ B_k=1+\sum_{i\le k}p_i$$

(verified exactly; on each rung the identity holds for the side whose masses are recursion-determined — the other side's top cumulative carries the leftover). Consequences: the comb telescopes, $A_k=(1+t)\prod_{j<k}(1+d_j)^2$, and

$$d_j'>0 \iff \big(\log A_{j+1}\big)' > \big(\log A_j\big)' \iff \frac{q_{j+1}'}{q_{j+1}} > \frac{A_j'}{A_j}$$

— gap-widening is exactly log-convexity of the cumulative mass sequence. This is elegant but not a reduction: since $q_{j+1}=A_j\,d_j(d_j+2)$,

$$\frac{q_{j+1}'}{q_{j+1}} - \frac{A_j'}{A_j} \;=\; d_j'\Big(\tfrac1{d_j}+\tfrac1{d_j+2}\Big),$$

so "new mass's log-derivative beats the cumulative's" is equivalent to $d_j'>0$, not weaker. The genuine obstruction is the two-sided barycenter coupling: $x_k A_k=\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell(1+y_\ell)$ needs the y's, $y_{j+1}B_j=\sum_{i\le j}p_i(1+x_i)$ needs the x's, and $d_j=y_{j+1}-x_j$ straddles both. Closing $d_j'>0,\ \delta_j'>0$ requires a joint invariant on the coupled (A,B)/(x,y) sweep — a real induction, not a one-liner. That joint invariant is the precise open problem; all the scaffolding (telescoping, phantom-zero barycenter, log-convexity equivalence, exact tangent recursion in the warm workspace) is in place to attack it.

Part II — The same reduction for a general convex cost

Added 2026-07-07. This part assumes the reader knows the model and the comb-march logic of Part I (a dial $t=q_1$ is fixed; the march then fills in every atom, mass and value of the rung one node at a time, in a fixed triangular order). It does not assume any of Part I's quadratic-specific algebra. Everything that Part I did over the rational-function field $\mathbb{Q}(q_1)$ has to be redone with a cost $c$ whose atoms are no longer rational in the dial; this part explains, in full and in order, how that is done — following Docs/general_cost_monotonicity_handoff.md (the reduction) and Docs/rg2_positivity_attack.md (the crux and the attack), whose numerical verifications are taken as given.

Summary of Part II. The reduction of Part I is cost-general in its skeleton: for any strictly convex $c$, "chain monotonicity" still reduces to "every comb gap widens as the dial $t$ increases," which still reduces to "every mass derivative is $\ge 0$." What is not cost-general is the proof of that last statement. For quadratic cost the atoms are rational in $t$ and a telescoping ladder proves it in one line. For any other cost the atoms are genuine algebraic functions of $t$, there is no closed recursion, and the mass-widening inequality — packaged as a single scalar $\mathrm{RG2}_k\ge 0$ per node — has to be proved by hand. The current attack rewrites $\mathrm{RG2}_k$ as the integral of a pointwise dominance margin $D_k(z)$ over one inter-atom interval, and reduces mass-widening to "$D_k\ge 0$ on the interval." That is proved completely for cubic cost ($\theta=3$, §13): $D_k$ is concave with non-negative endpoints. For $\theta\in(2,3)$ and $\theta>3$ the same margin is the object of study; §14 states exactly which one inequality is left open in each regime and why it is expected to hold.

7. Why the quadratic proof does not port, and the reframing that does

Part I's engine was that each P1 atom is a barycenter, $x_k=\big(\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell(1+y_\ell)\big)/\big(1+\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell\big)$, a rational function of the dial $t=q_1$. Rational atoms make the gaps $\delta_k,d_k$ rational, which gives a closed gap recursion over $\mathbb{Q}(t)$, which a log-derivative ladder telescopes into manifestly positive multipliers. The barycenter form is special: it comes from the indifference equation being linear in the atom, which happens exactly when $c'$ is affine.

Sharp obstruction. The comb atom is a rational function of $t$ if and only if $c'$ is affine if and only if $c$ is a convex quadratic $c(e)=ae^2+be$ ($a>0,\ b\ge0$) — the antiderivative, with $c(0)=0$, of an affine marginal cost $c'(e)=2ae+b$. The class keeps the linear term: $b=c'(0)$ may be strictly positive, and pure $c=\tfrac12ae^2$ is only the $b=0$ corner. (The term that must not be dropped is exactly that one: writing "$c'(e)=ae$ forces $c=\tfrac12ae^2$" silently imposes $c'(0)=0$ and collapses affine to linear — it is the affinity of $c'$, not homogeneity or a vanishing intercept, that keeps the atoms rational.) For any other convex cost — a power $c(e)=e^\theta$ with $\theta\ne 2$, or any mixed polynomial of degree $\ge 3$ — the atom is the root of a genuine degree-$\theta$ indifference equation, an algebraic (not rational) function of $t$. So there is no closed gap recursion to feed the ladder, and Part I's whole method stops. (Handoff §1–§3.) Power-cost homogeneity does not rescue it: a degree-$\theta$ power is homogeneous yet has $c'(e)=\theta e^{\theta-1}$ nonlinear, so its atoms stay algebraic. Homogeneity buys weaker gifts — a scale symmetry, a $\theta$-homotopy handle off $\theta=2$, single-power per-node equations — which is why power costs, not mixed polynomials, are the right next target.)

The whole affine-$c'$ class — the general quadratic $ae^2+be$ — therefore sits on the rational, ladder-provable side of this dichotomy, not just pure $e^2$. Chain monotonicity for it is proved in Docs/general_quadratic_monotonicity.md: the flat-field margin argument of §13.2 carries through verbatim (the constant term rides along inside $c'$-differences, where it cancels), with $c''\equiv 2a$ and $c'''\equiv0$ still supplying flat stiffness, centroid $\tfrac12$, and an affine margin $D_k$. The one new feature is the MC(0) floor $c'(0)=b>0$ — an existence constraint ($q_1^\ast>b/(2aw+b)$), not a monotonicity one; for symmetric values it degenerates rather than truncates the comb (Docs/mc0_dial.md).

The reframing. Do not chase a closed recursion. Observe instead that one structural fact is cost-general: the march is a triangular chain of $2\times 2$ root-finds. Each node consumes only already-settled quantities and produces exactly one new atom and one new mass. If we differentiate every defining equation of the march with respect to the dial $t$, the triangularity is inherited: we get a triangular linear system for the derivatives $(x_k',Y_k',p_k',q_k',\dots)$, solvable one node at a time by back-substitution, whose coefficients are values of $c'$ and $c''$ at the win-margins — no closed-form atom ever required. The quadratic ladder is simply the special case where this triangular solve has rational, sign-transparent entries. So the question "do the gaps widen?" becomes:

Cost-general target. Does the triangular $t$-Jacobian of the march have a sign pattern forcing $\delta_k'>0,\ \gamma_k'>0$ (hence $q_k',p_k'\ge0$, hence $F'>0$) using only $c''>0$ at the atoms? (Handoff §4.) The rest of Part II is the reduction of this to one scalar inequality per node, and the attack on that inequality.

8. Notation for the general-cost program (defined once, used throughout)

Everything below is at a fixed dial value $t$; primes denote $d/dt$. The reader should treat the comb as a function of $t$ and differentiate it. All symbols Part II uses:

Model constants. Values $v_1\ge v_2$; convex cost $c$ with $c(0)=0,\ c'>0,\ c''\ge0$. Reaches $w_i=c^{-1}(v_i)$ (the round-2 effort that just exhausts value $v_i$; so $c(w_i)=v_i$). Lead $\Delta$. The comb is written in shifted P2 coordinates with shift $f=w_2-w_1-\Delta$; we write $Y$ for the shifted P2 atom $\tilde y$ throughout, so $f$ appears only inside P2's own cost (Part I §2, "$f$ enters in exactly two places").

The comb. P2 plays atoms $Y_1=f<Y_2<\cdots$ with masses $q_1,q_2,\dots$; P1 plays atoms $x_1<x_2<\cdots$ with masses $p_1,p_2,\dots$. They interleave: $Y_1<x_1<Y_2<x_2<\cdots$. The dial is $t=q_1$ (P2's boundary mass), exactly as in Part I.

Win-margins (the round-2 lead that a player's atom concedes to, or takes from, a rival atom — the argument that goes inside $c$): $$\begin{aligned} r_l^{(j)} &:= w_1+Y_l-x_j &&(\text{P1 at its atom }x_j\text{ facing P2's atom }Y_l,\ l\le j),\\[0.5ex] s_i^{(k)} &:= w_2+x_i-Y_{k+1} &&(\text{P2 at }Y_{k+1}\text{ facing P1's atom }x_i,\ i\le k). \end{aligned}$$ These are the interior-branch arguments of Lemma 1 (QUICKSTART §0): P1's continuation against $Y_l$ is $v_1-c(r_l^{(j)})$, P2's against $x_i$ is $v_2-c(s_i^{(k)})$.

Gaps and widths. The two interleaving gaps and the two atom-spacings: $$\delta_j := x_j-Y_j>0\ (\text{"down-gap"}),\qquad \gamma_j := Y_j-x_{j-1}>0\ (\text{"up-gap"}),$$ $$h_k := x_{k+1}-x_k=\gamma_{k+1}+\delta_{k+1}\ (\text{P1 spacing}),\qquad g_k := Y_{k+2}-Y_{k+1}=\delta_{k+1}+\gamma_{k+2}\ (\text{P2 spacing}).$$ The diagonal margins collapse to a gap: $r_j^{(j)}=w_1-\delta_j$ and $s_k^{(k)}=w_2-\gamma_{k+1}$ (a player facing the rival atom just below its own). Correspondence with Part I: there the up-gap was called $d_j=y_{j+1}-x_j$; here that is $\gamma_{j+1}$. The down-gap $\delta_j$ is the same in both parts. So "$d_j'>0$ and $\delta_j'>0$" of §5–§6 is "$\gamma_{j+1}'\ge0$ and $\delta_j'\ge0$" here.

The march and its derivative chain. With $t=q_1$ pinned the march runs $$q_1=t\ \to\ x_1\ \xrightarrow{[B_1]}\ p_1,Y_2\ \xrightarrow{[C_1]}\ q_2,x_2\ \xrightarrow{[B_2]}\ p_2,Y_3\ \to\cdots$$ Each Step $B_k$ builds P2's atom $Y_{k+1}$ and mass $p_k$; each Step $C_k$ builds P1's atom $x_{k+1}$ and mass $q_{k+1}$. Differentiating in $t$ (primes) gives the parallel chain $q_1'=1\to x_1'\to[B_1]\,p_1',Y_2'\to[C_1]\,q_2',x_2'\to\cdots$, each derivative computed from already-known ones.

The two node operations (from Julia/converge/convex_march.jl). At each node the new atom is the root of an indifference equation and the new mass is a ratio of $c'$ values (a first-order condition). For Step $C$ (P1), with $Q(X)$ the cumulative P2 mass strictly below the atom being placed: $$\begin{gathered} \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{l\le k} q_l\,[v_1-c(r_l^{(k+1)})]+q_{k+1}[v_1-c(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)})]-c(x_{k+1})=u_1}_{\text{indifference: }x_{k+1}\text{ ties P1's value }u_1}\\[1.4ex] \underbrace{q_{k+1}=\frac{c'(x_{k+1})-\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,c'(r_l^{(k+1)})}{c'(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)})}}_{\text{FOC (marginal-cost balance)}} \end{gathered}$$ Step $B$ (P2) is the mirror with $s,w_2,f,u_2$ in place of $r,w_1,\cdot,u_1$. Both are used below; the point to hold onto is atom = indifference root (contains $c$), mass = FOC ratio (contains only $c'$).

Residual and equilibrium. The rung's one free scalar is the closing normalization; the shooting residual is $F(t)=\sum_\ell q_\ell(t)-1$ (total P2 mass minus one), and the equilibrium dial is its root $F(t^\ast)=0$. This is the same $F$ as Part I §1. $\text{Chain monotonicity}\Longleftrightarrow t^\ast\text{ moves the right way along the chain}\Longleftarrow F'(t)>0$.

The node residuals $\mathrm{RG1}_k,\mathrm{RG2}_k$ (P1) and their mirrors $\mathrm{RH1}_k,\mathrm{RH2}_k$ (P2). Differentiating the two node operations of Step $C_k$ in $t$ produces a triangular $2\times2$ linear system in the new derivatives $(x_{k+1}',q_{k+1}')$ (solved in §9.2). Isolating each unknown's own coefficient on the left, the two right-hand sides — assembled entirely from atoms and masses already placed below the node and their known derivatives — are named $\mathrm{RG2}_k$ (indifference row) and $\mathrm{RG1}_k$ (FOC row): $$q_{k+1}'\,\big(v_1-c(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)})\big)=\mathrm{RG2}_k,\qquad DX_k\,x_{k+1}'=\mathrm{RG1}_k+c'(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)})\,q_{k+1}',$$ $$DX_k:=c''(x_{k+1})+\sum_{l\le k+1}q_l\,c''(r_l^{(k+1)})>0,\qquad \mathrm{RG1}_k:=\sum_{l\le k+1}q_l\,c''(r_l^{(k+1)})\,Y_l'+\sum_{l\le k}c'(r_l^{(k+1)})\,q_l'.$$ Step $B_k$ (P2) gives $\mathrm{RH1}_k,\mathrm{RH2}_k$ verbatim with $(s,w_2,u_2)$ in place of $(r,w_1,u_1)$. Why "residual". Exactly as the shooting residual $F$ above is what the closing normalization leaves over (total mass minus one), each $\mathrm{RG}i_k$ is what the differentiated $i$-th equilibrium equation leaves over once the freshly-placed unknown's own term is moved to the left — the net push on the new atom/mass from every atom and mass already placed below it. The letters read: $\mathrm R$ = residual, $\mathrm G$ = P1's equation system (whose FOC field is the $G_k$ of §11), $\mathrm H$ = the P2 mirror; the trailing digit is the row (1 = FOC, 2 = indifference). The whole §9 reduction turns on the second one: since $v_1-c(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)})>0$, the sign of the new mass derivative $q_{k+1}'$ is precisely the sign of $\mathrm{RG2}_k$.

9. The reduction: $\text{chain monotonicity}\Longleftarrow\text{mass-widening}\Longleftarrow\text{one scalar per node}$

This section carries out the differentiation announced in §7 and shows that the entire monotonicity result rests on a single sign, $\mathrm{RG2}_k\ge0$ (and its mirror $\mathrm{RH2}_k\ge0$), at each node. All of it is cost-general; nothing here uses a closed atom formula.

9.1 The base node and the budget $u_1'$

The first atom $x_1$ solves the base indifference/FOC pair, which collapses to $E_0:=c'(x_1)-t\,c'(m_0)=0$, where $m_0:=w_1-\delta_1=w_1+f-x_1$ is P1's win-margin against the boundary atom $Y_1=f$. (Interpretation: at $x_1$, P1's marginal cost $c'(x_1)$ equals the dial-weighted marginal benefit of beating $Y_1$.) Differentiate $E_0=0$ in $t$: $$c''(x_1)\,x_1'-c'(m_0)-t\,c''(m_0)\,m_0'=0,\qquad m_0'=-x_1',$$ so $\big(c''(x_1)+t\,c''(m_0)\big)x_1'=c'(m_0)$, giving

$$\boxed{\;x_1'=\frac{c'(m_0)}{c''(x_1)+t\,c''(m_0)}>0\;}$$

— strictly positive from $c'>0,\ c''\ge0$ alone, no closed form needed. (For $c(e)=e^2$ this is exactly Part I's base derivative.) This is the seed of every positivity below: the first gap already widens, $\delta_1'=x_1'>0$.

The budget. P1's equilibrium value along the base is $u_1=t\,(v_1-c(m_0))-c(x_1)$. Differentiate and use the base FOC $c'(x_1)=t\,c'(m_0)$: $$u_1'=(v_1-c(m_0))+x_1'\big[t\,c'(m_0)-c'(x_1)\big]=(v_1-c(m_0))+x_1'\cdot 0,$$ so

$$\boxed{\;u_1'=v_1-c(m_0)>0\;}\qquad(\text{envelope theorem}).$$

The bracket vanishes because $x_1$ is chosen optimally (the envelope theorem: raising the dial $t$ has only the direct effect $v_1-c(m_0)$, P1's net win-value at the base margin). Crucially $u_1'$ is a fixed positive constant, the same at every node — it will turn out to be the entire "budget" that finances mass-widening. Verified $u_1'=1-0.7018^3=0.6539$ at symmetric $\theta=3$.

9.2 An interior node is a triangular $2\times2$ solve

Take Step $C_k$: the two defining equations (§8) are the FOC and the indifference, in the two new unknowns $(x_{k+1},q_{k+1})$. Differentiate both in $t$. Because every prior quantity ($x_i,Y_l,p_i,q_l$ for indices $\le k$, and $u_1$) already has a known derivative, the two differentiated equations form a linear system in $(x_{k+1}',q_{k+1}')$. Its node Jacobian is lower-triangular with positive diagonal:

$\dfrac{\partial(\text{FOC})}{\partial\,x_{k+1}}=c''(x_{k+1})+\sum_{l\le k+1}q_l\,c''(r_l^{(k+1)})=:DX_k>0$  — the diagonal, a pure sum of $c''$ terms, positive by convexity.

$\dfrac{\partial(\text{indiff})}{\partial\,x_{k+1}}=-(\text{FOC residual})\equiv0$ at the node  — the off-diagonal vanishes (this is again the envelope: the indifference value is stationary in the atom).

$\dfrac{\partial(\text{indiff})}{\partial\,q_{k+1}}=v_1-c(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)})=v_1-c(w_1-\delta_{k+1})>0$  — the other diagonal, P1's interior continuation value at the fresh margin.

$\text{Triangular with positive diagonal}\Longrightarrow\det>0$, and — this is the useful part — the mass derivative solves first (its equation, the differentiated indifference, does not contain $x_{k+1}'$ because the off-diagonal is zero), then the atom derivative by back-substitution. Recalling the two node residuals of §8 ($q_{k+1}'\cdot(v_1-c(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)}))=\mathrm{RG2}_k$ and $DX_k\,x_{k+1}'=\mathrm{RG1}_k+c'(r_{k+1})\,q_{k+1}'$):

$$q_{k+1}'=\frac{\mathrm{RG2}_k}{\,v_1-c(w_1-\delta_{k+1})\,},\qquad x_{k+1}'=\frac{\mathrm{RG1}_k+c'(r_{k+1})\,q_{k+1}'}{DX_k}.$$

The point of writing it this way: the denominator $v_1-c(w_1-\delta_{k+1})$ is positive, so the sign of the new mass derivative $q_{k+1}'$ is exactly the sign of the scalar $\mathrm{RG2}_k$. Everything reduces to that one scalar.

9.3 Gap-widening and $F'$ both follow from mass-widening

Recall the FOC residual (§8) $$\mathrm{RG1}_k=\sum_{l\le k+1}q_l\,c''(r_l^{(k+1)})Y_l'+\sum_{l\le k}c'(r_l^{(k+1)})\,q_l'\;>\;0,$$ a sum of products of prior derivatives, all $\ge0$ under the running induction. Hence from the boxed $x_{k+1}'$ formula, with $DX_k>0,\ \mathrm{RG1}_k>0,\ c'>0$:

$x_{k+1}'>0$ the instant $q_{k+1}'\ge0$; and (mirror, Step $B$) $Y_{k+1}'>0$ the instant $p_k'\ge0$. Therefore:

  • Gap-widening. $\delta_{k+1}'=x_{k+1}'-Y_{k+1}'$ and $\gamma_{k+1}'=Y_{k+1}'-x_k'$ are differences of positive atom-derivatives, and (as §11 makes precise) come out $\ge0$ once the masses widen.
  • $F'>0$. $F=\sum_\ell q_\ell-1$, so $F'=\sum_\ell q_\ell'$; if every $q_\ell'\ge0$ and $q_1'=1$ then $F'\ge1>0$. A strictly increasing $F$ crosses zero at most once — the equilibrium dial is unique and moves monotonically along the chain.
  • $u_1^{\ast\prime}>0$ by the envelope constant of §9.1, and $u_1^\ast$ increasing in $t^\ast$ then gives the payoff statement (Part I §3).

So the whole theorem — for any convex $c$ — rests on: every mass derivative is non-negative, i.e. $\mathrm{RG2}_k\ge0$ and $\mathrm{RH2}_k\ge0$ at every node.

What the two scalars are, explicitly. Writing out the differentiated indifference (P1 side) and its mirror (P2 side, which has no $u_2'$ term since $u_2^\ast\equiv0$):

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{RG2}_k=\ &\underbrace{\Big[\sum_{l\le k+1}q_l\,c'(r_l^{(k)})\,Y_l'+u_1'\Big]}_{\text{positive group: own-drift}+\text{budget}}\\[0.5ex] &-\underbrace{\Big[\sum_{l\le k}(v_1-c(r_l^{(k)}))\,q_l'\Big]}_{\text{rival mass-drift (fights)}}\ \ge\ 0, \end{aligned}$$ $$\mathrm{RH2}_k=\Big[\sum_{i\le k}p_i\,c'(s_i^{(k)})\,x_i'\Big]-\Big[\sum_{i\le k-1}(v_2-c(s_i^{(k)}))\,p_i'\Big]\ \ge\ 0.$$

In words: at each node the positive group (the rate at which the already-placed atoms drift right, weighted by marginal cost, plus the fixed budget $u_1'$) must dominate the negative group (the rate at which the rival's masses are growing, weighted by continuation value). The first node $C_1$ has an empty rival sum (nothing below it), so its negative group is $0$ — a clean induction base. Numerically the ratio (negative/positive) is always $<1$ (worst $\approx0.94$), and it creeps toward $1$ as the node index grows — that creep is the whole difficulty.

10. Why this is genuinely hard: the circularity obstruction

It is tempting to prove $\mathrm{RG2}_k\ge0$ from P1's indifference directly. That fails, and understanding why is what dictated the entire margin-field attack of §11.

The indifference identity is circular. Differentiating P1's indifference (which holds identically in $t$ at the atom $x_{k+1}$) yields $$(\bigstar)\qquad \sum_{l\le k+1}q_l'\,(v_1-c(r_l))=u_1'+\sum_{l\le k+1}q_l\,c'(r_l)\,Y_l'\;=\;\text{positive group}.$$ Subtracting the negative group from both sides gives, identically, $\text{posGroup}-\text{negGroup}=q_{k+1}'(v_1-c(r_{k+1}))=\mathrm{RG2}_k$. So the statement "positive group $\ge$ negative group" is logically identical to "$q_{k+1}'\ge0$" — it is not an independent bound. Indifference alone can never close the sign.

The FOC does not help either. One might try differencing the FOC at consecutive atoms. But the P1 FOC $c'(x_k)=\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,c'(r_l^{(k)})$ holds identically in $t$, so $\tfrac{d}{dt}[\text{FOC}_k]\equiv0$ (machine-zero, $\sim10^{-15}$, at every atom): differentiating it produces no new equation, only the relation the march already uses to back out $x_{k+1}'$. The node genuinely has two equations in two unknowns and no hidden third.

Circularity theorem (the negative result that shapes everything). Inside the closed algebra generated by the march's own identities — $(\bigstar)$, its P2 mirror, the differentiated FOC, and the mass balances — the target inequality carries no sign information: it rearranges to a tautology. Any proof of mass-widening must inject an inequality from outside that algebra. The only candidates are (i) convexity used as an inequality (not just $c''\ge0$ as an identity coefficient, but $c'$/$c''$/$c'''$ monotone comparisons across shifted margins), (ii) admissibility (masses positive; partial sums $<1$), (iii) the cross-player coupling, (iv) moment/Chebyshev structure of the atom distribution. The margin-field reformulation of §11 is built precisely to expose where an outside convexity inequality can enter — and it enters as a single term $J_k\ge0$ that is free whenever $c'''\ge0$.

Consequence for the proof architecture. Because no rearrangement of the equalities can work, the earlier plan — "prove $\mathrm{RG2}_k$ decays monotonically in $k$ (A), then prove the terminal node is positive (B)" — was abandoned: decay (A) is exactly the kind of statement the circularity theorem forbids proving from the identity algebra. The replacement is to stop reasoning node-to-node and instead resolve $\mathrm{RG2}_k$ within one interval, as an integral of a pointwise quantity whose positivity can be read off from convexity. That is §11.

11. The margin field — turning each node scalar into a pointwise integral

The idea: freeze the dial and look at P1's stage-$k$ problem as a function of a continuous effort $z$ ranging over one inter-atom interval $[x_k,x_{k+1}]$. Two "fields" on that interval — the FOC field and its $t$-derivative, the flux field — package all the node data, and the node scalar $\mathrm{RG2}_k$ turns out to be the integral over the interval of a single pointwise dominance margin $D_k(z)$. Positivity of the node scalar then becomes positivity of a function on an interval, attackable by endpoints + concavity. Everything in this section is an exact identity, verified in rg2_positivity_attack.md to the tolerances quoted; we take those as given and explain what each identity says and why it is the right object.

Prime convention (read before the fields — applies to §11 and §13). From here on a prime can mean one of two derivatives, and which one is fixed entirely by what it sits on — never ambiguous, because no symbol is ever both a march scalar and a spatial field:

  • On a march quantity — $x_k',\ q_k',\ p_k',\ \delta_k',\ \gamma_k',\ Y_k',\ u_1',\ F'$ — a prime is the dial derivative $d/dt$, exactly as fixed in §8. (These are scalars that depend on the dial $t$.)
  • On a field of the effort variable $z$ (or $Y$, for the P2 mirror) — $\varphi_k'(z),\ W_k'(z),\ G_k'(z),\ \Phi_k'(z),\ D_k'(z),\ D_k''(z),\ V_k'(Y),\ r_l'(z)$ — a prime is the spatial derivative $d/dz$ (resp. $d/dY$): the slope of that field along the interval. (These are functions of $z$ at a frozen dial.)
  • Cost derivatives $c',\ c'',\ c''',\ c^{(4)}$ are derivatives of the single-variable cost $c$ in its own (effort) argument — so $c'(r_l(z))$ means $c'$ evaluated at the margin $r_l(z)$, not $\tfrac{d}{dz}c(r_l(z))$.
  • Explicit forms are used whenever the variable is not the argument shown: $\partial_t$ marks a dial-derivative of a $z$-field (e.g. $\Phi_k(z):=-\partial_tG_k(z)$), and the cascade slope is written out as $dR_1/d\delta$ (§11.1) rather than primed.

So in a single expression like $D_k(z)=Y_{k+1}'\,W_k(z)-\Phi_k(z)$: the prime on $Y_{k+1}'$ is $d/dt$ (a march velocity), while $D_k''(z)$ later is $d/dz$ (a spatial curvature). The two live on different symbols and never collide.

11.1 Stiffness, the concave payoff-net, and the moment identities

Define P1's payoff-net against the first $k$ P2 atoms, as a function of a hypothetical effort $z$: $$\varphi_k(z):=\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,\big(v_1-c(r_l(z))\big)-c(z),\qquad r_l(z):=w_1+Y_l-z.$$ This is P1's expected round-continuation value from bidding $z$ when only $Y_1,\dots,Y_k$ lie below. Its derivatives: $$\varphi_k'(z)=\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,c'(r_l(z))-c'(z),\qquad \varphi_k''(z)=-\Big(c''(z)+\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,c''(r_l(z))\Big)=:-\,W_k(z).$$ The quantity $$\boxed{\,W_k(z):=c''(z)+\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,c''(r_l(z))>0\,}\qquad(\text{the }\textit{stiffness})$$ is strictly positive by convexity, so $\varphi_k$ is strictly concave, with its maximum exactly at the atom: $\varphi_k'(x_k)=0$ is the FOC. (The name: $W_k$ is the local curvature — the "stiffness" — of P1's objective; it is the diagonal $DX$ of §9.2 evaluated along the interval.)

Two exact moment identities come from reading the FOC at the next atom $x_{k+1}$ and the indifference between $x_k$ and $x_{k+1}$. The next FOC gives $\varphi_k'(x_{k+1})=-q_{k+1}c'(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)})$, and indifference gives $\varphi_k(x_k)-\varphi_k(x_{k+1})=q_{k+1}(v_1-c(r_{k+1}^{(k+1)}))$. Since $\varphi_k'=-\!\int W_k$ from $x_k$ (where $\varphi_k'=0$), these are moments of the stiffness:

$$\begin{aligned} M_0&:=q_{k+1}\,c'(w_1-\delta_{k+1})=\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}W_k(z)\,dz,\\[0.5ex] M_1&:=q_{k+1}\,(v_1-c(w_1-\delta_{k+1}))=\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}(x_{k+1}-z)\,W_k(z)\,dz. \end{aligned}$$

$M_0$ is the zeroth moment (total stiffness mass across the interval); $M_1$ is the first moment measured from the top endpoint $x_{k+1}$. Note $M_1$ is exactly the indifference gap $q_{k+1}(v_1-c(w_1-\delta_{k+1}))$ — the same product $v_1-c(w_1-\delta_{k+1})$ that is the denominator of $q_{k+1}'$ in §9.2. That is the bridge between "mass" and "margin."

The cascade and its centroid. Dividing the two moments, $$R_1(\delta_{k+1})=h_k\,\theta_k,\qquad R_1(\delta):=\frac{v_1-c(w_1-\delta)}{c'(w_1-\delta)},\qquad \theta_k:=\frac{\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}(x_{k+1}-z)W_k\,dz}{h_k\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}W_k\,dz}=\frac{M_1}{h_kM_0}\in(0,1).$$ $R_1$ is a fixed increasing function of the gap ($R_1(0)=0$, and $\dfrac{dR_1}{d\delta}>1$ — a derivative with respect to the gap-argument $\delta$, not the dial $t$; primes are reserved for $d/dt$, so this derivative is written out in full), and $\theta_k$ is the normalized stiffness centroid measured from the top — where the stiffness mass sits inside the interval, as a fraction of the interval width. This one scalar $\theta_k$ controls how gap-widening relates to mass-widening (§11.5). For quadratic cost $W_k$ is constant, so $\theta_k\equiv\tfrac12$ and the cascade $R_1(\delta_{k+1})=\tfrac12 h_k$ is exactly the harmonic gap law $1/\delta_{k+1}=1/\gamma_{k+1}+1/w_1$ of Part I. The mirror (P2) objects are $V_k(Y):=c''(Y-f)+\sum_{i\le k}p_i\,c''(s_i(Y))$, $R_2$, and centroid $\tilde\theta_k$ (tilde marks the P2 mirror, as in the base case $\tilde\theta_0=\tfrac13$ of §13.1).

11.2 The FOC field and the flux field

Now differentiate in $t$. Define the FOC field and, as minus its $t$-derivative, the flux field: $$G_k(z):=c'(z)-\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,c'(r_l(z))\ \big(=-\varphi_k'(z)\big),\qquad \Phi_k(z):=-\partial_t G_k(z)=\sum_{l\le k}\big[q_l'\,c'(r_l(z))+q_l\,c''(r_l(z))\,Y_l'\big].$$ Read them: $G_k(z)$ is P1's net marginal cost at effort $z$ (own marginal cost minus the marginal benefit of beating the atoms below). It vanishes at the atom, $G_k(x_k)=0$ (FOC), rises to $G_k(x_{k+1})=q_{k+1}c'(w_1-\delta_{k+1})=M_0$ at the next atom (next FOC), with slope $G_k'(z)=W_k(z)$ — the stiffness is literally the slope of the FOC field. The flux field $\Phi_k(z)$ is the rate at which that net-marginal-cost curve is being pushed down as the dial rises (masses growing, $q_l'\ge0$; atoms drifting, $Y_l'\ge0$) — both effects lower P1's net marginal cost, so $-\partial_tG_k=\Phi_k\ge0$ under the induction. The scalar $\Phi_k:=\Phi_k(x_k)$ evaluated at the left endpoint is, by the differentiated FOC, exactly $x_k'\,W_k(x_k)$: the atom's velocity times the local stiffness.

11.3 The two-term identity: $\mathrm{RG2}_k=\int D_k$

Since $M_1=\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}G_k(z)\,dz$ (integrate $G_k=-\varphi_k'$ by parts against the weight, using $G_k(x_k)=0$), differentiate this representation of $M_1$ in $t$. On the left, $M_1'=\mathrm{RG2}_k+M_0\,\delta_{k+1}'$ (product rule on $q_{k+1}(v_1-c(w_1-\delta_{k+1}))$). On the right, Leibniz on $\int G_k$ with the moving top endpoint $x_{k+1}$ and $G_k(x_k)=0$ gives $M_0\,x_{k+1}'-\int\Phi_k$. Since $x_{k+1}'=\delta_{k+1}'+Y_{k+1}'$, the $M_0\delta_{k+1}'$ terms cancel and one is left with the two-term identity:

$$\boxed{\ \mathrm{RG2}_k=M_0\,Y_{k+1}'-\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}\Phi_k(z)\,dz\ }\qquad(\text{verified }\le2.3\times10^{-15}).$$

Because $M_0=\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}W_k(z)\,dz$, the two terms combine under one integral sign:

$$\boxed{\ \mathrm{RG2}_k=\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}} D_k(z)\,dz,\qquad D_k(z):=Y_{k+1}'\cdot W_k(z)-\Phi_k(z)\ }$$

the dominance margin. This is the central object of the whole attack. Its meaning:

Phantom-velocity reading. Every level set of the field $G_k$ moves, as $t$ rises, at the "phantom velocity" $v_k(z):=\Phi_k(z)/W_k(z)$ (the level moves to keep $G_k$ constant: $\partial_tG_k+v_k\,G_k'=0$). The real atom $x_k$ is the zero level set, moving at $v_k(x_k)=x_k'$. So $$D_k(z)=W_k(z)\,\big(Y_{k+1}'-v_k(z)\big)\ge0\ \iff\ v_k(z)\le Y_{k+1}'\ \text{for all }z\in[x_k,x_{k+1}].$$ i.e. $\mathrm{RG2}_k\ge0$ follows if P2's next atom velocity $Y_{k+1}'$ outruns every phantom level-set velocity of P1's field across the gap. Empirically this holds with room: $\max_z v_k(z)/Y_{k+1}'\le0.99$ strictly, at every node of every configuration tested.

11.4 The left endpoint is the induction hypothesis

Evaluate $D_k$ at the left endpoint. There $\Phi_k(x_k)=x_k'W_k(x_k)$ (§11.2), so $$D_k(x_k)=Y_{k+1}'W_k(x_k)-x_k'W_k(x_k)=W_k(x_k)\,(Y_{k+1}'-x_k')=W_k(x_k)\,\gamma_{k+1}'.$$

That is: the left-endpoint value of the dominance margin is exactly the stiffness times the up-gap derivative $\gamma_{k+1}'$ — the very gap-widening quantity the induction is trying to propagate. So "$D_k(x_k)\ge0$" is the induction hypothesis (that the previous gap widened), imported for free. The whole game is to get from that one endpoint to positivity of $D_k$ across the entire interval.

11.5 Node identities and the shape condition (M)

Two "fresh-injection" node identities (verified $\le1.1\times10^{-14}$) relate the next gap's derivative to the right endpoint of the current margin:

$$(\mathrm{N1})\quad \delta_{k+1}'\,W_{+}(x_{k+1})=q_{k+1}'\,c'(w_1-\delta_{k+1})-D_k(x_{k+1}),\qquad (\mathrm{N2})\quad \gamma_{k+2}'\,V_{+}(Y_{k+2})=p_{k+1}'\,c'(w_2-\gamma_{k+2})-D_k^{\text{mir}}(Y_{k+2}),$$

($W_+,V_+$ are the frontier-inclusive stiffnesses). In words: the next gap widens iff the fresh mass-injection $q_{k+1}'c'(\cdot)$ beats the right-endpoint value $D_k(x_{k+1})$ of the current margin. Substituting $q_{k+1}'=\mathrm{RG2}_k/(v_1-c)=\int D_k/(v_1-c)$ into (N1) and using the cascade $R_1(\delta_{k+1})=h_k\theta_k$, all the value-content cancels and gap-widening becomes a pure statement about the shape of $D_k$ on the interval:

$$\boxed{\ (\mathrm{M}_k)\qquad \delta_{k+1}'\ge0\ \iff\ \bar D_k\ \ge\ \theta_k\,D_k(x_{k+1}),\qquad \bar D_k:=\frac{1}{h_k}\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}D_k=\frac{\mathrm{RG2}_k}{h_k}\ }$$

— the interval-mean of the margin must exceed the centroid-fraction $\theta_k$ of its right-endpoint value. This is pure algebra given (N1); it introduces no new inequality, but it is the right frame, because now both outputs we need — mass-widening ($\mathrm{RG2}_k=\int D_k\ge0$) and gap-widening ((M$_k$)) — are shape statements about the single function $D_k$ and the single centroid $\theta_k$.

11.6 The three shape identities: concavity, endpoints, centroid

These are the levers that will prove $D_k\ge0$ and (M$_k$). Each is exact.

(C) Concavity of the margin. Differentiating $D_k(z)=Y_{k+1}'W_k(z)-\Phi_k(z)$ twice in $z$ (using $W_k'=c'''(z)-\sum q_lc'''(r_l)\cdot(-1)$-type terms and $\Phi_k'(z)=-\partial_tW_k(z)$): $$D_k''(z)=Y_{k+1}'\,c^{(4)}(z)+\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,c^{(4)}(r_l(z))\,(Y_{k+1}'-Y_l')-\sum_{l\le k}q_l'\,c'''(r_l(z)).$$ Under the induction ($q_l'\ge0$, and $Y_{k+1}'\ge Y_l'$ = motion-ordering), every term is $\le0$ as soon as $c'''\ge0\ge c^{(4)}$ — the class $\theta\in[2,3]$. So $D_k$ is concave for that class, hence minimized at an endpoint and $\bar D_k\ge\tfrac12(D_k(x_k)+D_k(x_{k+1}))$.

(E2) Endpoint split. The right endpoint differs from the left by $$D_k(x_{k+1})-D_k(x_k)=Y_{k+1}'\,\Delta W+J_0,\qquad \Delta W:=W_k(x_{k+1})-W_k(x_k),$$ $$J_0:=\sum_{l\le k}q_l'\big[c'(r_l^{(k)})-c'(r_l^{(k+1)})\big]+\sum_{l\le k}q_lY_l'\big[c''(r_l^{(k)})-c''(r_l^{(k+1)})\big].$$ Since $x_{k+1}>x_k$ makes every margin shrink ($r_l^{(k+1)}<r_l^{(k)}$) and $c',c''$ increase, and $q_l',Y_l'\ge0$ under the IH, $J_0\ge0$ free. So $D_k(x_{k+1})\ge D_k(x_k)+Y_{k+1}'\Delta W$; the only threat to right-endpoint positivity is $\Delta W<0$ (stiffness dipping across the interval).

(E1) Trapezoid-error identity for the centroid. Because $M_1=\int G_k$ with $G_k(x_k)=0$ and $G_k''=W_k'$, the deviation of the centroid from $\tfrac12$ is exactly a bump-weighted average of the stiffness slope: $$\theta_k-\tfrac12=-\frac{1}{2h_kM_0}\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}(z-x_k)(x_{k+1}-z)\,W_k'(z)\,dz.$$ The weight $(z-x_k)(x_{k+1}-z)\ge0$ is the "bump" (zero at both ends, peak in the middle). So $\theta_k\le\tfrac12$ iff the bump-averaged stiffness slope is $\ge0$ — a strictly weaker condition than "$W_k$ increasing pointwise." This is the identity that decides the centroid side of (M$_k$).

11.7 The flux decomposition and the one outside inequality $J_k\ge0$

Integrating (I2) once more by parts against $W$ (rather than $G$) gives the equivalent but more transparent flux decomposition:

$$\begin{gathered} \mathrm{RG2}_k=\underbrace{\vphantom{\Big|}\mathrm{lead}_k}_{M_0Y_{k+1}'}\ -\ h_k\Phi_k\ +\ J_k,\qquad J_k:=\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}(x_{k+1}-z)\,\partial_tW_k(z)\,dz,\\[1ex] \partial_tW_k(z)=\sum_{l\le k}\big[q_l'\,c''(r_l(z))+q_l\,c'''(r_l(z))\,Y_l'\big]. \end{gathered}$$

Here $\mathrm{lead}_k=q_{k+1}c'(w_1-\delta_{k+1})Y_{k+1}'$ is the fresh atom's contribution, $h_k\Phi_k$ is the left-endpoint flux scaled by the width, and $J_k$ is the first moment of the stiffness drift $\partial_tW_k$. This last term is where the outside inequality the circularity theorem demands enters:

$J_k\ge0$ is free for $c'''\ge0$. Integrating $J_k$ by parts atom-wise splits it into $$J_k=\sum_{l\le k}q_l'\Big[\underbrace{\textstyle\int[c'(r_l^{(k)})-c'(u)]\,du}_{\ge0\text{ always }(c'\uparrow)}\Big]+\sum_{l\le k}q_lY_l'\Big[\underbrace{\textstyle\int[c''(r_l^{(k)})-c''(u)]\,du}_{\text{sign of }c'''}\Big].$$ The first bracket is $\ge0$ for any convex $c$. The second has the sign of $c'''$. So for $c'''\ge0$ (marginal cost convex — all powers $\theta\ge2$), $J_k\ge0$ under the induction ($q_l',Y_l'\ge0$). This is the genuine injection of convexity as an inequality, exactly what §10 said any proof must supply; it is not derivable from the identity algebra. (For $\theta\in(1,2)$, $c'''<0$ and $J_k$'s sign is not free — but numerically $J_k>0$ still, the $q'c''$ part dominating; that dominance is one of the open items of §14.)

How the pieces will be used. The two representations are equivalent ((I2) $\Leftrightarrow$ lead$-h\Phi+J$), and each suggests a sufficient condition:

  • Via (I2): prove $D_k(z)\ge0$ pointwise. Enough: both endpoints $\ge0$ (left = IH by §11.4; right by (E2)) and concavity (C). This is the route that closes $\theta=3$ (§13).
  • Via the flux form: prove $\mathrm{lead}_k\ge h_k\Phi_k$ (call it (G$_k$)) and add $J_k\ge0$. (G$_k$) is equivalent, since $M_0=h_k\bar W_k$, to the weighted motion-ordering $\bar W_k\,Y_{k+1}'\ge W_k(x_k)\,x_k'$; at quadratic cost (constant $W$) it is exactly gap-widening $\gamma_{k+1}'\ge0$. So the flux program is the correct cost-general lift of Part I's "gaps widen" — with the caveat (measured) that the (G) margin thins as $k$ grows while $J$ stays flat, so asymptotically $\mathrm{RG2}$ becomes $J$-carried and the honest target is the joint three-term positivity, not (G) alone.

Either way, the crux has been reduced from a node-to-node accounting to a statement about one scalar field $D_k$ (equivalently one flux comparison plus $J_k$) on one interval — self-contained, no downstream motions, no cross-player coupling. That is the "well-ordered, non-circular endgame" the circularity theorem forced us toward.

12. The on-window realization and the window lemma

Before the $\theta=3$ proof, one indispensable structural fact — discovered late, and the reason the cubic proof closes at all. Every failure of the shape identities (§11.6) ever observed numerically was off-window: at a dial pushed past the equilibrium, or on an asymmetric configuration where one player's partial masses had already summed past $1$. The proof only ever needs the dial on a restricted range.

On-window realization. To locate the equilibrium dial $t^\ast$ (the root of $F$) and to run the sweep up to it, one only needs $t\le \bar t:=\min(t_q^\ast,t_p^\ast)$, where $t_q^\ast,t_p^\ast$ are the dial values at which P2's, resp. P1's, cumulative mass first reaches $1$. On this window every partial mass is $<1$: $$Q_k(z):=\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,\mathbf 1[r_l(z)>0]<1,\qquad P_k<1\qquad\text{for all }k,\ \text{all }z\ \text{in play}.$$ This is not an assumption — it is forced by the value of the residual: on the window $F\le0$, and $Q_k=1+F-\sum_{l>k}q_l<1$ because both $F\le0$ and the tail $\sum_{l>k}q_l\ge0$. As $t\to0$, $F\to-1$ and $Q_k\to0$. (This is the "window lemma," proved from the definition of $\mathcal W=\{F\le0\}$; non-circular because it reads $Q_k$ off the value of $F$, not off the thing being proved.)

Why it matters: the cubic argument needs the stiffness to be increasing, $W_k'(z)\ge0$. For $c(e)=e^3$ we will see $W_k'(z)=6(1-Q_k(z))$, which is $\ge0$ exactly because $Q_k<1$ on-window. Off-window ($Q_k>1$) the stiffness would decrease and the endpoint/centroid controls would fail — which is precisely what the numerics showed at $1.8\,t^\ast$ and on asymmetric configurations that overshoot the $p$-window ($\sum p=1.35$ at $t_q^\ast$). Restricting to $\bar t$ removes all of that.

13. The theorem that closes: cubic cost ($\theta=3$)

Here the entire program of §9–§12 comes together into a complete proof, for $c(e)=e^3$ (and, verbatim, any convex cubic $ae^3+be^2+ce$ with $a>0$). This is the first monotonicity result beyond quadratic. It is stated as an induction on the comb node; each step cites an identity from §11, all taken as verified.

Cubic specializations. With $c(e)=e^3$: $c'=3e^2,\ c''=6e,\ c'''\equiv6,\ c^{(4)}\equiv0$. Then the stiffness and its slope are $$W_k(z)=6z+\sum_{l\le k}q_l\cdot 6\,r_l(z)\quad\Rightarrow\quad W_k'(z)=6-\sum_{l\le k}q_l\cdot6=6\big(1-Q_k(z)\big),$$ using $r_l'(z)=-1$ and the on-window count $Q_k=\sum_{l\le k}q_l$. So:

  • $W_k'(z)=6(1-Q_k)>0$ on-window (window lemma, §12) — the stiffness is strictly increasing.
  • $\Delta W=W_k(x_{k+1})-W_k(x_k)=6h_k(1-Q_k)>0$ — the endpoint stiffness jump is positive.
  • $D_k''(z)=-6\sum_{l\le k}q_l'\le0$ — from the concavity identity (C) with $c^{(4)}\equiv0$, the two motion-ordering terms vanish and only $-\sum q_l'\,c'''=-6\sum q_l'\le0$ survives. The margin is concave with no appeal to motion-ordering at all — this is what makes cubic clean where general cost is not.
  • $\theta_k-\tfrac12=-\dfrac{(1-Q_k)h_k^2}{2M_0}\le0$ — from the trapezoid identity (E1) with $W_k'=6(1-Q_k)$ constant in $z$: the bump integral is $\int(z-x_k)(x_{k+1}-z)\,dz=h_k^3/6$, giving the closed form. So $\theta_k\le\tfrac12$ exactly (verified $10^{-15}$).

13.1 The induction

Base case (node $C_1$). The first P1 atom: the mirror flux is empty (no P2 atom below except the boundary), so the two-term identity (I2) at $k=0$ reads $\mathrm{RH2}_1=\tilde M_0\,x_1'$ with $\tilde M_0>0$ and $x_1'>0$ from the boxed base derivative of §9.1. The centroid is $\tilde\theta_0=\tfrac13$ exactly (an atomless linear field $V_0$ vanishing at the frontier $f$), which is $\le\tfrac12$, so the base gap widens. Base verified to $10^{-16}$.

Inductive step. Assume through node $k$: all masses widened ($q_l',p_l'\ge0$, $l\le k$), all gaps widened ($\delta_l',\gamma_l'\ge0$, $l\le k$), and the motion-ordering $Y_{k+1}'\ge Y_l'\ge0$, $x_k'\ge x_l'\ge0$. Then:

  1. Left endpoint $\ge0$. $D_k(x_k)=W_k(x_k)\,\gamma_{k+1}'$ (§11.4). By the IH the previous up-gap widened, $\gamma_{k+1}'\ge0$ (this is $\gamma$ at index $k+1$, delivered by the previous node's (N2) — see step 6 of the prior node), and $W_k>0$. So $D_k(x_k)\ge0$.
  2. Right endpoint $\ge0$. Endpoint split (E2): $D_k(x_{k+1})=D_k(x_k)+Y_{k+1}'\,\Delta W+J_0$. Here $\Delta W=6h_k(1-Q_k)>0$ (on-window), $Y_{k+1}'\ge0$ (IH), and $J_0\ge0$ free ($c'''=6>0$, §11.7). So $D_k(x_{k+1})\ge D_k(x_k)\ge0$.
  3. Concavity. $D_k''=-6\sum_{l\le k}q_l'\le0$ (IH: $q_l'\ge0$). So $D_k$ is concave on $[x_k,x_{k+1}]$.
  4. $D_k\ge0$ on the whole interval. A concave function with non-negative endpoints is $\ge0$ throughout. Hence, pointwise, $D_k(z)\ge0$.
  5. Mass widens. $\mathrm{RG2}_k=\int_{x_k}^{x_{k+1}}D_k\ge0$ (identity (I2)). By §9.2, $q_{k+1}'=\mathrm{RG2}_k/(v_1-c(w_1-\delta_{k+1}))\ge0$. The new P2 mass widened.
  6. Gap widens. The shape condition (M$_k$): $\delta_{k+1}'\ge0\iff\bar D_k\ge\theta_k D_k(x_{k+1})$. Since $\theta_k\le\tfrac12$ (cubic centroid) and, by concavity, $\bar D_k\ge\tfrac12(D_k(x_k)+D_k(x_{k+1}))\ge\tfrac12 D_k(x_{k+1})\ge\theta_k D_k(x_{k+1})$, the condition holds. So $\delta_{k+1}'\ge0$; and the mirror node (N2) gives $\gamma_{k+2}'\ge0$. Both new gaps widened.
  7. Atom velocity and motion-ordering propagate. $x_{k+1}'=(\mathrm{RG1}_k+c'(r_{k+1})q_{k+1}')/DX_k>0$ (§9.2/§9.3, all inputs now $\ge0$), and $Y_{k+1}'>0$ by its mirror. The ordering $Y_{k+2}'\ge Y_{k+1}'$ follows from the fresh-injection sign in (N2). IH restored at node $k+1$.

The mirror (P2) steps are verbatim with $V_k'(Y)=6(1-P_k)>0$ on-window, closing $p_k'\ge0$ and $\gamma'\ge0$ in lockstep. $\text{Induction complete}\Longrightarrow\text{every }q_\ell'\ge0\Longrightarrow F'\ge1>0\Longrightarrow$ (§9.3) chain monotonicity, regularity and uniqueness for cubic cost. $\blacksquare$

What the cubic proof rests on, honestly. (i) The exact identities (I2), (N1)/(N2), (E1), (E2), (C) of §11 — machine-verified as EXACT symbolic identities with a generic cost $c$ (Wolfram residual $0$; the $10^{-14}$ numerics are now corroboration): pointwise skeleton in Julia/converge/_cubic_symbolic_cas.wl (2026-07-08), and since 2026-07-09 the integral glue itself — (I2)/(E1) via explicit antiderivatives checked by differentiation, the mirror pack, the base mirror, and the assembly algebra of this very induction (cascade, (M)-equivalence, exact trapezoid, endpoint-min, and the one-shot step-6 chain $D''\le0\,\wedge\,\text{endpoints}\ge0\,\wedge\,\theta\le\tfrac12\Rightarrow\bar D\ge\theta D(r)$ by quantifier elimination) — in Julia/converge/_cubic_symbolic_cas2.wl §G1–§G6b (worklist Docs/cubic_symbolic_gaps_worklist.md). Equilibrium enters only as FOC residuals + the indifference residual. (ii) The window lemma $Q_k<1$ — proved from the value of $F$, non-circular. (iii) Two inequalities from outside the identity algebra, both free at $c'''=6\ge0$: $J_0\ge0$ (right endpoint) and $D_k''\le0$ (concavity). No motion-ordering assumption is needed for concavity at cubic — that is the special gift of $c^{(4)}\equiv0$, and the exact place the general-cost proof still has work to do.

General cubic $ae^3+be^2+c_0e$ ($a>0$, $b,c_0\ge0$), base node. The base-case sentence above ("$\tilde\theta_0=\tfrac13$, an atomless linear field vanishing at the frontier") is exact for $e^3$; for the general class $\tilde\theta_0=c(\lambda)/(\lambda c'(\lambda))$ ($\lambda=Y_2-f$) can exceed $\tfrac12$ when $c'(0)=c_0>a\lambda^2$ — but the centroid route is not the operative base criterion: the base field form carries a boundary bonus $\mathrm{RH2}_1=\int\tilde D_0+c'(0)x_1'$ (interior nodes have none — the FOC kills it), and carrying it through (N2) gives the exact generic-$c$ criterion $V_+\gamma_2'=(x_1'/c(\lambda))\,[c'(\lambda)^2-c(\lambda)c''(\lambda)]$: base gap-widening $\iff$ cost log-concavity at $\lambda$. For the cubic class $c'^2-cc''=3a^2\lambda^4+4ab\lambda^3+2b^2\lambda^2+2bc_0\lambda+c_0^2>0$ termwise, so the extension is unconditional (_cubic_symbolic_cas2.wl §G6b; probe _cubic_c0_base_probe.jl).

13.2 The quadratic case as the degenerate margin field

The margin field also gives a self-contained re-proof of the quadratic lemma that §5–§6 left open — the "$\theta=2$ compression proof," one line per node. It is worth seeing because it exposes what the cubic machinery is generalizing.

At $c(e)=\tfrac12e^2$: $c''\equiv1$, $c'''\equiv0$. So the stiffness $W_k(z)\equiv 1+Q_k$ is constant in $z$ (flat), the centroid is exactly $\theta_k=\tfrac12$ (flat weight), and the concavity term $D_k''=0$ — the margin $D_k$ is affine. An affine function with non-negative endpoints is $\ge0$; the left endpoint is $W_k\gamma_{k+1}'\ge0$ (IH) and the right endpoint is $\ge$ left (since $\Delta W=0$ and $J_0\ge0$). So $D_k\ge0$, $\mathrm{RG2}_k\ge0$, and (M$_k$) holds with equality-margin because $\theta_k=\tfrac12$ and $\bar D_k=\tfrac12(D(x_k)+D(x_{k+1}))$. Mass and gap widen; induction closes. The cascade collapses to the harmonic law $1/\delta_{k+1}=1/\gamma_{k+1}+1/w_1$, $1/\gamma_{k+1}=1/\delta_k+1/w_2$ — exactly Part I. (Verified $10^{-14}$.)

So quadratic is the flat-field degenerate case ($W'=0$, $\theta=\tfrac12$, $D$ affine); cubic is the next case up ($W'>0$ constant sign on-window, $\theta<\tfrac12$, $D$ concave). The margin-field frame contains both, which is exactly why it is the right general-cost object.

14. The frontier: exactly what is open, regime by regime

The margin field reduces monotonicity, for any convex power cost, to controlling the single function $D_k$ on one interval via its endpoints and its curvature. Which of those controls is free, and which must be earned, flips with the exponent $\theta$. Here is the honest ledger of what remains.

$\theta>3$ (super-cubic) — one bounded-convexity lemma. The increasing-stiffness fact $W_k'\ge0$, which cubic got from $c'''=6$ and the window lemma, is free for all $\theta\ge3$ by a Hölder argument: the FOC gives $\sum_{l\le k}q_l\,r_l^{\theta-1}\le z^{\theta-1}$, and since $(\theta-3)/(\theta-1)\in(0,1)$, Hölder yields $\sum q_l r_l^{\theta-3}\le Q_k^{2/(\theta-1)}z^{\theta-3}<z^{\theta-3}$ on-window ($Q_k<1$). That gives the centroid bound $\theta_k\le\tfrac12$ (via E1) and $D_k(x_{k+1})\ge D_k(x_k)$ (via E2) for free. The only thing that breaks is concavity: $c^{(4)}>0$ now, so $D_k''$ picks up positive terms and the chord-deficit argument of step 4 fails. So $\theta>3$ = the cubic proof minus concavity, plus a bounded-convexity replacement for step 4. Budget: the Hölder slack $1-Q_k^{2/(\theta-1)}>0$. Numerics ($\theta=4$–$7$, $m\le16$) are clean and the shape margin (M$_k$)-slack grows in $m$ — the easier of the two non-cubic regimes.

$\theta\in(2,3)$ — the transfer problem (harder regime). Here $c^{(4)}<0$, so concavity (C) is free ($D_k''\le0$), but the increasing-stiffness/centroid side is not: one must transfer the FOC bound (pinning $\sum q_l\,c'(r_l)\le c'(z)$) up to the $c'''$ level, and the abstract transfer is false (as margins $\to0$ the $c'''$-sum can blow up). Two facts sharpen the target:

  • Centroid crossing (measured). The clean bound $\theta_k\le\tfrac12$ is false for $\theta\in(2,3)$: the last node's centroid crosses $\tfrac12$ on-window at $m=20,\ t=t^\ast$ (excess $+2.7\times10^{-6}$ at $\theta=2.1$). It is a pure last-node-at-$t^\ast$ corner effect (at $0.7t^\ast$ everything is clean, slack $+0.95$). So the naive centroid lemma cannot be the statement.
  • The true remaining lemma is buffered, (M-buf). The honest sufficient condition is $\theta_k-\tfrac12\le D_k(x_k)/2D_k(x_{k+1})$ — a buffered shape condition. Via (E1) and $M_0=h_k\bar W_k$ it reduces to $\theta_k-\tfrac12\le h_k\cdot\max(-W_k')_+/(12\bar W_k)$: one free power of $h_k$. At the crossing node $h_k\approx0.024$ and shrinking, against a flat buffer $\approx0.1$ — five orders of magnitude of room. Plus a right-endpoint positivity (TR-a) $D_k(x_{k+1})\ge0$, empirically $\ge2.4$. These two — (M-buf) via the $h^2$-budget, and (TR-a) — are the entire open content for $\theta\in(2,3)$.

$\theta\in(1,2)$ (sub-quadratic) — regime swap. Duality with the above: $c'''<0$, so the free/hard lemmas trade places. The shape/centroid condition (M) becomes free, but the right-endpoint positivity and the $J_k\ge0$ dominance become the hard part ($J_k$'s sign is no longer free; numerically the $q'c''$ piece still dominates the $qc'''Y'$ piece, but this must be proved). Not yet pursued in depth.
