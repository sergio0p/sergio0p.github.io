Where the dynamic contest sits in the HwangтАУRey-Bellet classification

Round-1 reduced game $\Gamma(\Delta,v_1,v_2)$ of the two-round all-pay contest, decomposed ├а la Hwang & Rey-Bellet (2020). Fully asymmetric: $v_1\neq v_2$, $\Delta\neq 0$. Kink-aligned discretization (the Julia/solver/ce_lp.jl convention): cases are indexed by rational reaches $(w_1,w_2,\Delta)$, $e_i\in[0,w_i]$, sharing one step $h=\gcd(w_1,w_2,\Delta)/m$; with $w_1=1$ and $w_2,\Delta\in\{k/9\}$ the gcd forces $h=\tfrac19$, so player 1 (favorite) gets exactly $10$ efforts (zero included) and player 2 the proportional count $w_2/h+1$ at the same step. Then every kink diagonal $e_1-e_2\in\{w_2-w_1-\Delta,\,w_2-\Delta,\,-w_1-\Delta\}$ and every cost argument is an exact lattice point, so branch selection is integer arithmetic тАФ no kink-straddling. Values are induced: $v_i=c(w_i)$ (so $v_2$ differs between quadratic and cubic at fixed reach). Three reach/lead variations, quadratic $c(e)=e^2$ and cubic $c(e)=e^3$, six cases; an even per-player grid ($10$ points each, step $w_i/9$) is run alongside for comparison. Source: Julia/solver/hrb_decomp.jl; theory notes Study/HwangReyBellet2020_decomposition_notes.md.

1. The game we are classifying

This is the contest in the last but one round: round 1 of the two-round game, with round 2 already folded in through its continuation value. A lead $\Delta = \text{score}_1-\text{score}_2$ stands after round 0; each player picks an effort $e_i\ge 0$ at cost $c(e_i)$; the round-2 lead is $\Delta_2 = \Delta + e_1 - e_2$. Payoffs use Lemma 1's continuation $U^T_i$ (asymmetric form; the rival's reach sits inside each interior cost):

$$u_1(e_1,e_2) = U^T_1(\Delta_2) - c(e_1), \qquad u_2(e_1,e_2) = U^T_2(\Delta_2) - c(e_2),$$ $$U^T_1(\Delta_2)=\begin{cases}0 & \Delta_2\le w_2-w_1\\ v_1-c(w_2-\Delta_2) & w_2-w_1<\Delta_2with reaches $w_i = c^{-1}(v_i)$. The point of interest: $U^T_1+U^T_2$ is not constant in $\Delta_2$. It equals $v_2$ for $\Delta_2\le -w_1$, falls to $0$ at the pivot $\Delta_2 = w_2-w_1$ (full dissipation: both continuations vanish), and rises to $v_1$ for $\Delta_2\ge w_2$. A constant-sum (zero-sum) game would need this sum flat. It is not тАФ and that single fact is what the decomposition below turns into a number.

2. HwangтАУRey-Bellet, the one result we use

HRB (Theorem 2.1) give a unique orthogonal decomposition of any finite or continuous normal-form game, under the inner product $\langle f,g\rangle = \sum_i \int f^{(i)}g^{(i)}\,dm$. Every game splits into four orthogonal pieces:

$$f \;=\; \underbrace{f_{\mathcal E}}_{\text{nonstrategic}} \;+\; \underbrace{f_{\mathcal B}}_{\substack{\text{own-strategy-only}\\\text{(dominant)}}} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathbf{SG}f}_{\mathcal I\cap\mathcal N\ \text{(identical interest)}} \;+\; \underbrace{(I-(\mathbf V+\mathbf P))f}_{\mathcal Z\cap\mathcal N\ \text{(zero-sum)}}$$

$\mathcal N$ is the normalized subspace (each player's payoff averages to zero over their own strategy); $\mathcal I$ identical-interest (all players share one payoff); $\mathcal Z$ zero-sum; $\mathcal E$ nonstrategic (a player's payoff ignores their own move тАФ strategically inert, dropped under strategic equivalence). The classification test (HRB D1):

$f$ is strategically zero-sum $\iff$ its identical-interest component $\mathbf{SG}f \equiv 0$.

A strategically zero-sum game has all Nash equilibria payoff-equivalent and (MoulinтАУVial 1978; Pavlov 2025) a unique correlated equilibrium. So $\mathbf{SG}f$ is exactly the obstruction to that collapse: $\lVert\mathbf{SG}f\rVert$ measures how far $\Gamma$ stands from the tractable class.

3. The decomposition made explicit for two players

With $A=u_1$, $B=u_2$ the $n\times n$ payoff matrices (rows = P1's efforts, columns = P2's efforts), the four projections are elementary means. Centering an index means subtracting the average over it.

Normalize (subtract each player's own-strategy mean тАФ kills $\mathcal E$): $\hat A_{ij}=A_{ij}-\tfrac1n\sum_i A_{ij}$ (columns of $\hat A$ sum to $0$) and $\hat B_{ij}=B_{ij}-\tfrac1n\sum_j B_{ij}$ (rows of $\hat B$ sum to $0$).

Peel the own-effort / dominant part $\mathcal B$: $g_i=\tfrac1n\sum_j\hat A_{ij}$ (depends on $i$ only), $h_j=\tfrac1n\sum_i\hat B_{ij}$ (depends on $j$ only). The cost $c(e_i)$ lands here in full тАФ it depends on own effort alone, so it never touches $\mathcal I$ or $\mathcal Z$.

What remains is doubly-centered, $\tilde A = \hat A-g$ and $\tilde B=\hat B-h$ (rows and columns sum to zero), and splits cleanly:

$$\mathbf{SG}f = (G,G),\ \ G=\tfrac12(\tilde A+\tilde B)\ \ (\mathcal I\cap\mathcal N); \qquad (H,-H),\ \ H=\tfrac12(\tilde A-\tilde B)\ \ (\mathcal Z\cap\mathcal N).$$

Orthogonality is immediate ($\langle(G,G),(H,-H)\rangle=0$) and was checked numerically: $\lVert\text{strategic}\rVert^2-(\lVert\mathcal B\rVert^2+\lVert\mathcal I\rVert^2+\lVert\mathcal Z\rVert^2)$ sits at $10^{-15}$ in every case. The zero-sum test reads off directly: $G=0 \iff \tilde A=-\tilde B$. We report $\lVert\mathcal I\cap\mathcal N\rVert=\sqrt2\,\lVert G\rVert$, $\lVert\mathcal Z\cap\mathcal N\rVert=\sqrt2\,\lVert H\rVert$, and the coupling share $\rho_{\mathcal I}=\lVert\mathcal I\rVert^2/(\lVert\mathcal I\rVert^2+\lVert\mathcal Z\rVert^2)$ тАФ the fraction of the genuinely-coupled interaction (the part where one player's move changes the other's incentives) that is common-interest rather than conflict.

5. Reading the numbers

The three regimes the table and the sweep below trace out:

(a) The exact $\rho_{\mathcal I}=\tfrac12$ in case V3 (both costs) is structural, not coincidence, and the aligned grid makes the threshold crisp. There $\Delta\ge 2w_2-w_1$ (for V3, $\tfrac29\ge 2\cdot\tfrac59-1=\tfrac19$), so the smallest $\Delta_2$ on the grid, $\Delta-w_2$, already meets or exceeds the pivot $w_2-w_1$: player 2 is shut out of round 2 everywhere ($U^T_2\equiv 0$), leaving $u_2=-c(e_2)$, pure own-effort. Then $\tilde B=0$, so $G=H=\tilde A/2$, giving $\lVert\mathcal I\rVert=\lVert\mathcal Z\rVert$ to every digit and the coupled interaction splitting exactly half common-interest, half zero-sum. The underdog's continuation has gone flat; all coupling now flows through $U^T_1$ alone. (This held to floating-point on the even grid too тАФ it does not depend on alignment, only on the underdog being shut out.)

(b) When the underdog is still in contention (V1, V2: pivot reachable on the grid), both continuations vary, $\tilde B\neq 0$, and $\rho_{\mathcal I}$ pulls away from $\tfrac12$ тАФ toward conflict ($\rho_{\mathcal I}<\tfrac12$) here, since the two players' normalized payoffs disagree more than they agree once both are active. Cubic cost amplifies this (sharper marginal cost near the kinks $\Rightarrow$ larger $\mathcal Z$), pushing $\rho_{\mathcal I}$ down to $0.40$.

(c) The common-interest obstruction dies as the lead trivializes round 2. Sweeping $\Delta=d/9$ on the aligned grid (quadratic, $w_1=1,\,w_2=\tfrac79$):

The interesting region is the live one, where $\lVert\mathcal I\cap\mathcal N\rVert$ is bounded away from zero.

6. How $\Gamma$ fits the classification, and why it matters

The classification verdict is uniform across costs, values, and leads, as long as round 2 is live: $\Gamma(\Delta,v_1,v_2)$ is an HRB-general game with a nonzero identical-interest component. It is neither a (strategically) zero-sum game nor a potential game. The nonzero $\mathcal I\cap\mathcal N$ is the direct fingerprint of the non-constant continuation sum $U^T_1+U^T_2$ тАФ the dynamic linkage that makes the round-1 game more than a relabeled static auction.

This is exactly the property the project needs. A strategically zero-sum game has all NE payoff-equivalent and a unique CE (MoulinтАУVial; Pavlov 2025; the static all-pay auction is in this class). The chain $u_1^\ast(1,1)>u_1^\ast(1,2)>u_1^\ast(2,2)>\cdots$ of payoff-distinct equilibria can only exist because $\Gamma$ escapes that class. The decomposition makes "escapes" quantitative and certifies it: the escape is witnessed by $\lVert\mathbf{SG}\,\Gamma\rVert>0$, and it shuts off precisely when the lead kills round 2 тАФ collapsing the dynamic contest back onto the static all-pay auction HillmanтАУRiley / BayeтАУKovenock / Pavlov already settled.

Caveats. (i) The grid is now kink-aligned, so branch selection is exact and the structural facts ($\rho_{\mathcal I}=\tfrac12$ when the underdog is shut out; $\lVert\mathcal I\rVert=\lVert\mathcal Z\rVert$ there; vanishing at $\Delta=2w_2$) are exact rather than approximate. It is still coarse: P1 carries $10$ efforts but P2 only $w_2/h+1\in\{6,8,9\}$ at the shared step, so absolute norms understate a continuum value and scale with cell count (the $5$тАУ$18\%$ even-vs-aligned gap is exactly this). The qualitative verdict ($\lVert\mathcal I\rVert>0$, sign of $\rho_{\mathcal I}-\tfrac12$) is grid-robust; refining $m$ (a finer shared step) would firm up the magnitudes. (ii) Norms are reported in the uniform counting measure on the effort grid; a different reference rescales the magnitudes but not the zero/non-zero verdict. (iii) This classifies the round-1 reduced game; the reduction to $U^T$ is payoff-safe because round-2 continuations are unique (see TODO.md CE caveat (i)). (iv) A bound relating the NE/CE payoff spread to $\lVert\mathbf{SG}\,\Gamma\rVert$ would be new work тАФ it is not in HRB (their Prop 4.1 is finite-symmetric only) тАФ and pairs naturally with the discretized CE LP probe in TODO.md.
