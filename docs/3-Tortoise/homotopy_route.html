The homotopy route to chain monotonicity

A step-by-step walkthrough of the continuation strategy for proving that player 1's payoff $u_1^*$ decreases along the rung chain at general convex costs. Prerequisites, if you want them: Docs/chain_monotonicity_reduction.html (Parts I–II) and Docs/comb_march_tutorial.html — but every concept used here is defined when it first appears.

Summary: Chain monotonicity — the claim that the equilibrium payoff $u_1^*$ strictly decreases along the ladder $\sigma(1,1)\to\sigma(1,2)\to\sigma(2,2)\to\cdots$ — is proved for quadratic cost but open for general convex costs. The incumbent proof program (the “cascade”) pushes node-by-node inequalities through the equilibrium construction, and at one known corner of cost space (quartics with a sign-changing third derivative) every one of its intermediate inequalities is false while the theorem itself keeps holding numerically. The homotopy route sidesteps the cascade entirely: it reformulates monotonicity as an ordering of scalar dials, proves that ordering follows from a single-crossing property of a scalar residual function $F$, and transports single-crossing from the proved quadratic case to any target convex cost by a continuous deformation $c_s=(1-s)e^2+s\,c$. A numerical stress test at the four nastiest known cost corners collapsed the route's hypothesis list to one analytic obligation: $F'>0$ at every zero of $F$, along the whole deformation path — an aggregate inequality needed only at roots, and the one quantity never observed to fail, anywhere. The stress test also falsified one original hypothesis (rungs can die along the path) and showed the failure is harmless: the ordering argument never needed the dying rungs. Off the symmetric slice one further obligation appears — the admissible dial window has a moving lower edge, and a root can slide out through it (§5.1).

1 · The theorem we want

The setting is the paper's two-round all-pay contest: two players exert costly effort in each of two rounds, the second round is a static contest with a lead, and round one is played anticipating the round-two continuation values. For every pair $(m,n)$ with $n\in\{m,m+1\}$, the paper constructs a mixed equilibrium $\sigma(m,n)$ in which player 1 randomizes over $m$ atoms and player 2 over $n$ atoms. The two indices carry fixed roles throughout — player 1 earns the rent, player 2 is held at zero payoff — and §3.0 makes those roles precise.

Definition — rung, chain
A rung is one member $\sigma(m,n)$ of the equilibrium family; a square rung has $n=m$, a rectangle rung has $n=m+1$. The chain is the ladder of rungs ordered by total support size, $$\sigma(1,1)\ \to\ \sigma(1,2)\ \to\ \sigma(2,2)\ \to\ \sigma(2,3)\ \to\ \sigma(3,3)\ \to\ \cdots$$ so squares and rectangles alternate, each step adding one atom to one player.
The claim (chain monotonicity). Along the chain, player 1's equilibrium payoff $u_1^*$ strictly decreases: $$u_1^*(1,1) \;>\; u_1^*(1,2) \;>\; u_1^*(2,2) \;>\; u_1^*(2,3) \;>\; \cdots$$ Economically: richer mixing dissipates more rent, marching the family toward the competitive (full-dissipation) pole where $u_1^*\to 0$.

Where the incumbent program stands, cost regime by cost regime:

cost regimestatus

Scope — read this before quoting any number below. Every measurement reported here was taken at symmetric primitives: $v_1=v_2=1$, no head start ($\Delta=0$). The target is the full primitive box $(v_1\gtrless v_2)\times(\Delta\lessgtr0)$ — four quadrants, run in both labelings (§3.0).

The symmetric slice is not a neutral sample. It is exactly the slice on which the quantity $f=w_2-w_1-\Delta$ vanishes (§3.0), hence the dial floor $t_0$ of §3.3 is identically zero, hence the admissible dial window is all of $(0,1)$ and the fourth boundary event of §5.1 cannot occur. So: the numbers are safe, and they are silent about the half of the box where the interesting failures live. Extending them is §13, item 4.

2 · Why the incumbent program is hard

The incumbent program — the cascade, Part II of chain_monotonicity_reduction.html — works at a fixed target cost and derives monotonicity from signed inequalities imposed at every node of the equilibrium construction.

Definition — termwise vs. aggregate; the cascade
The equilibrium assigns player 2's masses $q_1, q_2, \dots, q_n$ to her atoms, each a function of the construction parameter (the “dial,” defined in §3). Call a condition termwise (or pointwise) if it must hold at every node separately — e.g. “each mass derivative $q_{k+1}'\ge 0$” — and aggregate if it constrains only a sum — e.g. “$\sum_\ell q_\ell'>0$.” The cascade is the program that proves the termwise family: at each node $k$ an inequality $RG2_k\ge 0$ (“mass-widening at node $k$” — turning the dial up widens the mass at that node), realized through shape conditions on an auxiliary margin field $D_k(z)$.

Two structural facts make the cascade expensive:

  1. The circularity theorem (Part II §10). The construction's internal identity algebra can never sign $RG2$ by itself; a genuine convexity inequality must be imported from outside — and it has had to be re-imported, in a different form, for every regime ($\theta=2$, $\theta=3$, $\theta>3$, $\theta\in(2,3)$ each needed different lemmas).
  2. The $b<0$ quartic corner (Docs/quartic_monotonicity_findings.md). For quartic costs $c(e)=e^4+Be^3+D_2e^2$ with $B<0$, the third derivative $c'''$ changes sign on the effort domain. At this corner every pointwise anchor the cascade leans on is false — gap-widening fails, concavity of the margin field fails, centroid bounds fail — and yet chain monotonicity itself never fails numerically.
The moral. The cascade's intermediate conditions are strictly stronger than the theorem, and at the $b<0$ corner the gap between them is real: the strong conditions die, the theorem survives. That gap is the opening for a different route — find a formulation whose intermediate conditions are only as strong as the theorem itself.

3 · The dial picture: one axis for all rungs

Everything in this section is established (Part I of the reduction document) and holds for arbitrary convex costs. The homotopy route uses only these facts, so it is worth rebuilding them slowly. The one subtlety to watch for arrives in §3.3: the dial has an admissible range, and it is narrower than it looks.

3.0  Who the players are, and what the primitive box looks like

Definition — rent-earner, marginal player
The march imposes two roles by construction:
  • Player 2 is the marginal player: $u_2=0$. The march's Step B pins her indifference level to zero.
  • Player 1 is the rent-earner. Step C pins her indifference level to $u_1$ — a number the base equations compute, not one we choose.
These are roles, not physical players. They are also the reason the rung family is $n\in\{m,m+1\}$ and never $\sigma(m+1,m)$: the zero-payoff player is the one who can carry the extra atom. The cell in which the other physical player earns the rent is reached by the paper's relabeling symmetry $(v_1,v_2,\Delta)\mapsto(v_2,v_1,-\Delta)$, so the whole box is covered by this single family run in both labelings.

Two quantities organize the box. Write $w_i=c^{-1}(v_i)$ for player $i$'s reach (the effort at which winning is worth exactly its cost), and define

$$f \;=\; w_2 - w_1 - \Delta, \qquad\qquad IR_1 \;=\; w_2 - 2\,c^{-1}(v_1/2).$$
Definition — $f$, the favorite condition; $IR_1$; the competitive range
$f$ is where player 2's zero-effort atom sits ($Y_1=f$). Its sign is the final-round favorite condition: $$f\le 0 \iff \Delta \ge w_2-w_1 \iff \textbf{player 1 is Siegel's final-round favorite.}$$ Symmetric primitives sit exactly on the edge, $f=0$ — which is why everything that turns on the sign of $f$ stays invisible to the measurements of §§6–8.

$IR_1$ — individual rationality for player 1, the paper's $IR_A$ — is the existence threshold: the rung family is nonempty iff $\Delta\ge IR_1$. Convexity gives $2c^{-1}(v_1/2)\ge w_1$, hence $IR_1\le w_2-w_1$ always — so there is a band $IR_1\le\Delta<w_2-w_1$ where player 1 is not the favorite and still earns a rent. That band is the collusive phenomenon proper, and it is the region the paper's tortoise–hare equilibrium picks out: the words name a part of the box, never a player index.

The competitive range is $-w_1<\Delta<w_2$. Outside it one player has a decisive lead and the unique equilibrium is both players at zero effort; the march has nothing to say there, and the engine rejects such primitives up front rather than reporting a spurious convergence failure.

3.1  The march is rung-independent

Definition — comb march, dial
The comb march is the forward-shooting algorithm that constructs candidate equilibria (full tutorial: Docs/comb_march_tutorial.html). It treats one number as free — the dial $t = q_1 \in (0,1)$, player 2's probability mass on her zero atom — and then generates everything else by forward substitution: alternating $2\times 2$ steps (one first-order condition, one indifference condition) produce the sequence $$x_1(t),\; Y_2(t),\; p_1(t),\; q_2(t),\; x_2(t),\; Y_3(t),\; p_2(t),\; q_3(t),\;\dots$$ of atom locations and masses, each a well-defined function of the dial alone. Crucially, this sequence makes no reference to any rung: it depends only on the primitives $(v_1,v_2,\Delta,c)$ and on $t$.

A rung, in this picture, is nothing but a stopping-and-closure rule imposed on the shared sequence — stop after so many atoms, and require the appropriate masses to sum to one:

Definition — rung residual $F_R$
The residual of rung $R$ is the closure deficit as a function of the dial: $$F_{(m,m)}(t) \;\equiv\; \sum_{\ell \le m} q_\ell(t) \;-\; 1 \qquad\text{(squares: player 2's masses must total 1),}$$ $$F_{(m,m+1)}(t) \;\equiv\; \sum_{k \le m} p_k(t) \;-\; 1 \qquad\text{(rectangles: player 1's masses must total 1).}$$ The equilibrium dial $t^*_R$ is the first up-crossing of $F_R$ — the smallest $t$ where the deficit reaches zero from below — provided two side conditions hold:
  1. interiority: all masses in the profile are strictly positive;
  2. the root clears the dial floor: $t^*_R > t_0$, where $t_0$ is defined in §3.3.
Both side conditions are real, and both are invisible to the march itself, which solves first-order and indifference conditions and therefore cannot see a violated inequality. A root of $F$ carrying a negative mass is a genuine root of the equation but not an equilibrium; a root below the floor is likewise a genuine root, but one at which player 1 strictly prefers to abandon the contest. Both traps have caught working code, which is why they are flagged again in §12.

3.2  The payoff is also a dial-level object

Here is the reduction's first small miracle. Player 1's payoff $u_1$ is pinned down at the very base of the march, before any rung is chosen. The base pair $(x_1,u_1)$ solves the bottom-of-comb first-order and indifference conditions against player 2's zero atom. Writing $m_0$ for player 1's win-margin against that atom — the second-round effort she must still pay to see player 2 off, having arrived with lead $\Delta+x_1$ against a rival whose reach is $w_2$ —

$$m_0 \;=\; w_2 - (\Delta + x_1) \;=\; w_1 + f - x_1,$$

so a bigger bottom atom is a bigger lead, and a bigger lead leaves less to pay: $m_0$ falls as $x_1$ rises. The base pair and its derivatives are then

$$t\,c'(m_0) = c'(x_1), \qquad u_1(t) = t\,\big[v_1 - c(m_0)\big] - c(x_1),$$ $$x_1'(t) \;=\; \frac{c'(m_0)}{\,c''(x_1) + t\,c''(m_0)\,} \;>\; 0, \qquad\qquad u_1'(t) \;=\; v_1 - c(m_0) \quad(\text{envelope: the first-order condition kills the indirect terms}).$$

Everything downstream leans on the sign of that last derivative: $u_1$ must be increasing in the dial. Since $c$ is increasing and $c(w_1)=v_1$, that sign unwinds to something concrete:

$$u_1'(t)>0 \iff c(m_0) < c(w_1) \iff m_0 < w_1 \iff x_1(t) \;>\; f.$$

So it is not an algebraic triviality but an economic condition, and one we can read off the comb: player 1's base atom earns a margin only once it clears player 2's zero atom, which sits at $f$ (§3.0). When $f\le0$ that is automatic, since $x_1>0$ on the whole dial. When $f>0$ it is not — and because $x_1(t)$ rises from $x_1(0)=0$, the condition $x_1>f$ is nothing other than a floor on the dial. That floor is §3.3, and it is the reason the admissible dial set is not all of $(0,1)$.

The sign itself is free anyway. It just comes from economics rather than from algebra:

Standing hypothesis: $u_1 > 0$. Then $$u_1 > 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; t\,\big[v_1-c(m_0)\big] > c(x_1) > 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; v_1 - c(m_0) > 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; u_1'(t) > 0.$$ ($c(x_1)>0$ because the first-order condition $c'(x_1)=t\,c'(m_0)>0$ forces $x_1>0$.) Individual rationality — part of what “equilibrium” means — delivers the load-bearing sign. Nothing is assumed that the theorem does not already require. The price is only that the admissible dial set is now $\{t: u_1(t)>0\}$.
The master equivalence (valid on the whole primitive box, restricted to the admissible dial set). Since $u_1'(t)>0$: $$\textbf{chain monotonicity} \iff \textbf{the equilibrium dials decrease along the chain,}\quad t^*_{(1,1)} > t^*_{(1,2)} > t^*_{(2,2)} > \cdots$$ This is the single most important simplification in the whole program: we never have to compare payoffs across two different equilibrium structures (different support sizes, different mass patterns); we only have to order scalars $t^*$ on one shared axis.

3.3  The dial floor $t_0$ — the admissible window is $(t_0,1)$, not $(0,1)$

Where is the admissible set? Since $x_1'>0$, the margin $m_0$ is strictly decreasing in the dial, so $u_1' = v_1-c(m_0)$ is strictly increasing: $u_1$ is strictly convex in the dial, starts at $u_1(0)=0$, and leaves the origin with slope

$$u_1'(0) \;=\; v_1 - c(w_2-\Delta),$$

whose sign is precisely the favorite condition of §3.0. Two regimes follow (all of this verified numerically over 11 cells × three cost families, Julia/converge/_asym_base_audit.jl):

Definition — dial floor ($t_0$ = the highest $t$ with $u_1(t)=0$)
  • $f\le0$  (player 1 is the final-round favorite): $u_1>0$ and $u_1'>0$ on the entire dial. The floor is $t_0=0$, the admissible window is all of $(0,1)$, and Part I's statements hold verbatim. Symmetric primitives are this case — which is why no floor ever surfaces in the measurements of §§6–8.
  • $f>0$: $u_1$ dips negative near $t=0$, bottoms out at the dial where $x_1=f$ (the trough, by §3.2), and by convexity climbs back to zero at a unique dial floor $t_0>0$, strictly beyond the trough. Only $t\in(t_0,1)$ is admissible — and there, $u_1'>0$.
  • The family is nonempty at all iff $u_1(1)\ge0$ iff $\Delta\ge IR_1$ — an exact match with the paper's $IR_A$ in every cell tested.
The floor is not a technicality — it truncates chains. At $c=e^2$, $v_1=v_2=1$, $\Delta=-0.3$ (symmetric values, mild negative lead — well inside the competitive range) the floor sits at $t_0=0.6905$, and the roots of $\sigma(2,2)$, $\sigma(2,3)$, $\sigma(3,3)$ all lie below it. Those rungs have roots. They have interior combs. They are not equilibria: at their dial, player 1's best response is to walk away and take zero. In that cell the chain is truncated to $\sigma(1,1)$.

Why a first-order construction cannot see this: on $[0,x_1]$ player 1's payoff is $q_1\max\big(0,\,v_1-c(w_1+f-e)\big)-c(e)$, which for $e\in(0,f)$ wins nothing and simply falls to $-c(f)$, climbing back only above $e=f$ to reach $u_1$ at $x_1$. Her best response on that interval is $\max(0,u_1)$, attained at the corner $e=0$ whenever $u_1<0$. A march that solves first-order and indifference conditions is structurally blind to a corner deviation, so the floor has to enter the engine as an explicit gate: solve_rung reports the floor t0 alongside the root, and refuses a root that falls below it.

4 · The separator: birth mass + single-crossing

How do we order two dials? The reduction document's boxed identity (Part I §2) supplies a purpose-built tool — but the tool looks like a lucky coincidence until one sees where residuals come from, so we build it from the bookkeeping first.

4.1  Where a residual comes from: the leftover ladder

The march supplies every atom and every mass — with exactly one exception. The topmost atom of the comb has no rival atom above it, so no rival indifference condition ever references its mass, and the march never writes an equation for it. That mass is a leftover, pinned instead by its own player's “probabilities sum to one.” Spending one normalization that way leaves the other player's normalization unused — and that unused equation is the residual. Writing the two march partial sums (rung-free, like everything the march produces)

$$P_m(t) \;=\; \sum_{k\le m} p_k(t), \qquad\qquad Q_m(t) \;=\; \sum_{\ell\le m} q_\ell(t),$$

the bookkeeping is then forced:

rungtop atomleftover mass (pinned by $\textstyle\sum=1$)residual (the unused normalization)
$\sigma(m,m)$$x_m$  (player 1)$p_m = 1 - P_{m-1}(t)$$F_{(m,m)}(t) = Q_m(t)-1$
$\sigma(m,m{+}1)$$y_{m+1}$  (player 2)$q_{m+1} = 1 - Q_m(t)$$F_{(m,m+1)}(t) = P_m(t)-1$

So there are only two residual families, one per player, and the ladder alternates between them — because the ladder alternates which player grows an atom. That is the whole trick. Climb one rung: the new atom goes on top, and it belongs to the other player — the very player whose normalization the rung below was shooting to zero. Hence

The newborn atom is the new leftover atom. Its mass is therefore $1$ minus that player's march partial sum — which is exactly minus the residual of the rung below.

4.2  The birth-mass identity and the separator

Definition — birth mass; the birth-mass identity
Let $R\to\tilde R$ be consecutive rungs, so $\tilde R$ has exactly one atom more than $R$. The birth mass is the mass the deeper rung's profile assigns to its newborn atom, viewed as a function of the dial. By §4.1 that mass is the leftover, and reading the table off: $$\underbrace{q_{m+1} = 1-Q_m(t)}_{\sigma(m,m)\,\to\,\sigma(m,m+1)} \;=\; -\,F_{(m,m)}(t), \qquad \underbrace{p_{m+1} = 1-P_m(t)}_{\sigma(m,m+1)\,\to\,\sigma(m+1,m+1)} \;=\; -\,F_{(m,m+1)}(t),$$ which is one statement, the birth-mass identity: $$\text{newborn mass in } \tilde R \text{'s profile at dial } t \;=\; -\,F_R(t).$$ It is not a lemma but a piece of bookkeeping: both rungs read the same march, and whatever mass $R$'s closure failed to account for at dial $t$ is precisely what must sit on the one atom $\tilde R$ adds.

The name now explains itself. At $R$'s own dial the residual vanishes, so the newborn atom is born with exactly zero mass; it acquires mass only as the dial falls below $t^*_R$. The separator is the observation that it must acquire some.

One thing to keep straight. The march also generates a mass $q_{m+1}(t)$ from its own equation, and that number is not the leftover $1-Q_m(t)$ — at a generic dial the two differ (in the tutorial's worked trace the marched $p_2=0.6075$ against a reported $p_2=0.3946$). The march value is simply discarded on any rung whose top atom it would sit on. The two coincide only where the deeper rung closes.

Now evaluate at the deeper rung's own equilibrium dial $t^*_{\tilde R}$. There, the newborn mass is a mass in a genuine equilibrium profile, hence strictly positive — that is just interiority, part of what “$\tilde R$'s equilibrium exists” means. So, for free:

$$F_R\big(t^*_{\tilde R}\big) \;=\; -\,\big(\text{newborn mass}\big) \;<\; 0. \tag{4.1}$$
Definition — single-crossing
A function $F$ on the dial interval is single-crossing if it crosses zero exactly once, from below. Equivalently: $F<0$ strictly to the left of its unique root and $F>0$ strictly to the right. For a differentiable $F$, a sufficient certificate is transversality at roots: $F'>0$ at every zero of $F$ (a function that only ever crosses upward can cross only once), plus a definite sign at the accessible boundary.

Suppose $F_R$ is single-crossing with root $t^*_R$. Then the set where $F_R$ is negative is exactly $(0,\,t^*_R)$, and (4.1) says $t^*_{\tilde R}$ lies in that set. In one line:

The separator argument. $$\textbf{dial ordering } \big(t^*_{\tilde R} < t^*_R\big) \impliedby \underbrace{\textbf{successor interiority}}_{\text{definitional — free}} \;+\; \underbrace{\textbf{single-crossing of } F_R}_{\text{the only real obligation}}$$ Successor interiority costs nothing. All the weight of the theorem lands on single-crossing.
Note what the separator does not use: individual rationality. The birth-mass identity and inequality (4.1) need only that the successor's comb has strictly positive masses. They say nothing whatever about $u_1$, and they hold at a root whether or not that root clears the dial floor of §3.3. So the ordering of roots is available for every rung with an interior comb, and the floor of §3.3 sits on top of it as a pure filter. Hold on to that separation: it is what makes the moving floor of §5.1 absorbable rather than fatal.
Figure 1. The separator argument in one picture. The residual $F_R$ (blue) is single-crossing with root $t^*_R$. The successor's dial $t^*_{\tilde R}$ must sit where $F_R$ equals minus the newborn mass — strictly below zero (pink) — hence strictly to the left of $t^*_R$. The two quantities the probe tracks are the horizontal gap (separation) and the vertical gap (birth mass); to first order, birth $\approx F' \times{}$sep.

Single-crossing of $F$ is proved for the quadratic cost: Part I §4 identifies $F'$ at a root with the Schur complement of the equilibrium Jacobian, and Theorems 1 and 3 show it is positive there — regularity and uniqueness in one stroke.

Definition — Schur complement
For a block matrix $\begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D\end{pmatrix}$ with $A$ invertible, the Schur complement of $A$ is $D - C A^{-1} B$: the effective coefficient that remains on the $D$-block after the $A$-block variables are eliminated. Here the full equilibrium Jacobian has a large “comb block” (the interior atoms and masses) and one scalar closure equation; eliminating the comb block leaves a $1\times 1$ Schur complement, and Part I §4 shows this scalar is $F'(t^*)$. Its positivity therefore encodes, simultaneously, local uniqueness of the equilibrium and upward-crossing of the residual. The identification is march-structural — cost-general — but the quadratic proof of positivity uses quadratic algebra. Transporting that positivity is exactly the homotopy's job.

5 · The homotopy: transporting single-crossing

Definition — homotopy, continuation
A homotopy is a continuous one-parameter deformation connecting a problem you can solve to the problem you want to solve. Continuation is the accompanying argument (or numerical procedure) that tracks the solution as the parameter moves, certifying that nothing qualitative changes along the way. Here the deformation acts on the cost function: $$c_s(e) \;=\; (1-s)\,e^2 \;+\; s\,c(e), \qquad s\in[0,1],$$ sliding from the proved quadratic base ($s=0$) to the target convex cost $c$ ($s=1$). Every $c_s$ is a convex combination of convex functions, hence convex — the path never leaves the admissible family. Each rung's residual becomes a function of two variables, $F(t;s)$, jointly continuous wherever the march is defined.

The continuation logic is the standard one-dimensional index argument, and it is worth spelling out. At $s=0$ the residual has exactly one zero, and $F'>0$ there (§4). As $s$ increases, the number of zeros of $t\mapsto F(t;s)$ can change only through:

  1. A tangency: some $(\tau,s_0)$ with $F=0$ and $\partial F/\partial t=0$ simultaneously. Geometrically, the curve of roots in the $(t,s)$-strip folds back on itself — a pair of roots is born or dies in the interior.
  2. A boundary event: a root enters or exits through an edge of the dial domain. There are four edges, not three:
    1. $t\to 0^+$;
    2. $t\to 1^-$;
    3. the edge of a march-failure hole;
    4. the dial floor $t_0(s)$ — the lower edge of the admissible window $(t_0,1)$ of §3.3, which moves along the homotopy, because $c_s$ changes the reaches $w_1,w_2$ and hence $f$ and $IR_1$. This is the subtle one, and it gets §5.1 to itself.
Definition — march-failure hole
For some dial values the march simply cannot be run to the required depth: one of its node equations loses its solution (concretely, the bisection solving a $2\times2$ double step loses its bracket — an overshoot-geometry failure). On such a sub-interval of dials, $F$ is undefined, and the dial domain has a hole. Roots cannot be counted inside a hole, so a complete continuation argument must control roots at hole edges just as at $t=0$ and $t=1$. Empirically the holes sit in a single top-of-dial interval, far above the tracked root (§12, caveat 2).

Rule both events out along the whole path and the root count stays pinned at one; single-crossing — hence dial ordering, hence chain monotonicity over the surviving chain — transfers from $s=0$ to $s=1$:

The route in one sentence. The entire cascade is replaced by one transversality lemma ($F'>0$ at every zero of $F$, all $s$ — no tangency can then occur) plus boundary control ($F$ has a definite sign at the accessible boundary below the root — no root can sneak in from the edge), where boundary control must reach all four edges, the moving floor included.
Figure 2. The continuation strip. The root of $F(\cdot\,;s)$ traces a curve from the proved quadratic root at $s=0$ to the target cost's root at $s=1$. The root count can change only at a fold (a tangency $F=F'=0$, gray) or by a root crossing the domain edge (red), including the edges of march-failure holes (orange). Transversality at roots forbids the first; boundary control forbids the second. Empirically the holes sit far above the tracked root.
Relation to the archived Sard/degree-theory program. An earlier attempt ran degree-theoretic arguments on the full equilibrium system (all atoms and masses as unknowns) and stalled on uniform properness — ruling out solutions escaping to infinity along the deformation. That obstruction does not exist here: the dial lives in the compact box $[0,1]$ and the comb in a compact effort box. What replaces properness is the boundary/hole control above — a real obligation, but far more concrete, because the march's failure modes are explicit (a node bisection loses its bracket) rather than asymptotic.

5.1  The moving floor

The fourth edge is the one that does real damage, so take it slowly. Along the continuation the cost changes, so the reaches $w_1=c_s^{-1}(v_1)$ and $w_2=c_s^{-1}(v_2)$ change with it, so $f$ changes, so the dial floor $t_0$ of §3.3 changes. In the $(t,s)$-strip we therefore have two curves, not one: the root curve $t^*(s)$ and the floor curve $t_0(s)$. They can cross.

What a crossing costs. At a transversal crossing of $t^*(s)$ and $t_0(s)$ the rung stops being an equilibrium — discontinuously — while the residual $F$ does nothing remarkable at all: no tangency, no hole, no visible event of any kind. So the bookkeeping claim “the number of admissible roots is constant along $s$” is false. A continuation lemma that counts equilibria must handle this; a lemma that counts roots of $F$ never sees it.

That last sentence is the escape route, and §4 has already paid for it. The two objects have to be kept apart:

The intended resolution.
  1. Prove single-crossing of $F$ on the full march domain. $F$, the birth-mass identity, and the transversality lemma of §11 know nothing about $u_1$ and do not care where the floor sits. The floor does not touch this obligation.
  2. Apply the floor afterwards, as a post-filter. By the §4 note, the ordering of roots follows from single-crossing plus interiority of the combs alone — no appeal to individual rationality. So the roots are ordered, $t^*_{(1,1)} > t^*_{(1,2)} > \cdots$, whatever the floor is doing. And $t_0$ is a single dial value. Hence the admissibility test $t^*>t_0$ keeps an initial segment of the chain: the floor eats the chain from the deep end and cannot punch a hole in its middle.
If that holds, the theorem absorbs the moving floor exactly as it absorbs rung death (§9): chain monotonicity is asserted over the surviving prefix — which for $f>0$ is finite, and can be as short as $\{\sigma(1,1)\}$, as in the $\Delta=-0.3$ cell of §3.3. The transversality lemma is untouched.
What is still owed. The resolution above is an argument, not yet a proof. It leans on the roots being ordered before the filter is applied — single-crossing plus interiority — which is consistent with every cell measured but has been checked at only a handful of $f>0$ cells. And the interaction between the floor and negative-mass deaths (rungs that have roots but no interior comb, which the ordering argument never reaches) has not been examined at all. Until both are settled, the moving floor is an open item in the continuation lemma. It is why §13's item 3 is not bookkeeping.

At symmetric primitives $f=0$, so $t_0\equiv0$ for every $s$: the fourth edge coincides with the first, and the event cannot occur. Every number in §§6–8 was taken there, and none of them is affected. The event goes live the moment the route leaves that slice — which is where it is headed (§13, item 4).

8.1  The death of σ(1,2), deep inside the convex family

8.3  The rectangle-death ordering

Phenomenon. As the cost deforms toward the convexity boundary, rectangle rungs $\sigma(m,m+1)$ die bottom-up in $m$ — $\sigma(1,2)$ first ($s^*=0.5695$), then $\sigma(2,3)$, then $\sigma(3,4)$, with $\sigma(4,5)$ at the death's edge at $s=1$ — while every square rung $\sigma(m,m)$ survives.

This rhymes with two standing findings elsewhere in the project. Rectangles were already the fragile rung type in the asymmetric-value work — at asymmetric $v$ the square at $t_q^*$ is a non-equilibrium and the rectangle at $t_p^*$ has to carry the argument — and rung death and revival in one-parameter families is well documented on the Δ-dial and the MC(0)-dial. Cost shape is a third axis along which it happens, and the only one that does so at fully symmetric primitives.

9 · Interiority absorbs rung death

This is the probe's structural payoff, and it changes the shape of the proof obligation — so it gets the slow treatment.

The naive worry. The separator of §4 links adjacent ladder rungs. If an intermediate rung — say $\sigma(2,3)$ — dies along the path, the ladder $\sigma(2,2)\to\sigma(2,3)\to\sigma(3,3)$ has a hole, the telescoping chain of inequalities breaks, and the comparison $u_1(\sigma(2,2)) > u_1(\sigma(3,3))$ seems orphaned.

The resolution. Look again at where the birth-mass identity lives: on the shared dial recursion, not on the intermediate rung's equilibrium. Because $q_1(t), q_2(t), q_3(t), \dots$ are rung-independent functions of the dial (§3.1), the residual $F_{(2,2)}(t)=q_1(t)+q_2(t)-1$ is a perfectly good function that can be evaluated at any other rung's equilibrium dial — whether or not $\sigma(2,3)$ exists in between. Evaluate it at $\sigma(3,3)$'s dial. Since $\sigma(3,3)$'s closure is $q_1+q_2+q_3=1$ at $t^*_{(3,3)}$,

$$F_{(2,2)}\big(t^*_{(3,3)}\big) \;=\; q_1+q_2-1 \;=\; -\,q_3\big(t^*_{(3,3)}\big) \;<\; 0,$$

and the positivity of $q_3(t^*_{(3,3)})$ is nothing but $\sigma(3,3)$'s own interiority — definitional for a valid equilibrium; no rectangle needed anywhere. Combined with single-crossing of $F_{(2,2)}$, this forces $t^*_{(3,3)} < t^*_{(2,2)}$ directly, and since $u_1(t)$ is dial-level and increasing, the payoffs stay ordered across the hole.

Figure 3. Dead rungs are invisible to the ordering. The residual $F_{(2,2)}$ lives on the shared dial recursion, so it can be evaluated at $\sigma(3,3)$'s dial directly; the answer is minus $\sigma(3,3)$'s own newborn mass $q_3$ — negative by the survivor's own interiority. The dead rectangle in between is never consulted.

The probe verified this numerically across the actual Q163 holes:

$$-F_{(2,2)}\big(t^*_{(3,3)}\big) = 0.29444 = q_3 \text{ newborn at } \sigma(3,3), \qquad -F_{(3,3)}\big(t^*_{(4,4)}\big) = 0.24590.$$
General statement. For any two surviving rungs $R\to S$ with $S$ deeper in the chain — any number of dead rungs skipped in between — $F_R(t^*_S)$ equals minus the total mass that $S$'s equilibrium assigns beyond $R$'s atom set: strictly negative by $S$'s own interiority. Combined with single-crossing of $F_R$, this yields $t^*_S < t^*_R$ directly. Rung death costs the route nothing. The obligations collapse to:
  1. (definitional) each surviving rung is an interior equilibrium — free;
  2. (the lemma) single-crossing of each surviving rung's residual — §11.

11 · The one remaining lemma

Lemma (transversality at roots — target form). Fix primitives $(v_1,v_2,\Delta)$ inside the competitive range, a convex cost $c$, and a chain rung $R$. For every $s\in[0,1]$ and every $\tau$ in the interior of the march domain with $F_R(\tau;s)=0$: $$F_R'(\tau;s) \;>\; 0.$$ Moreover $F_R$ has a definite (negative) sign at the accessible boundary of the march domain below the tracked root, so no root enters from the boundary.

Two things about the statement. The root variable is $\tau$; the symbol $t_0$ stays reserved for the dial floor of §3.3. And the lemma is deliberately about $F$ on the full march domain, not on the admissible window $(t_0,1)$ — that is the §5.1 split. Transversality is a statement about the residual; individual rationality is a filter applied afterwards. Keeping the two apart is exactly what leaves this lemma untouched by the moving floor.

Three observations about why this is a better target than the cascade's lemmas:

(a)  It is aggregate, not termwise

For square rungs, $F' = \sum_{\ell\le m} q_\ell'(t)$ — the sum of the mass derivatives, i.e. aggregate mass-widening. The cascade proves $RG2_k\ge0$ node by node, which gives $q_{k+1}'\ge0$ termwise — much stronger than the sum being positive. At the $b<0$ quartic corner the termwise ladder collapses while the aggregate survives everywhere measured (the quartic findings' “$\partial_t W\ge0$ aggregate-only”; the probe: $F'$ at roots between 2.0 and 40, never near zero). The route asks precisely for the inequality that nature seems to satisfy — no more.

(b)  It is needed only at zeros of $F$

At a root, the comb is an equilibrium candidate: every partial mass is available, and the on-window facts hold.

Definition — on-window
A fact holds on-window if it is asserted only on the sub-interval of dials at or below the equilibrium root, where all partial mass sums are strictly less than one (Part II §12's window lemma is exactly this kind of statement) — as opposed to pointwise facts asserted at every dial. The cubic proof's decisive trick was realizing its inequalities on-window rather than pointwise; here, the restriction to the set $\{F=0\}$ is built into the lemma's statement from the start.

(c)  The cascade, where it works, already implies it

For $\theta\in\{2,3\}$ (and conditionally for $b\ge0$ quartics) the proven termwise results sign $F'$ everywhere, hence in particular at roots. So the homotopy route is conservative: it can only extend, never contradict, what the cascade has closed. Its added value is exactly the regimes where termwise fails but aggregate holds — $b<0$, and possibly $\theta\in(2,3)$ at a lower price than the two remaining buffered lemmas ((M-buf)+(TR-a)).

Attack lines, in rough order of promise

  1. Schur complement at the root, on-window. Part I §4's identification of $F'(t^*)$ with the Schur complement of the equilibrium Jacobian is march-structural — cost-general; only the positivity proof is quadratic. Redo the positivity argument using only on-window facts (partial masses < 1, window lemma) plus convexity. Test bench: $\theta\in(2,3)$, where the answer is known to be “true” numerically and the cascade's remaining lemmas are already buffered — if transversality-at-roots is cheaper than (M-buf)+(TR-a), the regime closes via homotopy from $\theta=2$.
  2. Joint $(t,s)$ index argument. The zero set $\{F(t;s)=0\}$ is a smooth 1-manifold wherever $(F_t,F_s)$ does not vanish simultaneously; a root can only be created or destroyed at $F=F_t=0$. One can therefore trade “sign $F_t$ at all roots” for “rule out the codimension-2 event $F=F_t=0$” — sometimes easier, because at such a point the comb would be a degenerate equilibrium candidate, and degeneracy may contradict interiority or the window structure.
  3. Boundary control as a separate, concrete lemma. The march-failure set is explicit: a node bisection loses its bracket (overshoot geometry). Empirically the failure region is a single top-of-dial interval, far above the tracked root (at the worst quartic: $\sigma(5,5)$ fails only for $t\gtrsim0.86$, $\sigma(6,6)$ for $t\gtrsim0.60$ — roots at $0.23$, $0.18$). Conjecture to prove: below the first root the march is defined and $F<0$; failure can only occur above. This would seal the boundary once and for all.

12 · Methodological caveats

  1. A first crossing of $F$ is not an equilibrium — and this trap has two faces. solve_rung returns the first up-crossing of $F$ and reports validity separately. Probe 2's first Q163 chain table read the raw crossings and was garbage at $(2,3)$ and $(3,4)$ (invalid roots, $u_1$ apparently non-monotone); probe 3's validity-tagged census supersedes it entirely. A residual root carrying a negative mass is a genuine object — it is how the second kind of rectangle death manifests — but it is not an equilibrium.

    The other face appears off symmetric primitives: the first up-crossing can sit below the dial floor $t_0$ (§3.3), where $u_1<0$ and player 1 deviates to the corner $e=0$. The march cannot see it, for the same structural reason it cannot see a negative mass — it solves equalities. Both gates live in the engine: solve_rung tests the competitive range, the floor, and individual rationality, and returns exists plus a reason (:root_below_floor, :ir_fail, :negative_mass, :decisive_lead, :no_ir_window, …) and the floor t0. A harness that reads exists inherits all of them; one that reads the raw root and marches on does not. (Trap: Julia/converge/general_march.jl defines its own ungated solve_rung. Nothing includes it today; if something ever does, alongside convex_march.jl, the later definition wins and the gates silently vanish.)
  2. March coverage holes. At Q163 the march fails on a single top-of-dial interval: $t\in[0.864,1)$ for $\sigma(5,5)/\sigma(5,6)$, $t\in[0.602,1)$ for $\sigma(6,6)$ (roots at $0.234/0.226/0.184$ — far below). Single-crossing is certified only on the covered segment; nothing can be said about roots inside a hole. The boundary conjecture (§11, attack line 3) would close this gap; the probe data are consistent with it everywhere.
  3. Grids. Dial scans at dstep 0.002–0.004 (0.002 for $m=20$), $s$-grid 0.1 (zoom 0.025), local tracking windows $\pm0.08$. The $s^*$ death point is a bisection on $\max_t F$, grid-limited to about $\pm10^{-3}$. None of the conclusions are near these resolutions except the $\sigma(4,5)$ near-death margins ($4\times10^{-5}$ separation), which were confirmed by 80-iteration bisection at machine precision and by the independent Nash check.
  4. Q081 sister uncensused (§8.4 note). Its raw pair numbers should not be quoted until a probe-3-style census is run.
  5. Scope — and why this slice is exactly the blind one. Symmetric primitives only ($v_1=v_2=1$, $\Delta=0$), chains to $m=12$ with an $m=20$ spot check. The untested region is the full primitive box $(v_1\gtrless v_2)\times(\Delta\lessgtr0)$, in both labelings.

    This matters more than a scope note usually does. The probe's slice is the one where $f=0$, hence $t_0\equiv0$, hence the admissible window is all of $(0,1)$ and the moving floor cannot bite. The numbers above are safe — and they are silent about the half of the box where the interesting failures live: a rung-death mode the probe never saw (death by the floor), and a continuation event the route's lemma does not yet handle (§5.1). The first thing a full-box probe should do is track $t_0(s)$ alongside $t^*(s)$ and look for crossings.

13 · Next steps, ordered by leverage

  1. Prototype the on-window Schur argument (attack line 1) at $\theta\in(2,3)$. Success criterion: a proof of $F'>0$ at roots whose only regime-specific input is convexity-class information already available on-window. If it lands, $\theta\in(2,3)$ closes — and the template likely covers $\theta>3$ too.
  2. Prove the boundary conjecture (attack line 3): march defined and $F<0$ strictly below the first root. This is march-geometry, not inequality-cascading, and the probe suggests it is clean.
  3. Formalize the continuation lemma, moving floor included. Tangency-free + boundary-free $\Rightarrow$ constant root count, with the march's actual regularity (piecewise-smooth in $(t,s)$, holes allowed above the root). This is not bookkeeping: the boundary has a fourth edge, the moving dial floor $t_0(s)$, and the lemma must either rule out root/floor crossings or absorb them. §5.1 gives the intended absorption — prove single-crossing on the full march domain, apply the floor as a post-filter, show it truncates the chain to a prefix rather than holing it. Turning that argument into a proof is this item.
  4. Extend the probe to the full primitive box. The box is $(v_1\gtrless v_2)\times(\Delta\lessgtr0)$, run in both labelings (§3.0), and the half that matters is $f>0$, where the floor exists and chains can truncate. Concretely: track $t_0(s)$ alongside $t^*(s)$ along the continuation and hunt for crossings; check whether the floor really eats a suffix of the chain, or whether a negative-mass death can let it punch an interior hole; then the rest — how rectangles at $t_p^*$ behave at asymmetric $v$, adaptive $s$-refinement near death points, and a census of march-failure holes near the convexity boundary. The engine gates are in place, so a harness reading exists inherits them.
  5. Characterize the rectangle-death surface: $\max_t F_{(m,m+1)}(t;c)=0$ is an implicit equation in cost space — the cost-space analogue of the (★)/(†) collapse equations from the asymmetric program. Even a one-parameter slice ($s^*(m)$ along the Q163 path: $s^*(1)=0.5695$, $s^*(4)\approx1$) would say how deaths march up the chain.
Verdict. The homotopy route survives its stress test in strengthened form. The two hypotheses that were supposed to be dangerous (existence, no-collision) turned out to be either not the route's job — existence is conditional by the theorem's own statement — or absorbable by interiority (§9); and the hypothesis that remains, $F'>0$ at every zero of $F$, is an aggregate-at-roots inequality that held with growing margins in every regime measured, including the corner that falsifies all of the cascade's termwise anchors.

That is a verdict about cost space, taken along one slice of primitive space. On the symmetric slice the route is one lemma away. On the full box it is not, until the moving floor of §5.1 is either ruled out or absorbed — and no number here speaks to that, because at these primitives the floor is identically zero.
