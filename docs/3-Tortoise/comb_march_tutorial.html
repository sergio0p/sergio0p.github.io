The comb march, from scratch

A constructive tutorial on the forward shooting algorithm that builds the mixed equilibria $\sigma(m,n)$ — for an arbitrary strictly convex cost $c$ (with $c(0)=0$), asymmetric valuations $v_1\neq v_2$, and a lead $\Delta\neq0$.

Summary: The comb march computes a two-round all-pay contest equilibrium by a single one-dimensional shoot. It treats one number — $q_1$, the trailing player's mass on the zero atom — as a dial. Turning the dial fixes everything else by forward substitution: a strictly triangular cascade of double steps, each of which solves a $2\times2$ system (one first-order condition + one indifference condition) by slaving a probability mass to the FOC and then bisecting a single atom location. The cascade leaves exactly one leftover mass, pinned by "probabilities sum to one"; the other normalization becomes a scalar residual $F(q_1)$, and the equilibrium is the dial value with $F(q_1)=0$. Everything below is the anatomy of that sentence, with arrow diagrams, the exact equations the solver evaluates, and three fully worked numerical traces you can re-derive by hand. A closing correctness checklist is the part to read if your goal is to audit the algorithm.
Prerequisites & scope. This page is self-contained but assumes you know what a mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium is. It is the missing companion to the derivation docs, which assume the march rather than explain it: symmetric_quadratic_recursion.html (the quadratic closed form), power_cost_recursion.html (power costs), monotonicity_general_case.html and Monotone_Status.html (what is proven about the dial). The algorithm itself lives in Julia/converge/convex_march.jl (general convex cost) and its power-cost sibling general_march.jl; the interactive front-end is Julia/solver/web_march/. Nomenclature here matches QUICKSTART.md §0/§2 exactly.

1. The game: a two-round all-pay contest

Two players compete in two rounds. Each round, a player exerts effort $e\ge0$ and pays its cost $c(e)$ whether or not it wins — that is what "all-pay" means; the cost is sunk. The cost $c$ is the same for both players and any round: it satisfies $c(0)=0$, $c'>0$, and is strictly convex ($c''>0$ on $e>0$). The whole tutorial works for any such $c$; the quadratic $c(e)=e^2$ is just the case with closed forms.

The primitives are written $\Gamma(\Delta,v_1,v_2)$:

  • Valuations $v_1\ge v_2\ge 0$: the prize each player attaches to ultimately winning.
  • Lead $\Delta$: a head-start (score difference) player 1 carries into round 2. The round-2 lead is $\Delta_2=\Delta+e_1-e_2$, so round-1 effort buys position for round 2.

1.1 Round 2 is a Siegel contest — the Lemma 1 continuation

The last round, taken on its own with a fixed entering lead $\Delta_2$, is a static all-pay contest with a head start. Its value to each player — what they get by playing round 2 optimally — is a piecewise, kinked function of $\Delta_2$, the "Lemma 1" continuation. Writing the reach $w_i=c^{-1}(v_i)$ (the effort that would exactly burn the whole prize, defined in §3):

$$ U^{T}_1(\Delta_2)=\begin{cases}0,& \Delta_2\le w_2-w_1\\[2pt] v_1-c(w_2-\Delta_2),& w_2-w_1<\Delta_2\lt w_2\\[2pt] v_1,& \Delta_2\ge w_2\end{cases} \qquad U^{T}_2(\Delta_2)=\begin{cases}v_2,& \Delta_2\le -w_1\\[2pt] v_2-c(w_1+\Delta_2),& -w_1<\Delta_2\lt w_2-w_1\\[2pt] 0,& \Delta_2\ge w_2-w_1\end{cases} $$
The one easy mistake. In its interior branch, player 1's continuation carries the rival's reach $w_2$ (inside $c(w_2-\Delta_2)$), and player 2's carries $w_1$. The two interior branches cross zero together at the pivot $\Delta_2=w_2-w_1$. In the symmetric case $w_1=w_2=w$ the pivot collapses to $0$ and a single $w$ hides this — do not mechanically replace $w$ by $w_i$ when you generalize.

Three regions, three economic stories: (i) so far behind that round 2 is hopeless → continuation $0$ for P1; (ii) a contested interior where effort matters; (iii) so far ahead the prize is locked → continuation $v_1$. The march only ever lives in region (ii), the interior branch; the outer two are the "free-win / abandonment" branches, and a solve that lands on them is a wrong-regime non-answer (§10).

1.2 Round 1: the reduced game each player maximizes

Fold round 2 into its value. In round 1 each player picks effort, pays for it now, and collects the continuation through the induced lead $\Delta_2=\Delta+e_1-e_2$. The reduced round-1 payoffs are

$$ u_1(e_1,e_2)=U^{T}_1(\Delta+e_1-e_2)-c(e_1),\qquad u_2(e_1,e_2)=U^{T}_2(\Delta+e_1-e_2)-c(e_2). $$

Because the rival's effort enters through the continuation (not merely a win indicator), $\Gamma$ is itself not a standard contest, and $\Delta$ does not change-of-variables away. This reduced game is the object the comb march solves.

1.3 Labelling: player 2 is the zero-payoff player

By relabelling we always arrange that the player whose equilibrium payoff is zero is player 2: $u_2^\ast=0$. This is the player whose individual-rationality (participation) constraint binds. One consequence is used everywhere below: player 2's lowest atom sits at zero effort, $y_1=0$. Because it is a boundary point, P2's optimality there is an inequality, not an equality — so $y_1$ carries no FOC. Player 1 is the "favored"/leading player and earns $u_1^\ast=u_1\ge 0$.

2. What the march outputs: the comb $\sigma(m,n)$

In equilibrium each player randomizes over a finite set of effort levels. We call those levels atoms and their probabilities masses:

symbolmeaning
$x_1\lt x_2<\dots\lt x_m$player 1's $m$ atoms (effort levels it mixes over)
$p_1,\dots,p_m$player 1's masses, $\sum_k p_k=1$
$y_1\lt y_2<\dots\lt y_n$player 2's $n$ atoms, with $y_1=0$
$q_1,\dots,q_n$player 2's masses, $\sum_\ell q_\ell=1$; $q_1$ is the dial
$u_1$P1's common equilibrium payoff (its value across all its atoms)

$\sigma(m,n)$ denotes the equilibrium in which P1 uses $m$ atoms and P2 uses $n$. Each player is indifferent across its own support: every atom it actually uses yields the same payoff — $u_1$ for P1, and $0$ for P2.

2.1 The comb: interleaving and why $n\in\{m,m+1\}$

The defining geometric fact is that the two players' atoms interleave on the effort axis. In the shifted coordinate $\tilde y_\ell=y_\ell+f$ of §3 they alternate strictly:

$$ \tilde y_1\lt x_1<\tilde y_2\lt x_2<\tilde y_3<\cdots $$

This alternating chain of teeth is the comb. Between two consecutive rival atoms a player's payoff is strictly concave and admits exactly one local maximum, so it can plant exactly one atom there. That forces the two supports to run in lockstep, $n=m$, or with P2 exactly one tooth ahead, $n=m+1$. Hence the entire equilibrium family is the alternating ladder

$$ \sigma(1,1)\to\sigma(1,2)\to\sigma(2,2)\to\sigma(2,3)\to\sigma(3,3)\to\cdots $$

The two shapes have a name worth fixing now, because the closing residual differs between them (§7):

  • Square rung $\sigma(m,m)$: the topmost atom of the whole comb is P1's $x_m$.
  • Step-up rung $\sigma(m,m+1)$: the topmost atom is P2's $y_{m+1}$.
Figure 1. A solved comb (the live trace of §8). The top atom is $x_2$, so this is a square rung and $p_2$ is the leftover mass. Atoms interleave; masses sit under each tooth.

3. The coordinate machinery: reach, shift, $\tilde y$, $\tilde c$

Four definitions turn the kinked Lemma-1 payoffs into one clean set of equations. They are exactly the quantities the solver computes first.

3.1 Reach $w_i=c^{-1}(v_i)$

The reach of player $i$ is the effort at which the cost equals the prize, $c(w_i)=v_i$. Beyond $w_i$ a player would spend more than the prize is worth, so reaches bound the supports. For $c(e)=e^2$ and $v=1$, $w=1$; for $v_1=1.2$, $w_1=\sqrt{1.2}\approx1.095$. The solver finds $w_i$ in closed form for power costs and by bracket-and-bisect otherwise (reach_numeric).

3.2 Shift $f$ and the shifted P2 frame $\tilde y$

Define the shift $$ f \;=\; w_2-w_1-\Delta, $$ and the shifted P2 atoms $\tilde y_\ell=y_\ell+f$. Since $y_1=0$, the lowest shifted atom is $\tilde y_1=f$. The shift is the change of variables that lines the two combs up: it cancels $f$ out of the interleaving order and out of every win-margin, so that both players' "beat-the-rival" terms become $c(w_i+\text{rival atom}-\text{own atom})$ with no stray $\Delta$. The asymmetry does not vanish — it survives in two places: the values $v_1\neq v_2$ make the two indifference ladders non-mirror, and $f$ reappears only on P2's own-cost side, because P2's effort is $y=\tilde y-f$. Read it as: P2 plays the symmetric comb, but against the translated cost $c(\,\cdot-f\,)$.

Figure 2. The shift $f=w_2-w_1-\Delta$ moves the whole P2 support into the frame where it interleaves with P1. (Numbers from the asymmetric trace of §9: $v_1=1.2,\,v_2=1,\,\Delta=0.1\Rightarrow f=-0.195$.)

3.3 The zero-extension $\tilde c$

Define $\tilde c(e)=c(e)$ for $e\ge0$ and $\tilde c(e)=0$ for $e<0$ (and likewise $\tilde c'(e)=0$ for $e<0$). This single convention makes one formula simultaneously correct on Lemma-1's interior branch and its free-win branch: when a win-margin argument goes negative — meaning the player has already locked the win — $\tilde c$ reads $0$ and the payoff collapses to the flat prize. In the code this is the line cp(e)=e>0 ? … : 0.0. (It does not rescue the abandonment branch, where the continuation is $0$ for a different reason; that must be excluded structurally — see wrong regime, §10.)

3.4 The dial $q_1$

The whole march hangs from one free number: $q_1=t$, player 2's mass on the zero atom $y_1=0$. In the quadratic closed form it appears directly, e.g. $u_1^\ast=q_1^2/(1+q_1)$. Why this number is the unique degree of freedom is §5.

4. The two equations living at every atom

At each interior atom of each player there are exactly two equilibrium conditions. They do different jobs, and the march exploits the division of labour.

First-order condition (FOC) — uses $c'$, locates the atom. An atom is a local maximum of that player's reduced payoff, so the derivative vanishes there. The marginal cost of effort equals the mass-weighted marginal gain from out-bidding the rival atoms below.

Indifference condition — uses $c$, ties the support and pins masses & the value. Every atom in a player's support yields the same payoff ($u_1$ for P1, $0$ for P2). This is what makes a mixed strategy optimal.

In the shifted frame, with P1 summing over rival atoms at or below ($\ell\le k$) and P2 summing strictly below, the conditions read:

Player 1, at its atom $x_k$:

$$ \underbrace{\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\,c'(w_1+\tilde y_\ell-x_k)=c'(x_k)}_{\text{FOC}} \qquad \underbrace{\sum_{\ell\le k} q_\ell\bigl[v_1-c(w_1+\tilde y_\ell-x_k)\bigr]-c(x_k)=u_1}_{\text{indifference}} $$

Player 2, at its atom $\tilde y_\ell$ (none at $\tilde y_1=f$, the boundary atom):

$$ \underbrace{\sum_{k<\ell} p_k\,c'(w_2+x_k-\tilde y_\ell)=c'(\tilde y_\ell-f)}_{\text{FOC}} \qquad \underbrace{\sum_{k<\ell} p_k\bigl[v_2-c(w_2+x_k-\tilde y_\ell)\bigr]-c(\tilde y_\ell-f)=0}_{\text{indifference}} $$

Note the asymmetric truncation — P1 ties the rival at equal effort ($\ell\le k$), P2 breaks it strictly ($k<\ell$). That single asymmetry is why $x_1>0$ but $y_1=0$.

4.1 The value identity

P1's value $u_1$ is itself unknown, but it is fixed by the lowest atom and then reused. Evaluating P1's indifference at $x_1$ (where the only rival atom at-or-below is $\tilde y_1=f$):

$$ u_1 \;=\; q_1\bigl[v_1-c(w_1+f-x_1)\bigr]-c(x_1),\qquad\text{and recall } w_1+f=w_2-\Delta. $$

This is the value identity. In the symmetric quadratic case it simplifies to the universal $u_1^\ast=q_1^2/(1+q_1)$ (strictly increasing in the dial). Once $u_1$ is in hand, every later P1 indifference equation is "$=u_1$", which is what lets the cascade march forward.

5. Why it is triangular: one source, the march order

Naively, $\sigma(m,n)$ is a big simultaneous nonlinear system: $2m$ P1 conditions, $2(n-1)$ P2 conditions, two normalizations, in the unknowns $\{x_k,p_k,y_\ell,q_\ell,u_1\}$. The key structural observation is that the dependency graph is acyclic with a single source, the dial $q_1$. Each unknown is produced by one equation that references only earlier-produced unknowns. So you never solve the system jointly — you forward-substitute.

The trick that makes each link a one-liner: the "moments" that appear (the running sums $\sum q_\ell$, $\sum q_\ell c'(\cdots)$, …) are totals of masses already placed. A newly produced mass folds into the totals only downstream of where it is used, so there is no cycle. The order is:

Figure 3. The march as a directed acyclic graph with a single source $q_1$. Each arrow is "solve one equation using only what is already to its left." Steps B and C alternate; Figure shows the $\sigma(2,2)$ instance. The last Step B (k=2) is vestigial for a square rung — see §6.3.

Spelled out linearly, the order is

$$ q_1 \;\to\; x_1 \;\to\; u_1 \;\to\; (p_1,\tilde y_2)\;\to\;(q_2,x_2)\;\to\;(p_2,\tilde y_3)\;\to\;(q_3,x_3)\;\to\cdots $$

Stationarity (the FOC half of each double step) reads each player's atoms off the rival's masses; indifference (the other half) reads each player's masses off the rival's atoms. They cross-feed, which is why the comb grows one tooth at a time, alternating colours.

6. The double-step engine

Every link "$(p_k,\tilde y_{k+1})$" or "$(q_{k+1},x_{k+1})$" is a $2\times2$ system: one new atom and one new mass, pinned by one FOC and one indifference. The engine reduces each to a single 1-D bisection by a slaving trick. Read this section with the code open at convex_march.jl lines 229–276; the names below are the code's names.

Figure 4. The double step. The FOC is linear in the new mass, so the mass is slaved in closed form; back-substitution leaves a single scalar residual in the new atom, solved by bisection. This avoids the spurious all-zeros root a joint Newton solve would find.

6.1 Step B — place the next P2 atom $\tilde y_{k+1}$, slave P1 mass $p_k$

Write the P2 win-margin $s_B(i,\tilde y)=w_2+x_i-\tilde y$. Solve P2's FOC at $\tilde y$ for the new mass (it appears linearly):

$$ p_k=\mathrm{Pof}(\tilde y)=\frac{c'(\tilde y-f)-\sum_{i\lt k}p_i\,c'(s_B(i,\tilde y))}{c'(s_B(k,\tilde y))}. $$

Substitute into P2's indifference (which equals $0$) to get the scalar residual, and bisect for $\tilde y_{k+1}$ on the bracket $\bigl(x_k,\;x_k+w_2\bigr)$:

$$ g_B(\tilde y)=\sum_{i\lt k}p_i\bigl[v_2-c(s_B(i,\tilde y))\bigr]+\mathrm{Pof}(\tilde y)\bigl[v_2-c(s_B(k,\tilde y))\bigr]-c(\tilde y-f)=0. $$

6.2 Step C — place the next P1 atom $x_{k+1}$, slave P2 mass $q_{k+1}$

Symmetric, with P1 win-margin $r_C(\ell,x)=w_1+\tilde y_\ell-x$, the new mass from P1's FOC,

$$ q_{k+1}=\mathrm{Qof}(x)=\frac{c'(x)-\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell\,c'(r_C(\ell,x))}{c'(r_C(k+1,x))}, $$

and the indifference residual (which equals $u_1$, not $0$ — that is the only difference from Step B), bisected for $x_{k+1}$ on $\bigl(x_k,\;w_1\bigr)$:

$$ h_C(x)=\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell\bigl[v_1-c(r_C(\ell,x))\bigr]+\mathrm{Qof}(x)\bigl[v_1-c(r_C(k+1,x))\bigr]-c(x)-u_1=0. $$
Why a quadratic gets a formula and a general cost gets a bisection. When $c(e)=e^2$, every margin term $c(\cdot)$ is a quadratic in the atom, so $g_B,h_C$ are quadratics with one in-window root — solvable in closed form (that is the $G_i=t/(1+it)$ recursion of the quadratic doc). For a general convex $c$, strict convexity still guarantees a unique root inside the interleaving window (the payoff is strictly concave on each cell), but you find it numerically. Nothing else changes — "closed form is a quadratic bonus," the march itself is cost-agnostic.

6.3 A subtlety worth knowing: the vestigial last Step B on a square rung

The loop runs Step B then (unless it is the last) Step C, for $k=1,\dots,m$. On a square rung $\sigma(m,m)$ the final iteration $k=m$ still executes a Step B, which dutifully slaves a mass $p_m$ and places a tooth $\tilde y_{m+1}$ — but $\sigma(m,m)$ has no $(m{+}1)$-th P2 atom. Both outputs are discarded: $\tilde y_{m+1}$ never enters the residual, and $p_m$ is overwritten by the leftover $1-\sum_{k\lt m}p_k$ (§7). Its only live role is the convergence flag — if that bisection returns NaN the rung is marked non-converged. You will see this in the trace of §8: the marched $p_2=0.6075$ is not the reported $p_2=0.3946$. This is expected, not a bug; the generic loop simply overruns by one harmless half-step. (For step-up rungs the analogous early stop is deliberate and load-bearing — see the long comment at convex_march.jl:250.)

7. Closing the system: the leftover mass and the dial shoot

The forward sweep, seeded by $q_1$, produces every atom, $u_1$, and every mass except one. The exception is the mass on the topmost atom of the whole comb: there is no rival atom above it, so no rival indifference condition ever references it. It is a free leftover, pinned by its own player's "sum to one." That leaves the other normalization unused — and that is the scalar equation the dial must satisfy.

rungtop atomleftover mass (from $\sum=1$)closing residual $F(q_1)$ (shot to $0$)
square $\sigma(m,m)$$x_m$ (P1)$p_m=1-\sum_{k\lt m}p_k$$F=\sum_{\ell=1}^{m} q_\ell-1$
step-up $\sigma(m,m{+}1)$$y_{m+1}$ (P2)$q_{m+1}=1-\sum_{\ell\lt m+1}q_\ell$$F=\sum_{k=1}^{m} p_k-1$

So an apparently $m^2$-sized elimination collapses to one unknown, one equation: find the dial $q_1^\ast$ with $F(q_1^\ast)=0$. The solver sweeps $q_1$ across $(0,1)$, keeps only converged points, locates the first up-crossing of $F$, and bisects it. Empirically $F$ is strictly increasing with a single crossing on every rung tested — the constructive route to existence and uniqueness of $\sigma(m,n)$ — though "$F$ single-crossing for all convex $c$" is exactly the open monotonicity question that Monotone_Status.html tracks.

10. The verdict: when a comb is (not) an equilibrium

The march will run on inputs that have no equilibrium of the requested shape; it returns a comb anyway (the "failed comb"). Validity is decided by two post-hoc output checks, never by blocking the march. Both are worth understanding, because a passing march is necessary but not sufficient.

10.1 Admissibility — are all masses positive? (the death mechanisms)

A comb is admissible iff every mass is strictly positive and the interleaving is strict. diagnose reports which constraint binds first as a knob ($\Delta$, $v_1$, or cost curvature) is pushed. Because masses are gap quantities (e.g. $q_{k+1}\propto$ the off-diagonal gap $\tilde y_{k+1}-x_k$), mass positivity is interleaving, and a death is a tooth-collision:

  • SUPPORT-merge $x_k\ge y_k^{\text{raw}}$: P1's atom fails to cover P2's physical atom — opens a profitable upward deviation for P1. (The binding margin in both §8 and §9.1.)
  • APEX-mass collapse $p_m\to0$: the favored player's leftover top mass vanishes.
  • $\tilde y$-diagonal $x_k-\tilde y_k\to0$ (this is $p_k$) and $\tilde y$-off-diagonal $\tilde y_{k+1}-x_k\to0$ (this is $q_{k+1}$): interior teeth merging in the shifted frame.
  • no $q_1$ root: the dial shoot found no $F=0$ — there is no candidate comb at all.

10.2 Global best response — the only deviation the march does not pin

A fully assembled comb pins indifference + FOC at every support atom, including each player's top atom, so all between-atom and below-top deviations are clamped by construction. Exactly one deviation is left unconstrained: a jump strictly above the combined top of support $T$. And it can pay off for only one player — the non-owner of the apex:

Figure 6. Above the combined top $T$, the apex owner's payoff only falls (no rival atoms above to win), so the owner is automatically safe. The non-owner can open one fresh concave hump by overtaking the apex; its peak gain is the genuine equilibrium test.

So the check (check_best_response) computes a single number: the non-owner's best payoff strictly above $T$, minus its support value. On a square rung the apex is P1's, so it tests P2 (value $0$): gain $g_2$. On a step-up rung the apex is P2's, so it tests P1 (value $u_1$): gain $g_1$. The rung is a genuine Nash equilibrium iff that gain is $\le 0$. In §8, $g_2=-0.042$ — P2 strictly prefers to stay; equilibrium confirmed. A comb that solves the system but has a positive gain is the deviable verdict — it is the failure that kills $\sigma(1,1)$ past a cost-curvature threshold $\theta^\star$.

10.3 The five front-end verdicts

The GUI (web_march) collapses all of the above into five labels:

verdictmeaningcomb drawn?
solutionmarch closed, all masses $>0$, interior branch, no profitable deviation — a genuine $\sigma(m,n)$.yes
negative masscomb closed but a mass came out $\le0$ (a death of §10.1) — support over-built.yes (failed comb)
deviablemasses fine, but a profitable upward deviation exists (§10.2), or the slack player's participation payoff goes negative (the blue $U_A$ curve "sinks" below $0$).yes (failed comb)
wrong regimethe solve landed on a free-win / abandonment branch, not the all-interior comb.no
no convergenceno dial root $q_1^\ast$ — no candidate comb to draw.no

The front-end's "butterfly" figure draws P1's payoff $U_A$ upward (blue), P2's $U_B$ downward (red); atoms are interleaved teeth on the axis; the shift $f$ is a frame note, not a drawn glyph. A failed comb is still drawn so you can see the death — teeth merging (negative mass) or the blue curve diving under the zero floor (a sunk participation curve = deviable).

11. A correctness checklist

If your aim is to audit the algorithm, these are the invariants to verify — each is cheap and each catches a distinct class of bug.

  1. Setup constants. $w_i=c^{-1}(v_i)$ should satisfy $c(w_i)=v_i$ to machine precision; $f=w_2-w_1-\Delta$; and $w_1+f=w_2-\Delta$ (the identity the $x_1$ step relies on).
  2. Value identity. Recompute $u_1=q_1[v_1-c(w_1+f-x_1)]-c(x_1)$ independently and confirm it equals P1's payoff evaluated at every $x_k$ (indifference). For $c=e^2$ it must equal $q_1^2/(1+q_1)$.
  3. FOC residuals. At each atom, plug the final profile back into the FOC of §4 and confirm it is $0$: $\sum_{\ell\le k}q_\ell c'(w_1+\tilde y_\ell-x_k)-c'(x_k)=0$, and the P2 analogue. (These are not the equations the march re-uses to close, so they are an independent check.)
  4. Indifference residuals. P1's payoff equal across $\{x_k\}$ (to $u_1$); P2's equal across $\{y_\ell\}$ (to $0$), including the boundary $y_1=0$.
  5. Interleaving & masses. $\tilde y_1\lt x_1<\tilde y_2<\cdots$ strictly; all $p_k,q_\ell>0$; $\sum p=\sum q=1$. A negative mass is a non-existence, not a numerical hiccup.
  6. Closing residual. $F(q_1^\ast)\approx0$ to bisection tolerance; and $F$ should be monotone through the root on the scanned grid (if it is not, the single-crossing assumption is violated for that input — a research signal, logged as nFcross).
  7. Leftover consistency. On a square rung, confirm you understand that the marched $p_m$ is discarded and $p_m=1-\sum_{k\lt m}p_k$ is reported (§6.3). Do not "fix" the discrepancy.
  8. Global best response. The non-owner's above-$T$ gain $\le0$; the owner's is identically $0$. A positive gain ⇒ deviable, not a solution, however clean the comb looks.
  9. Cross-checks. PowerCost(2.0) must equal PolyCost([0,0,1]) (two routes to $e^2$) to $\sim10^{-9}$; and the whole march must match the independent Newton engine to $\sim10^{-11}$ (convex_march_xval.jl). A power cost must reproduce general_march.jl bit-for-bit.
  10. Regime. Confirm every win-margin argument is positive (interior branch), i.e. you are not silently on a free-win/abandonment branch (wrong regime).
